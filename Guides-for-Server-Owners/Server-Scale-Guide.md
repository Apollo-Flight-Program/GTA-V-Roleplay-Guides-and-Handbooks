# Server Scale Guide

Running a ten-player server and running a thousand-player server are not variations of the same job. They are fundamentally different operations — different pacing, different staff needs, different economies, different story rhythms, and entirely different failure modes. This guide breaks down what changes at each scale and what you need to do differently at each tier.

The most common mistake server owners make is running a fifty-player server with the systems of a five-hundred-player server, or vice versa. Complexity that protects a large server will strangle a small one. Simplicity that works beautifully at ten players collapses under its own weight at five hundred.

Know your scale. Build for it.

---

## Micro Server — Up to 10 Players

### What This Scale Feels Like

A micro server is not a small version of a big server. It is something closer to a tabletop campaign — an intimate, character-driven experience where every person in the world is known to every other person, IC and OOC. There are no strangers. There are no background NPCs. Everyone is somebody.

This is both its greatest strength and its greatest fragility.

### Story and Pacing

At this scale, **the players are the story**. There is no need to invent dramatic events — the relationships, rivalries, and tensions between ten real people are already enough. A GM at this scale is less of an event designer and more of a facilitator: someone who asks the right questions, introduces the right complications, and keeps threads moving when they stall.

Crime at this scale should be rare and consequential. A single robbery is a city-wide event. A murder is seismic. Do not push event frequency — let moments breathe and let their consequences fully unfold before introducing the next disruption.

Investigation RP thrives here. With only a handful of suspects and a community small enough that everyone knows everyone else's habits, even a simple crime can generate weeks of genuine detective work, suspicion, alliances, and betrayal.

### Economy

A micro-server economy is extremely fragile. One or two players with a disproportionate share of resources can destabilize the entire system. Keep the economy simple and flat — minimal wealth gap, straightforward income sources, and no systems that allow exponential accumulation. The goal is not realism; it is keeping everyone able to participate in scenes without feeling economically left behind.

Avoid complex business systems, stock markets, or multi-tier property ownership at this scale. They are not wrong — they are just premature.

### Staff Structure

You do not need a staff hierarchy at ten players. You need one trusted, fair person with admin tools who plays the game alongside everyone else. Formalizing a staff structure at this scale creates artificial distance and bureaucratic overhead where none is needed.

The server owner typically handles everything: rule clarification, moderation, and GM duties simultaneously.

### What to Encourage

- Long-form character development scenes — conversations, relationships, backstory reveals
- Persistent consequences that affect the whole community (everyone was in the bar when it happened)
- Player-driven plot hooks rather than scripted GM events
- Direct character-to-character conflict and resolution
- World-building through player histories and shared lore

### What to Avoid

- Complex faction systems with formal ranks and territories (not enough people to sustain them)
- High-frequency action events — they burn out a small community fast
- Economy systems with high income variance
- Large-scale department structures (one-person police department should not have a chain of command with six ranks)

### Common Pitfalls

**One dominant player.** In a ten-person server, one highly active or aggressive player can shape the entire server's direction. If their vision conflicts with everyone else's, or if they become the de facto protagonist of every story, other players disengage. Actively distribute narrative weight.

**Over-formalization.** Some owners at this scale try to build out a full government, economy, and faction structure before the player base can sustain it. Empty systems create a ghost town feeling even when people are online.

**OOC drama bleeding into IC.** Small communities are gossip machines. When players know each other closely, personal OOC tensions contaminate IC storylines fast. Enforce the IC/OOC separation firmly and early.

## Small Server — 11 to 30 Players

### What This Scale Feels Like

The small server is the first scale where genuine faction dynamics become possible. You have enough players to sustain two or three distinct groups — a law enforcement presence, one or two criminal factions, and a civilian population — without everyone being in the same one. For the first time, groups can form with their own internal culture, hierarchy, and goals.

The server starts to feel like a world rather than a room.

### Story and Pacing

Faction identity drives story at this scale. The rivalry between two gangs, the tension between a well-organized police department and a clever criminal organization, the political intrigue between civilian businesses — these are the engines of narrative. A GM's primary job is to ensure faction tensions remain productive rather than collapsing into pure conflict loops.

Crime is still relatively infrequent — perhaps two or three significant incidents per week — but now there are enough players that response and investigation can be properly staffed. An investigation has a detective, witnesses, suspects who have their own motivations, and a community watching the outcome. This is where investigation RP earns its full dramatic weight.

**Prolonged investigation is not just possible here — it is the correct pacing.** A robbery that takes three sessions to investigate, with false leads, corrupted witnesses, and a reveal that implicates someone unexpected, is the kind of story players remember and talk about for months.

### Economy

At thirty players, the economy begins to have meaningful mass. You can support legitimate businesses with actual customer bases, criminal income pipelines that require multiple participants, and some variance in player wealth without it feeling broken.

Introduce business systems cautiously. A café owner, a mechanic, a gun dealer — these work well because they create economic relationships between players. Complex investment or property systems are still premature.

### Staff Structure

Two to three staff members is appropriate at this scale. A clear lead admin for rule enforcement and one or two moderators is sufficient. A GM role, separate from moderation, becomes genuinely valuable here — someone dedicated to watching player interactions and pulling narrative threads.

Do not build a staff hierarchy beyond three tiers. The overhead of maintaining a complex staff structure with four levels is not justified by thirty players.

### What to Encourage

- Inter-faction diplomacy, alliances, and betrayals
- Shared server history — events that become referenced lore ("remember when the Moretti family burned down the hardware store")
- Investigation RP extending across multiple sessions
- Player-run businesses and services that create economic interdependence
- New player mentorship built into faction onboarding (each faction sponsors its own recruits)

### What to Avoid

- Too many competing factions — two criminal groups maximum at this scale; more fractures the player base too thin
- Rushing to the next event before the current one's consequences have played out
- Allowing one faction to become so dominant that others stop competing
- Whitelisted role lock-outs — at this scale, rigid application gatekeeping keeps the server permanently understaffed

### Common Pitfalls

**Faction imbalance.** One faction recruits more actively and suddenly has fifteen of the server's thirty players. The other groups feel outnumbered and leave. Monitor faction population actively and manage it before it becomes a problem.

**Consequence amnesia.** Events happen, things change, and then the server acts as if nothing occurred. At thirty players, there are enough people to remember — and they will be disappointed when the world does not. Track outcomes and build on them.

**New player trickle causing culture drift.** Each new player at this scale represents more than three percent of the population. A cluster of new arrivals with different expectations can shift the server's culture visibly. Onboarding and mentorship are not optional at this scale.

## Medium Server — 31 to 100 Players

### What This Scale Feels Like

The medium server is the first tier where the city feels genuinely populated. There are enough people that you cannot know everyone, enough activity that something is usually happening in multiple parts of the map simultaneously, and enough economic mass that a real player-driven economy is possible.

This is also the first tier where the server can sustain players who are not heavily invested in the main narrative — people who run legitimate businesses, work odd jobs, or just live their character's life without being entangled in major events. The world feels inhabited rather than staged.

### Story and Pacing

At this scale, major crimes — bank robberies, kidnappings, high-value heists — happen roughly once a week. This is not a limitation; it is an opportunity. When an event is rare, it carries weight. Every player in the server knows about the bank robbery. It becomes a shared reference point. The investigation that follows has genuine stakes because the whole community is watching.

**This is the ideal scale for extended, multi-session investigation RP.** One major crime can sustain a full week of in-character activity: initial response, evidence gathering, false leads, witness interviews, political pressure on the police department, criminal attempts to destroy evidence, corrupt officials being leaned on, and ultimately a conclusion that either brings justice or fails visibly and consequentially.

Do not rush this. A GM who resolves a major case in one session is leaving ninety percent of the dramatic potential on the table. Let it breathe. Let the wrong person be suspected. Let the investigation become personal.

Between major events, the city runs on ambient RP — business disputes, personal character arcs, small-scale crimes, faction politics. The GM's role between events is maintenance and seeding, not driving.

### Economy

The medium server's economy can support genuine player-driven complexity. Businesses have real customer bases. Criminal supply chains require coordination. Legitimate and illegitimate economic flows intersect in interesting ways — a legal business that launders money, a restaurant that serves as a meeting point for organized crime, a mechanic who chops stolen vehicles.

Design the economy with built-in roles rather than pure sandbox systems. "There is demand for a weapons supplier" gives a player something to pursue and creates automatic economic relationships. Purely emergent economies at this scale often fail to generate engagement quickly enough to retain new players.

### Staff Structure

A functioning medium server requires five to eight staff members with clear role separation:

```
Server Owner
└── Head Admin
    ├── Senior Admin (2)
    │   └── Moderator (2-3)
    └── Game Master (1-2, separate track)
```

The GM track should be explicitly separate from the moderation track. A moderator's job is to enforce rules and resolve disputes. A GM's job is to create content. These are incompatible focuses when combined in one person.

### What to Encourage

- Multi-session investigation arcs as the dominant response to major crimes
- Reputation systems — players becoming known IC for specific behaviors, skills, or allegiances
- Inter-departmental conflict and cooperation (police vs. district attorney, two rival gangs negotiating, legitimate businesses choosing sides)
- Player-driven server news — an IC newspaper, radio station, or social media presence that documents what happens
- Department hierarchy and structure within factions (internal promotions, internal politics)

### What to Avoid

- Daily major events — they cheapen the weight of each one and exhaust your GM team
- One-session investigation resolution — resist the temptation to wrap up a case tidily and quickly
- Leaving the city "empty" between major events — ambient RP support (business events, faction meetings, character-driven scenes) needs active GM attention
- Power vacuums — if a dominant faction collapses, fill the vacuum deliberately rather than letting it create server-wide paralysis

### Common Pitfalls

**GM exhaustion.** A medium server creates enough demand for content that a single GM burns out quickly. Two GMs with shared lore documentation, complementary shifts, and explicit handoff processes is the minimum viable structure.

**Griefer infiltration.** At this scale, bad actors begin to appear regularly — players whose goal is to disrupt RP rather than participate in it. The moderation and GM tracks being separate ensures that GM work is not constantly interrupted by moderation demands.

**Wealth inequality becoming a narrative problem.** Some players have been on the server since launch and have accumulated significant resources. New players arrive with nothing. The gap in character capability between a veteran player and a newcomer starts to feel unfair. Introduce soft economy resets, active revenue caps, or high-cost prestige systems that drain accumulated wealth naturally.

## Large Server — 101 to 500 Players

### What This Scale Feels Like

The large server operates more like a living city than a managed story. At five hundred players, the server is active across multiple time zones, there are always people online, and more roleplay is happening simultaneously than any one person can observe. The GM cannot watch everything. The admin team cannot respond to everything in real time. Systems start to matter more than individual oversight.

This is the first scale where the server begins to feel genuinely emergent — things happen that nobody planned, stories develop without GM involvement, and the world evolves through player action rather than GM design. That is the goal. It is also the primary challenge.

### Story and Pacing

At five hundred players, significant crimes — robberies, kidnappings, organized crime operations — happen multiple times per week and sometimes daily. This changes the calculus of investigation RP fundamentally.

**Not every crime warrants a full investigation arc.** At this scale, attempting to extend every robbery into a multi-session investigation would overwhelm the law enforcement player base and create a perpetual backlog that never resolves. Instead:

- **Tier your crimes.** Small crimes (petty theft, assault, street-level drug deals) are handled and closed quickly — a patrol response, an arrest, and a court date if applicable. No extended investigation.
- **Reserve deep investigation for major events.** A cartel hit, a political assassination, a major heist — these earn the full multi-session treatment. But they must be genuinely rare to hold that weight.
- **Let some cases go cold.** In a real city, not every crime is solved. An unsolved murder that lingers as an open case is more narratively interesting than a forced resolution. Cold cases can be reopened, can generate their own stories, can become political pressure points.

The GM team at this scale shifts from storytelling to **world maintenance**. They are less focused on running individual events and more focused on ensuring the factions, economy, and power structures remain balanced and interesting.

### Economy

The large server economy has enough mass to be genuinely self-sustaining but also genuinely unstable. Money accumulation accelerates at this scale — players with established income pipelines grow wealthy faster than the economy can absorb. Without active management, a small number of veteran players can dominate the economy entirely.

Mandatory systems at this scale:

- **Wealth sinks** — meaningful places for accumulated money to go (property, vehicles, legal fees, taxes, bribes, business operating costs)
- **Resource depletion** — criminal supply chains that require active work to maintain, not passive income
- **Economy monitoring** — regular staff review of economic health indicators (average player wealth, income distribution, inflation in goods pricing)

### Staff Structure

A large server requires a full staff organization, not just a hierarchy:

```
Server Owner
├── Head Admin / Co-Owner
│   ├── Admin Department
│   │   ├── Senior Admins
│   │   └── Moderators
│   ├── Game Master Department
│   │   ├── Lead GM
│   │   └── GM Team
│   ├── Development Team (separate from moderation)
│   └── Community Management
│       └── Support / Helper roles
```

Staff shifts are necessary. No admin team should be expected to cover five hundred players at all hours with six people. Define coverage windows and make sure each window has a minimum staffed headcount.

### What to Encourage

- Faction wars with negotiated truces and political consequences
- Player-driven criminal organizations with enough internal structure to function semi-independently
- Specialized investigation units within law enforcement that handle only major cases
- Economy-driven RP — business rivalries, economic warfare, resource control
- Server-wide events every two to four weeks rather than every week

### What to Avoid

- Trying to extend investigation RP to cover every crime — the volume is too high; quality over quantity
- Single-GM events at this scale — any event large enough to affect the server's direction requires a full GM team
- Treating all players identically in content delivery — a veteran player and a first-week player have very different content needs; design for both
- Ignoring time zone coverage — a server with five hundred players is active at 3 AM; staff coverage must reflect that

### Common Pitfalls

**Content drought.** Player demand for new content at this scale is relentless. A GM team that can only produce one major event per month will see players complain of boredom even in a vibrant server. Build a content pipeline — a calendar of planned events with buffer for reactive storytelling.

**Faction monopoly.** One criminal organization becomes dominant, recruits aggressively, and gradually squeezes out competition. Other faction players leave. The "dominant gang vs. police" loop becomes the entire server's story. Break monopolies proactively through world events, law enforcement pressure, or GM-introduced rival threats.

**Staff becoming a caste.** At this scale, staff and players can become socially separated communities. Staff who no longer engage with the player base as players lose their sense of what the community actually needs. Encourage active staff to maintain regular player characters.

## Massive Server — 501 to 1000+ Players

### What This Scale Feels Like

A thousand-player server is not a roleplay community in the traditional sense. It is a roleplay platform — an infrastructure on which dozens of simultaneous communities, subcultures, and narratives coexist. At this scale, no single person, faction, or story defines the server. The server is too big for that.

The challenge is not creating enough story. The challenge is preventing the scale from destroying the thing that makes RP worth doing: immersion, meaning, consequence.

### Story and Pacing

At a thousand players, crime is constant background noise. Robberies happen multiple times daily. Gang shootouts are regular occurrences. At this frequency, treating each incident with full narrative weight is not just impractical — it would make the server feel satirical rather than serious.

**The investigation RP model must be fundamentally restructured at this scale:**

- **Daily incidents are procedural.** They happen, they are responded to, they are logged, and they move on. A robbery is a report number, an arrest or a miss, and a statistic. This is not a failure of RP quality — it is appropriate pacing for a world where crime is common.
- **Major investigations are events.** A high-profile murder, a cartel takeover, a political corruption scandal — these are GM-designated high-priority cases that receive the full investigation arc treatment. They should happen once or twice per month, not weekly.
- **Tiered law enforcement.** Patrol officers handle daily crime procedurally. Detective divisions handle mid-tier cases. A specialized task force, managed with GM involvement, handles the major server-wide investigations. Each tier has appropriate depth.

The GM team at this scale operates more like a narrative production team than individual storytellers. They work from a content roadmap, coordinate events across shifts, and maintain the server's overarching narrative direction across months of play.

### Economy

The massive server economy requires active anti-inflation systems. With a thousand players generating income simultaneously, currency accumulation happens at a rate that, without intervention, rapidly devalues money and makes economic RP feel meaningless.

Required systems:

- **Progressive taxation** — IC property taxes, business licensing fees, or government levies that scale with wealth
- **Degrading assets** — vehicles that require maintenance, properties that need upkeep, equipment with limited lifespan
- **High-cost prestige items** — things that absorb large amounts of currency for social status rather than functional advantage (vanity vehicles, custom properties, character titles)
- **Regular economic review** — a staff role dedicated to monitoring and adjusting economic parameters

### Staff Structure

At this scale, staff is an organization with professional-level expectations:

```
Server Owner / Executive Team
├── Operations Director
│   ├── Moderation Division
│   │   ├── Senior Moderation Team
│   │   └── Moderation Team (tiered by time zone)
│   ├── Game Master Division
│   │   ├── Lead GM / Narrative Director
│   │   ├── Senior GMs
│   │   └── GM Team
│   ├── Development Division
│   └── Community Division
│       ├── Community Managers
│       └── Support Team
```

Burnout management is not optional at this scale — it is a core operational concern. Staff rotation schedules, mandatory time-off policies, and active mental health support within the staff team are necessary, not optional.

### What to Encourage

- Tiered server participation: open public areas with lighter RP expectations alongside whitelisted high-RP zones
- Server-wide narrative arcs that span months, with major turning points every four to six weeks
- Player faction autonomy — large organizations managing their own internal events, drama, and stories with only occasional GM touchpoints
- Specialized law enforcement tiers that each serve a different RP depth
- IC journalism, media, and information systems that distribute world events to all players without requiring GM announcements

### What to Avoid

- Expecting consistent deep RP across the entire server — quality tiers are a feature, not a failure
- Running major events too frequently — at this scale, the community needs recovery time between major disruptions
- Trying to manage everything centrally — delegate narrative ownership to trusted faction leaders and senior GMs
- Comprehensive investigation RP for daily crime — it is exhausting for law enforcement players and creates an insurmountable backlog

### Common Pitfalls

**RP quality dilution.** With a thousand players, the average RP quality across the server is lower than at smaller scales simply because the player pool includes more people at every experience level. This is unavoidable. Manage it through quality zones (whitelisted areas with higher expectations), mentorship programs, and positive reinforcement for excellent RP rather than purely punitive enforcement of bad RP.

**The theme park problem.** At this scale, players can begin to feel like customers consuming content rather than participants creating it. Events feel like scheduled entertainment. Organic player stories get drowned out by the GM production schedule. Counter this by actively amplifying and building on player-driven stories — when two players generate something interesting, a GM should notice and give it narrative weight.

**Server culture fragmentation.** At a thousand players, distinct subcultures form — the serious RP crowd, the car culture players, the criminal empire builders, the casual players. These groups have conflicting expectations and often mutual resentment. The server's identity must be broad enough to encompass all of them without losing coherence. Clear server values, consistently enforced, hold the culture together better than rules.

## Cross-Scale Principles

Regardless of server size, certain fundamentals never change:

**Match your complexity to your population.** Every system you build must have enough players to sustain it meaningfully. An empty courtroom, a faction with one member, a business with no customers — these feel worse than not having the system at all.

**Consequence always matters.** Whether you have ten players or a thousand, a world that does not remember what happened to it feels hollow. The memory of the world is whatever scale of documentation and follow-through you can sustain. Even at micro scale, track what happened and make sure the world reflects it.

**Pacing is a design choice.** The frequency of major events should be deliberately calibrated to your player count, your staff capacity, and the kind of emotional investment you want players to have in each event. Too rare and players disengage. Too frequent and nothing carries weight.

**Build for the server you have, not the server you want.** The most common scaling error is building systems and structures for a future server size rather than the current one. Build for now. Plan for growth. Scale the systems when the population justifies it.
