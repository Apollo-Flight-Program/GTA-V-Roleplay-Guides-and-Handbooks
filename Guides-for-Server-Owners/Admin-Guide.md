# Admin Handbook

Being a roleplay server admin is one of the most thankless jobs in online gaming. You are a volunteer who absorbs player frustration, makes difficult judgment calls with incomplete information, and is blamed regardless of which way you decide. This handbook exists to give you the tools, language, and principles to do the job well — fairly, consistently, and without burning out.

---

## What Admins Are (and Are Not)

### You Are:
- An enforcer of the server's rules — not your personal preferences
- A mediator between players in conflict
- A representative of the server's culture and standards
- A protector of the roleplay experience for everyone

### You Are Not:
- A judge, jury, and executioner with unchecked authority
- A player who happens to have extra powers
- A customer service representative obligated to satisfy every complaint
- Above the rules yourself

The moment you treat your admin role as a personal power tool rather than a community responsibility, you become the problem you are supposed to solve.

---

## Core Principles

### Consistency Over Comfort

The hardest part of admin work is applying rules consistently to people you like. A rule violation from your friend is the same rule violation from a stranger. If you cannot enforce rules against people you are close to, you should not be staff.

Inconsistent enforcement destroys community trust faster than almost anything else. Players talk. They compare notes. They notice when the same behavior gets a warning for one person and a ban for another.

### Evidence Before Action

Never take punitive action without evidence. Screenshots, video clips, server logs, chat records — document everything before you act on anything. A ban issued without evidence is a ban that can be successfully appealed and will damage your credibility.

When in doubt:
1. Gather evidence first
2. Consult a senior admin if unsure
3. Act only when you are certain

### Assume Good Faith First

The majority of rule violations are not malicious. New players metagame because they do not understand the rule yet. Players fail RP because they are nervous or inexperienced. Before reaching for punishment, ask whether education would solve the problem.

A well-handled warning that teaches a player what they did wrong creates a better community member. A ban issued without explanation creates a resentful ex-player who tells their friends the server is run by power-tripping admins.

### Separate IC from OOC

Players will come to you angry about IC events — someone robbed them, killed them, arrested them. Your job is to determine whether a rule was broken — not whether the player enjoyed the experience.

Getting robbed is not a rule violation. Getting killed during a fair roleplay scenario is not a rule violation. Players are allowed to have bad days inside the story. Admins do not exist to ensure everyone always wins.

## Handling Reports

### The Report Process

When a player submits a report, follow this sequence every time:

1. **Acknowledge** — Confirm receipt of the report so the player knows it was seen
2. **Gather** — Collect evidence from both sides before speaking to anyone
3. **Separate** — Speak to the reporting player and the reported player independently
4. **Review** — Check server logs, clips, or screenshots
5. **Decide** — Make a ruling based on evidence and rules, not emotion
6. **Communicate** — Inform both parties of the outcome and reasoning
7. **Document** — Log the incident in your admin records

Never skip step 2 or 3. Never make a ruling based on one player's account alone.

### Talking to Players in Reports

**Stay neutral in tone.** You are not an interrogator and you are not a therapist. Keep your language calm, factual, and professional.

**Do not share one player's statements with the other** during an active investigation. This tips off the reported player about what evidence exists.

**Do not argue.** If a player becomes combative, disengage and escalate to a senior admin. You are not obligated to absorb hostility.

**Give players a chance to explain.** Sometimes context completely changes a situation. A player who appeared to be metagaming may have had a legitimate IC reason for their actions that is not immediately obvious.

### When to Escalate

Escalate to a senior admin when:
- The reported player is a staff member
- The situation involves allegations against yourself
- You are personally close to either party involved
- The evidence is genuinely ambiguous and you are unsure how to rule
- The player is threatening legal action, chargebacks, or public campaigns against the server

There is no weakness in escalating. Recognizing the limits of your authority is a sign of good judgment.

## Punishment Guidelines

### The Punishment Ladder

Most violations should follow a progressive system before reaching a permanent ban:

```
Verbal Warning (no record)
    ↓
Formal Warning (logged)
    ↓
Temporary Mute / Kick
    ↓
Short Temporary Ban (24–72 hours)
    ↓
Long Temporary Ban (1–2 weeks)
    ↓
Permanent Ban
```

Not every violation starts at the bottom. Severe violations — cheating, hacking, sexual harassment, credible threats — skip directly to permanent ban. But most situations warrant starting lower and escalating only if the behavior continues.

### Factors That Affect Severity

**Increase severity:**
- Prior warnings or bans for the same behavior
- Deliberate and premeditated rule-breaking
- Impact on multiple players
- Lying to staff during investigation
- Targeting specific players or staff members

**Decrease severity:**
- Genuine first offense with no prior record
- Player self-reported or admitted the mistake
- Clear misunderstanding rather than intentional violation
- New player with limited server time

### What to Include in a Ban

Every ban should be logged with:
- Player name and identifier
- Date and time
- Rule violated (specific rule number or name)
- Summary of the incident
- Evidence attached or linked
- Duration and reason
- Admin who issued the ban

A ban with no documentation is a ban that cannot be consistently enforced, appealed fairly, or learned from.

## Common Violation Types

### RDM (Random Deathmatch)
Killing another player without a valid roleplay reason or sufficient prior interaction. Distinguish between true RDM and a situation where roleplay context existed but was not communicated clearly.

### VDM (Vehicle Deathmatch)
Using a vehicle as a weapon to injure or kill players without roleplay justification.

### Metagaming
Using out-of-character information to influence in-character actions. The hardest violation to prove — requires demonstrating that the player could not have known the information through IC means. Do not assume; gather evidence.

### Fail RP
Acting in ways that make no realistic sense for the situation. Subjective by nature — use judgment and consult senior staff when unsure.

### Combat Logging
Disconnecting during an active hostile RP scene to avoid consequences. Check server logs for disconnect timestamps relative to reported incidents.

### Cop Baiting
Deliberately provoking law enforcement repeatedly with no roleplay purpose beyond generating a chase or conflict. Pattern of behavior matters more than a single incident.

### Staff Disrespect
Abusive, threatening, or harassing behavior directed at staff members. Document the exact language used. This is a zero-tolerance area on most servers.

## Admin Conduct Standards

### In-Game Conduct
- Never use admin powers to benefit your own character or characters you are affiliated with
- Do not spectate players for entertainment — only for active investigations
- Do not teleport into scenes unless responding to an active report
- Always announce yourself as admin when entering a player interaction in an official capacity

### In Discord / OOC Conduct
- Do not discuss active investigations publicly
- Do not share player personal information
- Do not make fun of players — even privately in staff channels
- Do not air internal staff disputes in public channels

### Conflicts of Interest
If a report involves:
- Your character's in-game enemies or allies
- A player you are friends with outside the game
- A situation your character was directly involved in

Recuse yourself immediately and hand the report to another admin. Transparency about conflicts of interest builds far more trust than pushing through a biased ruling.

## Dealing with Difficult Players

### The Arguer
Refuses to accept any ruling and will argue indefinitely. State your decision clearly once, provide your reasoning, and close the conversation. "This decision is final. If you wish to appeal, please use the appeals process." Do not re-argue.

### The Threatener
Threatens to "expose" the server, post negative reviews, charge back donations, or DDoS. Do not negotiate. Document the threat, issue appropriate punishment for the behavior, and inform senior staff immediately.

### The Ban Evader
Returns to the server on alternate accounts after a ban. This is a permanent ban offense on virtually every server. Use identifiers beyond just username to detect and act on evasion.

### The Frequent Reporter
Files reports constantly, often for minor or subjective situations, frequently targeting specific players. Address patterns rather than individual reports. If a player is weaponizing the report system to harass others, that itself becomes a violation.

### The Streamer / Content Creator
Requires exactly the same treatment as every other player. Visible players sometimes expect leniency — do not provide it. Conversely, do not be harsher to visible players either. Same rules, same process, same outcome.

## Self-Care and Burnout Prevention

Admin burnout is real and it happens fast. Signs you are burning out:

- You feel angry before even reading a report
- You are making decisions quickly just to close tickets
- You are avoiding logging on because of the workload
- You are venting about specific players constantly
- The server feels like a job you hate

When this happens:
- Take a scheduled break — communicate it to your team so reports are covered
- Step back from high-conflict situations temporarily
- Talk to other staff about what you are experiencing
- Remember why you started doing this

No server is worth your mental health. Stepping back temporarily is better than burning out completely and leaving without warning.

---

## Final Words

Good admin work is invisible. When you do it well, players feel like the server is fair and safe without ever thinking about why. Nobody files a report saying "thanks for all the background work keeping this place honest."

That invisibility is the job. Do it anyway.

The roleplay experiences players remember for years — the scenes that felt cinematic, the stories that felt real — only happen because someone maintained the environment that made them possible.

That someone is you.
