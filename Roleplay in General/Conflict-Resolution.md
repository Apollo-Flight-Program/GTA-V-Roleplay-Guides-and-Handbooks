# Conflict Resolution in Roleplay

Conflict is the engine of storytelling. Without it, roleplay is just people standing around agreeing with each other. But conflict in an RP server exists on two levels — the conflict between characters (IC) and the conflict between players (OOC). Knowing how to handle both is what separates a healthy community from a toxic one.

This guide covers both types, how to manage them, and how to keep your RP experience enjoyable even when things get heated.

---

## Understanding IC vs OOC Conflict

The most critical skill in conflict resolution is knowing which type of conflict you are dealing with.

| | IC (In Character) Conflict | OOC (Out of Character) Conflict |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | Characters disagreeing, fighting, competing, or betraying each other | Players disagreeing about rules, behavior, fairness, or personal issues |
| **Where it happens** | Inside the game world, between fictional characters | Outside the game world, between real people |
| **Is it healthy?** | Yes — it drives narrative and makes the server interesting | Only if handled maturely and through proper channels |
| **Examples** | Gang wars, business rivalries, custody disputes, political campaigns, betrayal arcs | "That player metagamed," "They always target me," "The admins are biased," "That was RDM" |
| **How to resolve** | Through IC actions — negotiation, court, violence, alliances, character growth | Through OOC channels — admin tickets, private messages, mediation, cooling off |

**The golden rule:** Never use OOC channels to resolve IC problems, and never use IC actions to punish OOC disagreements.

---

## IC Conflict — The Lifeblood of RP

IC conflict is not a problem to be solved — it is a feature. A server without IC conflict is boring. The key is that all participants understand the conflict is between characters, not between the people playing them.

### Types of IC Conflict

**Character Rivalries**
Two characters who genuinely dislike each other. This can stem from personality clashes, competing interests, or past history. Great rivalries are built over time and have clear reasons both players understand.

**Faction Wars**
Gang vs. gang, department vs. department, business vs. business. Faction conflict involves multiple players on each side and can span weeks or months. The best faction wars have escalation, negotiation, betrayal, and resolution — not just endless shootouts.

**Business Competition**
Two characters running competing businesses — mechanic shops, nightclubs, car dealerships. This creates organic economic RP and gives civilian characters something to fight over without violence.

**Legal Disputes**
Lawsuits, restraining orders, custody battles, and criminal trials. These bring the DOJ into play and create structured, high-quality RP that can involve dozens of players.

**Personal Betrayal**
A character betrays someone they were close to — leaves a gang, steals from a partner, or reveals a secret. This is some of the most emotionally powerful RP available, but it requires trust between the players involved.

### How to Handle IC Conflict Well

1. **Communicate OOC when needed.** Before a major conflict arc, talk to the other player OOC to make sure they are comfortable with the direction. This does not mean scripting the outcome — it means ensuring consent and boundaries.

2. **Escalate gradually.** The best conflicts build over time. Start with tension, move to verbal confrontation, then to action. Do not jump from "we met yesterday" to "I'm going to kill you."

3. **Let the other side win sometimes.** If your character always comes out on top, the conflict becomes one-sided and the other player stops engaging. Take losses. They make better stories.

4. **Keep it consistent.** Your character's actions in the conflict should align with their personality and motivations. Do not suddenly become a different character because you want to win.

5. **End it when it is time.** Not every conflict needs to end in death. Truces, compromises, and even grudging respect are satisfying endings that leave the door open for future interaction.

## OOC Conflict — The Threat to Community

OOC conflict is the opposite of IC conflict — it is destructive, it spreads, and if left unchecked, it kills servers. The most common causes are:

- A player feels another player broke a rule
- A player feels an admin was unfair
- IC conflict bled into OOC feelings
- One player believes another is specifically targeting them
- Miscommunication or misunderstanding about intent

### The "Comply First, Report Later" Principle

This is the single most important conflict resolution tool in RP:

> **If you believe a rule was broken during a scene, finish the scene first. Then file a report afterward.**

Do not:
- Stop the scene to argue about rules
- Break character to call someone out
- Refuse to continue the scene as a form of protest
- Combat log because you feel the other player is wrong

Instead:
- Complete the scene as best you can
- Record or screenshot evidence if you believe a violation occurred
- File a formal report through the server's ticket system after the scene ends
- Let admins review the situation with full context

**Why this matters:** Stopping a scene mid-way ruins the experience for everyone involved — not just the two people in conflict. Other players in the scene, dispatchers, EMS, backup units — they are all affected. Finish the scene, then handle it properly.

## How to Talk to Someone OOC About an IC Issue

Sometimes you need to have an OOC conversation about something that happened in character. This is normal and healthy — but how you approach it matters.

### Do

- Open with a friendly, non-accusatory tone
- Acknowledge that the RP was good before raising your concern
- Use "I" statements: "I felt like my character didn't get a chance to react" instead of "You powergamed me"
- Ask questions before making accusations: "Hey, was that intentional or did I misread the situation?"
- Be specific about what happened and what you would prefer in the future
- Thank them for the conversation regardless of the outcome

### Do Not

- Open with "You broke the rules"
- Message them while you are still angry
- Bring other people into the conversation without both parties' consent
- Threaten to report them as a way to win the argument
- Bring up past incidents that have already been resolved
- Post about the situation publicly (in Discord general channels, on social media, etc.)

### Template for OOC Conversations

> "Hey, I wanted to talk about that scene we had earlier. Overall I thought it was really solid, but there was one moment where [specific thing] happened and I felt like [your perspective]. Was that how you intended it? I just want to make sure we're on the same page for next time."

This approach de-escalates ninety percent of OOC disagreements before they become real problems.

## Cooling Off Periods

When emotions run high — and they will — taking a break is not a sign of weakness. It is the most mature thing you can do.

**Signs you need to cool off:**

- You are typing an angry message to someone
- You cannot separate the player from the character
- You are rehearsing arguments in your head
- You are venting about a specific player to multiple people
- You are considering quitting the server over a single interaction

**What to do:**

1. Step away from the game for at least 30 minutes
2. Do something unrelated to RP
3. Revisit the situation once you are calm
4. Decide whether it is worth addressing or if you can let it go
5. If it still bothers you, follow the OOC conversation template above

## When to Involve Admins

Not every disagreement needs admin intervention. Admins should be your last resort, not your first response.

| Situation | Handle It Yourself? | Involve Admins? |
|---|---|---|
| Minor miscommunication between players | Yes — a quick DM resolves it | No |
| Someone accidentally broke a minor rule | Yes — mention it OOC and move on | Only if it happens repeatedly |
| Clear rule violation (RDM, VDM, metagaming) | Report it, but do not confront them aggressively | Yes — file a ticket with evidence |
| Harassment or personal attacks | Do not engage | Yes — immediately |
| Pattern of toxic behavior from one player | Document it | Yes — with documented examples |
| Disagreement about an IC outcome | Try to resolve IC first | Only if IC resolution is impossible |
| Feeling targeted or bullied by another player | Talk to them first if safe | Yes — if the conversation does not resolve it |

### How to File a Good Admin Report

| Element | What to Include |
|---|---|
| **What happened** | Clear, factual description of the events |
| **When** | Date and approximate time |
| **Who** | Character names and player names if known |
| **Evidence** | Screenshots, clips, timestamps — the more the better |
| **What rule was broken** | Reference the specific server rule |
| **What you want** | Be clear about what outcome you are looking for |

## Mediation

Some servers offer formal mediation — an admin sits with both players OOC and facilitates a conversation. This is effective for:

- Long-running disputes between players
- Faction-level conflicts that have bled OOC
- Situations where both parties have legitimate grievances
- Restoring a relationship between players who need to continue interacting IC

**How mediation works:**

1. Both players agree to participate in good faith
2. An admin or mediator sets ground rules (no interrupting, no personal attacks)
3. Each player states their perspective without interruption
4. The mediator identifies common ground and areas of disagreement
5. Both players agree on a path forward
6. The mediator follows up to ensure the agreement holds

## Avoiding Toxicity

Toxicity is not just outright hostility. It includes:

- Passive-aggressive behavior in OOC channels
- Gossip and rumor-spreading about other players
- Clique behavior that excludes others from RP
- Win-at-all-costs mentality that prioritizes personal success over collective storytelling
- Refusing to RP with certain players as punishment for OOC disagreements
- Weaponizing admin reports (filing reports in bad faith to get someone banned)

**The antidote to toxicity is empathy.** Before reacting, ask yourself:

- How would I feel if I were in their position?
- Is it possible I misunderstood their intent?
- Will this matter to me in a week?
- Am I contributing to the problem or the solution?

## Keeping IC and OOC Separate During Heated Moments

This is the hardest skill in roleplay. When your character is being humiliated, robbed, betrayed, or killed, it is natural to feel those emotions as a player. The key is recognizing that separation and maintaining it consciously.

**Techniques that help:**

- **Remind yourself it is a story.** Your character's loss is a narrative beat, not a personal failure.
- **Compliment the other player OOC after intense scenes.** A simple "That was great RP, well done" resets the dynamic between players.
- **Debrief with friends.** Talk about what happened from a storytelling perspective, not a grievance perspective.
- **Recognize when you are blending.** If you catch yourself angry at the player — not the character — pause and recalibrate.
- **Take the L gracefully.** Losing in RP with dignity earns you more respect than winning with toxicity.

---

## Summary

- IC conflict is healthy and drives stories. Embrace it.
- OOC conflict is destructive and must be handled through proper channels.
- Comply first, report later — always finish the scene.
- Talk to people directly before involving admins.
- Cool off before reacting.
- File clear, evidence-backed reports when needed.
- Separate the character from the player — especially during heated moments.

The strongest communities are not the ones without conflict. They are the ones that handle conflict with maturity, empathy, and respect.
