## FOREWORD

Welcome to the San Andreas Military Airforce.

You have earned your place here. The training you have completed, the chain of command you have respected, and the standards you have met — these are not formalities. They are the foundation upon which every mission we fly is built.

This handbook does not contain everything you need to know. Aviation cannot be compressed into pages. But it contains everything you are required to know as a member of this air arm — your duties, your rights, the procedures that govern your every action in this uniform, and the values that define this service.

Read it. Study it. Live it.

The moment you strap into an aircraft bearing the markings of the San Andreas Military Airforce, you carry the authority and the responsibility of this service with you. There is no halfway in this cockpit. You are either ready, or you are not flying.

Be ready.

## CHAPTER 1: ORGANIZATION AND COMMAND AUTHORITY

### 1.1 Chain of Command

The San Andreas Military Airforce (SAMAF) operates as the air arm of the San Andreas Military (SAM) under the overall authority of the Fort Zancudo Base Commander. The chain of command is as follows:

```
Governor of San Andreas
    └── Secretary of Defense / Military Oversight Council
        └── Fort Zancudo Base Commander (Colonel)
            └── Air Wing Commander (Lieutenant Colonel)
                ├── Fighter Squadron Commander (Major)
                ├── Transport Squadron Commander (Major)
                ├── Rotary Wing Squadron Commander (Major)
                └── Flight Operations Officer (Captain)
```

**All personnel will address those senior to them by rank at all times.** "Sir" or "Ma'am" is the standard form of address for officers above your grade. Senior enlisted personnel are addressed by rank.

### 1.2 Your Chain of Command Is Not Optional

Orders flow from senior to junior. Junior personnel do not debate orders in front of subordinates, in front of civilians, or in front of other agencies. If you believe an order is unlawful, you follow established grievance procedures — you do not refuse in the field unless the order is a direct violation of the Laws of Armed Conflict or this handbook.

**A standing order from command is effective until revoked by the same authority or higher.** An order given verbally by the Air Wing Commander in the field is as binding as a written directive.

### 1.3 Scope of SAMAF Authority

SAMAF personnel operate under military jurisdiction within:

| Area | Authority |
|---|---|
| Fort Zancudo — airspace and ground | Full authority, no civilian agency supersedes |
| Designated Military Operating Areas (MOAs) | Full authority during declared operations |
| State of San Andreas airspace during emergency activation | Authority granted by governor's order |
| Civilian airspace outside declared emergency | No authority — coordinate with civil ATC |
| Civilian ground areas | No authority except in activated deployment |

**You are not law enforcement outside of a declared activation.** A uniform and a rank do not give you arrest authority in Los Santos. If you are off-base and off-duty, you are a civilian in a shirt with a rank badge. Conduct yourself accordingly.

## CHAPTER 2: STANDARDS OF CONDUCT AND BEARING

### 2.1 Military Bearing

Military bearing is the visible manifestation of discipline. It is how you stand, how you speak, how you carry yourself on and off the flight line. There is no off switch for bearing in uniform.

**Required at all times while in uniform:**

- Stand at attention when addressed by an officer senior to you until told to stand at ease
- Salute officers of higher rank when outdoors and within recognition distance
- Refer to yourself in the third person by rank and callsign on the radio — not "I" or "me"
- Do not walk with your hands in your pockets
- Do not argue with a superior in a public setting
- Report for duty on time, in proper uniform, with equipment inspected

**Military bearing in front of civilians and other agencies is non-negotiable.** How you conduct yourself in joint operations, at checkpoints, and during public deployments reflects not only on you but on every member of this service. One undisciplined pilot embarrasses every qualified aviator in this air wing.

### 2.2 On-Base Conduct

Fort Zancudo is a restricted military installation. The following apply at all times on base:

- All personnel entering the base present valid military credentials at the main gate
- Visitors and civilian contractors must be escorted by a military member at all times — no exceptions
- Weapons are not drawn on base except at the firing range, arming area, or under a declared base security threat
- The flight line is a restricted area — only authorized flight crew and maintenance personnel may enter without a signed escort pass
- Alcohol consumption on base is prohibited while on duty; off-duty conduct that results in an incident on base is a punishable offense

### 2.3 Uniform Standards

Personnel will wear the correct uniform for the duty being performed:

| Duty Type | Uniform |
|---|---|
| Flight operations | Flight suit, rank insignia, name tape, unit patch, helmet with visor |
| Base duty (non-flight) | Service uniform, cover outdoors, rank insignia displayed correctly |
| Field deployment | Combat uniform, body armor as ordered, rank insignia subdued |
| Formal occasions | Dress uniform as specified in the event order |

Unauthorized modifications to the uniform — civilian items, non-issued patches, personal accessories worn visibly — are prohibited. If you are unsure whether something is authorized, it is not.

### 2.4 Code of Conduct

All SAMAF personnel are bound by the following principles:

**I. I am a member of the San Andreas Military Airforce. I serve the state of San Andreas and its people. I place the mission and my unit above my personal interests while in this service.**

**II. I will never abandon a fellow service member while I have the ability to act. I will not leave a pilot down if a rescue is within my capability and authority.**

**III. I will follow lawful orders without hesitation. I will refuse unlawful orders without exception.**

**IV. I will not fire my weapons without authorization. I will not endanger civilians, protected personnel, or friendly forces through careless, reckless, or unauthorized weapons employment.**

**V. I will maintain the aircraft entrusted to me. I will report every discrepancy. I will never fly an aircraft I know to be unairworthy.**

**VI. I will be honest in my after-action reports. What happened in the air gets written down accurately — not what I wish had happened.**

## CHAPTER 3: FLIGHT OPERATIONS PROCEDURES

### 3.1 Pre-Mission Requirements

No aircraft departs Fort Zancudo without the following being completed and documented:

**Step 1 — Mission Tasking**
A mission must exist before an aircraft moves. The mission is assigned by the Air Wing Commander or the Flight Operations Officer. Personal training flights require supervisor approval and a logged training record entry. You do not decide on your own to take an aircraft airborne for any reason.

**Step 2 — Mission Brief**
Before any flight, a brief is conducted. The brief covers:

| Brief Element | Content |
|---|---|
| Mission type | What you are doing and why |
| Aircraft and crew | Who is flying, in what airframe |
| Airspace | Where you are operating, altitude blocks, no-fly zones |
| Weather | Current and forecast for the mission area |
| Threats | Known hazards — hostile aircraft, ground threats, civilian traffic |
| Frequencies | Tower, Air Ops, Ground Tactical, Command Net, Guard |
| ROE | The specific rules of engagement for this mission, verbally confirmed by each pilot |
| Abort criteria | What conditions cause the mission to be cancelled or terminated early |
| Emergency procedures | Ejection, MAYDAY, lost comms, bingo fuel actions |

A pilot who cannot repeat the ROE brief at the end of the mission brief does not fly that mission.

**Step 3 — Aircraft Pre-Flight**
Every aircraft receives a physical walkaround inspection before each flight. The pre-flight is conducted by the pilot and the assigned crew chief. If a discrepancy is found — a damage report, a fuel irregularity, a weapons loading error — the flight is delayed until the discrepancy is resolved or the aircraft is substituted.

Pre-flight items are logged in the aircraft's mission log by the crew chief. The pilot signs the log, accepting the aircraft. Your signature means you accept the aircraft as airworthy.

**Step 4 — Weapons Authorization**
If the mission requires live weapons, a Mission Authorization document must be signed by the Air Wing Commander (minimum) before weapons are loaded. The armament crew chief loads weapons at the arming area only — not at the hangar, not on the flight line. The pilot acknowledges the weapons load verbally with the crew chief before taxiing.

**Step 5 — Tower Clearance**
Contact Zancudo Tower on the tower frequency. State your callsign, aircraft type, number of aircraft if in formation, and departure intention. Do not roll onto the runway without explicit tower clearance.

---

### 3.2 Departure Procedures

**Standard departure phraseology:**

> *"Zancudo Tower, [Callsign], [Aircraft type], [Flight of X if formation], request [departure direction], [mission type]."*

Example:
> *"Zancudo Tower, Viper One, Flight of Two, Lazer, request departure to the north, training sortie."*

Tower will clear you for departure with runway designation, wind, and altimeter setting. Read it back in full. Do not acknowledge and depart — read back the clearance.

After departure, contact Air Ops on the assigned frequency and check in with your callsign, aircraft type, altitude, and destination operating area.

---

### 3.3 In-Flight Procedures

**Airspace discipline:**

- Maintain your assigned altitude block. Do not descend below or climb above the assigned block without authorization from Air Ops or command.
- Restricted airspace around Fort Zancudo extends to fifteen nautical miles. No civilian or unidentified aircraft may operate in this zone without SAMAF clearance.
- Overflight of populated areas at low altitude is prohibited without a specific operational reason. Low-altitude flights over civilian neighborhoods generate complaints, political attention, and administrative review.

**Formation discipline:**

- Wingmen do not maneuver without lead's call or an immediate collision avoidance situation.
- All formation position changes are called on Air Ops: *"Viper Two, crossing right"* before you move.
- The lead is responsible for the formation's navigation and communications with external agencies. The wingman monitors and executes. Wingmen do not initiate contact with external agencies unless lead is incapacitated or specifically directs it.

**Radio discipline in flight:**

- State your callsign before every transmission on every frequency.
- Keep transmissions short. The air is busy during operations. Do not narrate your actions — report them.
- "Over" when you expect a response. "Out" when the exchange is complete. Never both.
- Guard frequency (emergency) is monitored at all times during flight. Do not transmit on Guard unless it is a genuine emergency or an interception.

---

### 3.4 Return and Landing Procedures

**Bingo fuel call:**
When fuel reaches the bingo state — the minimum required to return to base with a safe reserve — transmit immediately on Air Ops:

> *"Air Ops, [Callsign]. Bingo fuel. Approximately [X] minutes remaining. Returning to Zancudo unless redirected."*

Do not wait until you are below bingo to call it. Fuel emergencies that result from ignoring bingo state are pilot error.

**Approach and landing:**
Contact Zancudo Tower when you are ten nautical miles out. State callsign, aircraft type, number of aircraft, and inbound intention. Tower will provide landing clearance, active runway, and traffic advisories. Read back the clearance.

**Post-flight:**
After landing, the aircraft returns to its assigned parking area. The pilot conducts a post-flight walkaround with the crew chief, notes any discrepancies in the aircraft log, and signs the sortie record. If weapons were loaded, the armament crew chief supervises unloading at the arming area before the aircraft returns to the hangar. No aircraft is parked in the hangar with live weapons aboard.

The sortie log — at minimum — must be completed before the pilot is released from duty. A sortie that is not logged did not happen.

## CHAPTER 4: RULES OF ENGAGEMENT

### 4.1 The Foundation

The Rules of Engagement govern every weapons decision you make from the moment you strap into an aircraft. They are not suggestions. They are the line between a lawful military action and a war crime. Between an honorable discharge and a court martial. Between a clean conscience and something you will carry for the rest of your career.

**The default state of all SAMAF weapons is SAFE.** You do not arm weapons, point weapons, or threaten with weapons unless you have been given specific authorization by the appropriate authority for that specific mission. There is no general standing authorization to fire. Ever.

### 4.2 Authorization Levels

| State | Meaning | Minimum Authority to Authorize |
|---|---|---|
| **WEAPONS SAFE** | Weapons on safe, no targeting. Default state. | — |
| **WEAPONS HOLD** | Do not fire except in immediate self-defense against direct fire on your aircraft | Standing default |
| **WEAPONS TIGHT** | Engage only confirmed, positively identified hostile targets actively firing on SAMAF or allied forces | Air Wing Commander |
| **WEAPONS FREE (Defensive)** | Engage hostile aircraft or vehicles that have fired on military assets | Air Wing Commander |
| **WEAPONS FREE (CAS)** | Engage designated ground targets per valid 9-line brief | Colonel / Command Authority |
| **WEAPONS FREE (Full)** | Unrestricted engagement of hostile forces | Command Authority + Server-level authorization |

**If you are uncertain which state applies, the answer is WEAPONS HOLD.** Call command for clarification. Waiting costs time. Firing without authorization costs everything.

### 4.3 Positive Target Identification (PID)

Before any weapons employment, you must be able to answer YES to each of the following:

1. **Have I positively identified this target as hostile?**
   Hostile means actively engaging SAMAF or allied forces, confirmed by command as a designated target, or meeting the criteria specified in the current ROE card.

2. **Am I certain this is not a civilian, protected person, or friendly unit?**
   If there is doubt — any doubt — you do not fire. You call command.

3. **Do I have authorization from the appropriate authority?**
   Not assumed authorization. Not "they probably would authorize it." Actual, voiced, confirmed authorization on a recorded frequency.

4. **Have I confirmed friendly positions and is there adequate separation?**
   Weapons do not care about intent. Ordnance that lands on friendlies kills friendlies regardless of what you meant to do.

5. **Is this weapon type proportional to this threat?**
   A cannon strafing run on an armed vehicle convoy — appropriate. A cannon strafing run on a single individual on foot — not appropriate, regardless of their threat status. Match the weapon to the threat.

### 4.4 Absolute Prohibitions

Regardless of any order, any ROE card, any circumstance — the following are unlawful:

| Prohibition | Reason |
|---|---|
| Engaging unarmed civilians | Unlawful under all circumstances |
| Engaging EMS, medical aircraft, or hospitals | Protected personnel and facilities |
| Engaging surrendered or clearly incapacitated combatants | No longer a threat — engagement is unlawful |
| Engaging friendly aircraft or ground forces | Fratricide — career-ending and subject to court martial |
| Firing weapons without authorization | Unauthorized weapons employment — immediate grounding and review |
| Strafing or bombing populated civilian areas | Prohibited regardless of target proximity |

**An order that requires you to violate any of the above is an unlawful order.** You will not follow it. You will document that you received it and refused it. You will report it through the chain of command. This is not insubordination — this is your lawful obligation.

### 4.5 Post-Engagement Reporting

Every weapons employment — a single cannon round, a burst, a rocket, a missile — generates a mandatory post-engagement report. The report contains:

- Time of engagement
- Weapon type and quantity expended
- Target description and target designation authority
- Observed effects (damage, confirmed kill, miss)
- Any collateral concerns identified

This report is filed with the Air Wing Commander within one hour of landing. Missing reports result in grounding until the report is submitted and reviewed.

## CHAPTER 5: INTERCEPTION PROCEDURES

### 5.1 Scramble Authority

A scramble is ordered by the Air Wing Commander, the Tower, or command when an unidentified or unauthorized aircraft enters restricted airspace. The on-alert pilot acknowledges the scramble order and is airborne within five minutes.

Alert status rotates by duty roster. The on-alert pilot is in the ready room, in flight suit, within two minutes of the aircraft at all times during their alert window.

### 5.2 Interception Sequence

All interceptions follow this sequence. Steps are not skipped.

| Phase | Action | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Scramble** | Alert pilot launches on Tower authorization | Intercept heading and initial altitude assigned by Tower |
| **2. Climb** | Climb to intercept altitude — typically above the target | Do not approach at the same altitude — fly above and assess |
| **3. Visual ID** | Close to visual identification distance | Identify aircraft type, registration, markings, behavior |
| **4. Report to Command** | Transmit ID report before any further action | "Command, interceptor contact — [aircraft type], [markings], [behavior], [position]" |
| **5. Warning Broadcast** | Transmit on Guard frequency | See 5.3 for standard phraseology |
| **6. Wing Signals** | Close to formation position and signal | Standard wing rock — 20-degree bank each side, twice |
| **7. Escort** | If target complies — escort to designated landing field | Sandy Shores Airfield is the standard diversion field |
| **8. Escalation** | If target does not comply — report to command for further instructions | Do NOT escalate without command authorization |
| **9. Forced Landing** | If authorized — position to force the target down | Demonstrated by positioning, not by weapons |
| **10. Weapons Authorization** | If target is confirmed hostile and is an immediate threat — weapons require command authorization | Final step — requires the highest authorization available |

### 5.3 Standard Interception Phraseology

**Initial warning — Guard frequency:**
> *"Unidentified aircraft, this is San Andreas Military Air Control on Guard. You are operating in restricted military airspace. You are ordered to depart immediately on heading [bearing] and contact [frequency]. Failure to comply will result in interception. Acknowledge."*

**Repeat warning — alongside the aircraft:**
> *"[Aircraft type], San Andreas Military. I am alongside you to your left. Rock your wings if you understand. Turn heading [bearing] and follow me. You are being intercepted."*

**Escort confirmation:**
> *"Command, Interceptor One. Target is complying. Escorting to Sandy Shores. ETA [X] minutes. Request ground security staged for arrival."*

**Non-compliance report:**
> *"Command, Interceptor One. Target is not complying. Has ignored two verbal warnings and visual signals. Requesting guidance. What are your instructions?"*

## CHAPTER 6: CLOSE AIR SUPPORT PROCEDURES

### 6.1 CAS Is Never Self-Initiated

Close Air Support is directed fire support for friendly ground forces. The pilot does not decide who to engage from the air. The ground commander identifies the target, requests the support, the Air Mission Commander authorizes the strike, and the pilot executes.

A pilot who initiates an attack without a valid CAS brief is conducting an unauthorized strike. This is an ROE violation of the highest order regardless of whether the target was actually hostile.

### 6.2 The 9-Line CAS Brief

All CAS requests use the 9-line format. The pilot reads back the entire 9-line to confirm receipt before entering the target area.

| Line | Element | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **1** | Initial Point (IP) | A known landmark used as the starting point for the attack run |
| **2** | Heading from IP to Target | Magnetic heading from IP to the target |
| **3** | Distance from IP | Distance in kilometers or nautical miles |
| **4** | Target Elevation | Approximate elevation above sea level |
| **5** | Target Description | What the target looks like — vehicle, building, personnel |
| **6** | Target Location | Specific coordinates or grid reference |
| **7** | Mark Type | How the ground unit will mark the target — smoke color, laser, strobe |
| **8** | Friendly Location | Where friendly forces are relative to the target |
| **9** | Egress Direction | Where the aircraft departs after the strike |

**Read-back is mandatory.** If any element of the read-back is incorrect, the ground commander corrects it before the run begins.

### 6.3 Attack Run Calls

All calls during the attack run are mandatory and standardize what every element on the net hears.

| Call | When Made | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **"[Callsign] IN [direction]"** | When committing to the attack run | I am beginning my attack run from the stated direction |
| **"[Callsign] CLEARED HOT"** | Ground commander's response | You are authorized to fire on this pass |
| **"[Callsign] ABORT"** | Any party at any time | Do not fire — abort immediately |
| **"[Callsign] OFF [direction]"** | After weapons release or abort | I have exited the attack run |
| **"SPLASH"** | When ordnance impacts | Ordnance has hit — look for effects |
| **"[Callsign] BDA"** | Battle Damage Assessment | Pilot reporting observed effects of the strike |

**If ABORT is called by anyone — air or ground — the pilot immediately exits the run without firing.** The reason for the abort is established after the aircraft is clear of the target. ABORT is not debated in the air.

## CHAPTER 7: EMERGENCY PROCEDURES

### 7.1 MAYDAY

MAYDAY is the international distress signal. It is used when life or aircraft is in immediate danger. It is transmitted three times at the beginning of the emergency call.

**Standard MAYDAY call:**
> *"MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. [Callsign], [Emergency type], [Position], [Altitude], [Souls on board], [Intentions]."*

Example:
> *"MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. Viper Two, engine fire, east of Fort Zancudo, eight hundred feet, two souls, ejecting now."*

MAYDAY is transmitted on Guard frequency first. It is then repeated on Air Ops so command and other aircraft are immediately aware.

When you hear a MAYDAY, you clear the frequency immediately and respond only if you have information or assistance to offer. Do not fill the Guard frequency with unnecessary transmissions when someone is in distress.

### 7.2 PAN-PAN

PAN-PAN is an urgency call — the situation is serious but not immediately life-threatening. It follows the same format as MAYDAY but opens with "PAN-PAN, PAN-PAN, PAN-PAN."

Examples of PAN-PAN vs MAYDAY:
- Engine performance is degrading but the aircraft is still flyable — PAN-PAN
- Engine has failed and the aircraft is going down — MAYDAY
- Fuel is low and you may not make it back — PAN-PAN
- You are out of fuel and the engine is flaming out — MAYDAY

### 7.3 Emergency Procedures by Type

**Engine failure — fixed wing:**

1. Pitch for best glide speed — do not let the nose drop
2. Declare MAYDAY on Guard
3. Attempt engine restart — one attempt only, brief
4. Identify a landing area — Fort Zancudo if reachable, open flat terrain otherwise
5. If no landing area is reachable and ejection is available — eject over unpopulated area
6. Report your position continuously until clear of the aircraft

**Engine failure — rotorcraft:**

1. Immediately lower collective — initiate autorotation
2. Declare MAYDAY on Guard
3. Identify a landing area — flat and clear of obstacles
4. Manage the descent — maintain rotor RPM in the green band
5. Flare before touchdown to arrest vertical speed
6. If autorotation is not achievable — execute forced landing

**Onboard fire:**

1. Declare MAYDAY
2. Execute the fire checklist — engine shutdown on the affected system, vent cabin
3. If fire does not extinguish within thirty seconds — immediate landing or ejection
4. A fire in flight does not wait. Land immediately or get out.

**Radio failure:**

1. Check all frequencies — attempt every available channel
2. Return to Fort Zancudo on the filed flight plan route
3. Enter the traffic pattern at Zancudo and rock wings — the universal signal for radio failure
4. Tower will respond with light gun signals: Green = cleared to land; Red = stop; Flashing green = cleared to approach
5. Follow light gun signals exactly

**Loss of wingman:**

1. Immediately check the last known position
2. Transmit on Air Ops: *"Air Ops, Viper One. Lost sight of Viper Two at [position]. Last contact [time]."*
3. Begin a visual search of the last known area — do not abandon a wingman without a thorough search
4. If no contact is established in sixty seconds, declare PAN-PAN for the missing aircraft
5. Continue to search until command orders otherwise or the aircraft is located

## CHAPTER 8: COORDINATION WITH OTHER AGENCIES

### 8.1 Joint Operations with Law Enforcement

When SAMAF is activated in support of LSPD, BCSO, or other law enforcement agencies, the following principles govern the relationship:

- **We support. We do not command.** The senior law enforcement officer on scene commands the ground operation. Air support is provided on request, not on SAMAF initiative.
- **Communicate before you do anything from the air.** A surprise low pass over an active scene can compromise the operation, startle civilians, and create dangerous situations for ground personnel.
- **Frequency coordination happens before the mission begins.** Know what channel the police tactical net is on before you arrive. Do not ask for it in the middle of an operation.
- **Rules of engagement do not change because we are working with police.** SAMAF ROE applies to SAMAF weapons, period. A police officer cannot authorize SAMAF weapons employment.

### 8.2 Joint Operations with EMS

- EMS aircraft and vehicles are protected. You do not fly near EMS assets in a threatening manner. You do not interfere with CASEVAC operations.
- When coordinating a CASEVAC or dustoff, you clear the airspace and provide escort if requested — you do not take over the medical operation.
- Communicate hospital locations, landing zones, and threat status to the EMS aircraft before they enter the area.

### 8.3 Air Traffic Control — Civilian

When operating outside restricted airspace, SAMAF aircraft coordinate with civil ATC as any other aircraft would. Military callsigns and military operations do not exempt pilots from civil ATC instructions outside of designated Military Operating Areas.

If civil ATC issues an instruction that conflicts with your mission, acknowledge it, advise your status ("San Andreas Air Defense, operating under military tasking"), and request coordination. You do not simply ignore the instruction.

## CHAPTER 9: DOCUMENTATION AND ACCOUNTABILITY

### 9.1 The Aircraft Log

Every aircraft has a log. Every flight is recorded in it. The log is the legal record of the aircraft's status and history.

The log records:
- Date and time of flight
- Pilot name and rank
- Co-pilot / crew chief name and rank
- Mission type
- Departure and landing time
- Fuel state on departure and landing
- Weapons loaded (type and quantity)
- Weapons expended (type, quantity, target, authority)
- Discrepancies found on pre-flight or post-flight
- Squawks (maintenance issues to be addressed)

**The aircraft log is signed by the pilot. Your signature is your certification that the information is accurate.** Falsifying an aircraft log is a court martial offense.

### 9.2 Sortie Log

In addition to the aircraft log, each pilot maintains a personal sortie log recording their individual flight history. The sortie log is reviewed by the Air Wing Commander during qualification reviews, currency checks, and disciplinary proceedings.

Pilots who cannot produce an up-to-date sortie log are grounded until the log is reconciled.

### 9.3 Incident Reports

Any of the following require an immediate incident report submitted to the Air Wing Commander within two hours of the event:

- Any weapons employment
- Any MAYDAY or PAN-PAN call, regardless of outcome
- Any aircraft damage
- Any airspace incursion by an unidentified aircraft
- Any deviation from the mission brief
- Any ROE question or judgment call made in the field
- Any loss of situational awareness that affected mission execution
- Any communication failure
- Any ground personnel injury or equipment damage caused by aircraft operations

The incident report is not a punishment. It is how the command learns what happened and improves the force. Pilots who hide incidents do not stay pilots.

## CHAPTER 10: MILITARY AVIATION JUSTICE

### 10.1 The Uniform Code of Military Justice

All SAMAF personnel are subject to the Uniform Code of Military Justice (UCMJ) in addition to applicable civil law. The UCMJ covers:

- Insubordination and disrespect
- Dereliction of duty
- Unauthorized use of military equipment
- Reckless endangerment
- Unauthorized weapons employment
- Fratricide or friendly fire incidents
- False official statements
- Conduct unbecoming of a military member

UCMJ proceedings are conducted by the Military Police and JAG (Judge Advocate General) under the authority of the Base Commander.

### 10.2 Pilot Discipline — The Grounding Authority

The Air Wing Commander has authority to immediately ground any pilot for:

- ROE violation of any kind
- Unauthorized flight
- Unauthorized weapons employment
- Failure to maintain currency
- Behavior that demonstrates a lack of fitness for airborne duties
- Any action that placed an aircraft, crew, or third parties at risk

A grounding order is effective immediately and remains in effect until formally lifted. A grounded pilot does not fly. There are no exceptions.

### 10.3 Court Martial

Serious violations are referred to a formal court martial board. The board consists of the Base Commander, the Air Wing Commander, and senior enlisted advisors. The accused has the right to present their account.

**Offenses that result in automatic referral to court martial:**

- Unauthorized air-to-ground weapons employment
- Fratricide
- Abandoning a wingman in a declared emergency without cause
- Falsification of official documents
- Conduct that endangered civilian lives
- Desertion during an active operation

The outcome of a court martial ranges from formal reprimand and rank reduction to discharge from the SAMAF.

## CHAPTER 11: VALUES OF THE SAN ANDREAS MILITARY AIRFORCE

### 11.1 Mission First

The mission does not exist to serve you. You exist to serve the mission. Personal preferences, personal grudges, personal glory — none of it has a place in the cockpit. You fly the brief. You execute the plan. You bring everyone home. The story of the mission belongs to the force, not to the individual.

### 11.2 Wingman Before Self

Your wingman's life is your responsibility from the moment you depart the runway until the moment you both shut down. You do not maneuver that exposes your wingman. You do not take an action that leaves your wingman behind. You do not land while your wingman is still unaccounted for. The two-ship is a single tactical unit. It lives and dies together.

### 11.3 Discipline Makes the Pilot

Talent gets you into the cockpit. Discipline keeps you in it. The most gifted natural pilot is useless to this service if they cannot follow the ROE, complete the paperwork, respect the chain of command, and be trusted with the aircraft by every member of this unit. Discipline is not about restriction. It is about reliability. A reliable pilot is worth ten gifted ones.

### 11.4 Honesty in the Debrief

The debrief is where we learn. It is not a performance review and it is not a blame session. What happened is examined clearly, honestly, and without ego. If you made a mistake in the air, you own it in the debrief. The only shame in the debrief room is the shame of lying about what you did. Honest mistakes are recoverable. Hidden mistakes repeat.

### 11.5 Represent This Service

You are not just a pilot. You are a member of the San Andreas Military Airforce. Every interaction you have with civilians, with other agencies, with the public — you represent every pilot and every service member in this uniform. When you conduct yourself with professionalism, you build trust that enables this service to operate with authority and respect. When you do not, you take something from every member of this unit that you cannot return.
