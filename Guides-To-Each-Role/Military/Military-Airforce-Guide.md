# Military Airforce Guide — Air Power Over San Andreas

The sky above San Andreas belongs to everyone — until it doesn't. When the governor signs an activation order, when a hostile aircraft enters restricted airspace, or when ground forces need fire support that nothing on the road can provide, the San Andreas Military Airforce lifts off from Fort Zancudo and the equation changes completely.

Military aviation is the most technically demanding, most restricted, and most powerful role in all of GTA V roleplay. A single fighter jet overhead communicates authority better than a dozen police cruisers. A transport aircraft can move an entire squad across the map in the time it takes a convoy to clear the city. An attack helicopter can end a standoff that has lasted hours.

This guide covers the full scope of military aviation RP — the aircraft, the ranks, the missions, the rules of engagement, the culture, and the discipline that separates a military pilot from someone who just happens to fly a jet.

---

## Why a Dedicated Military Airforce Exists in RP

Most servers have police aviation. Some have EMS helicopters. Far fewer have a genuine military air arm — and those that do operate on a completely different level.

**What military aviation adds that nothing else can:**

- **Air superiority** — the ability to control who flies and where. Hostile aircraft, criminal aviation, and unauthorized airspace intrusions become answerable to a faction with interceptors.
- **Close air support (CAS)** — fire support for ground operations that is impossible to match with any road vehicle. Attack runs, suppression, covering extraction.
- **Strategic transport** — moving personnel, vehicles, and equipment across the entire map in minutes.
- **Reconnaissance** — high-altitude surveillance that is invisible to suspects on the ground and covers terrain no ground unit can quickly reach.
- **Psychological presence** — a Lazer making a low pass over a riot tells the crowd something that a line of soldiers simply cannot.

Military aviation is not a convenience. It is a capability that fundamentally shapes what operations the server can run. Servers without it are limited to what wheels can carry. Servers with it can do things that feel genuinely cinematic.

## Unit Structure

The San Andreas Military Airforce is a branch within the broader military RP structure. It does not operate independently — it serves the chain of command and provides air support to ground forces when tasked.

### Organizational Structure

```
Commanding Officer (Colonel / Base Commander)
└── Air Wing Commander (Lieutenant Colonel)
    ├── Fighter Squadron (Interceptors and CAS)
    │   ├── Lead Pilot
    │   ├── Wingman
    │   └── Reserve / Student Pilots
    ├── Transport Squadron (Logistics and Troop Movement)
    │   ├── Lead Pilot
    │   ├── Co-Pilot / Loadmaster
    │   └── Reserve Pilots
    ├── Rotary Wing Squadron (Helicopters)
    │   ├── Lead Pilot
    │   ├── Co-Pilot / Door Gunner
    │   └── Reserve Pilots
    └── Flight Operations (Ground-Based Support)
        ├── Air Traffic Control (Tower)
        ├── Ground Crew / Maintenance
        └── Intelligence / Mission Planning
```

**In practice**, most RP servers do not have enough active military aviation players to fill every slot. A skeleton structure works fine — a Wing Commander, two or three qualified pilots, and a ground crew. Depth is added as the faction grows. Never inflate rank requirements just to make the org chart look complete.

## Rank Structure

Military aviation uses standard military ranks but with aviation-specific designations and qualifications layered on top.

### Officer Ranks (Pilots)

| Rank | Abbreviation | Role in Aviation |
|---|---|---|
| Second Lieutenant | 2LT | Student pilot — learning under supervision, restricted to trainer aircraft |
| First Lieutenant | 1LT | Qualified junior pilot — cleared for transport and rotary operations |
| Captain | CPT | Mission pilot — qualified across multiple airframes, leads small flights |
| Major | MAJ | Flight lead — commands a section of two to four aircraft on operations |
| Lieutenant Colonel | LTC | Squadron Commander — commands the air wing, approves mission tasking |
| Colonel | COL | Base Commander — final authority on all aviation assets (often server owner / senior admin role) |

### Enlisted Ranks (Aviation Support)

| Rank | Role |
|---|---|
| Private — Specialist | Ground crew, fueling, vehicle marshalling, base security around the flight line |
| Sergeant | Crew chief, maintains assigned aircraft, supervises ground crew |
| Staff Sergeant | Senior crew chief, handles technical inspections, coordinates maintenance |
| Sergeant First Class | Maintenance supervisor, technical authority for the squadron |
| Warrant Officer 1–2 | Technical aviation specialists — may fly as co-pilots or crew chiefs on transport aircraft |

**Aviation warrant officers** deserve special mention. In the U.S. Army, the majority of helicopter pilots are warrant officers rather than commissioned officers. Many servers adopt this model — warrant officers (WO1, CWO2, CWO3) fly helicopters and transports while commissioned officers handle leadership and fighter operations. This creates a cleaner division and opens up pilot roles without requiring officer rank.

## Fort Zancudo Flight Operations

Fort Zancudo is the heart of all military aviation. The flight line, the tower, and the hangars are where every mission starts and ends.

### Key Locations on the Flight Line

| Area | Function |
|---|---|
| **Control Tower** | ATC operations for all Zancudo flight activity |
| **Hangar 1 (Large)** | Heavy aircraft — Titan, Valkyrie, Annihilator |
| **Hangar 2 (Medium)** | Fighter aircraft — Lazer, Hydra, Besra |
| **Hangar 3 (Small)** | Light aircraft — Maverick, Buzzard, utility birds |
| **Flight Line** | Aircraft parking, pre-flight areas, immediate response staging |
| **Fuel Points** | Two aircraft refueling stations, one near each hangar |
| **Arming Area** | Weapons loading for armed sorties — restricted to armament crew |
| **Maintenance Bay** | Aircraft inspections, repairs, scheduled maintenance |
| **Mission Planning Room** | Briefing room adjacent to the hangar, maps, communications |
| **Tower** | Elevated ATC position overlooking all runways and taxiways |

### Runway and Taxiway Layout

Fort Zancudo has a single main runway oriented roughly east-west. Aircraft depart to the east over the water or to the west over the flat terrain toward the Alamo Sea. The tower designates active runway direction based on wind. Helicopter operations use the western apron and the dedicated helipad area north of the main hangars.

**Standard startup and taxi flow:**
1. Pilot reports to maintenance to sign the aircraft log
2. Pre-flight walkaround with the crew chief
3. Engine start — crew chief supervises and signals clear
4. Contact tower for taxi clearance
5. Taxi to holding point for active runway
6. Tower clearance for departure
7. Departure on assigned heading

## Mission Types and Operations

### 1. Airspace Control and Interception

The most unique military aviation mission. When an unauthorized aircraft enters restricted airspace — Fort Zancudo's exclusion zone, military operating areas, or airspace during a declared state of emergency — the fighter squadron responds.

**Interception procedure:**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| **1. Scramble** | Tower or command authorizes scramble. Pilot launches within 5 minutes of alert. |
| **2. Intercept** | Pilot climbs to intercept altitude and closes on the target. |
| **3. Visual ID** | Pilot identifies the target aircraft type, markings, and behavior. |
| **4. Warning** | Pilot transmits on Guard frequency: "*[Aircraft type], you are entering restricted military airspace. Depart immediately heading [bearing] or you will be intercepted and forced to land.*" |
| **5. Wing rock / visual signal** | Fly alongside the aircraft and rock wings — the universal signal to follow. |
| **6. Escort** | If the target complies, escort them out of restricted airspace and hand off to ATC. |
| **7. Forced landing** | If the target refuses, escalate to forced landing instruction with command authorization. |
| **8. Weapons free** | If the target is confirmed hostile and poses a threat, weapons authorization requires command clearance. |

> `/me banks the Lazer into a hard left turn, throttle back to match speed with the target aircraft. He slides into formation off the right wing, close enough to see the pilot's face, and rocks the aircraft twice.`
>
> *Radio — Guard frequency:* "Unidentified aircraft, San Andreas Military Air Control. You are in restricted airspace. Acknowledge this frequency and follow me to Sandy Shores Airfield immediately. This is your final warning before escalation."

### 2. Close Air Support (CAS)

CAS is fire support provided to friendly ground forces in contact with enemy combatants. It is the highest-stakes, highest-risk mission in military aviation and requires precise coordination between the pilot and the ground commander.

**CAS is not — and can never be — freely initiated.** The pilot does not decide to attack a target. The ground commander requests CAS, the Air Mission Commander authorizes it, and the pilot executes the strike according to the briefing.

**CAS brief format (9-Line):**

| Line | Element | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | IP (Initial Point) | "IP is the radio tower on Route 1" |
| 2 | Heading to target | "Heading two-seven-zero from IP" |
| 3 | Distance from IP | "Two kilometers" |
| 4 | Target elevation | "Approximately sea level" |
| 5 | Target description | "Single-story warehouse, white roof, armed personnel outside" |
| 6 | Target location | GPS coordinates or grid reference |
| 7 | Mark type | "I will mark with red smoke" |
| 8 | Friendlies | "Friendly forces are one hundred meters north of the target" |
| 9 | Egress | "Egress north after strike, no threats north" |

> **Ground Commander:** "Viper One, this is Alpha-6. CAS request follows. 9-line — IP is the water tower on Route 1, heading two-seven-zero, two kilometers. Target is armed hostile group in the open, grid [coordinates]. I will mark with red smoke. Friendlies are north of the smoke, one hundred meters. Egress north. Read back."
>
> **Pilot:** "Copy Alpha-6. IP water tower, heading two-seven-zero, two kilometers. Armed hostiles in the open, marking with red smoke, friendlies one hundred north. Egress north. Viper One is ready, inbound in sixty seconds."

**CAS rules in RP:**
- Never fire without a valid 9-line brief from an authorized ground commander
- Confirm friendly positions before every pass
- If smoke marks don't match the brief, abort the run and clarify
- Call "IN" when beginning the attack run, "OFF" when pulling away, "Splash" when ordnance impacts
- After the strike, give a post-strike report: damage assessment and any concerns

### 3. Tactical Transport

Moving troops, vehicles, equipment, and personnel across the map. Not glamorous — essential.

**Transport missions include:**

| Mission | Aircraft | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Troop Insertion** | Valkyrie, Annihilator | Land at designated LZ, offload personnel, depart |
| **Vehicle Air-Lift** | Titan (cargo hold) | Drive a Crusader or truck into the Titan's cargo ramp |
| **CASEVAC** | Maverick (medevac) | Wounded personnel evacuation to surgical facility |
| **Resupply** | Titan, Cuban 800 | Ammunition, food, medical supplies to forward positions |
| **Command Transport** | Swift, Maverick | Moving senior officers or key personnel securely |
| **Prisoner Transport** | Annihilator with escort | High-value detainee movement |

**Landing Zone (LZ) procedures:**

The receiving ground element establishes and clears the LZ before the aircraft arrives. The aircraft does not land until the LZ is declared secure.

> **Ground Commander:** "Viper Transport, Alpha-6. LZ Bravo is hot — we are taking light fire from the east. Abort LZ Bravo. Divert to LZ Charlie, two clicks north. LZ Charlie is green, marked with purple smoke. Confirm."
>
> **Pilot:** "Alpha-6, Viper Transport. I see purple smoke at LZ Charlie. Aborting LZ Bravo. Inbound LZ Charlie in ninety seconds. Keep that LZ clear."

### 4. Reconnaissance and Intelligence Gathering

High-altitude or offset orbit to observe targets, document activity, and build intelligence products for command.

**Reconnaissance operations:**
- Operate at high altitude (1,500+ feet AGL) to remain invisible to subjects on the ground
- Use offset orbits — never circle directly overhead, which reveals exactly what you are watching
- Document everything: vehicle descriptions, personnel counts, activity patterns, timeframes
- Never engage during a reconnaissance mission — the moment you fire, the mission has failed
- Report to intelligence/command, not directly to ground units (to protect sources)

> `/me sets the aircraft into a wide clockwise orbit at fifteen hundred feet, camera zoomed to maximum, slow-scanning the compound below. He keys the secure frequency.`
>
> "Command, Recon One. Compound is active. I count six vehicles in the lot — two technical trucks, one sedan, three unidentified cargo vans. Twelve personnel visible outside. Activity consistent with a logistics operation. Recommend holding ground forces until we can identify what is in those vans."

### 5. Search and Rescue (CSAR)

Combat Search and Rescue — recovering downed aircrew, missing soldiers, or isolated personnel from hostile territory.

**CSAR differs from police SAR** in that the threat environment is active. The rescue helicopter may be flying into contested airspace to recover someone. A combat escort (fighters or attack helicopter) typically accompanies the rescue aircraft.

**CSAR elements:**
- **Rescue helicopter** — Annihilator or Maverick, with medical crew aboard
- **Escort** — Buzzard or Lazer providing suppression and perimeter security
- **SERE code** — the isolated person uses a pre-briefed authentication code to confirm they are not a trap
- **Survivor marking** — signal mirror, smoke, strobe, or radio signal
- **Hot extraction** — if under fire, the helicopter does not land — crew members descend on ropes or the survivor runs to the heli under escort fire

> **Isolated Pilot on emergency frequency:** "Any station, this is Viper Two — I am down, grid [coordinates], no injuries, authentication is Golf-Seven. Heavy hostile activity to the east. I need extraction."
>
> **Rescue Pilot:** "Viper Two, this is Dustoff One. Authenticate Lima-Four."
>
> **Isolated Pilot:** "Lima-Four confirmed."
>
> **Rescue Pilot:** "Good authenticate. We are twelve minutes out. Pop smoke when you hear rotors. What color?"
>
> **Isolated Pilot:** "Smoke will be orange."

## Rules of Engagement — Air Power

Air weapons are the most destructive assets on any server. ROE for military aviation is more restrictive than for any other role, and for good reason.

### Authorization Levels

| Authorization | Description | Who Can Grant |
|---|---|---|
| **Weapons Hold** | Do not fire under any circumstances | Default state for all operations |
| **Weapons Tight** | Fire only at confirmed, positively identified hostile targets that are actively firing | Air Wing Commander or Ground Commander with authority |
| **Weapons Free (Defensive)** | Engage aircraft or vehicles that have fired on military assets | Air Wing Commander minimum |
| **Weapons Free (Offensive CAS)** | Engage designated targets per valid 9-line brief | Colonel or Server Admin-level command only |

**Lethal air weapons are never self-authorized.** A pilot who fires an air-to-ground weapon without a valid CAS brief is guilty of an unlawful strike and will face IC court martial and likely OOC removal from the faction. The firepower available to military aviation demands a proportional level of oversight.

### Collateral Damage Avoidance

Even with a valid authorization, the pilot is responsible for:
- **Positive target identification** before firing — the target must be clearly hostile
- **Civilian area consideration** — if the target is near civilians, abort and request guidance
- **Friendly fire avoidance** — confirm friendly positions before every weapons pass
- **Proportionality** — a cannon strafing run is appropriate for an armed vehicle; it is not appropriate for a single person on foot

### No-Strike Targets

Regardless of ROE, the following are never legitimate targets:

| Category | Reason |
|---|---|
| Unarmed civilians | Not lawful targets under any circumstances |
| EMS personnel and vehicles | Protected by the laws of war |
| Identified journalists | Protected non-combatants |
| Surrendered or clearly wounded combatants | No longer a threat |
| Hospitals, churches, schools | Protected structures |
| Other military aircraft unless confirmed hostile | Friendly fire incident — career-ending |

## Communication Procedures

Military aviation uses layered radio discipline. Every pilot manages multiple frequencies simultaneously.

### Frequency Architecture

| Frequency / Channel | Purpose | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| **Tower (Zancudo)** | ATC for takeoff and landing at Fort Zancudo | All Zancudo departures and arrivals |
| **Air Ops (Internal)** | Air-to-air communication between military aircraft | Formation flying, mission coordination |
| **Ground Tactical** | Air-to-ground coordination with ground units | CAS, transport, escort |
| **Command Net** | Communication with the mission commander and Air Wing Commander | Reporting, authorization requests, updates |
| **Guard (Emergency)** | Interception communications, emergency calls | Intercept procedures, MAYDAY |
| **FIB / Police Liaison** | Coordination with law enforcement during joint operations | Joint operations only |

### Standard Transmissions

**Departure from Zancudo:**
> *"Zancudo Tower, Viper One, Flight of Two, Lazer, request departure to the north, fighter training mission."*
> *"Viper Flight, cleared departure Runway 27, wind is calm. Altimeter 2992. Contact Air Ops on departure."*

**Mission check-in:**
> *"Air Ops, Viper One is airborne, flight of two, climbing through five hundred for three thousand, proceeding to assigned operating area."*

**Calling in contact:**
> *"Command, Viper One. I have visual on the target aircraft. Single-engine fixed-wing, white with red trim, no markings, operating at eight hundred feet over the Alamo Sea. Requesting authorization to intercept."*

**Bingo fuel:**
> *"Command, Viper One. I am at bingo fuel. I have approximately fifteen minutes before I am committed to Zancudo. Do I have a replacement, or am I returning to base?"*

**Mayday:**
> *"Mayday, mayday, mayday. Viper Two, engine failure, departing the aircraft over the Alamo Sea. Last position [grid/visual reference]. I am ejecting now."*

## Pilot Selection and Training

Military aviation is the hardest role to qualify for in the entire military RP structure.

### Prerequisites for Entry

- Minimum two weeks of active, documented service in the ground military faction
- Demonstrated military bearing — no disciplinary actions, no ROE violations
- Recommendation from current commanding officer
- Basic understanding of aircraft and aviation principles (OOC knowledge is encouraged but not mandatory — the training program covers it)
- Consistent availability — aviation operations require pilots who show up

### Training Pipeline

**Phase 1 — Ground School (1–2 sessions)**

Cover aviation basics IC: aircraft systems, weather, navigation, radio procedures, military aviation law, ROE for air assets, interception procedures, emergency procedures. Conducted in the mission planning room or classroom at Fort Zancudo. A written or verbal knowledge check at the end.

**Phase 2 — Basic Flight (2–3 sessions)**

Flying with an instructor in a Maverick or Mammatus (if available). Pre-flight walkarounds, startup sequences, normal operations, traffic patterns, basic maneuvers, emergency procedures. Student is evaluated on smoothness, communication, and situational awareness — not just stick-and-rudder skill.

**Phase 3 — Advanced Type Training**

Separate courses for each airframe category:

| Course | Aircraft | Duration | Evaluates |
|---|---|---|---|
| Rotary Basic | Maverick, Annihilator | 1–2 sessions | Hover control, confined area landing, LZ operations |
| Rotary Assault | Valkyrie, Savage | 2 sessions | Combat approaches, hot LZ operations, door gunner coordination |
| Transport | Titan | 1–2 sessions | Heavy aircraft handling, cargo operations, airdrop procedures |
| Jet Basic | Besra | 2 sessions | High-speed handling, formation flying, basic intercept geometry |
| Fighter Advanced | Lazer, Hydra | 2–3 sessions | Air-to-air intercept, CAS procedures, weapons employment (with ROE brief) |

**Phase 4 — Mission Qualification**

A final evaluation mission flown with the Air Wing Commander or a designated check pilot. The student is given a mission scenario — no briefing beyond the mission type — and must demonstrate end-to-end competence: mission planning, pre-flight, execution, and debrief.

**Phase 5 — Wingman before Flight Lead**

Newly qualified pilots fly as wingmen for a minimum period before being authorized to lead a flight. A wingman's job is to protect the lead, maintain formation discipline, and execute orders — not to freelance. The instinct to lead before you have followed is the most dangerous trait in a junior pilot.

## Formation Flying

Military aircraft rarely operate alone. Formation flying is a fundamental skill.

### Basic Formations

| Formation | Description | Used For |
|---|---|---|
| **Vic** | Three aircraft in a V — lead forward, two wingmen behind and to each side | Standard transit formation |
| **Line Abreast** | Aircraft in a horizontal line side by side | Sweep searches, CAS approach |
| **Trail** | Aircraft in a column, one behind the other | Mountain or canyon transits, approach to LZ |
| **Echelon** | Staggered diagonal line off one wingtip | Photography, parade, visual demonstration |
| **Combat Spread** | Wide line abreast, 500m+ spacing | Combat maneuver, mutual support in threat environment |

**Formation rules:**
- Wingman watches the lead constantly and matches any maneuver immediately
- Radio calls from formation use flight designators: "Viper One (lead), Viper Two (right wingman), Viper Three (left wingman)"
- No maneuvering without clearing with lead first unless evading a collision
- If lead is lost, the next aircraft in sequence assumes lead and declares: "Viper Two has lead"

## Coordination with Ground Forces

The airforce does not win battles alone. It enables ground forces to win. This relationship requires mutual respect and clear communication.

### Joint Operation Framework

| Phase | Air's Role | Ground's Role |
|---|---|---|
| **Planning** | Brief capabilities, limitations, fuel endurance, ordnance | Brief the target, friendly positions, desired effects |
| **Insertion** | Transport, LZ security, suppression | Clear the LZ, confirm readiness, mark friendlies |
| **Support** | CAS, reconnaissance relay, communications relay | Direct fire, manage the objective, call for CAS |
| **Extraction** | Recovery helicopter, escort, overwatch | Consolidate, mark LZ, confirm secure |
| **Debrief** | Report air observations, aircraft performance | Report ground actions, battle damage assessment |

**The cardinal rule of joint operations:** Ground owns the objective. Air supports it. A pilot who starts making ground-level tactical decisions from the cockpit — deciding when the assault begins, routing ground elements, calling shots that should be called by ground leadership — has stepped outside their lane. Fly the brief. Report what you see. Wait to be tasked.

## Armament and Ordnance Management

Armed military aircraft carry weapons that have RP consequences regardless of whether they are fired.

### Arming Procedures

Aircraft do not carry live weapons while parked. Weapons are loaded at the arming area under supervision of the armament crew chief and are logged by mission.

| Document | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Weapons Load Sheet** | Records what ordnance is loaded on what aircraft, by whom, and when |
| **Mission Authorization** | Signed order that authorizes live weapons for a specific mission |
| **ROE Brief Document** | Written confirmation that the pilot has received and understands the ROE for this specific mission |
| **Post-Mission Weapons Report** | Account of all ordnance expended, on what targets, with what authority |

**Unloading procedures:** When a mission returns without expending weapons, the arming crew unloads all ordnance before the aircraft enters the hangar. Aircraft are never stored with live weapons aboard without written command authorization.

### Ordnance Types and Authority

| Weapon Type | GTA V Equivalent | Authorization Required |
|---|---|---|
| Cannon / Minigun | Buzzard cannon, Savage minigun | Flight lead decision in declared combat zone |
| Unguided rockets | Buzzard / Savage rockets | Air Wing Commander authorization per target |
| Air-to-air missiles | Lazer / Hydra missiles (A2A mode) | Air Wing Commander + ROE card confirming threat |
| Air-to-ground missiles | Lazer / Hydra missiles (A2G mode) | Colonel + Mission Authorization document |
| Bombs / Heavy ordnance | Hydra bombs | Command level — server admin tier authorization |

## Aircraft Emergencies

Every pilot must know emergency procedures before they take the runway.

### Emergency Types and Actions

**Engine failure — fixed wing:**
1. Maintain aircraft control — pitch for best glide speed
2. Declare emergency on all frequencies: "Mayday, mayday, mayday — [callsign] — engine failure — [position] — [number of people aboard]"
3. Attempt restart procedures
4. Identify landing area — runway if reachable, open flat ground otherwise
5. If not reachable: eject if available, or ditch in open terrain away from populated areas

**Engine failure — helicopter:**
1. Lower collective immediately — enter autorotation
2. Declare emergency
3. Identify landing area
4. Manage the descent — flare before touchdown to arrest vertical speed

**Fire onboard:**
1. Declare emergency
2. Execute fire suppression checklist
3. If fire does not extinguish — emergency landing or eject

**Loss of radio communication:**
1. Attempt frequency changes
2. Return to base on flight plan
3. Enter the Zancudo traffic pattern and rock wings to signal radio failure
4. Tower will respond with light gun signals (red = stop, green = proceed, flashing = caution)

> `/me sweeps through the emergency checklist on the kneeboard, left hand steady on the stick, voice controlled.`
>
> *Guard frequency:* "Mayday, mayday, mayday. Viper Two, engine fire, departing controlled flight east of Fort Zancudo. Two souls on board. Ejecting now."
>
> `/me reaches for the ejection handle, exhales once, and pulls.`

## After-Action Report (AAR)

Every sortie — whether training, routine, or combat — is documented.

### Sortie Log (Minimum Required)

```
DATE:
AIRCRAFT TAIL NUMBER:
PILOT(S):
MISSION TYPE:
DURATION:
WEAPONS EMPLOYED (yes/no, type, quantity, target):
INCIDENTS:
DISCREPANCIES (aircraft squawks):
PILOT NOTES:
```

### Full AAR (For Combat or Significant Sorties)

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Mission Brief Summary** | What was the plan |
| **Execution** | What actually happened, in chronological order |
| **Weapons Employment** | Every trigger pull — type, target, authorization, observed effect |
| **Deviations from Brief** | Any time the mission departed from the plan, and why |
| **Successes** | What went right and should be repeated |
| **Failures** | What went wrong and how to prevent recurrence |
| **Recommendations** | Changes to procedures, equipment, or training |
| **Intelligence Gathered** | Enemy positions, behavior, capabilities observed |

AARs are not optional. A sortie without documentation does not officially exist. In the context of RP investigations, discipline boards, or incident reviews — the AAR is the primary record.

## OOC Considerations for Military Aviation

Military aviation requires more OOC awareness than almost any other role in GTA V RP.

**Things to understand before you apply:**

**1. You are handling server-tier assets.**
A Lazer with weapons free can end an entire server event in thirty seconds. The server has trusted you with something consequential. That trust comes with the expectation that you exercise more restraint than almost any other player.

**2. Military aviation and rule violations overlap more than any other role.**
Unauthorized weapon use from an aircraft is almost certainly RDM/VDM at a catastrophic scale. Combat logging while airborne during a joint operation affects every ground unit depending on you. Know your server's rules cold before you touch a military aircraft.

**3. The admin team is watching.**
Servers with military aviation track its use. Admin logs of who accessed what vehicle, when, and what happened afterward are standard. This is not because they distrust you — it is because the aircraft are powerful enough to warrant oversight. Accept this.

**4. Your patience will be tested.**
Most shifts in military aviation are quiet. You maintain readiness, log training hours, and wait. The dramatic CAS run happens twice a month. Everything else is pre-flight checks, formation training, and sitting alert. If you joined for the jet runs, you will be disappointed. If you joined to be part of a disciplined, capable unit that the server trusts with its most powerful assets — this is exactly the right role.

**5. Coordinate before you fly — always.**
Never take a military aircraft airborne without notifying command and, if applicable, ATC. A surprise military jet overfly of a hostage situation will cause chaos below. An unannounced troop transport landing in the middle of an active police operation will break that scene for everyone. One radio call prevents all of it.

## Final Thoughts

Military aviation is not the loudest role on the server. When it is done right, it is barely visible — a presence overhead during a crisis, a transport that puts the right people in the right place at the right time, a voice on the radio that changes the calculus of a situation without ever firing a shot.

The pilots who do this role best are the ones who understand that the aircraft is not the point. The mission is. The people depending on you from the ground are. The story being built between every faction on the server is.

A Lazer at full afterburner over Los Santos is impressive. A disciplined pilot who used that aircraft exactly when it was needed, exactly as authorized, with exactly the outcome that command intended — that is what military aviation is supposed to produce.

Fly the brief. Protect your flight. Come home.
