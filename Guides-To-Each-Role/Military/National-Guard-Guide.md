# National Guard Guide — Called to Serve San Andreas

The National Guard is the most common military RP framework in GTA V servers. It exists as a part-time force — soldiers with civilian lives who are activated when the state needs them. This dual identity creates rich RP opportunities. Your character might be a mechanic at the LS Customs during the week and a Sergeant running a checkpoint in Davis on the weekend. When the governor signs an activation order, you drop everything and report to Fort Zancudo.

This guide covers every aspect of National Guard RP — from activation to deactivation, from disaster response to civil unrest, and everything in between.

---

## What the National Guard Is (And Is Not)

The National Guard is not the regular army. Understanding this distinction is critical for staying in your lane during RP.

| Aspect | National Guard | Active Duty Military |
|---|---|---|
| Status | Part-time, activated when needed | Full-time, always on duty |
| Authority | Only during activation orders | Continuous military authority on base |
| Civilian life | Members have civilian jobs and identities | Military is their full-time career |
| Deployment | Within San Andreas for domestic emergencies | Can be deployed anywhere (rarely RP'd) |
| Equipment | Standard military gear, limited heavy weapons | Full arsenal access |
| Chain of command | Reports to state governor through military command | Reports through federal military command |
| Typical missions | Disaster relief, civil support, emergency response | Base operations, combat readiness, defense |

**Key point:** When the Guard is not activated, members are civilians. They have no military authority. They cannot wear uniforms in public, carry military weapons, or exercise any military power. Activation is what turns them from civilians into soldiers.

## Activation Procedures

Activation is the process that brings the Guard from standby to operational. It is a political act as much as a military one.

**Who can activate the Guard:**
- The Governor of San Andreas (most common)
- The Mayor of Los Santos (for city-specific emergencies, with governor approval)
- The Lieutenant Governor (in the governor's absence)
- Server admins (OOC, for events or storylines)

**Activation process:**

| Step | Action | Who Does It |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Emergency identified | Law enforcement, EMS, or government officials |
| 2 | Request for military support submitted | Police chief, mayor, or emergency management |
| 3 | Governor reviews and signs activation order | Governor (player or NPC) |
| 4 | Activation order transmitted to Guard command | Government channel or in-game notification |
| 5 | Guard commander issues mobilization order | Commanding officer |
| 6 | Personnel report to Fort Zancudo for briefing | All available Guard members |
| 7 | Equipment drawn, vehicles staged, mission briefing | On base before deployment |
| 8 | Units deploy to assigned areas | Convoy out of Fort Zancudo |

> `/me checks his phone, reads the activation text, and sets down his coffee mug. He grabs his keys and heads for the door.`
> "Fort Zancudo. Full mobilization. This isn't a drill."

**Activation should feel significant.** When the Guard gets called up, it means something serious is happening. Players should RP the weight of that moment — leaving their civilian lives behind, saying goodbye to family, reporting to base with a sense of urgency and purpose.

## Disaster Response

Natural disasters are the most common reason for Guard activation and produce some of the most rewarding military RP.

**Common disaster scenarios in San Andreas:**

| Disaster | Affected Area | Guard Tasks |
|---|---|---|
| Wildfire | Blaine County hills, Raton Canyon, Mount Chiliad | Evacuation, roadblocks, supply transport, aerial water drops |
| Earthquake | Downtown Los Santos, infrastructure collapse | Search and rescue, debris clearance, emergency shelters |
| Flooding | Zancudo River area, low-lying Paleto Bay | Sandbagging, boat rescue, evacuation routes |
| Industrial accident | Port of Los Santos, chemical plants | Hazmat perimeter, evacuation, decontamination |
| Major storm | Coastline, Vespucci Beach, Del Perro | Evacuation orders, emergency supply distribution |

**Disaster response procedures:**
1. **Assess the situation** — Get a briefing from local authorities. What happened? Where? How many affected?
2. **Establish a command post** — Set up a forward operating base near the disaster zone. Coordinate with LSPD, BCSO, EMS, and fire.
3. **Prioritize life safety** — Find survivors, evacuate endangered areas, establish casualty collection points for EMS.
4. **Secure the area** — Prevent civilians from entering dangerous zones. Set up roadblocks and checkpoints.
5. **Sustain operations** — Rotate personnel, manage supplies, maintain communication with base command.
6. **Transition to recovery** — As the emergency passes, shift from response to support. Help restore normalcy.

> `/me kneels beside the collapsed wall, listening for sounds underneath the rubble. He holds up a fist to silence the team.`
> "Hold. I heard something. Get the jacks and the medic over here. We might have a survivor."

## Civil Unrest Support

When riots, protests, or large-scale disturbances exceed police capacity, the Guard steps in. This is the most politically sensitive deployment and requires careful RP.

**Principles for civil unrest operations:**
- **You are supporting police, not replacing them.** LSPD and BCSO maintain law enforcement authority. You provide manpower, crowd control, and security.
- **Minimum force necessary.** Tear gas before rubber bullets. Rubber bullets before live rounds. Live rounds only if lives are in immediate danger.
- **Protect, do not provoke.** Your presence should calm the situation, not escalate it. Standing in a line with riot shields is a deterrent. Pointing rifles at protesters is a provocation.
- **Respect the right to protest.** Peaceful assembly is legal. Your job is to contain violence, not suppress speech.

**Civil unrest equipment:**

| Equipment | Purpose | Authorization Level |
|---|---|---|
| Riot shields | Physical barrier, crowd control | Standard issue during unrest |
| Batons | Close-quarters crowd management | Standard issue |
| Tear gas | Area denial, crowd dispersal | Supervisor authorization |
| Rubber bullets | Less-lethal crowd control | Commander authorization |
| Armored vehicles | Mobile barriers, personnel protection | Commander authorization |
| Water cannon (if available) | Crowd dispersal | Commander authorization |
| Live ammunition | Lethal force against armed threats only | ROE Weapons Tight or above |

## Checkpoint Operations

Checkpoints are a core Guard activity during activation. They control movement, verify identities, and provide security.

**Setting up a checkpoint:**

1. **Select the location** — High-traffic intersections, highway on-ramps, bridges, or approaches to restricted areas. Good locations include the Route 68 bridge, the Great Ocean Highway entrance to Paleto Bay, or the Elysian Island access road.
2. **Establish the layout** — Use barriers or cones to funnel traffic into a single lane. Position vehicles to provide cover and block bypass routes.
3. **Post personnel** — At minimum: one soldier checking IDs, one providing overwatch, one managing traffic flow. A supervisor should be on site.
4. **Coordinate with police** — LSPD or BCSO should have an officer at or near the checkpoint for law enforcement authority. Soldiers detain — police arrest.
5. **Run the checkpoint** — Stop each vehicle. Request ID. Ask destination and purpose of travel. Check for contraband if authorized. Process and release or detain.

**Checkpoint interaction example:**

> `/me steps to the driver's window, left hand resting on the vehicle door, right hand visible and open.`
> "Good evening. National Guard checkpoint. May I see your identification, please? Where are you headed tonight?"
>
> *After receiving ID:*
> `/me examines the ID, checks it against the list on his clipboard, then hands it back.`
> "Thank you. Drive safe out there. Curfew is at 2200 — be where you're going before then."

## Patrol of Restricted Areas

During activation, the Guard may be assigned to patrol areas declared off-limits to civilians.

**Common restricted areas:**
- Fort Zancudo perimeter and surrounding roads
- Disaster zones where structural collapse or hazmat risks exist
- Government buildings during elevated threat levels
- Critical infrastructure — power stations, water treatment facilities, communication towers
- Areas under curfew orders

**Patrol guidelines:** Move in pairs — never alone. Maintain radio contact with your checkpoint and command at all times. Challenge anyone encountered — identify, verify authorization, escort out if unauthorized. Do not use force against unarmed trespassers unless they resist. Document and report suspicious activity to both military command and local law enforcement.

## Humanitarian Aid

Not all Guard operations involve weapons and checkpoints. Humanitarian missions are some of the most impactful RP scenarios — supply distribution at Legion Square or behind Pillbox Medical Center, establishing emergency shelters for displaced civilians, providing first aid and transport to hospitals, clearing roads for utility crews, and setting up mobile radio stations for areas where cell service is down.

> `/me unloads a case of water bottles from the back of the transport truck and carries it to the distribution table.`
> "We've got water, MREs, and blankets. One per person, one per family for the blankets. Line forms here. Nobody gets skipped."

## Coordination with LSPD and BCSO

The Guard does not operate in a vacuum. Every deployment involves working alongside civilian law enforcement.

**Coordination protocols:**

| Situation | Guard Role | Police Role |
|---|---|---|
| Checkpoint | Physical security, ID checks, vehicle searches | Arrest authority, warrant checks, legal decisions |
| Riot control | Crowd containment, barrier lines, area security | Arrest teams, less-lethal deployment decisions, dispersal orders |
| Disaster zone | Search and rescue, debris removal, perimeter security | Traffic management, civilian liaison, crime scene preservation |
| Curfew enforcement | Area patrol, civilian contact, escort to safe zones | Arrests for curfew violations, citation authority |
| VIP protection | Outer perimeter security, convoy escort | Close protection, intelligence coordination |

**Key rule:** Soldiers do not arrest civilians. They detain and hand off to police. Military authority exists to support, not supplant, civilian law enforcement. Overstepping this boundary is the fastest way to create OOC conflict between departments.

## Rules of Engagement for Domestic Deployment

Domestic ROE is more restrictive than combat ROE. You are operating among your own citizens.

**Standard domestic ROE:**
1. **Verbal commands first** — always attempt to de-escalate with words before any physical action
2. **Physical control second** — restraint, escort, hands-on guidance for non-compliant individuals
3. **Less-lethal force third** — batons, shields, tear gas for groups that are actively violent
4. **Lethal force only as last resort** — when there is an imminent threat to life that cannot be resolved by any other means

**What lethal force requires:**
- A positively identified armed threat
- An immediate risk of death or serious injury to soldiers, police, or civilians
- No less-lethal alternative available
- Authorization from the on-scene commander (unless the threat is so immediate that waiting would cost lives)

## Equipment Restrictions

The Guard operating domestically does not use its full arsenal. Equipment is matched to the mission.

| Mission Type | Authorized Equipment | Restricted Equipment |
|---|---|---|
| Humanitarian aid | Transport vehicles, personal sidearms, medical kits | Armored vehicles, automatic weapons, explosives |
| Disaster response | Transport vehicles, rescue equipment, sidearms | Attack aircraft, heavy weapons |
| Civil unrest | Riot gear, armored vehicles, less-lethal weapons | Attack helicopters, tanks, heavy ordnance |
| Counter-terrorism | Full tactical loadout, armored vehicles, helicopters | Indiscriminate area weapons, fighter jets |
| Martial law | Full arsenal as authorized by command | Only what command specifically authorizes |

**OOC note:** Pulling out a tank to deal with a protest is powergaming. Equipment escalation should match the threat, and server admins typically control access to heavy military assets.

## Deactivation Procedures

When the emergency ends, the Guard stands down. Deactivation is the reverse of activation and should be RP'd with the same care.

**Deactivation process:**
1. Governor or commanding authority issues the stand-down order
2. Units are recalled from deployed positions
3. Equipment is returned, inspected, and stored
4. Personnel are debriefed — what happened, what went well, what needs improvement
5. After-action reports are filed by unit leaders
6. Guard members return to civilian life

> `/me removes his plate carrier and sets it on the rack in the armory. He runs a hand through his hair and exhales slowly.`
> "Been a long week. Time to go home and remember what normal feels like."

**Deactivation is RP too.** The transition from soldier back to civilian creates powerful character moments — the adjustment, the stories, the things you saw that your civilian friends will never understand.

## Training Exercises

Between activations, the Guard trains. Training keeps skills sharp and provides consistent RP during peacetime. Key training types include weapons qualification at Fort Zancudo's range, tactical drills (room clearing, convoy procedures, checkpoint operations), combat first aid and casualty evacuation, communication exercises with encrypted channels, and full-scale exercises simulating disaster or unrest — sometimes involving civilian and police players.

---

## Rank Structure

National Guard RP typically uses the same rank structure as the broader military, but with fewer active ranks due to the smaller scale.

| Rank | Typical RP Role |
|---|---|
| Private (PVT) | New recruit, in training |
| Specialist (SPC) | Trained soldier, handles basic assignments |
| Corporal (CPL) | Fire team leader, first supervisory role |
| Sergeant (SGT) | Squad leader, primary NCO |
| Staff Sergeant (SSG) | Senior squad leader, mentors NCOs |
| Second Lieutenant (2LT) | Platoon leader, junior officer |
| First Lieutenant (1LT) | Executive officer, experienced platoon leader |
| Captain (CPT) | Company commander, highest active officer in most RP setups |

Promotions should be earned through participation, leadership during activations, and time in service. Instant promotions for OOC reasons undermine the military RP structure.

---

## Final Thoughts

National Guard RP is about service — to the state, to the people, and to the story. You are the force that stands between order and chaos when the police lines break, when the fires burn out of control, when the city needs more than badges and squad cars can provide. But you are also the soldier who goes home after the crisis, hangs up the uniform, and goes back to being a regular person in Los Santos.

That duality — soldier and civilian, warrior and neighbor — is what makes National Guard RP unlike anything else on the server.

Stand ready. Stand down when ordered. And always remember who you serve.
