# Military Police Guide — Law and Order Behind the Wire

Military Police are the law enforcement arm of the military. While the National Guard protects the state and special operations handle high-threat missions, MPs keep order within the military itself. They guard the gates, enforce the Uniform Code of Military Justice, investigate crimes committed by service members, and bridge the gap between military and civilian law enforcement. In GTA V RP, MPs are the players who turn Fort Zancudo from a static map location into a living, breathing military installation.

This guide covers every aspect of Military Police RP — from gate security to criminal investigations, from prisoner transport to cooperation with LSPD and BCSO.

---

## What Military Police Do

MPs wear two hats — they are soldiers and they are cops. This dual identity defines every aspect of the role.

| Core Function | Description | Where It Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Base security | Protecting Fort Zancudo from unauthorized entry and internal threats | Gates, perimeter, interior patrols |
| Law enforcement | Enforcing military law (UCMJ) among service members | On base and during military operations |
| Criminal investigation | Investigating crimes committed by or against military personnel | Wherever the crime occurred |
| Prisoner operations | Detaining, transporting, and guarding military prisoners | Brig, transport routes, court facilities |
| Traffic control | Managing vehicle movement on base and during convoys | Base roads, convoy routes, operation zones |
| VIP escort | Providing security for high-ranking officers and visiting officials | Base to destination routes, event venues |
| Force protection | Assessing and mitigating threats to military personnel and facilities | Base-wide, deployed locations |

**Key distinction:** MPs enforce military law on military personnel. They do not have authority over civilians outside of base property and active military operations. A civilian speeding on the highway is an LSPD problem. A soldier speeding on the highway is still an LSPD problem — but an MP problem too, because that soldier's conduct reflects on the military.

## Gate Access Control

The main gate at Fort Zancudo is the most visible MP post and the one where most civilian-military interaction happens.

**Gate procedures:**

| Step | Action | Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Vehicle approaches gate | MP signals vehicle to stop at the checkpoint barrier |
| 2 | Identification check | Request military ID from service members or government-issued ID from authorized visitors |
| 3 | Purpose verification | Ask the reason for entry — official business, delivery, scheduled appointment |
| 4 | Vehicle inspection | Visual check of the vehicle exterior and interior. Full search if threat level is elevated. |
| 5 | Authorization check | Verify the individual is on the access list or has a valid pass |
| 6 | Entry or denial | Grant access and log the entry, or deny and explain why |
| 7 | Documentation | Record the name, ID number, vehicle, time of entry, and stated purpose |

**Gate interaction examples:**

*Military personnel returning to base:*
> `/me steps to the driver's window and extends his hand.`
> "Welcome back. Military ID, please."
> `/me checks the ID against the active roster on his tablet, then hands it back.`
> "You're good, Sergeant. Head straight to the motor pool — Captain Davis wants all NCOs for a 1400 briefing."

*Unauthorized civilian:*
> `/me holds up an open palm toward the vehicle.`
> "This is a restricted military installation. I'm going to need to see some identification and know your reason for being here."
> *After determining no authorization:*
> "I'm sorry, but you're not on our access list and I don't have authorization to admit you. You'll need to turn around here. If you have official business, contact the base public affairs office and they can arrange a visit."

*Suspicious individual:*
> `/me takes a step back from the vehicle, right hand moving to his sidearm.`
> "Sir, I need you to turn off the engine and keep your hands where I can see them. Do not exit the vehicle."
> `/me keys his radio.`
> "Gate-1 to MP Dispatch, I've got a suspicious individual at the main gate. Requesting backup and a supervisor."

## UCMJ Enforcement — The Uniform Code of Military Justice

The UCMJ is the military's legal code. MPs enforce it the way civilian police enforce the penal code.

**Common UCMJ offenses in RP:**

| Article | Offense | Description | Typical Consequence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Article 86 | AWOL / Absence Without Leave | Failure to report for duty or leaving without authorization | Extra duty, reduction in rank, brig time |
| Article 89 | Disrespect Toward a Superior | Contemptuous language or behavior toward a commissioned officer | Reprimand, extra duty, possible reduction |
| Article 91 | Insubordination | Willful disobedience of a lawful order from an NCO | Reduction in rank, brig time |
| Article 92 | Failure to Obey Order | Violating a lawful general order or regulation | Reprimand to brig time depending on severity |
| Article 112 | Drunk on Duty | Being intoxicated while on duty or in a duty status | Reduction in rank, extra duty, possible discharge |
| Article 121 | Larceny / Theft | Stealing military property or another service member's belongings | Brig time, reduction in rank, discharge |
| Article 128 | Assault | Physical attack on another service member | Brig time, reduction in rank, discharge |
| Article 134 | General Article | Conduct prejudicial to good order and discipline (catch-all) | Varies widely based on the specific conduct |

**Enforcement process:**
1. **Observe or receive report** of the offense
2. **Investigate** — gather statements, evidence, and witness accounts
3. **Detain** the suspect if necessary (flight risk, danger to others, continued offense)
4. **Report** findings to the commanding officer
5. **Commanding officer decides** — non-judicial punishment (Article 15), court-martial referral, or dismissal of charges

**MPs investigate. Commanders adjudicate.** An MP does not decide punishment. They present the evidence and the commanding officer makes the call. This separation of roles is critical for fair RP.

## Investigating Military Crimes

Military criminal investigation goes beyond catching someone breaking curfew. Serious crimes require thorough investigation.

**Types of investigations:**

| Crime Type | Investigative Approach | Coordination Required |
|---|---|---|
| Theft of military equipment | Inventory audit, witness interviews, surveillance review | Armory staff, supply NCO |
| Assault between soldiers | Victim statement, witness interviews, medical records from base medic | Medical staff, unit leadership |
| Unauthorized discharge of weapon | Ballistics, weapon serial verification, range log review | Armory, range master |
| Drug use / distribution | Informant network, search of quarters, urinalysis results | Medical staff, unit leadership |
| Fraternization | Witness accounts, communication records, pattern of behavior | Unit leadership, JAG |
| Desertion | Last known contacts, civilian law enforcement BOLO, financial records | LSPD, BCSO, SASP (for off-base search) |
| Espionage / information leak | Communication audit, access log review, counterintelligence coordination | FIB, military intelligence, command staff |

**Investigation principles:** Document everything — statements, evidence locations, timestamps, chain of custody. Maintain objectivity — find the truth, not prove a conclusion. Respect rights — military members have the right to remain silent and legal counsel (JAG). Preserve evidence — secure the scene, photograph everything, bag and tag physical evidence.

> `/me enters the barracks room with his partner, pulling on latex gloves.`
> "Nobody touches anything. I want photos of the entire room before we move a single item. Start with the desk and footlocker."

## Working with Civilian Law Enforcement

Military and civilian law enforcement have overlapping but distinct jurisdictions.

| Scenario | Primary Authority | Supporting Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Crime on base by military personnel | Military Police | None — civilian police have no authority on base |
| Crime on base by civilian visitor | Military Police for initial response | LSPD/BCSO for prosecution |
| Crime off base by military personnel | LSPD/BCSO for criminal charges | MPs for UCMJ charges (separate systems) |
| Crime off base by civilian | LSPD/BCSO | No MP involvement |
| Joint operation (terrorism) | Incident commander (usually civilian) | MP provides military support |

**Cooperation:** When a soldier gets arrested off base, LSPD/BCSO notifies the MPs. When civilians commit crimes on base, MPs share evidence with civilian prosecutors. During heightened security, MPs and local police may run joint patrols near Fort Zancudo. LSPD officers do not give orders to MPs on base. MPs do not give orders to LSPD in the city.

## Prisoner Transport

Moving prisoners — whether to the brig, to a court-martial hearing, or to civilian custody — requires security and procedure.

**Transport protocols:**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Search the prisoner thoroughly before transport — check for weapons, contraband, anything that could be used to escape or cause harm |
| 2 | Secure the prisoner with restraints — handcuffs at minimum, leg irons for high-risk prisoners |
| 3 | Brief the transport team — who is the prisoner, what are they charged with, what is their risk level |
| 4 | Vehicle inspection — check the transport vehicle for hidden weapons, ensure locks function, verify communication equipment |
| 5 | Route planning — primary and alternate routes, avoid high-traffic areas, have rally points in case of emergency |
| 6 | Two-vehicle minimum — lead vehicle with the prisoner, follow vehicle with additional security |
| 7 | Maintain communication — radio checks every five minutes during transport |
| 8 | At destination — verify receiving authority, complete custody transfer paperwork, confirm prisoner is secured |

**Prisoner transport RP is underrated.** The drive from Fort Zancudo to a courthouse in the city creates opportunities for dialogue between the MP and the prisoner, ambush scenarios from the prisoner's allies, and tense convoy RP through civilian areas.

> `/me opens the rear door of the transport vehicle and gestures toward the seat.`
> "Watch your head getting in. Keep your hands in your lap and don't reach for anything. We're looking at a thirty-minute drive. You need water or a bathroom break before we roll?"

## VIP Escort

Protecting high-ranking officers, visiting officials, or other important persons is a specialized MP function.

**VIP escort procedures:** An advance team scouts the route and destination first. The convoy uses lead vehicle, VIP vehicle, and trail vehicle at minimum. Routes avoid choke points, high-crime areas, and construction zones with an alternate pre-planned. A dedicated radio channel provides continuous updates. At the destination, MPs establish a perimeter and maintain eyes on the VIP.

| From | To | Recommended Route | Avoid |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fort Zancudo | City Hall (Downtown LS) | Route 68 to Great Ocean Highway, south to Downtown exits | Davis, Strawberry |
| Fort Zancudo | Pillbox Medical Center | Route 68 to freeway south, exit at Vespucci Boulevard | Highway construction zones |
| LSIA (Airport) | Fort Zancudo | Airport exits north to freeway, west on Route 68 | Congested airport roads |
| City Hall | Bolingbroke Penitentiary | Freeway north, exit at Route 68, east to facility | Sandy Shores |

## Perimeter Security During Operations

When the military conducts operations off base — disaster response, civil unrest, special events — MPs establish and maintain the perimeter.

**Perimeter security:** Define the boundary with barriers, vehicles, or personnel. Control access points — everyone gets checked. Maintain observation in all directions. Report activity to the operation commander. React proportionally to breaches — challenge, detain, escalate only if necessary. A civilian wandering near a disaster zone is not a combatant — they need redirecting, not a rifle pointed at them.

## Traffic Control on Base

Base traffic management is routine but essential. Fort Zancudo has internal roads, motor pool areas, flight lines, and restricted zones that require controlled movement.

**Traffic control responsibilities:**
- **Speed enforcement** — Base speed limits are typically 15–25 MPH. Soldiers who drive like they are on the freeway get stopped.
- **Convoy routing** — When convoys form up in the motor pool and move to the gate, MPs manage the route to prevent conflicts with other base traffic.
- **Flight line safety** — No unauthorized vehicles near the hangars or runway. Aircraft have the right of way. Always.
- **Emergency vehicle priority** — Base emergency vehicles (fire, medical, security response) have priority over all traffic.
- **Parking enforcement** — Unauthorized vehicles in restricted lots get towed. Officers' parking is for officers. The commissary lot is not overflow parking for the motor pool.

> `/me steps into the road and holds up a closed fist toward the approaching Crusader.`
> "Hold up, Corporal. We've got a Titan taxiing across to Hangar 3. Nobody moves until that aircraft is clear and the flight line gives the all-clear. Kill your engine."

## MP Rank and Career Progression

Military Police follow the standard military rank structure, but with roles specific to MP operations.

| Rank | MP Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| Private (PVT) | Gate guard / patrol officer | Gate duty, base patrol, report writing |
| PFC / Specialist | Senior patrol / escort duty | Experienced patrol, prisoner transport, VIP escort |
| Corporal (CPL) | Shift leader | Supervises a gate or patrol shift, assigns tasks |
| Sergeant (SGT) | Investigations NCO | Leads criminal investigations, trains junior MPs |
| Staff Sergeant (SSG) | Operations NCO | Plans security operations, coordinates with civilian LE |
| Second Lieutenant (2LT) | Platoon leader | Commands an MP platoon, reports to company commander |
| Captain (CPT) | MP Company commander | Overall command of MP operations on base |

**Specialization paths for MPs:**
- **Criminal Investigation Division (CID)** — Plainclothes investigations of serious crimes
- **K9 handler** — Working with military working dogs for detection and patrol
- **Close protection** — VIP security specialization
- **Forensics** — Evidence collection and analysis
- **Corrections** — Brig management and prisoner rehabilitation programs

## The Brig

The brig is the military jail. It is where military prisoners are held pending trial, serving sentences, or awaiting transfer.

**Brig operations:**
- **Intake** — Search, inventory personal belongings, issue prison uniform, assign cell, medical screening
- **Daily routine** — Meals, exercise period, work details, hygiene, lights out
- **Visitation** — Scheduled visits from legal counsel (JAG), family (if approved), and command
- **Security** — Regular headcounts, cell inspections, contraband checks
- **Release** — Upon completion of sentence or acquittal, prisoner is processed out, belongings returned, escorted off premises

**Brig RP is slow-burn and powerful.** A soldier sitting in the brig facing a court-martial has time to reflect, build relationships with guards, or prepare their defense. The brig is not just a holding cell — it is a story location.

## Daily Life as an MP

Most MP days involve routine that creates the backbone of base RP. A typical shift: report for duty and receive the briefing, check equipment (sidearm, radio, cuffs, body armor), take your post assignment (gate, patrol, brig watch, or dispatch), patrol the base checking buildings and restricted areas, respond to calls (barracks noise complaints, motor pool accidents, unauthorized personnel near the flight line), write incident reports and update the daily log, then brief the incoming shift on everything that happened.

> `/me leans against the front desk at the MP station, flipping through the shift log from the previous watch.`
> "Anything I need to know about today?"
> *The outgoing sergeant shrugs.*
> "Quiet night. Two barracks noise complaints, one gate runner who turned out to be a lost pizza delivery driver. Brig count is three — all stable."

## Final Thoughts

Military Police RP is the bridge between two worlds — the structured discipline of the military and the investigative complexity of law enforcement. You are not just a soldier standing at a gate. You are the person who keeps Fort Zancudo running safely, who holds service members accountable to the code they swore to uphold, and who represents the military in every interaction with the civilian world outside the wire.

It is not glamorous. It is not always exciting. But when a case comes together, when a base threat is neutralized, when a prisoner is safely transported through hostile territory — there is a quiet satisfaction that comes from knowing you did the job right.

Hold the line. Keep the peace. Enforce the code.
