# Medical Terminology — Speaking the Language of EMS and Hospital RP

Nothing breaks immersion faster than a paramedic who says "he got shot in the tummy" or a doctor who describes a patient as "really hurt." Medical terminology is the language of EMS and hospital RP. You do not need a medical degree, but you need enough vocabulary to sound credible, communicate clearly, and match the level of detail that quality medical RP demands.

This guide is a reference sheet for the most commonly used medical terms in GTA V roleplay — organized by category so you can find what you need during a scene.

---

## Vital Signs Terminology

Vital signs are the first thing you check on any patient. Knowing the terms and normal ranges makes your assessment RP convincing.

| Vital Sign | Abbreviation | Normal Adult Range | What It Tells You |
|---|---|---|---|
| Heart rate / Pulse | HR | 60–100 bpm | How fast the heart is beating |
| Blood pressure | BP | 120/80 mmHg (approximate) | Force of blood against vessel walls — systolic/diastolic |
| Respiratory rate | RR | 12–20 breaths/min | How fast the patient is breathing |
| Oxygen saturation | SpO2 | 95–100% | How much oxygen is in the blood |
| Temperature | Temp | 97.8–99.1°F / 36.5–37.3°C | Body temperature — fever indicates infection or heat illness |
| Blood glucose | BGL | 70–140 mg/dL | Blood sugar level — too low or too high causes altered mental status |

**Reporting vitals in RP:**
> "Vitals are — BP 138 over 92, pulse 110 and thready, resps 24 and shallow, SpO2 at 91% on room air. She's tachycardic and mildly hypertensive — consistent with hemorrhagic shock compensation."

**Abnormal vital sign terms:**

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Tachycardia | Heart rate above 100 bpm |
| Bradycardia | Heart rate below 60 bpm |
| Hypertension | High blood pressure (above 140/90) |
| Hypotension | Low blood pressure (below 90/60) — sign of shock |
| Tachypnea | Rapid breathing (above 20/min) |
| Bradypnea | Slow breathing (below 12/min) |
| Hypoxia | Insufficient oxygen in the tissues |
| Hypoglycemia | Low blood sugar |
| Hyperglycemia | High blood sugar |
| Febrile | Having a fever |
| Afebrile | No fever present |

## Injury Types

The bread and butter of trauma RP. Know how to describe what you are looking at.

### Gunshot Wounds (GSW)

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| GSW | Gunshot wound — the standard abbreviation |
| Entry wound | Where the bullet entered the body — typically small, round |
| Exit wound | Where the bullet exited — typically larger, more irregular |
| Through-and-through | Bullet entered and exited — both wounds present |
| Retained projectile | Bullet is still inside the body |
| Fragmentation | Bullet broke apart inside the body, causing multiple wound tracks |
| Cavitation | Temporary and permanent tissue damage caused by the bullet's energy |

> `/me examines the wound on the patient's upper right chest. Small entry wound, approximately 9mm, no exit wound palpated on the posterior.`
> "Single GSW to the right upper chest, no exit wound — the round is retained. Possible pneumothorax. I need a chest seal."

### Blunt Force Trauma

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Contusion | Bruise — bleeding under the skin from impact |
| Hematoma | Localized collection of blood outside vessels, causing swelling |
| Concussion | Mild traumatic brain injury from impact — altered consciousness |
| Laceration | Torn or jagged wound from blunt impact (not a clean cut) |
| Abrasion | Scrape — superficial skin loss from friction |
| Ecchymosis | Discoloration of the skin from underlying bleeding — a bruise |

### Lacerations and Penetrating Injuries

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Laceration | Irregular wound caused by tearing |
| Incision | Clean, straight wound caused by a sharp object (knife, glass) |
| Puncture wound | Deep, narrow wound from a pointed object (stab wound, nail) |
| Avulsion | Skin or tissue partially or fully torn away |
| Impaled object | Foreign object still embedded in the wound — do not remove in the field |
| Evisceration | Abdominal organs protruding through a wound — cover with moist sterile dressings |

### Fractures

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Closed fracture | Bone is broken but skin is intact |
| Open (compound) fracture | Bone is broken and protruding through the skin |
| Hairline fracture | Small crack in the bone — painful but structurally intact |
| Comminuted fracture | Bone shattered into multiple pieces |
| Displaced fracture | Bone ends are misaligned |
| Crepitus | Grinding sensation or sound when fractured bone ends rub together |

> `/me palpates the patient's left forearm and feels a deformity midshaft with crepitus. The skin is intact but significant swelling is present.`
> "Closed fracture of the left radius, midshaft. Deformity with crepitus. I'm going to splint this — don't move it."

### Burns

| Classification | Depth | Appearance |
|---|---|---|
| Superficial (1st degree) | Epidermis only | Red, painful, no blisters — like a sunburn |
| Partial thickness (2nd degree) | Epidermis and part of dermis | Blisters, very painful, moist appearance |
| Full thickness (3rd degree) | Through entire dermis | White, leathery, or charred — painless (nerves destroyed) |
| Fourth degree | Through skin into muscle/bone | Charred, exposed deep tissue |

**Rule of Nines** — estimating burn area:

| Body Region | Percentage of Body Surface |
|---|---|
| Head and neck | 9% |
| Each arm | 9% |
| Chest (front) | 9% |
| Abdomen (front) | 9% |
| Upper back | 9% |
| Lower back | 9% |
| Each upper leg (thigh) | 9% |
| Each lower leg | 9% |
| Groin | 1% |

> "We've got partial and full thickness burns covering approximately 27% body surface — both arms and the anterior chest. This is a critical burn patient. We need to get him to the burn unit."

## Treatment Terminology

Know the names of the procedures you are performing.

| Term | Definition | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| Intubation | Inserting a tube into the trachea to secure the airway | Patient cannot breathe on their own or protect their airway |
| Extubation | Removing the endotracheal tube | Patient can breathe independently again |
| IV (Intravenous) access | Inserting a catheter into a vein for fluid or medication delivery | Almost every serious patient gets an IV |
| IO (Intraosseous) access | Inserting a needle into bone marrow for emergency access | When IV access cannot be obtained — cardiac arrest, severe shock |
| Tourniquet (TQ) | Device to compress a limb and stop arterial bleeding | Life-threatening extremity hemorrhage |
| Splinting | Immobilizing a broken or injured limb | Fractures, dislocations, severe sprains |
| Chest seal | Occlusive dressing applied to a chest wound | Penetrating chest trauma — prevents or treats pneumothorax |
| Needle decompression | Inserting a needle into the chest to release trapped air | Tension pneumothorax (collapsed lung under pressure) |
| CPR | Cardiopulmonary resuscitation — chest compressions and ventilations | Cardiac arrest — no pulse, no breathing |
| Defibrillation | Delivering an electrical shock to the heart | Specific cardiac arrest rhythms (V-fib, pulseless V-tach) |
| Suturing | Stitching a wound closed | Lacerations that need closure after bleeding is controlled |
| Irrigation | Flushing a wound with sterile fluid | Cleaning contaminated wounds before closure |
| Reduction | Realigning a dislocated joint or displaced fracture | Dislocations, some fractures — usually done by a doctor |
| Tracheostomy | Surgical airway through the neck | When the mouth/nose airway is completely obstructed |
| Thoracotomy | Emergency chest surgery to access the heart or lungs | Penetrating chest trauma with cardiac arrest |
| Fasciotomy | Cutting open the fascia to relieve compartment pressure | Compartment syndrome from crush injuries or severe swelling |

## Medical Conditions

Common conditions you will encounter and need to recognize.

| Condition | What It Is | Signs You Will See |
|---|---|---|
| Cardiac arrest | Heart has stopped pumping | No pulse, no breathing, unresponsive |
| Myocardial infarction (MI) | Heart attack — blocked blood supply to the heart | Chest pain, shortness of breath, sweating, nausea |
| Stroke (CVA) | Blocked or burst blood vessel in the brain | Facial droop, arm weakness, slurred speech (FAST) |
| Hemorrhagic shock | Life-threatening blood loss | Rapid pulse, low BP, pale/cool skin, altered consciousness |
| Hypovolemic shock | Shock from fluid loss (blood, dehydration) | Similar to hemorrhagic shock — rapid deterioration |
| Anaphylaxis | Severe allergic reaction | Swelling (throat, face), hives, difficulty breathing, low BP |
| Pneumothorax | Collapsed lung — air leaking into the chest cavity | Chest pain, difficulty breathing, decreased breath sounds on one side |
| Tension pneumothorax | Collapsed lung with pressure building — life-threatening | All above plus tracheal deviation, distended neck veins |
| Sepsis | Systemic infection — body's response is damaging its own tissues | Fever, rapid heart rate, confusion, low BP |
| Overdose (OD) | Toxic level of a substance in the body | Depends on substance — opioids cause slow breathing and pinpoint pupils |
| Hypothermia | Core body temperature drops dangerously low | Shivering, confusion, slow pulse, eventually unresponsive |
| Hyperthermia / Heat stroke | Core body temperature rises dangerously high | Hot/dry skin, confusion, rapid pulse, seizures |
| Seizure | Abnormal electrical activity in the brain | Uncontrolled shaking, loss of consciousness, postictal confusion |
| DKA (Diabetic Ketoacidosis) | Dangerously high blood sugar with acid buildup | Fruity breath odor, deep rapid breathing, nausea, confusion |

## Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)

The GCS is a standardized way to rate a patient's level of consciousness. It is critical for head injury assessment.

| Component | Response | Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Eye Opening** | Spontaneous | 4 |
| | To voice | 3 |
| | To pain | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Verbal Response** | Oriented and conversing | 5 |
| | Confused conversation | 4 |
| | Inappropriate words | 3 |
| | Incomprehensible sounds | 2 |
| | None | 1 |
| **Motor Response** | Obeys commands | 6 |
| | Localizes pain | 5 |
| | Withdraws from pain | 4 |
| | Abnormal flexion | 3 |
| | Extension | 2 |
| | None | 1 |

**Total GCS: 3 (worst) to 15 (best)**

| GCS Range | Classification |
|---|---|
| 13–15 | Mild brain injury |
| 9–12 | Moderate brain injury |
| 3–8 | Severe brain injury — intubation typically required |

> "GCS is 7 — eyes opening to pain only, making incomprehensible sounds, withdrawing from painful stimuli. This is a severe head injury. Preparing for intubation."

## Anatomical Directional Terms

Using proper anatomical terms when describing wound locations adds realism.

| Term | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Anterior | Front of the body | "GSW to the anterior chest" |
| Posterior | Back of the body | "Exit wound on the posterior right shoulder" |
| Lateral | Toward the side | "Laceration to the lateral left thigh" |
| Medial | Toward the midline | "Bruising along the medial forearm" |
| Superior | Above / upper | "The wound is superior to the clavicle" |
| Inferior | Below / lower | "Swelling inferior to the right knee" |
| Proximal | Closer to the body's center | "Apply the tourniquet proximal to the wound" |
| Distal | Further from the body's center | "Check distal pulses in the foot" |
| Bilateral | Both sides | "Bilateral breath sounds present and equal" |
| Unilateral | One side only | "Unilateral decreased breath sounds on the left" |

## Putting It All Together — Example Scene

> `/me kneels beside the patient and checks for a carotid pulse — weak and rapid, approximately 120 bpm. Skin is pale, cool, and diaphoretic.`
> "I've got a 30-year-old male, single GSW to the left anterior abdomen, no exit wound. He's tachycardic at 120, BP is 82 over 54, resps at 28 and shallow. GCS is 12 — eyes opening to voice, confused verbal, obeying commands. He's showing signs of hemorrhagic shock. Starting two large-bore IVs and applying a pressure dressing to the wound. We need to load and go — Pillbox, notify trauma surgery."

That paragraph uses over a dozen medical terms correctly and naturally. That is the standard to aim for.

---

## Final Thoughts

You do not need to memorize every term on this page. Bookmark it. Reference it during scenes. Over time, the vocabulary becomes second nature and your medical RP will sound authentic without effort.

The words you use matter. They set the tone, build immersion, and tell other players that you take the role seriously. When you say "tachycardic and hypotensive with signs of hemorrhagic shock" instead of "his heart is fast and his blood pressure is low" — the entire scene elevates.

Learn the language. Speak it fluently. Your patients deserve it.
