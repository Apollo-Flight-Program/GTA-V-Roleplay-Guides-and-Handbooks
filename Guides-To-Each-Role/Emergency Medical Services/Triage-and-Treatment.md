# Triage and Treatment — Managing Mass Casualties in Los Santos

A normal call has one patient. Maybe two. You assess, treat, and transport. But when a bomb goes off outside the Maze Bank, a pileup shuts down the Senora Freeway, or a shooting at a nightclub leaves a dozen people on the ground — normal goes out the window. Mass casualty incidents demand a fundamentally different approach. You stop being a caregiver for one and become a resource manager for many.

This guide covers mass casualty triage, the incident command system, field operations, psychological first aid, and the debrief process that keeps responders sane after the worst day of their career.

---

## What Is a Mass Casualty Incident (MCI)?

An MCI occurs when the number of patients exceeds the resources immediately available to treat them. This is not a fixed number — three patients can be an MCI if only one medic is on duty.

**Common MCI scenarios in Los Santos RP:**

| Scenario | Typical Patient Count | Challenges |
|---|---|---|
| Multi-vehicle accident on the freeway | 4–10 | Extrication, hazardous roadway, traffic control |
| Shooting at a nightclub or public event | 5–20+ | Active threat, ongoing police operation, high-acuity patients |
| Explosion (bomb, gas leak, meth lab) | 5–15 | Blast injuries, structural collapse, secondary device risk |
| Building collapse | 3–10 | Trapped patients, prolonged extrication, dust/debris |
| Hazmat exposure | Variable | Decontamination required before treatment, specialized PPE |
| Natural disaster (earthquake, flood) | City-wide | Infrastructure damage, communication failures, resource scarcity |
| Bus or train accident | 10–30+ | High patient count, varied injury severity, access challenges |

**The moment you recognize an MCI, your mindset changes.** You are no longer trying to provide the best possible care to one person. You are trying to do the most good for the most people with the resources you have.

## The START Triage System

START stands for **Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment.** It is the most widely used MCI triage system and the standard for RP.

**How START works:**
Walk through the scene and assess each patient in under 60 seconds. You are not treating — you are sorting. Tag each patient with a color based on their condition.

**START triage flowchart:**

| Step | Assessment | Action |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Can they walk? | Ask all patients who can walk to move to a designated area | Walking patients are tagged **GREEN** (minor) |
| 2. Check breathing | Is the patient breathing? | If NO — open the airway. If they breathe after repositioning, tag **RED**. If still no breathing, tag **BLACK**. |
| 3. Respiratory rate | How fast are they breathing? | If over 30 breaths/min, tag **RED** (immediate) |
| 4. Check circulation | Check radial pulse or capillary refill | If no radial pulse or capillary refill over 2 seconds, tag **RED** |
| 5. Mental status | Can they follow simple commands? ("Squeeze my hand") | If they cannot follow commands, tag **RED**. If they can, tag **YELLOW** (delayed) |

---

## Triage Color Categories

| Color | Priority | Meaning | Treatment Timing | Examples |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **RED** | Immediate | Life-threatening but survivable with immediate treatment | Treat and transport first | Severe hemorrhage, airway compromise, tension pneumothorax |
| **YELLOW** | Delayed | Serious injuries but can wait for treatment without dying | Treat after all RED patients | Fractures without hemorrhage, moderate burns, abdominal wounds (stable) |
| **GREEN** | Minor | Walking wounded — injuries are real but not life-threatening | Treat last or self-aid | Sprains, minor lacerations, psychological distress, bruises |
| **BLACK** | Expectant / Deceased | Dead or injuries so severe that survival is not expected with available resources | No treatment during the acute phase | Cardiac arrest in MCI (no CPR), massive unsurvivable trauma |

**The hardest decision in triage:** Tagging someone BLACK who might survive with significant resources. In a normal call, you would do CPR for twenty minutes. In an MCI, those twenty minutes could save three RED patients. Triage forces you to make utilitarian choices — and that creates powerful, difficult RP.

> `/me kneels beside the patient, checks for breathing — absent. Opens the airway — still no breathing. Pauses for a moment. Places a black tag on the patient's chest and moves to the next victim.`

## Incident Command System (ICS)

The ICS provides structure to chaos. Without it, multiple agencies step on each other, resources are wasted, and patients die from disorganization rather than their injuries.

**Key ICS positions for EMS at an MCI:**

| Position | Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| Incident Commander (IC) | Overall scene authority | Sets objectives, coordinates all agencies, makes strategic decisions |
| Medical Branch Director | EMS command | Manages all medical operations at the incident |
| Triage Officer | Sorts patients | Oversees triage operations, ensures accurate tagging |
| Treatment Officer | Manages treatment areas | Sets up treatment zones, assigns medics to patients by priority |
| Transport Officer | Coordinates transport | Assigns ambulances, tracks which patients go to which hospitals |
| Staging Officer | Manages incoming resources | Directs arriving units to staging areas, deploys as needed |

**Unified command:** When police, fire, and EMS all have significant roles at a scene, they share command. Each agency manages their area of expertise — police handle the threat, fire handles the hazard, EMS handles the patients — but they coordinate through a shared command structure.

> **Medical Branch:** "Incident Command, Medical Branch. I have 14 patients triaged — 3 RED, 5 YELLOW, 4 GREEN, 2 BLACK. I need a minimum of three additional ambulances for transport. Requesting Mount Zonah receive two of our REDs — Pillbox is going to be overwhelmed."
> **IC:** "Copy, Medical Branch. Three ambulances being dispatched from staging. I'll have dispatch coordinate with Mount Zonah. Do you need additional personnel?"
> **Medical Branch:** "Affirmative. Two more paramedics for the treatment area and one EMT for the GREEN zone."

## Resource Allocation

In an MCI, resources are finite. Allocating them correctly saves lives. Allocating them poorly costs them.

**Resource priorities:**

| Resource | Allocation Rule |
|---|---|
| Paramedics | Assign to RED patients first — advanced skills where they matter most |
| EMTs | Assign to YELLOW patients and assist with GREEN zone management |
| Ambulances | First available ambulance takes the most critical RED patient |
| Equipment | Trauma bags, airway kits, and IV supplies go to the treatment area — not scattered across the scene |
| Hospital capacity | Distribute patients across multiple hospitals — do not overwhelm one facility |
| Air transport | Reserve for the most critical patient who needs a facility that ground transport cannot reach in time |

**Do not hoard resources.** If you have four trauma bags and two patients, send the extra bags to someone who needs them. Situational awareness extends beyond your immediate area.

## Field Hospital Setup

When the scene will be prolonged or transport is delayed, establish field treatment areas.

**Treatment area layout:**

| Zone | Purpose | Location Guidance |
|---|---|---|
| RED treatment | Immediate priority patients | Closest to transport staging — these patients leave first |
| YELLOW treatment | Delayed patients receiving ongoing care | Adjacent to RED but separate — do not mix priorities |
| GREEN treatment | Walking wounded self-aid and monitoring | Further from the action — can be managed with minimal staff |
| BLACK / Morgue | Deceased patients | Out of view from other patients and bystanders if possible |
| Medical staging | Equipment and reserve personnel | Central location, accessible but not in the work areas |

> `/me drops the orange cones in a line separating the RED and YELLOW treatment areas. Points to the EMT arriving with a stretcher.`
> "RED patients go north of the cones, YELLOW south. If you don't know, ask me — don't guess. Every minute a YELLOW takes a RED's ambulance slot, someone else doesn't make it."

## Communication During Mass Events

Communication failures are the leading cause of preventable deaths in MCIs. Keep it organized.

**Communication rules during an MCI:**

| Rule | Why |
|---|---|
| Use your assigned ICS call sign, not your unit number | Prevents confusion between incident and normal dispatch traffic |
| Keep transmissions under 15 seconds | The radio channel is shared by dozens of responders |
| Repeat critical information (patient count, resource requests) | Ensures nothing is missed in the chaos |
| Use plain language, not codes | Codes vary between agencies — plain language is universal |
| Designate a single radio channel for medical operations | Keeps medical traffic separate from fire and police operations |
| Face-to-face communication when possible | The most reliable form of communication at a noisy scene |

**Signal 100:** When called, all units hold radio traffic except for emergency transmissions. Used during the most critical moments of an incident.

> **Medical Branch:** "All medical units on Tac-2, Signal 100. I am redistributing transport assignments. Stand by for your updated assignments. Break."
> "Medic-3, you are transporting RED Patient 1 to Pillbox. Medic-7, you are transporting RED Patient 2 to Mount Zonah. Medic-11, you are transporting RED Patient 3 to Pillbox. All units acknowledge in order."

## Psychological First Aid (PFA)

Not all injuries are physical. MCIs traumatize survivors, bystanders, and even responders.

**Providing PFA to survivors and bystanders:**

| Action | How |
|---|---|
| Contact | Approach calmly, introduce yourself, ask if they need help |
| Safety | Move them away from the scene to a quiet area |
| Stabilization | Help them regulate breathing, orient them to the present |
| Information | Tell them what is happening — uncertainty increases distress |
| Connection | Help them contact family or friends |
| Referral | Provide information about counseling and support resources |

> `/me sits down next to the woman on the curb, keeping a respectful distance. Speaks quietly.`
> "Hey. I'm a paramedic. You're safe now. Can you tell me your name? Is there someone I can call for you?"

**Green zone patients often need PFA more than bandages.** A walking wounded patient with a scraped knee who just watched three people die is not a minor patient — they are a person in psychological crisis.

## Working with Law Enforcement During Active Scenes

Some MCIs involve ongoing threats — active shooters, ongoing gang violence, secondary explosive devices. EMS must coordinate closely with police.

**Rescue Task Force (RTF) operations:**
In an active threat, EMS does not enter the hot zone. Instead, police and EMS form Rescue Task Forces — small teams of officers and medics who enter together. Police provide security, medics provide care.

| Phase | EMS Role |
|---|---|
| Active threat | Stage in the cold zone. Do NOT enter. |
| Threat neutralized / contained | Enter with police escort as part of an RTF |
| Scene secured (Code 4) | Normal EMS operations — full access to all patients |

> **LSPD Sergeant:** "Medical Branch, we have the shooter contained on the second floor. The first floor is clear. I can provide a two-officer escort for an RTF to access the first-floor victims."
> **Medical Branch:** "Copy, Sergeant. Sending two paramedics to your position for RTF entry. They'll need vests."

**Key rules:**
- Never enter an unsecured scene — your death helps no one
- Wear ballistic protection if provided
- Stay with your police escort at all times
- Perform rapid triage and drag-to-safety extractions — do not stop to treat in the hot zone

## Debrief Procedures

After an MCI, debriefing is essential — both operationally and emotionally.

**Operational debrief (hot wash):**
Held within hours of the incident. All responding units participate.

- What happened — timeline of events
- What went well — effective decisions, good communication, successful saves
- What went wrong — delays, miscommunication, resource gaps
- Lessons learned — what to change for next time
- Documentation review — ensure all patient records are complete

**Emotional debrief (CISM — Critical Incident Stress Management):**
Held within 24–72 hours. Voluntary but strongly encouraged.

- Led by a trained peer support member or mental health professional
- Responders share their experience — what they saw, what they felt, what is bothering them
- No judgment, no critique — this is about processing, not performance review
- Normalize stress reactions — difficulty sleeping, replaying the scene, emotional numbness
- Provide resources for ongoing support if needed

> **Captain, addressing the shift:** "We lost two patients today. That's going to sit with all of us for a while. If you need to talk — to me, to each other, to the department counselor — do it. There's no shame in that. What we went through today was not normal, and it's okay to not be okay about it."

## Common MCI RP Scenarios

**The freeway pileup:**
> A six-car pileup on the Senora Freeway near Harmony. Eight patients. Two trapped in vehicles requiring fire extrication. One ejected from their vehicle and found thirty feet from the wreck. Traffic is backed up for a mile. You have two ambulances and one fire engine on scene with more en route. Start triaging.

**The nightclub shooting:**
> Gunfire erupts inside Tequi-la-la. Twelve victims. Police are clearing the building. You are staged two blocks away. When you get the all-clear, you enter to find a scene of chaos — screaming, blood, broken glass. Two victims are clearly deceased. Three are critical. Seven are walking wounded. Your triage tags are the only thing standing between organized rescue and pandemonium.

**The gas station explosion:**
> A gas leak at the RON station in Strawberry ignites. The explosion damages surrounding buildings. Five burn patients, two with blast injuries, three with structural collapse injuries. Fire is still burning. Hazmat is a concern. You cannot even access three of the patients until fire suppresses the blaze.

## Final Thoughts

Mass casualty incidents are the hardest calls you will ever run. They demand that you set aside the instinct to help the person in front of you and instead think about the fifteen people you cannot see yet. They require discipline, communication, and the willingness to make decisions that haunt you.

But when you get it right — when the triage is accurate, the resources are allocated correctly, and patients who should have died are loaded into ambulances still breathing — there is nothing in EMS RP that comes close.

Train for it. Prepare for it. And when it happens, trust your system.
