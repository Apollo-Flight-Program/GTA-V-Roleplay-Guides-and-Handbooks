# EMS Basics — The Lifeline of Los Santos

Emergency Medical Services are the pulse of any roleplay server. Without EMS, gunshot victims bleed out with no story. Car crashes have no aftermath. Overdoses end with a respawn instead of a scene. EMS roleplay transforms what would be a death screen into a fifteen-minute interaction that pulls in bystanders, police, hospital staff, and the patient themselves. A well-run EMS department does not just save lives — it creates some of the most emotionally intense RP on the server.

This guide covers everything a new or experienced EMS player needs to understand about the role — from department structure and radio protocols to shift life and cross-department cooperation.

---

## What EMS Does in Roleplay

EMS is the bridge between crisis and recovery. Your job is to respond to emergencies, stabilize patients, transport them to medical facilities, and hand them off for further care. But more than that, EMS is the calm in the chaos. You arrive at scenes where people are panicking, bleeding, and dying — and you take control.

| Core EMS Functions | What It Looks Like in RP |
|---|---|
| Emergency response | Responding to 10-52 (medical emergency) calls with lights and sirens |
| Patient assessment | Using /me to check vitals, assess injuries, determine severity |
| Stabilization | Applying tourniquets, starting IVs, administering medications |
| Transport | Loading patients into the ambulance and driving to Pillbox Medical |
| Hospital handoff | Briefing ER staff on what happened and what you did |
| Scene support | Assisting police and fire at multi-agency incidents |
| Documentation | Writing reports on calls, treatments administered, and outcomes |
| Public education | Teaching civilians basic first aid, CPR awareness events |

EMS is not just about the medical mechanics — it is about the human moments. Holding a dying character's hand. Telling a worried friend in the waiting room that their person is going to be okay. Pronouncing someone dead and watching the room react.

## Department Structure

Most RP servers model their EMS department after real-world structures. The specific ranks may vary, but the hierarchy generally follows this pattern:

| Rank | Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| EMS Director / Chief | Department head | Policy, hiring, interdepartmental relations, budget |
| Assistant Chief | Second in command | Operations oversight, training program management |
| Captain | Shift commander | Manages a shift, assigns units, handles escalations |
| Lieutenant | Senior supervisor | Mentors paramedics, leads complex scenes, quality review |
| Paramedic | Advanced provider | Full scope of emergency care, medication administration |
| EMT (Emergency Medical Technician) | Standard responder | Basic life support, patient transport, scene assistance |
| Probationary EMT | Trainee | Rides with a senior partner, learning protocols and procedures |

**Chain of command matters.** If you are an EMT and disagree with how a scene is being managed, you do not argue on the radio. You follow orders and bring it up with your lieutenant after the call. Breaking chain of command during an active scene creates chaos and destroys immersion.

## Radio Protocols and Communication

EMS radio communication should be clear, concise, and professional. Sloppy radio work breaks immersion faster than almost anything else.

**Standard radio format:**
> `[Unit ID], [Dispatch or recipient], [Message]`

**Common EMS radio codes:**

| Code | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 10-4 | Acknowledged / Copy |
| 10-8 | In service, available |
| 10-7 | Out of service |
| 10-52 | Medical emergency |
| 10-52A | Motor vehicle accident with injuries |
| 10-52B | Gunshot wound |
| Code 3 | Responding with lights and sirens |
| Code 4 | Scene is secure / situation under control |
| Signal 100 | All units hold radio traffic (emergency only) |

**Example radio exchange:**

> **Dispatch:** "Medic-3, respond to a 10-52 at the intersection of Innocence Boulevard and Capital Boulevard, multiple victims reported, Code 3."
> **Medic-3:** "Medic-3 copy, responding Code 3 to Innocence and Capital."
> *(arrives on scene)*
> **Medic-3:** "Medic-3 on scene, I have two patients — one conscious, one unresponsive. Requesting a second unit."

**Radio discipline rules:**
- Keep transmissions short — say what you need to say and release the channel
- Do not chat casually on the emergency frequency
- Identify yourself every time you transmit
- Repeat critical information (addresses, patient count, hazard types)
- Use plain language when codes might be misunderstood

## Responding to Calls

When a call comes in, your response follows a predictable pattern that keeps scenes organized and immersive.

**Response phases:**

1. **Dispatch** — You receive the call with a location, nature of the emergency, and any known hazards
2. **Response** — Drive Code 3 (lights and sirens) or Code 2 (no lights/sirens) depending on severity
3. **Arrival** — Park the ambulance strategically — not blocking the scene but close enough to work
4. **Scene size-up** — Before touching anyone, assess the scene. Is it safe? How many patients? What resources do you need?
5. **Patient contact** — Begin assessment and treatment
6. **Stabilization** — Do what you can in the field
7. **Transport decision** — Does the patient need hospital care? Can they be treated and released on scene?
8. **Transport** — Load and go to Pillbox Medical Center or Mount Zonah Medical Center
9. **Handoff** — Brief the hospital staff and transfer care
10. **Clear** — Mark yourself available for the next call

**Driving to calls:**
Drive with urgency but not recklessness. You are no good to anyone if you crash the ambulance on the way. Use your siren, call out intersections if needed, and slow at red lights even when running Code 3. Real paramedics do this — so should you.

## Interaction with Police and Fire

EMS rarely operates alone. Most calls involve at least one other agency, and how you work together defines the quality of the scene.

### Working with Law Enforcement

Police often arrive first, especially at violent scenes. Respect their scene control.

- **Wait for scene security** — If there is an active shooter or violent suspect, do not rush in. Stage nearby and wait for police to declare the scene safe.
- **Work around their investigation** — Do not move a body before detectives process the scene unless the patient is alive and needs immediate transport.
- **Share information carefully** — You can tell police what injuries you observed, but patient statements made during treatment are often considered confidential in RP.
- **Custody patients** — If police need the patient in custody, coordinate transport. An officer may ride in the ambulance or follow to the hospital.

> **Officer:** "Medic, the suspect has a GSW to the right shoulder. Scene is clear, he's cuffed. He's yours."
> **Medic:** "Copy. I'll need those cuffs moved to the front so I can access the wound. Can you stay with us during transport?"

### Working with Fire Department

Fire handles vehicle extrication, hazmat, and structural rescue. EMS handles the patients they free.

- **Let fire do their job** — Do not crawl into a wrecked vehicle to treat a patient while fire is cutting it apart. Wait for extrication.
- **Communicate patient status** — Tell the fire crew what the patient's condition is so they know how urgently they need to work.
- **Unified command** — On large scenes, fire and EMS leadership should coordinate who is managing what.

## Shift Life and Routines

EMS is not just call after call. Between emergencies, paramedics have a life at the station.

**Station activities:**
- Check and restock the ambulance at the start of every shift
- Clean equipment and the rig after messy calls
- Train new EMTs — quiz them on protocols, run practice scenarios
- Cook meals together in the station kitchen
- Work out in the station gym
- Handle paperwork from previous calls
- BS with your partner about life, cases, and the city

> `/me opens the rear doors of the ambulance and begins checking the supply bags, counting gauze packs and verifying the oxygen tank pressure.`

**Shift change RP:**
When you hand off the shift to the next crew, brief them on anything relevant — ongoing situations, equipment issues, patients still at the hospital, areas with increased call volume.

## EMS Etiquette — The Unwritten Rules

| Rule | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| Never refuse a call | Even if the RP seems low-effort, respond professionally — you may turn it into something great |
| Do not pronounce death immediately | Give the patient time to roleplay their injuries before making a call |
| Ask before treating | Use /me to describe what you are doing and give the patient a chance to respond |
| Match the patient's energy | If they are doing detailed injury RP, match that level of detail in your treatment |
| Do not metagame injuries | Treat what you can observe, not what you know OOC happened |
| Stay in character at the hospital | Your job does not end at the ER doors — do the handoff properly |
| Respect death RP | If a character is perma-ing, make the scene meaningful and emotional |
| Debrief heavy scenes | After intense calls, check in with your partner IC and OOC |

## Common Mistakes New EMS Players Make

**The speed healer:** Arrives, uses a healing mechanic, and leaves in thirty seconds. No RP, no interaction, no story. You just turned a potential ten-minute scene into a vending machine transaction.

**The hero complex:** Rushing into unsafe scenes without waiting for police, ignoring hazards, trying to save everyone single-handedly. Realistic EMS waits for scene safety.

**Ignoring the patient's RP:** The patient describes a complex injury and you respond with "You're fine, get in the ambulance." Match their effort. If they describe a compound fracture, roleplay splinting it.

**Breaking character during downtime:** EMS downtime at the station is still RP time. Do not go AFK or break character just because there are no calls. Use the downtime to build relationships with your crew.

**Forgetting documentation:** Many servers expect EMS reports. Even if they do not, writing up your calls in a quick IC report adds professionalism and creates a paper trail that other departments can reference.

**Not coordinating with other units:** You arrive at a mass casualty and start treating the first person you see without coordinating with other medics. Use triage, assign roles, and communicate.

## Why EMS RP Matters for the Server Ecosystem

EMS is the connective tissue of the server. Your interactions touch every faction:

- **Criminals** get medical care after shootouts, creating vulnerable moments where story happens
- **Police** rely on your medical assessments for their reports and investigations
- **Civilians** experience realistic consequences for accidents and injuries
- **Hospital staff** receive patients you stabilize and transport
- **Fire department** works alongside you at complex scenes
- **Government** funds your department and responds to public health crises you identify

A server without active EMS feels hollow. Injuries mean nothing. Violence has no weight. The gap between action and consequence disappears. EMS fills that gap with human connection, medical drama, and the kind of slow-burn RP that stays with players long after the scene ends.

---

## Final Thoughts

EMS roleplay is not glamorous. You will not be in high-speed chases or dramatic shootouts. You will be kneeling next to someone on the pavement at two in the morning, asking them where it hurts while sirens wail in the background. You will hold pressure on a wound while a police officer takes a statement. You will tell someone's family that they did not make it.

And those moments — quiet, intense, deeply human — are why EMS RP matters more than most players realize.

Put on the uniform. Answer the call. Save the city one patient at a time.
