# Paramedic Guide — Advanced Prehospital Care in Los Santos

The paramedic is the highest-trained field responder in EMS. While EMTs provide basic life support, paramedics assess, diagnose, and treat patients in the field using advanced techniques. You are the one who decides whether a patient lives or dies before they ever reach a hospital. In roleplay, the paramedic role demands medical knowledge, scene management skills, and the ability to create compelling RP under pressure.

This guide covers every aspect of paramedic operations — from patient assessment and stabilization to mass casualty response, combative patients, and the hardest call you will ever make: pronouncing someone dead.

---

## Responding to 10-52 Calls

A 10-52 is a medical emergency — the bread and butter of your shift. Every 10-52 follows the same fundamental structure, but no two are ever the same.

**Dispatch information to listen for:**
- Location (street address, landmark, or GPS coordinates)
- Nature of the emergency (GSW, MVC, overdose, medical, trauma)
- Number of patients
- Known hazards (weapons, fire, hazmat, hostile individuals)
- Whether police or fire are already on scene

**Response types:**

| Response Level | When to Use | Driving |
|---|---|---|
| Code 3 | Life-threatening emergency | Lights and sirens, expedited |
| Code 2 | Urgent but not immediately life-threatening | Lights, no sirens, prompt but cautious |
| Code 1 | Non-emergency transport or follow-up | Normal driving, no lights or sirens |

> **Dispatch:** "Medic-7, 10-52 Bravo at the Vanilla Unicorn parking lot, single victim, reported gunshot wound to the abdomen. PD is en route, scene not yet secure."
> **Medic-7:** "Medic-7 copy, responding Code 3 to Vanilla Unicorn. Will stage at Elgin Avenue until PD advises Code 4."

**Staging:** When the scene is not secure, stage one or two blocks away. Do not drive into an active crime scene. Wait for police to clear the area before approaching. Your dead body does not help anyone.

## Patient Assessment — The ABC Method

Patient assessment is the core of paramedic RP. This is where you demonstrate medical knowledge and create detailed, immersive interactions.

**The ABC approach:**

| Step | What You Check | RP Actions |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Airway** | Is the airway clear? Can the patient breathe? | `/me tilts the patient's head back and lifts the chin, checking for obstructions in the airway.` |
| **B — Breathing** | Is the patient breathing? How fast? How deep? | `/me places an ear near the patient's mouth, listening for breath sounds while watching the chest for rise and fall.` |
| **C — Circulation** | Does the patient have a pulse? Is there major bleeding? | `/me presses two fingers against the patient's neck, feeling for a carotid pulse while scanning the body for visible bleeding.` |

After ABCs, perform a **rapid trauma assessment** or **focused medical assessment** depending on the call type:

**Trauma (injuries):**
> `/me runs gloved hands along the patient's arms, legs, and torso, feeling for deformities, swelling, or tenderness. Checks the back by carefully log-rolling the patient with the help of their partner.`

**Medical (illness):**
> `/me checks the patient's pupils with a penlight — equal and reactive. Asks: "Can you tell me your name? What day is it? Do you know where you are?"`

**SAMPLE history** — ask the patient or bystanders:

| Letter | Question |
|---|---|
| S | Signs and symptoms — What is wrong? What hurts? |
| A | Allergies — Are you allergic to any medications? |
| M | Medications — Are you taking anything? Prescribed or otherwise? |
| P | Past medical history — Any conditions I should know about? |
| L | Last oral intake — When did you last eat or drink? |
| E | Events leading up — What happened before this started? |

> "Ma'am, I need you to focus on my voice. Can you tell me what happened? Were you feeling anything unusual before you collapsed? Are you on any medications?"

## Stabilization Procedures

Once you have assessed the patient, stabilize them for transport. This is where your medical RP shines.

**Common stabilization actions:**

| Procedure | When to Use | /me Example |
|---|---|---|
| Tourniquet | Severe limb bleeding that direct pressure cannot control | `/me pulls a CAT tourniquet from the trauma bag and wraps it tightly above the wound on the patient's right thigh, twisting the windlass until the bleeding slows.` |
| Pressure dressing | Moderate bleeding from wounds | `/me packs the wound with hemostatic gauze and applies firm pressure with a bandage, wrapping it tightly.` |
| Cervical collar | Suspected spinal injury, car accidents | `/me stabilizes the patient's head with both hands and instructs the partner to apply a C-collar, maintaining manual in-line stabilization.` |
| Splint | Suspected fractures | `/me places a SAM splint along the patient's forearm, padding it with gauze, and secures it with an elastic bandage to immobilize the fracture.` |
| IV access | Fluid replacement, medication administration | `/me finds a vein in the patient's left forearm, cleans the site with an alcohol swab, and inserts an 18-gauge IV catheter, connecting a bag of normal saline.` |
| Oxygen | Difficulty breathing, low oxygen saturation | `/me places a non-rebreather mask over the patient's face and sets the oxygen flow to 15 liters per minute.` |
| Intubation | Patient cannot protect their own airway | `/me opens the intubation kit, uses a laryngoscope to visualize the vocal cords, and carefully passes an endotracheal tube, confirming placement by listening to breath sounds bilaterally.` |
| Narcan (Naloxone) | Suspected opioid overdose | `/me draws up 2mg of Naloxone and administers it intranasally, monitoring the patient for response.` |
| CPR | Cardiac arrest, no pulse detected | `/me begins chest compressions, locking elbows and pushing hard and fast in the center of the chest — thirty compressions, two breaths, checking for a pulse every two minutes.` |

## Transport Decisions

Not every patient goes to the hospital. The transport decision is a judgment call that demonstrates your experience.

**Transport immediately (load and go):**
- Uncontrolled hemorrhage
- Cardiac arrest or severe cardiac event
- Penetrating trauma to the chest or abdomen
- Severe head injury with altered consciousness
- Respiratory failure

**Treat on scene, then transport:**
- Stable fractures
- Moderate lacerations
- Overdose responding to Narcan
- Allergic reactions controlled with epinephrine

**Treat and release:**
- Minor cuts and bruises
- Anxiety attacks
- Minor intoxication with no medical risk
- Patients who refuse transport (get a refusal signature)

> `/me completes the assessment and turns to the patient. "Look, I'd strongly recommend we take you in to get checked out. You took a hard hit to the head and I want to make sure there's nothing going on underneath. But it's your call — I can't force you. If you refuse, I'll need you to sign this form acknowledging the risks."`

## Hospital Handoff

The handoff to hospital staff is the final act of your prehospital care. Do it right.

**SBAR format for handoff:**

| Component | What to Communicate |
|---|---|
| **S — Situation** | "We have a 28-year-old male with a single GSW to the left shoulder." |
| **B — Background** | "Found him conscious and alert in the parking lot at the Yellow Jack Inn. No significant medical history per the patient." |
| **A — Assessment** | "Entry wound left anterior shoulder, no exit wound. Controlled bleeding with pressure dressing. Vitals stable — BP 130/80, pulse 98, SpO2 96% on room air." |
| **R — Recommendation** | "He'll need imaging to locate the round and surgical consult. IV access established, left forearm, running normal saline." |

> **Paramedic:** "Doc, I've got a 32-year-old female, single MVC, driver's side impact. She was unresponsive on arrival — GCS of 8. We intubated in the field, C-spine immobilized. Suspected left femur fracture and possible internal bleeding — her BP has been dropping. Two large-bore IVs running wide open. She needs trauma surgery."

## Mass Casualty Response

When multiple patients overwhelm available resources, you switch from individual care to triage and resource management. This is the hardest transition for a paramedic to make — you have to accept that you cannot save everyone.

**Your role at an MCI:**
1. First arriving paramedic establishes **medical command** until relieved by a higher authority
2. Begin **START triage** — sort patients by severity using color tags
3. Call for additional resources immediately — do not wait to see if you can handle it
4. Assign incoming units to treatment areas
5. Coordinate transport — the sickest go first, to the closest appropriate facility
6. Communicate with incident command on patient count and resource needs

> **Medic-7:** "Dispatch, Medic-7 declaring a mass casualty incident at the intersection of San Andreas Avenue and Prosperity Street. I have at minimum eight patients from a multi-vehicle pileup. Requesting four additional medic units, fire for extrication, and notify Pillbox and Mount Zonah to prepare for incoming trauma patients."

## Equipment and Your Trauma Bag

Know your tools. A paramedic who fumbles through their bag breaks immersion.

| Equipment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Trauma shears | Cut away clothing to access wounds |
| Stethoscope | Listen to heart, lung, and bowel sounds |
| Blood pressure cuff | Measure blood pressure |
| Pulse oximeter | Measure blood oxygen saturation |
| Penlight | Check pupil response |
| Cervical collar | Immobilize the neck |
| Backboard and straps | Full spinal immobilization |
| IV kit | Establish intravenous access |
| Drug box | Medications — epinephrine, naloxone, morphine, etc. |
| Cardiac monitor / AED | Monitor heart rhythm, deliver defibrillation |
| Airway kit | Oral/nasal airways, intubation equipment |
| Suction unit | Clear airway of blood, vomit, or secretions |
| Burn kit | Sterile dressings, cool water irrigation supplies |
| Obstetric kit | Emergency childbirth supplies |

> `/me kneels beside the patient and unzips the trauma bag, pulling on a fresh pair of nitrile gloves before reaching for the trauma shears.`

## Dealing with Combative Patients

Not every patient wants your help. Intoxicated, mentally distressed, or adrenaline-fueled patients may fight, scream, or run.

**De-escalation first:**
> "Hey, hey — I'm not a cop. I'm a paramedic. I'm here to help you. Nobody's getting arrested right now. Let me just take a look, alright? You're bleeding pretty bad."

**If de-escalation fails:**
- Request police assistance for patient restraint
- Do not physically fight your patient — you are not trained for that and it is not your role
- Document combative behavior for your report
- If the patient is a danger to themselves or others, request a psychiatric hold (5150)

> `/me takes a step back and raises both hands, speaking calmly. "I understand you're upset. I'm not going to touch you without your permission. But you've got a wound that's going to get worse if we don't take care of it. Can we at least talk about it?"`

**Patients in police custody:**
- Treat the patient regardless of what they are accused of — you are not a judge
- Coordinate with the arresting officer about restraint accommodation
- Document any injuries that were present before police contact (this protects everyone)

## Death Pronouncement

The hardest part of the job. Sometimes you do everything right and the patient still dies.

**When to pronounce:**
- CPR has been ongoing for an extended period with no response (server-dependent, usually 10-15 minutes of RP)
- Injuries are incompatible with life (massive trauma, decapitation — obvious death)
- The patient's player indicates they are perma-ing their character

**How to pronounce:**
> `/me checks for a pulse one final time, then looks at the cardiac monitor showing a flat line. Pauses. Looks at the clock on the wall.`
> "Time of death... 22:47."

**After pronouncement:**
- Cover the body with a sheet
- Notify law enforcement if the death involves potential crime
- Speak to family or friends present — this is some of the most impactful RP you will ever do
- Step away and debrief with your partner

> `/me pulls the sheet up over the patient's face and stands slowly. Removes the gloves and drops them in the biohazard bag. Stands in silence for a moment before turning to the woman waiting by the door.`
> "Ma'am... I'm sorry. We did everything we could."

## Documentation and Reports

After every call, write a patient care report. This creates a paper trail for hospitals, police investigations, and your own department records.

**What to include:**
- Date, time, location of the call
- Patient information (name, age, gender — whatever you obtained)
- Chief complaint and mechanism of injury
- Assessment findings (vitals, injuries, mental status)
- Treatments administered
- Transport destination or refusal documentation
- Handoff summary

Even a brief report adds professionalism to your RP and gives other players something to reference.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The overdose in an alley:**
> You arrive at an alley behind the Vanilla Unicorn. A young woman is unresponsive, barely breathing, with a syringe near her hand. Her friend is panicking. You administer Narcan. She wakes up swinging. Now what?

**The gang shooting:**
> Four GSW victims outside a house in Davis. Two are from one gang, two from another. They are screaming at each other even while bleeding. Police are trying to separate them. You need to triage and treat while keeping yourself safe.

**The car accident with a child:**
> An SUV rolled on Route 68 near Harmony. The driver is pinned. There is a child in the back seat crying but appears uninjured. Fire is cutting the driver out. The child is asking for their parent. You need to manage both the medical and the emotional scene.

**The refusal:**
> A construction worker fell from scaffolding near the Mile High Club building. He seems fine but you suspect a concussion. He refuses transport — says he cannot miss work. You have to explain the risks and get him to sign a refusal form.

## Final Thoughts

Being a paramedic in Los Santos means carrying the weight of every call. You will save lives that should have been lost. You will lose lives you did everything to save. You will kneel in the rain at three in the morning on the side of the Great Ocean Highway holding pressure on a wound while your partner drives.

The medicine matters. The protocols matter. But what matters most is the human connection — looking someone in the eye and telling them they are going to be okay, and meaning it.

Answer the radio. Grab the bag. Go to work.
