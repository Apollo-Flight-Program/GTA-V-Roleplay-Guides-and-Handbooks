# Fire Department Guide — When Los Santos Burns

Firefighters are the first through the door when the world is on fire — literally. Fire department roleplay is physically demanding, tactically complex, and deeply rewarding. You are not just putting out flames. You are cutting people out of wrecked cars on the Senora Freeway, ventilating a burning building in Strawberry so your crew can search for survivors, and standing in a hazmat suit outside a meth lab in Sandy Shores wondering what is about to explode.

This guide covers every aspect of firefighter RP — from structure fires and vehicle extrication to hazmat awareness, station life, and cooperation with EMS and law enforcement.

---

## What the Fire Department Does in RP

The fire department handles far more than fires. You are an all-hazards response agency.

| Function | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|
| Structure fires | Attacking fires in buildings, searching for trapped victims, ventilation |
| Vehicle fires | Suppressing car, truck, and motorcycle fires on roads and highways |
| Wildfire response | Brush fires in Blaine County, the Vinewood Hills, and along the coast |
| Vehicle extrication | Cutting victims out of wrecked vehicles using hydraulic tools |
| Technical rescue | Cliff rescues, water rescues, confined space operations |
| Hazmat response | Chemical spills, meth lab explosions, gas leaks |
| Fire investigation | Determining cause and origin of fires, identifying arson |
| EMS assist | Responding to medical calls alongside or in place of EMS |
| Public education | Fire safety demonstrations, smoke detector checks, community events |

## Department Structure

Fire departments in RP typically follow a paramilitary hierarchy.

| Rank | Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| Fire Chief | Department head | Policy, budget, public relations, interdepartmental coordination |
| Deputy Chief | Assistant commander | Operations oversight, strategic planning |
| Battalion Chief | District commander | Manages a battalion covering specific areas of the city |
| Captain | Station commander | Runs a fire station, leads a company on calls |
| Lieutenant | Crew leader | Leads a team within a company, reports to captain |
| Engineer / Driver | Apparatus operator | Drives the engine or ladder truck, operates the pump |
| Firefighter | Line responder | Attacks fires, performs rescues, does the physical work |
| Probationary Firefighter | Trainee | Learns the job under close supervision, proves themselves |

**The fire station hierarchy is strict.** The probie cooks, cleans, and checks equipment. The captain makes tactical decisions on scene. The chief handles politics and policy. Respect the chain — it exists for safety.

## Structure Fires

Structure fires are the signature call for any fire department. This is what you trained for.

**Fireground operations — the sequence:**

1. **Dispatch** — Receive the call. Note the address, type of structure, reports of trapped occupants.
2. **Response** — Roll the engine and ladder truck. Engineer drives, crew gears up en route.
3. **Size-up** — The first-arriving officer evaluates the fire from outside. What is showing? Where is the fire? What is the building construction? Are there people inside?
4. **Establish command** — "Engine-1 on scene, two-story residential, heavy smoke showing from the second floor, assuming Paleto Bay Command."
5. **Attack** — Pull the hose line, force entry if needed, advance on the fire.
6. **Search and rescue** — A team searches for victims while the attack team fights the fire.
7. **Ventilation** — Open the roof or windows to release heat and smoke, improving visibility for interior crews.
8. **Overhaul** — After the fire is knocked down, check for hidden fire in walls, ceilings, and floors.
9. **Investigation** — Determine cause and origin. Was it accidental or arson?

**Example fire attack RP:**

> `/me pulls the charged 1¾-inch hose line off the engine and advances toward the front door, crouching low. Smoke is banking down from the ceiling.`
> "Engine-1 Interior to Command — we're making entry through the front door. Heavy smoke conditions, zero visibility. Advancing toward the seat of the fire."

> `/me drops to a knee at the doorway, opens the nozzle in a short burst to cool the overhead gases, then advances down the hallway, sweeping the stream ahead.`

**Critical safety rules:**
- Never enter a burning building alone — always work in pairs
- Monitor your air supply — when the low-air alarm sounds, get out
- Maintain communication with your crew and incident command at all times
- If conditions deteriorate rapidly, call a MAYDAY and get out

## Vehicle Fires

Vehicle fires are common on the streets and highways of Los Santos.

**Approach:**
- Park the engine uphill and upwind from the burning vehicle
- Watch for fuel leaks running downhill
- Be aware of potential airbag deployment in the heat
- Never open the hood of a fully involved vehicle — attack through the grill or wheel wells first

> `/me pulls the booster line and approaches the burning Bravado Buffalo from the front quarter, staying low. Opens the nozzle and directs the stream at the base of the flames under the hood.`

**Electric vehicles (if applicable on your server):**
- Battery fires burn differently — they can reignite after being extinguished
- Use massive amounts of water for cooling
- Maintain distance — battery cells can explode

## Wildfire Response

Blaine County, the Vinewood Hills, and the hillsides around the Tataviam Mountains are prime wildfire territory.

**Wildfire operations:**
- Establish a fire line — clear vegetation ahead of the advancing fire
- Protect structures in the fire's path — wet down roofs and clear brush away from buildings
- Use the engine's pump to draft water from natural sources if hydrants are unavailable
- Coordinate with air support if your server has firefighting aircraft
- Monitor wind changes — wildfires can shift direction in seconds

> `/me drags the hose line to the edge of the property and begins wetting down the brush between the advancing fire and the ranch house, watching the wind.`
> "Command, Engine-3 — the wind is shifting to the northwest. If it holds, the fire is going to push toward Grapeseed. We need to start evacuations on Joshua Road."

## Vehicle Extrication — Jaws of Life

When a vehicle crash traps occupants, the fire department cuts them free. This is high-stakes, technical RP.

**Extrication sequence:**

| Step | Action | RP Detail |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Stabilization | Crib and chock the vehicle | `/me places step chocks and cribbing under the vehicle frame to prevent rocking.` |
| 2. Glass management | Remove or protect against broken glass | `/me covers the patient with a blanket and uses the window punch to remove the side glass.` |
| 3. Access | Cut or spread the vehicle apart | `/me positions the hydraulic spreader at the door seam and begins pumping, forcing the door open.` |
| 4. Disentanglement | Free the patient from wreckage | `/me uses the hydraulic cutter to remove the steering column that is pinning the patient's legs.` |
| 5. Patient removal | Carefully extract on a backboard | `/me works with the paramedic to slide the backboard behind the patient and secure them for removal.` |

**Working with EMS during extrication:**
- EMS accesses the patient from the opposite side while you work
- Communicate before every cut — "Cutting now!" so EMS can protect the patient
- Share patient status — EMS tells you how urgent the extraction is
- Once the patient is free, step back and let EMS take over medical care

> **Captain:** "Medic, how's the patient?"
> **Paramedic:** "Conscious but her BP is dropping. We need her out in the next five minutes."
> **Captain:** "Copy. We're taking the roof. Stand by."

## Hazmat Awareness

Hazmat calls are rare but dangerous. Meth labs, chemical spills at the docks, gas leaks — all fall under fire department response.

**The first rule of hazmat: Do not become a victim.** If you smell chemicals, see unusual vapors, or notice dead animals near a scene — stop, back up, and call for specialized resources.

**Hazmat zones:**

| Zone | Distance | Who Can Enter |
|---|---|---|
| Hot zone | Immediate danger area | Only hazmat-trained personnel in full protective equipment |
| Warm zone | Decontamination corridor | Decon teams, exiting hot zone personnel |
| Cold zone | Safe area | Command, staging, medical triage |

> `/me holds up a hand and stops the crew fifty yards from the structure. A chemical smell hangs in the air.`
> "Nobody goes past this point. I'm calling for the hazmat unit. Engine-2, set up a perimeter at the intersection and start turning people back."

## Fire Investigation Basics

After every fire, someone needs to determine how it started. On larger departments, dedicated fire investigators handle this. On smaller ones, the captain or senior firefighter does an initial assessment.

**Cause categories:**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Accidental | Cooking fire, electrical malfunction, unattended candle |
| Natural | Lightning strike, spontaneous combustion |
| Incendiary (arson) | Deliberately set — accelerants, multiple points of origin |
| Undetermined | Not enough evidence to determine cause |

**Signs of arson:**
- Multiple points of origin (fire starting in more than one place)
- Pour patterns on the floor (accelerant trails)
- Fire burning hotter or faster than the fuel load should allow
- Missing belongings (owner removed valuables before the fire)
- Recent insurance policy changes on the property

> `/me kneels near the area of origin and examines the burn patterns on the floor. The V-pattern on the wall points to the corner near the window. Notices an unusual chemical smell inconsistent with the building's contents.`
> "Captain, I'm seeing what looks like a pour pattern here. This might not be accidental. We should get PD's arson unit down here."

**Cooperation with law enforcement:**
Fire investigation and police arson units work together. The fire department determines origin and cause. The police department handles the criminal investigation. Do not step on each other's roles — share information and respect jurisdictional boundaries.

## Ladder Operations and Ventilation

**Ladder operations:**
- Aerial ladders provide elevated access for rescue and master stream operations
- Ground ladders are placed against buildings for firefighter access to upper floors and roofs
- Always have someone footing the ladder at the base

> `/me raises the ground ladder against the second-story window, sets the angle, and begins climbing with a Halligan tool slung over one shoulder.`

**Ventilation types:**

| Type | Method | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Vertical | Cut a hole in the roof | Releases heat and smoke straight up, improves interior conditions |
| Horizontal | Open or break windows | Cross-ventilation to clear smoke on a specific floor |
| Positive pressure (PPV) | Place a fan at the entrance | Forces fresh air in, pushing smoke out through an exit point |

**Ventilation must be coordinated with the interior attack team.** Opening the wrong vent at the wrong time can push fire toward crews inside. Always communicate before cutting or opening.

## Fire Station Life

Station life between calls is where firefighter relationships are built.

**Station routines:**
- Morning equipment checks — every tool, every hose, every SCBA bottle
- Apparatus wash and maintenance
- Training drills — forcible entry, hose advancement, ladder raises, search techniques
- Meals together — cooking is a sacred tradition; the probie usually cooks
- Physical fitness — maintain the strength and endurance the job demands
- Station cleaning — every surface, every day
- Educational sessions — reviewing SOPs, case studies of notable fires

> `/me hangs up the freshly cleaned turnout gear on the rack and starts wiping down the engine's chrome with a rag, glancing at the alert board.`
> "Three calls last shift and two of them were dumpster fires in Davis. If I have to put out one more dumpster, I'm transferring to Paleto."

**The probie experience:**
New firefighters are tested. You cook, you clean, you check equipment twice, and you do not complain. This tradition builds humility and ensures new members earn their place.

## Responding with EMS

Fire and EMS share many calls. Understanding each other's roles makes scenes run smoothly.

- On medical calls without fire involvement, firefighters assist EMS with CPR, lifting, and crowd control
- On trauma scenes, fire may assist with patient packaging and extrication while EMS manages medical care
- On structure fires, EMS stages outside to treat any injured firefighters or civilians brought out
- Always know where the medic unit is parked — if a firefighter goes down, you need them immediately

## Common Mistakes in Fire RP

**The lone wolf:** Entering a burning building alone. In real fire service and in good RP, you never go in without a partner and a safety line.

**Ignoring the engineer:** The person operating the pump keeps water flowing to your hoseline. Treat them with respect and communicate your water needs.

**Skipping size-up:** Rushing inside without assessing the building, fire conditions, and potential hazards. Size-up saves lives.

**Forgetting your air:** Your SCBA has a limited air supply. Roleplay monitoring it. When the alarm sounds, leave — no exceptions.

**Not coordinating ventilation:** Cutting a vent hole without telling the interior crew can get people killed. Communicate before every action.

---

## Final Thoughts

Firefighting RP is about controlled chaos. You walk into situations everyone else is running from. You make split-second decisions that determine whether people live or die. And when the fire is out and the smoke clears, you go back to the station, wash the rig, cook dinner, and wait for the next alarm.

The fire does not care about your backstory, your rank, or your plans for the evening. When the tones drop, you go.

Stay low. Stay together. Get the job done.
