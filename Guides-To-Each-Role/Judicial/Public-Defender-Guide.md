# Public Defender Roleplay Guide — Justice on a Shoestring Budget

Everyone deserves a defense. That's the principle. The reality is different. You're assigned a case file thirty minutes before arraignment. Your client has a record. The evidence looks bad. The prosecutor is offering a deal that sounds generous until you read the fine print. And you have four more cases today.

Public defender RP is one of the most emotionally complex roles on any server. You're underfunded, overworked, and often defending people who did exactly what they're accused of. But the system doesn't work without you. Without public defenders, every arrest becomes a conviction, and the scales of justice don't just tip — they collapse.

This guide covers case assignments, client relations, building a defense on limited resources, plea negotiations, trial prep, ethical obligations, and the unique emotional toll of this role.

---

## Why Public Defender RP Matters

Public defenders exist because the legal system promises that everyone — regardless of wealth — has the right to legal representation. In RP, this means:

- **Every arrest creates potential for legal RP** — Not just for private attorneys, but for court-appointed defenders.
- **Public defenders challenge the system** — They force prosecutors to prove their case rather than steamroll unrepresented defendants.
- **Underdog narrative** — Playing a public defender means constantly fighting uphill, which creates compelling character arcs.
- **Ecosystem balance** — Without defense attorneys, the judicial system is just a prosecution factory.

## Receiving Case Assignments

Public defenders don't choose their clients. Cases are assigned by the court.

### How Assignment Works

1. **A defendant is arrested and booked.**
2. **The defendant cannot afford private counsel** — or refuses to retain one.
3. **The court assigns a public defender** — typically the next available attorney.
4. **The public defender receives the case file** — charges, police reports, evidence summaries.
5. **Clock starts ticking** — Arraignment is usually within 24-48 hours IC.

| Assignment Factor | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Case severity** | Serious felonies may be assigned to senior defenders |
| **Caseload** | Junior defenders carry lighter loads initially, then get buried |
| **Conflicts of interest** | Cannot defend a client if you've previously defended their co-defendant's victim |
| **Specialty** | Some defenders develop expertise in specific crime types (drug offenses, violent crimes, white-collar) |

> `/me opens the manila folder on her desk and scans the arrest report. Aggravated assault. Two witnesses. One of them is a cop.`
> "Great. Another one where the deck's already stacked."

## Meeting Clients in Custody

Your first meeting with your client sets the tone for the entire case.

### The Initial Meeting

The first meeting typically happens at Bolingbroke Penitentiary or the Mission Row holding cells.

**Goals of the first meeting:**

1. **Introduce yourself** — Explain that you're their court-appointed attorney.
2. **Explain the charges** — Many defendants don't fully understand what they're charged with.
3. **Hear their side** — Let them tell their story. Don't interrupt. Don't judge.
4. **Assess the case** — Compare their account to the police report.
5. **Set expectations** — Be honest about the strength of the evidence and possible outcomes.
6. **Build trust** — This person needs to know you're on their side, even if nobody else is.

> **Public Defender:** "Mr. Torres, my name is Jessica Hartley. I've been appointed by the court to represent you on the aggravated assault charge. Before we talk about anything else — everything you tell me is confidential. I cannot and will not share it with anyone. That's the attorney-client privilege, and it's absolute."
> **Client:** "I didn't start it. He came at me first. I was just defending myself."
> **Public Defender:** "Okay. Walk me through exactly what happened, from the beginning. Don't leave anything out."
> `/me clicks her pen and begins writing on the yellow legal pad, maintaining eye contact as the client speaks.`

### Difficult Clients

Not every client is cooperative. Some are:

- **Hostile** — "I don't need a public defender. You work for the state."
- **Dishonest** — They lie about what happened, making your job harder.
- **Indifferent** — "Just tell them I'm guilty. I don't care."
- **Panicked** — First-time offenders who are terrified and can't think clearly.

Each type requires a different approach, but the core principle remains: **you are their advocate, regardless of how they treat you.**

## Building a Defense with Limited Resources

Private attorneys hire investigators, expert witnesses, and forensic consultants. Public defenders have a legal pad and a coffee-stained office.

### Defense Strategies with Limited Resources

| Strategy | How It Works |
|---|---|
| **Challenge the evidence** | Look for procedural errors — was the search legal? Was Miranda given? Was evidence handled properly? |
| **Challenge witness credibility** | Do witnesses have motives to lie? Prior convictions? Inconsistencies in their statements? |
| **Find holes in the timeline** | Does the prosecution's version of events actually make sense chronologically? |
| **Self-defense argument** | If applicable, shift the narrative from aggressor to victim. |
| **Plea bargain** | Sometimes the best defense is negotiating the least damaging deal. |
| **Alibi verification** | Can anyone confirm your client was somewhere else? |
| **Request disclosure** | Force the prosecution to hand over all evidence, including exculpatory material. |

> `/me spreads the police report, witness statements, and booking photos across the desk and leans back, looking for the gap.`
> "The arresting officer says he observed the assault from across the street. At night. Through a chain-link fence. At fifty yards. That's our opening."

## Plea Negotiations

Most cases don't go to trial. Plea bargaining is where the real work happens.

### The Plea Process

1. **Prosecutor makes an initial offer** — Usually harsher than they expect to settle for.
2. **You evaluate the offer** — Is it reasonable given the evidence? What's the risk at trial?
3. **Discuss with your client** — Present the offer, explain the pros and cons, and advise.
4. **Counter-offer** — Negotiate for reduced charges, shorter sentences, or alternative sentencing.
5. **Client decides** — Ultimately, the client accepts or rejects the plea. You advise; they choose.

### What Makes a Good Plea Deal

| Factor | Favors Accepting the Deal | Favors Rejecting the Deal |
|---|---|---|
| **Evidence strength** | Strong evidence against your client | Weak evidence, procedural issues |
| **Charge severity** | Plea reduces a felony to a misdemeanor | Plea still carries significant time |
| **Client's record** | Prior convictions make jury sympathetic to prosecution | Clean record, sympathetic defendant |
| **Witness reliability** | Multiple credible witnesses | Single witness with credibility issues |
| **Alternative sentencing** | Plea includes probation, community service instead of jail | Only option is incarceration |
| **Trial risk** | Conviction at trial means maximum sentence | Acquittal is realistic |

> **Prosecutor:** "I'll drop the aggravated down to simple assault. Six months with credit for time served. Final offer."
> **Public Defender:** "Six months for a first-time offender on what's clearly a mutual combat situation? I want misdemeanor battery. Thirty days suspended, one year probation."
> **Prosecutor:** "We have two witnesses who say your client threw the first punch."
> **Public Defender:** "You have two witnesses who were in a bar at midnight. I'll put their blood alcohol on the stand. Let's find something we can both live with."

## Trial Preparation on Short Notice

When a case goes to trial, public defenders often have days — not weeks — to prepare.

### Rapid Trial Prep Checklist

| Task | Time Required | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| Review all discovery materials | 1-2 hours | Critical |
| Interview your client in detail | 30-60 minutes | Critical |
| Prepare cross-examination questions | 1-2 hours | Critical |
| Draft opening statement | 30 minutes | High |
| Prepare closing argument outline | 30 minutes | High |
| Prep your client for testimony (if testifying) | 30-60 minutes | High |

### Trial Conduct

- **Opening statement** — Frame the narrative. Plant doubt early.
- **Cross-examination** — Ask leading questions. Control the witness. Expose inconsistencies.
- **Direct examination of defense witnesses** — Open-ended questions. Let your witnesses tell the story.
- **Closing argument** — Tie everything together. Emphasize reasonable doubt.

> **Public Defender (opening statement):** "Ladies and gentlemen of the jury, the prosecution is going to tell you that my client, Marcus Torres, assaulted a man outside the Vanilla Unicorn on the night of March 3rd. What they won't tell you is that three minutes before that alleged assault, the so-called victim was in my client's face, shoving him, threatening him, and refusing to leave him alone. Marcus Torres didn't start a fight. He finished one. And the law allows that."

## Working with Overloaded Caseloads

The defining challenge of public defender work is volume. You have too many cases and not enough time.

### Caseload Management

- **Triage** — Which cases are going to trial? Which are plea deals? Which can be dismissed?
- **Prioritize hearings** — Know your court calendar. Miss a hearing, lose a client.
- **Standardize processes** — Use templates for motions, plea documents, and discovery requests.
- **Delegate when possible** — If you have a paralegal or intern, use them.
- **Accept imperfection** — You cannot give every case the attention it deserves. That's the tragedy of the role.

> `/me rubs her eyes and looks at the stack of case folders on her desk. Seven active cases. Two arraignments tomorrow. A trial on Thursday.`
> "There aren't enough hours. There never are."

## Ethical Obligations

Public defenders are bound by the same ethical rules as any attorney, often under more difficult circumstances.

| Obligation | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Zealous advocacy** | You must fight for your client's interests, even if you believe they're guilty |
| **Confidentiality** | Everything your client tells you stays between you — no exceptions |
| **Competence** | You must provide adequate representation despite your caseload |
| **Candor to the court** | You cannot lie to the judge or present evidence you know is fabricated |
| **No conflicts of interest** | You cannot represent clients with competing interests |
| **Duty to advise** | You must give honest advice, even when it's not what the client wants to hear |

## Burnout and Moral Conflict

Public defender RP is emotionally heavy. Your character will lose cases, watch clients go to prison, and question whether any of it matters.

### Signs of Character Burnout

- Cynicism toward the system they once believed in
- Drinking or substance use to cope
- Short temper with clients, prosecutors, or judges
- Withdrawal from social relationships
- Considering quitting law entirely

These are RP goldmines. A burned-out public defender who still shows up every morning is one of the most compelling characters you can play.

> `/me pours a second glass of whiskey and stares at the case file for the Ramirez trial. Three months of work. Guilty verdict. Twenty years.`
> "I told him to take the deal. Eight years. He wanted to fight it. And now he's doing twenty. Was I not convincing enough? Or was the system just going to eat him no matter what?"

## Relationship with Prosecutors

The relationship between public defenders and prosecutors is complicated. You're adversaries in court and often professional acquaintances outside it.

| Dynamic | Description |
|---|---|
| **Professional respect** | Good prosecutors and defenders respect each other's role |
| **Plea negotiation** | Most of your interaction is horse-trading deals |
| **Occasional conflict** | When a prosecutor withholds evidence or plays dirty, things get personal |
| **Shared exhaustion** | Both sides are overworked. There's a grudging empathy there. |
| **Courtroom rivalry** | In front of the jury, you're opponents. Fully. |

> **Public Defender:** "Katherine, we've been going back and forth on the Davis case for a week. Your evidence is circumstantial at best. Drop it to a misdemeanor and we both go home."
> **Prosecutor:** "Circumstantial doesn't mean weak, Jessica. I've got motive, opportunity, and a witness who puts him at the scene."
> **Public Defender:** "A witness who was two hundred feet away in the dark. Take the deal or I'll make her look unreliable on the stand."

## Common Mistakes in Public Defender RP

| Mistake | Why It's a Problem | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Phoning it in because "they're guilty anyway" | Violates the core ethic of the role | Fight every case as if your client is innocent |
| Pushing clients to plead guilty without evaluating the case | Lazy and unfair to the client | Always review the evidence before advising a plea |
| Taking the prosecutor's side | You're the defense, not a mediator | Your loyalty is to your client, always |
| Ignoring the emotional weight of the role | Misses the best RP opportunities | Lean into the burnout, the doubt, the moral conflict |
| Never going to trial | Trials are the climax of legal RP | Take winnable cases to trial — it's great RP for everyone |

---

## Final Thoughts

Public defender RP isn't glamorous. You won't drive a sports car or live in a Vinewood mansion. You'll eat vending machine dinners, argue with ungrateful clients, and lose more cases than you win. But you'll also be the person who stands between the power of the state and a human being who has nobody else.

That matters. Even when it doesn't feel like it.
