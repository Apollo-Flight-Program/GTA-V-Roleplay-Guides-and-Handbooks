# Prosecutor Roleplay Guide — The State's Sword and Shield

The prosecutor sits at the center of the criminal justice system. Every arrest funnels through your desk. Every charge is your decision. Every trial rises or falls on how well you built the case. You don't just represent a client — you represent the People of the State of San Andreas. That weight is enormous, and it never lets up.

Prosecutor RP is intellectually demanding, politically complex, and deeply satisfying when done well. You're not chasing convictions for the sake of numbers — you're seeking justice. And sometimes justice means dismissing a case because the evidence isn't there, even when the public is screaming for blood.

This guide covers receiving and evaluating cases, charging decisions, plea bargaining, trial preparation, working with law enforcement, and the ethical and political pressures that define this role.

---

## Why Prosecutor RP Matters

Without prosecutors, the legal system has no engine. Arrests are meaningless without charges. Charges are meaningless without someone to present them in court. The prosecutor is the bridge between police work and courtroom justice.

- **Accountability for criminals** — You're the reason crimes have consequences.
- **Quality control for police** — You reject cases with bad evidence, which holds officers to higher standards.
- **Narrative engine** — Prosecutions create multi-session storylines: investigation → arrest → arraignment → trial → verdict.
- **Political RP** — District Attorneys are elected officials. Your decisions have political consequences.
- **Adversarial balance** — Without a strong prosecution, defense attorneys have nothing to fight against.

## Receiving Cases from Police

When law enforcement makes an arrest, the case file comes to the District Attorney's office for review.

### What's in a Case File

| Document | Contents |
|---|---|
| **Arrest report** | Officer's account of the arrest — who, what, when, where, how |
| **Witness statements** | Written or recorded statements from witnesses |
| **Evidence log** | Physical evidence collected — weapons, drugs, documents, clothing |
| **Miranda documentation** | Proof that the suspect was read their rights |
| **Booking information** | Suspect's personal details, prior record, mugshot |
| **Supplemental reports** | Detective follow-ups, forensic results, surveillance footage descriptions |

You review every document. You look for gaps, inconsistencies, and problems before deciding whether to file charges.

> `/me opens the case file from the Davis homicide and spreads the contents across the desk — arrest report, three witness statements, forensic summary, and a stack of photographs.`
> "Let's see what Detective Morales brought me this time."

## Evaluating Evidence Sufficiency

Not every arrest becomes a prosecution. The prosecutor's first job is to evaluate whether the evidence is strong enough to proceed.

### The Charging Standard

The question is not "Did they do it?" The question is: **"Can I prove beyond a reasonable doubt that they did it?"**

| Evidence Factor | Strong Case | Weak Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical evidence** | DNA, fingerprints, weapon recovered | No physical evidence linking suspect |
| **Witness testimony** | Multiple independent witnesses, consistent accounts | Single witness with credibility issues |
| **Motive** | Clear motive established by evidence | No apparent motive |
| **Suspect statements** | Confession (properly obtained) | Suspect invoked rights, no statement |
| **Alibi** | No alibi or alibi disproven | Alibi supported by multiple witnesses |
| **Chain of custody** | Evidence properly collected and stored | Chain of custody issues, contamination risk |
| **Constitutional compliance** | Legal search, Miranda given, proper procedure | Search warrant issues, Miranda violations |

### Declining to Prosecute

Sometimes you decline a case. This is professional judgment, not failure.

> **Detective:** "What do you mean you're not filing charges? We arrested him with the stolen car."
> **Prosecutor:** "You arrested him sitting in a car that was reported stolen three hours earlier. He says he bought it from a guy at the docks. You have no witnesses to the theft, no surveillance, and his story isn't implausible. I can't prove he knew it was stolen."
> **Detective:** "He's got two prior GTA convictions."
> **Prosecutor:** "Prior convictions aren't evidence of this crime. Bring me something more and I'll reconsider."

## Charging Decisions

Once you decide to prosecute, you must determine the specific charges.

### Charging Considerations

| Factor | Influence on Charging |
|---|---|
| **Severity of the crime** | More serious conduct warrants more serious charges |
| **Evidence available** | Charge only what you can prove |
| **Defendant's record** | Prior convictions may justify enhanced charges |
| **Victim's wishes** | Victims may want maximum charges or may prefer to drop the matter |
| **Public interest** | High-profile cases carry public expectation |
| **Plea bargaining room** | Sometimes you charge higher to negotiate down |
| **Sentencing guidelines** | Ensure the potential sentence fits the conduct |

### Common Charges in RP

| Charge | Typical Scenario |
|---|---|
| **Murder (1st Degree)** | Premeditated killing — planned and intentional |
| **Manslaughter** | Unintentional killing through reckless conduct |
| **Aggravated Assault** | Attack causing serious injury or involving a weapon |
| **Armed Robbery** | Theft with threat or use of a weapon |
| **Drug Trafficking** | Distribution or possession with intent to distribute |
| **Grand Theft Auto** | Theft of a motor vehicle |
| **Possession of Illegal Firearms** | Unlicensed weapon, convicted felon with a firearm |
| **RICO / Organized Crime** | Pattern of criminal activity by an organized group |

## Grand Jury Proceedings

For serious felonies, some servers use grand jury proceedings where the prosecutor presents evidence to determine probable cause to indict. The prosecutor controls the presentation — no defense attorney is present — and proceedings are sealed.

> `/me stands before the grand jury panel, holding crime scene photographs.`
> "The evidence will show that the defendant entered the Pacific Standard Bank with a firearm, threatened four employees, and left with approximately two hundred thousand dollars."

## Plea Bargaining Strategies

Most cases resolve through plea bargaining. The prosecutor's goal is to secure a just outcome without the risk and expense of trial.

| Tactic | When to Use |
|---|---|
| **High initial offer** | Start with the maximum to create negotiating room |
| **Charge reduction** | Reduce a felony to a misdemeanor in exchange for a guilty plea |
| **Sentence recommendation** | Agree to recommend a lighter sentence to the judge |
| **Charge stacking** | File multiple charges, offer to drop some in exchange for a plea on the primary |
| **Cooperation agreement** | Offer a deal in exchange for testimony against co-defendants |
| **Deadline pressure** | "This offer expires when we walk into the courtroom" |

### The Art of the Deal

> **Public Defender:** "My client is willing to plead to simple assault. Misdemeanor. Time served."
> **Prosecutor:** "He stabbed someone, counselor. Simple assault is insulting. Aggravated assault, two years."
> **Public Defender:** "The victim was armed too. There's a self-defense angle here."
> **Prosecutor:** "Your client chased him two blocks before the stabbing. Self-defense requires retreat in this jurisdiction. Aggravated assault, eighteen months. That's my best."
> `/me closes the case file and meets the defense attorney's eyes.`
> "Take it to your client. This offer is good until Thursday."

## Trial Preparation

When a case goes to trial, thorough preparation is the difference between conviction and acquittal.

| Task | Description |
|---|---|
| **Organize discovery** | Ensure all evidence has been disclosed to the defense |
| **Prepare witness list** | Identify every witness you'll call and the order of testimony |
| **Draft opening statement** | Tell the story of the crime — narrative structure, not a legal lecture |
| **Prepare direct examination** | Write questions for each prosecution witness |
| **Anticipate cross-examination** | Prepare witnesses for defense attorney questions |
| **Prepare exhibits** | Photographs, evidence, documents — organized for presentation |
| **Draft closing argument** | Summarize the evidence, connect it to the charges, demand a verdict |

## Working with Detectives

The prosecutor-detective relationship is a partnership. You need their evidence. They need your legal guidance.

| Prosecutor's Role | Detective's Role |
|---|---|
| Advise on legal standards for evidence collection | Collect and preserve evidence |
| Review search warrant applications | Write and execute warrants |
| Identify evidence gaps and request follow-up | Conduct additional investigation |
| Prepare officers for testimony | Provide truthful, detailed testimony |
| Make charging and plea decisions | Present findings objectively |

> **Prosecutor:** "Detective Morales, I need you to go back to the scene. The defense is going to argue that the blood evidence was contaminated because your report doesn't mention when you gloved up. Get me a supplemental report with the exact timeline of evidence collection."
> **Detective:** "You think they'll really go after chain of custody?"
> **Prosecutor:** "They'll go after everything. That's their job. Let's make sure there's nothing to find."

## High-Profile Cases

Some cases attract city-wide attention — gang leader indictments, police corruption, mass shootings, political scandals. These cases bring unique pressures.

### High-Profile Case Considerations

| Pressure | Impact |
|---|---|
| **Media scrutiny** | Every decision is covered by the press |
| **Public opinion** | Citizens demand results — conviction or acquittal |
| **Political pressure** | Elected officials may try to influence charging decisions |
| **Witness intimidation** | High-profile defendants may have resources to threaten witnesses |
| **Security concerns** | Threats against the prosecutor, witnesses, or jury |
| **Career implications** | A win makes your career. A loss defines it. |

> `/me reads the morning's Weazel News article about the Carter prosecution and drops the paper on the desk.`
> "They're already calling it 'the trial of the decade' and we haven't even picked a jury yet. No pressure."

## Political Pressure and Ethics

District Attorneys are elected officials. That means political considerations inevitably influence the office — but ethical prosecutors resist crossing the line.

### Ethical Obligations

| Obligation | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Seek justice, not just convictions** | Dismissing a case can be the right call |
| **Disclose exculpatory evidence** | If you have evidence that helps the defense, you MUST disclose it (Brady rule) |
| **Don't overcharge** | Filing excessive charges to pressure a plea is unethical |
| **No selective prosecution** | You cannot target someone for prosecution based on race, politics, or personal grudges |
| **Honesty to the court** | Never mislead the judge or jury |
| **Respect the defense** | The defense attorney is doing their job. Don't obstruct them. |

### The Brady Rule

This is the most important ethical rule for prosecutors: **If you possess evidence that is favorable to the defendant, you must disclose it.** Failure to do so is a constitutional violation that can overturn a conviction and end your career.

> **ADA (internal):** "The witness changed her story in the second interview. She originally said she wasn't sure if she saw the defendant. Now she says she's certain. I have to disclose both statements to the defense. Even if it weakens my case."
> `/me places both witness statements into the discovery package and seals the envelope.`
> "It's the right thing to do. And it's the law."

## Common Mistakes in Prosecutor RP

| Mistake | Why It's a Problem | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Charging every case that comes across your desk | Not every arrest is a prosecutable case | Evaluate evidence objectively before filing |
| Refusing to plea bargain | Clogs the court, wastes everyone's time | Negotiate in good faith when appropriate |
| Withholding evidence from the defense | Brady violation — career-ending | Disclose everything. Always. |
| Treating defendants as automatically guilty | Undermines the presumption of innocence | Present evidence and let the jury decide |
| Ignoring victims | Victims are central to the justice process | Keep them informed, prepared, and respected |
| Making charging decisions based on politics | Erodes public trust in the justice system | Charge based on evidence and law, not pressure |
| Not preparing witnesses | Witnesses fall apart on cross-examination | Prepare every witness thoroughly |

---

## Final Thoughts

Prosecution is power. The power to charge, the power to negotiate, the power to put someone in prison. But with that power comes a responsibility that most people never have to carry: the obligation to be fair, even when being unfair would be easier.

The best prosecutors don't win every case. They win the right ones, lose the ones they should, and sleep at night knowing they did it honestly. That's not just good RP — that's the standard.

Carry the weight. Seek the truth. Do justice.
