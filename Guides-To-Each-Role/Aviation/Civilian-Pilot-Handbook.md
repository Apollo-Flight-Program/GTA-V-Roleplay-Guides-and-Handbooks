## FOREWORD

Congratulations on your civilian pilot certification.

You have earned the right to fly in the airspace over one of the most complex and dynamic environments in the country — a city of millions, a military installation, active law enforcement aviation, emergency medical helicopters, commercial airliners, and the full spectrum of general aviation activity compressed into the skies above Los Santos and Blaine County.

This is not the sky over a quiet rural county. This is San Andreas.

With that comes responsibility. This handbook is your guide to operating in this environment safely, legally, and with the professionalism that the civilian aviation community depends on. It covers your rights, your obligations, the rules of the airspace you fly in, how to handle emergencies, how to interact with other agencies, and what happens when things go wrong.

Read it thoroughly. Keep it with your flight bag. And when you are in the air and uncertain about something — ask yourself whether the answer is in here before you make a decision that cannot be undone.

Welcome to the civilian pilot community of San Andreas. Fly well.

## CHAPTER 1: WHO THIS HANDBOOK APPLIES TO

### 1.1 Scope

This handbook applies to every person who holds a civilian pilot license issued by the San Andreas Department of Aviation (SADA) and operates a non-military, non-law-enforcement aircraft anywhere in San Andreas airspace.

This includes:

- Private pilots flying for personal recreation or travel
- Commercial pilots operating charter, cargo, or tour flights for hire
- Airline transport pilots commanding scheduled or irregular passenger services
- Flight instructors conducting training under a CFI rating
- Student pilots flying under instructor supervision
- Rotorcraft pilots holding a Rotorcraft Rating (RR)

This handbook does **not** govern:

- Military aviation (governed by SAMAF Publication 1-1)
- Law enforcement aviation (governed by departmental policy)
- Air ambulance operations (governed by HEMS operational procedures)

If you hold a civilian license but are flying in support of a law enforcement or military operation at their request, the lead agency's procedures take precedence. Coordinate with them before departure.

### 1.2 Your License Is Personal

Your license authorizes **you** to fly. It does not authorize an aircraft. It does not authorize a passenger to fly. If you allow an unlicensed person to operate the controls of an aircraft you are aboard, you are jointly responsible for that violation.

Lending your license to another person, flying with someone else's license, or misrepresenting your rating to fly an aircraft you are not qualified for are all grounds for immediate revocation and potential criminal charges.

## CHAPTER 2: THE SAN ANDREAS AIRSPACE SYSTEM

Understanding the airspace you fly in is not optional. Airspace violations are among the most serious offenses in civil aviation — and ignorance of the airspace structure is not a legal defense.

### 2.1 Airspace Classes in San Andreas

| Airspace Class | Location | Requirements to Enter | Who Controls It |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Class Bravo** | Surrounding LSIA (Los Santos International Airport) | ATC clearance required — you must ask and be told "cleared" | LSIA ATC |
| **Class Charlie** | Sandy Shores Airfield in busy periods | Two-way radio contact required — establish contact, receive acknowledgment | Sandy Shores ATC when active |
| **Class Delta** | Smaller controlled airports when tower is operating | Two-way radio contact required before entering | Local tower |
| **Class Echo** | Transition airspace around controlled airports | No clearance needed, but ATC awareness varies | Advisory only |
| **Class Golf** | Uncontrolled airspace in Blaine County and rural areas | No clearance needed — see and avoid rules apply | No ATC — pilot's responsibility |
| **Restricted / Prohibited** | Fort Zancudo Military Airspace | **DO NOT ENTER** without explicit military authorization | San Andreas Military Airforce |

### 2.2 Fort Zancudo Restricted Airspace — The Line You Do Not Cross

Fort Zancudo's exclusion zone extends fifteen nautical miles from the base and has a vertical ceiling that effectively covers all practical civilian operating altitudes in the area.

**What happens if you enter Fort Zancudo's restricted airspace without authorization:**

1. Military radar identifies you as an intruder
2. A fighter intercept is scrambled
3. You will receive a radio call on Guard frequency ordering you to depart
4. If you do not comply, a military aircraft will position on your wing and signal you to follow
5. Non-compliance escalates. The military has authority over this airspace. They will use it.

There is no routine civilian reason to be in Fort Zancudo's airspace. If you are near it accidentally, the correct action is to turn away immediately, contact LSIA ATC for guidance, and declare yourself if the military contacts you first. Honesty and immediate compliance are always better than evasion.

**The western boundary of the Blaine County MOA (Military Operating Area) is marked on sectional charts.** Know where it is before you plan a flight that takes you anywhere near the Zancudo area.

### 2.3 Altitude Considerations

| Altitude | Rules |
|---|---|
| **Below 500 feet AGL** | Restricted to landing, departing, and approved low-level operations. No casual low flying over populated areas. |
| **500–1,000 feet AGL** | Minimum safe altitude over populated areas. You must be high enough to glide clear of a congested area if the engine fails. |
| **1,000–3,000 feet AGL** | Standard general aviation cruising altitude for short hops. |
| **3,000–8,000 feet MSL** | Standard cruise band for cross-country flights in San Andreas. |
| **Above 10,000 feet MSL** | High-altitude operations — multi-engine and airline transport territory. Supplemental oxygen considerations apply for extended operations. |

**VFR altitude rule:** When flying VFR above 3,000 feet AGL on a heading between 0 and 179 degrees (roughly east), fly at odd thousands plus 500 feet (3,500 / 5,500 / 7,500). On a heading between 180 and 359 degrees (roughly west), fly at even thousands plus 500 feet (4,500 / 6,500 / 8,500). This is how VFR traffic remains separated.

### 2.4 Right of Way Rules in the Air

When two aircraft are on a collision course, the following priority applies:

| Priority | Aircraft Type |
|---|---|
| 1 (Highest) | Aircraft in distress — give way to all aircraft declaring an emergency |
| 2 | Balloons |
| 3 | Gliders |
| 4 | Airships |
| 5 | Aircraft towing or refueling another aircraft |
| 6 | Powered aircraft |

**Between powered aircraft:** When converging, the aircraft on the right has right of way. When overtaking, the aircraft being overtaken has right of way — the overtaking aircraft passes to the right.

**Landing traffic always has right of way over airborne traffic.** An aircraft on final approach is not to be interfered with by aircraft that could wait.

## CHAPTER 3: STANDARDS OF CONDUCT

### 3.1 You Are Always a Pilot

Your license comes with you everywhere. Off duty, in civilian clothes, not flying — you are still a licensed pilot. Your conduct on the ground reflects on the entire civilian aviation community. A pilot with a history of reckless behavior on the ground will have that history examined when an aviation incident puts their judgment under scrutiny.

This does not mean pilots must be perfect people. It means you must understand that your license is a public trust. You operate in shared airspace with other pilots, with emergency services, with military aviation. They depend on you to be predictable, professional, and trustworthy.

### 3.2 Fitness to Fly

You must not fly when:

- You have consumed alcohol within eight hours of flight ("eight hours bottle to throttle" is the minimum standard — erring on the side of twelve hours is professional practice)
- You are under the influence of any medication that impairs judgment, vision, or reaction time
- You are ill to a degree that affects your cognitive or physical capability
- You are fatigued — if you cannot drive safely, you cannot fly safely
- You are emotionally compromised — personal distress affects decision-making in ways that are not always apparent

**Pilots self-certify fitness to fly.** There is no pre-flight test for impairment. Your integrity is the only gate. If you are not fit, you do not fly. The aircraft will still be there when you are ready.

### 3.3 Reckless Flying

The following are specifically prohibited and will result in enforcement action:

| Prohibited Action | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Low flying over populated areas below minimum safe altitude | Fine, license suspension |
| Aerobatics over populated areas or without appropriate safety margins | License suspension, investigation |
| Buzzing vehicles, buildings, or people | License suspension, potential criminal charges |
| Racing or tailgating other aircraft | License suspension |
| Unauthorized entry into restricted airspace | License suspension, investigation, potential military involvement |
| Flying directly over crowds or public gatherings at low altitude | Fine and suspension |
| Simulated attacks on vehicles or buildings | Immediate license suspension, criminal investigation |
| Flying while intoxicated | Immediate revocation, criminal charges |

**Reckless flying is not impressive flying.** The civilian aviation community is watched closely by the authorities because of what irresponsible pilots can do. Every idiot with a PPL doing low passes on the highway is one incident away from SADA imposing restrictions that affect every legitimate pilot in San Andreas.

### 3.4 Conduct with Passengers

Your passengers trust you with their lives when they board your aircraft. This is not a casual responsibility.

Before any flight with passengers:

- Ensure they are briefed on safety equipment location and usage
- Ensure they are secured and the aircraft is properly loaded (weight and balance)
- Ensure they understand they must not distract you during critical phases of flight — takeoff, approach, and landing
- Ensure you have confirmed your own currency to carry passengers

Passenger safety briefing is not optional and is not a formality. An emergency that occurs while your passenger did not know where the emergency exit handle was is a failure that belongs to you.

## CHAPTER 4: PRE-FLIGHT PROCEDURES

### 4.1 Every Flight Begins Before the Engine Starts

There are no shortcuts in pre-flight. A missed item on the walkaround is a problem you carry into the air. The time to find a fuel leak is on the ground.

**Pre-flight sequence:**

**Step 1 — Check the weather**
Before you go near the aircraft, know the weather. San Andreas weather changes quickly — coastal marine layer, mountain turbulence above the Chiliad range, and wind shear around the canyons are all hazards that kill pilots who did not check before they flew.

VFR minimums are:
- **3 statute miles visibility** and **1,000-foot cloud ceiling** — the absolute legal minimum. Fly with more margin than this whenever possible.
- If visibility is degrading, your destination weather matters as much as your departure weather. Check both. Check a midpoint too.

**Step 2 — NOTAMs (Notices to Air Missions)**
NOTAMs are official notices of airspace restrictions, temporary flight restrictions (TFRs), closed runways, and hazards. Before any flight, you are legally required to check NOTAMs for your route. Common causes of NOTAMs in San Andreas:

- Government or military operations activating a Temporary Flight Restriction
- Special events (air shows, high-profile gatherings) creating exclusion zones
- Runway or taxiway closures at controlled airports
- Military exercises expanding the Fort Zancudo MOA boundary

Flying into a TFR is a serious violation even if you did not know it was there. Check before you fly.

**Step 3 — Fuel planning**
Calculate your fuel required for:
- Taxi and runup
- Flight to destination at cruise power
- Alternate airport diversion (if conditions could close your destination)
- Reserve — minimum 30 minutes for day VFR, 45 minutes for night

Never depart with less than the reserve you calculated. Fuel starvation is 100% preventable and 100% the pilot's fault.

**Step 4 — Weight and balance**
Every aircraft has a maximum gross weight and a center of gravity envelope. If you load passengers and cargo outside these limits, the aircraft may not handle correctly — or may not fly at all. Check the aircraft's placards or documentation and verify your loading is within limits before departure.

**Step 5 — Aircraft walkaround**
Walk around the aircraft in a systematic order. Check:

| Item | What You Are Looking For |
|---|---|
| Fuselage and tail | No visible damage, control surfaces free and correct, all inspection panels secure |
| Control surfaces | Proper deflection in the correct direction, no obstructions or damage |
| Wings | No structural damage, fuel caps secure, fuel drain checked for water contamination |
| Landing gear | Tires inflated, no cracking, brake lines intact, no hydraulic leaks |
| Engine area | Oil level within limits, no fuel or oil leaks, cowl secure, intake clear of birds or debris |
| Propeller | No nicks, cracks, or damage |
| Cockpit | Windscreen clean, pitot tube cover removed (this kills pilots who forget it) |

**Step 6 — Cockpit check and engine start**
Complete the aircraft's checklist in order. Every item, every time. The checklist is not a suggestion — it is a memory aid for a sequence that has been designed and refined by people whose job was to keep you alive.

### 4.2 Filing a Flight Plan

A flight plan is your declaration of where you are going, what route you are taking, what aircraft you are in, and when you expect to arrive. It is not legally required for VFR flight in Class Golf airspace — but it is professional practice and potentially life-saving.

If you file a flight plan and do not arrive within your expected arrival time plus 30 minutes, search and rescue is activated. A flight plan is the difference between "missing pilot found alive" and "wreckage discovered three weeks later."

**File a flight plan if:**
- You are flying cross-country (more than 15 nautical miles from departure)
- You are flying over water or terrain with no road access
- You are flying at night
- You are operating near restricted airspace and want your presence documented
- You are carrying passengers

**How to file:** Contact LSIA ATC or Sandy Shores ATC in person or by radio before departure. Provide your callsign, aircraft type, departure point, destination, route, cruising altitude, estimated departure time, and estimated en route time.

## CHAPTER 5: RADIO COMMUNICATIONS

### 5.1 Every Civilian Pilot Must Have a Radio

You are not required to have radio contact for flight in uncontrolled airspace — but you are required to make traffic advisory calls at all uncontrolled airports and you are strongly advised to monitor relevant frequencies at all times.

An aircraft without a working radio in San Andreas airspace is a hazard. Military aircraft, police aviation, and air ambulances all operate on radio and expect other aircraft to do the same. You will not receive a warning from a military interceptor before they are on your wing if you are not monitoring Guard.

**Minimum radio equipment:** A functioning radio capable of reaching at least LSIA Tower and Guard frequency. If your radio fails in flight, return to base immediately. Land at the nearest suitable airport and do not continue flight until the radio is repaired.

### 5.2 Controlled Airport Procedures

At airports with an operating control tower (Class Bravo or Delta), radio contact is not optional — it is a legal requirement before entering the airspace or taxiing on the ground.

**At LSIA (Class Bravo):**
- You must receive an explicit ATC clearance to enter Class Bravo airspace. "Cleared to enter Bravo" must be said specifically — radio contact alone is not sufficient.
- Request progressive taxi instructions if you are unfamiliar with the airport layout. There is no shame in asking. The shame is in taxiing onto an active runway because you guessed.

**Standard LSIA arrival:**
> *"Los Santos Approach, Cessna November-four-seven-Bravo, inbound from the north, ten miles out, two thousand feet, request landing LSIA."*

ATC will issue instructions: transponder code, runway, expected sequence, and when to descend. Follow them precisely and read them back.

**Standard LSIA departure:**
> *"Los Santos Ground, Cessna November-four-seven-Bravo, at the general aviation ramp, request taxi to runway, VFR departure to the north."*

Ground will give you taxi instructions. When you reach the hold-short line, switch to Tower frequency and request departure clearance.

### 5.3 Uncontrolled Airport Procedures

At airports without an operating tower (Class Golf, Sandy Shores when ATC is not active), you use CTAF — Common Traffic Advisory Frequency — to self-announce your intentions to other traffic.

**CTAF calls at uncontrolled airports:**

| Phase | Call |
|---|---|
| Taxiing | *"[Airport name] traffic, [Callsign], taxiing runway [X], [airport name]."* |
| Departing | *"[Airport name] traffic, [Callsign], departing runway [X], [direction], [airport name]."* |
| Entering the pattern | *"[Airport name] traffic, [Callsign], [position in pattern], runway [X], [airport name]."* |
| On final | *"[Airport name] traffic, [Callsign], final runway [X], full stop, [airport name]."* |
| Clear of runway | *"[Airport name] traffic, [Callsign], clear of runway [X], [airport name]."* |

Nobody tells you when to go at an uncontrolled field. You announce, listen, look, and use good judgment. The see-and-avoid principle is your primary collision avoidance tool when ATC is not present.

### 5.4 Guard Frequency

Guard is the international aviation emergency frequency. Every aircraft monitors Guard at all times.

**You transmit on Guard only for:**
- A genuine emergency (MAYDAY or PAN-PAN)
- A response to a military interception call (if you are being intercepted, you respond on Guard)
- A distress relay (you are relaying a MAYDAY call you have received from another aircraft)

Guard is not a chat channel. It is not a curiosity channel. Do not transmit on Guard to test your radio, to ask non-emergency questions, or because you cannot find the right frequency. Guard chatter that is not an emergency delays a real emergency response.

### 5.5 Professional Phraseology

The way you talk on the radio tells every other aviation professional who is listening exactly who you are and how seriously you take this.

**Key principles:**
- State your callsign before every transmission on every frequency
- Be concise — say what needs to be said, nothing more
- Read back all clearances and instructions in full
- "Affirm" and "Negative" — not "yep" and "nope"
- "Say again" — not "what?" or "huh?"
- Never say "over and out" — it is either "over" (awaiting reply) or "out" (conversation complete)

> See the [Radio Etiquette Guide](../../References/Radio-Etiquette.md) and [Airforce Operational Terms and Jargon](../../References/Airforce-Operational-Terms-and-Jargon.md) for comprehensive radio language references.

## CHAPTER 6: IN-FLIGHT PROCEDURES

### 6.1 Departure

The first few minutes of every flight are the most critical. Engine failures are most likely shortly after takeoff, you are low and slow, and your options are the most limited.

**After takeoff:**
- Maintain runway heading until you have sufficient altitude to make a turn safely
- Trim the aircraft for the climb attitude — do not fight the controls the whole way up
- Switch to the appropriate departure frequency after contacting tower or completing your CTAF call
- Climb to your assigned or intended altitude before beginning the transit

**Engine failure after takeoff:** The decision is made before the flight, not in the emergency. Know where your forced landing areas are on the departure end of your runway. If the engine fails below 500 feet, land straight ahead or within 30 degrees of the runway heading. Do not attempt to turn back to the runway unless you are high enough — the "impossible turn" kills more people than landing straight ahead in a field.

### 6.2 En Route

**Cruise flight:**
- Maintain your planned VFR altitude
- Monitor your heading and cross-check with landmarks
- Scan for traffic regularly — look for moving objects on the windscreen, not just blips on instruments
- Maintain radio monitoring — Guard at minimum, plus area advisory frequencies
- Cross-check fuel quantity every 30 minutes on long flights

**Weather deterioration:**
If visibility or ceiling begins dropping toward your minimums, do not press on hoping it improves. The correct action is to divert to an alternate or land at the nearest suitable airport and wait. The weather that traps pilots who press on is the same weather that hides the terrain they fly into.

**VFR into IMC (Instrument Meteorological Conditions)** — flying into weather where you can no longer see — is statistically one of the deadliest situations in general aviation. A VFR pilot without instrument training loses control of the aircraft within an average of three minutes of entering a cloud. If you see a cloud wall ahead and you do not have an instrument rating, turn around. Now. Not in a minute. Now.

**Lost procedure:**
If you become uncertain of your position:
1. Maintain your current heading and altitude — do not start randomly turning
2. Try to identify a landmark — the Alamo Sea, Vinewood Sign, Mount Chiliad, the city grid of Los Santos
3. Contact LSIA ATC or Sandy Shores ATC for a position fix — they can identify you on radar
4. If still unable to locate yourself — declare PAN-PAN and request vectors

There is no embarrassment in calling ATC and saying "I'm not sure exactly where I am." Pilots who are embarrassed to ask for help are the ones who end up as accident reports.

### 6.3 Traffic Pattern at Uncontrolled Fields

The standard traffic pattern is flown on the left side of the runway unless the airport designates right traffic.

**Pattern sequence:**

```
Crosswind leg (after departure turn)
    → Downwind leg (parallel to runway, opposite direction)
        → Base leg (perpendicular turn toward runway)
            → Final approach (aligned with runway, landing)
```

**Pattern altitude:** Typically 1,000 feet AGL above the airport elevation. Enter the pattern at pattern altitude and descend only on the base-to-final turn.

**Spacing:** At a busy uncontrolled field, you may have multiple aircraft in the pattern. Extend your downwind leg to create spacing behind the aircraft ahead of you. Do not rush the pattern to close the gap — you need to touch down after the aircraft ahead is clear of the runway.

### 6.4 Approach and Landing

A stabilized approach is the foundation of a safe landing. By the time you are on final approach, the aircraft should be:

- Configured for landing — flaps set, landing gear down if applicable
- Trimmed for the approach speed — you should not be fighting the aircraft
- On centerline — if you are drifting, correct early and smoothly
- On glide path — if you are high, reduce power; if you are low, add power
- In radio contact — if at a controlled field, cleared to land

**If any of these are not right at 500 feet above the runway, go around.** A go-around is not a failure. Landing when you are not configured or stable is a failure.

**Go-around procedure:**
1. Apply full power immediately
2. Establish a positive climb attitude
3. Retract flaps incrementally as airspeed builds — not all at once
4. Maintain runway track until clear, then turn crosswind
5. Announce go-around on the appropriate frequency

After landing, clear the runway promptly. Backtrack on the runway only if there is no exit available and you have confirmed no aircraft are on final. An aircraft sitting on the runway is a hazard to every aircraft on approach.

## CHAPTER 7: EMERGENCY PROCEDURES

### 7.1 MAYDAY

MAYDAY is the international distress signal. It is used when the aircraft or a person aboard is in immediate danger. It takes priority over all other radio communications.

**Standard MAYDAY call:**
> *"MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. [Callsign], [Emergency type], [Position], [Altitude], [Souls on board], [Intentions]."*

Example:
> *"MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY. Cessna November-four-seven-Bravo, engine failure, ten miles north of LSIA, one thousand five hundred feet, two souls, attempting forced landing in open field."*

Transmit on Guard frequency. If you have previously been in contact with ATC, also transmit on that frequency. ATC will clear the Guard frequency and coordinate with emergency services.

**Squawk 7700.** If your aircraft has a transponder, dial in 7700 immediately. This is the emergency squawk code — every radar in San Andreas will see it.

### 7.2 PAN-PAN

PAN-PAN is an urgency call — the situation is serious and requires assistance but is not immediately life-threatening.

> *"PAN-PAN, PAN-PAN, PAN-PAN. [Callsign], [Urgency type], [Position], [Assistance required]."*

Examples of PAN-PAN situations:
- You are lost and cannot determine your position
- You have a passenger who has become medically ill
- Your fuel is critically low but you have an airport in reach
- Your radio is intermittent and may fail

### 7.3 Engine Failure in Cruise

1. **Maintain aircraft control** — pitch for best glide speed immediately
2. **Identify a landing area** — look for flat, open terrain clear of obstacles and populated areas
3. **Troubleshoot** — fuel selector, mixture, primer, ignition (one attempt — do not spend time troubleshooting at the expense of flying the aircraft)
4. **Declare MAYDAY** — transmit your position and intentions
5. **Squawk 7700**
6. **Execute the forced landing** — aim for the far end of your selected field, not the near end

**An engine failure is survivable if the aircraft is under control.** More pilots die from loss of control after an engine failure than from the landing itself. Fly the airplane first.

### 7.4 Fire in Flight

**Engine fire:**
1. Throttle — idle
2. Fuel selector — off
3. Master switch — off
4. Cabin heat — off (cuts off air supply to the fire)
5. Declare MAYDAY
6. Glide to an immediate landing — this is not a "turn back to the airport" situation unless you are already on final
7. Land as soon as possible — a fire does not wait

**Cabin fire:**
1. Identify the source
2. Use the fire extinguisher
3. Ventilate the cabin if doing so does not feed the fire
4. Declare MAYDAY
5. Land immediately

### 7.5 Inadvertent IMC (Flying into Cloud)

If you fly into a cloud or lose visual reference unexpectedly:

1. **Maintain straight and level flight** — do not bank or turn. Trust the instruments, not your body.
2. **Do not pull up** — a pull combined with a bank in cloud is how aircraft enter a graveyard spiral
3. **Reverse course slowly** — execute a standard rate turn (3 degrees per second) for 60 seconds to complete a 180-degree reversal
4. **Climb if terrain permits** — gaining altitude gives you separation from what you cannot see
5. **Declare MAYDAY** — advise ATC you are in IMC without an instrument rating and request immediate assistance

Your instincts in cloud are almost certainly wrong. The aircraft will feel like it is banked when it is level. It will feel like it is climbing when it is descending. Believe the instruments.

### 7.6 Radio Failure

1. Squawk 7600 on the transponder (lost communications code)
2. Return to the departure airport or divert to the nearest controlled airport
3. At a controlled airport, enter the traffic pattern and rock your wings — the universal signal for radio failure
4. Watch for light gun signals from the tower:
   - Steady green: cleared to land
   - Flashing green: return for landing
   - Steady red: give way — do not land
   - Flashing red: airport unsafe — do not land
5. Land and report to ATC in person immediately after shutdown

## CHAPTER 8: FLYING AT NIGHT

### 8.1 Night Rating Requirements

You must hold a Night Rating to fly after official sunset. Night flight without a night rating is a violation regardless of how familiar you are with the route.

A Night Rating requires additional ground school in night operations and a minimum number of night flight hours with an instructor.

### 8.2 Night Operations

Night flying is fundamentally different from day flying. The most important differences:

- **No terrain visibility.** You cannot see what is below you. Your altimeter and your knowledge of the terrain are your only protection against controlled flight into terrain. Do not descend below your planned altitude over unfamiliar terrain at night — ever.
- **Reduced traffic visibility.** Other aircraft are harder to spot. Lights help, but you must actively scan more aggressively.
- **Airport identification is harder.** Runway lighting at controlled airports is clear — at uncontrolled airports, verify your position with multiple references before committing to a landing approach.
- **Physiological effects.** Night vision takes 30 minutes to fully develop. Your peripheral vision is better than your central vision at night (use it). Spatial disorientation is easier to induce.

**Night minimum equipment:** All required position lights (red left, green right, white tail) must be functioning before night flight. Landing lights are strongly recommended. If your position lights fail at night, land at the nearest airport immediately.

### 8.3 Requesting Night NOTAM Checks

Before any night cross-country flight, check for lighting NOTAMs at your destination. A dark runway at night is extremely dangerous. Confirm the runway lights are operational before committing to a night arrival at a field you cannot divert away from.

## CHAPTER 9: SPECIAL OPERATIONS AND PROCEDURES

### 9.1 Flying Near Other Agencies

San Andreas skies are shared. Military aviation, police aviation, air ambulances, and news helicopters all operate in the same airspace. Understanding how to interact with each keeps everyone safe.

**Military aircraft:**
- Military aircraft have right of way in Military Operating Areas during active operations
- If you are asked by a military aircraft to change course, altitude, or frequency — comply and confirm
- Do not close on military aircraft out of curiosity — a military pilot who does not know your intentions may take defensive action
- If a military aircraft pulls alongside you, it is an interception — follow the procedures in Chapter 10

**Law enforcement aviation:**
- Police helicopters conducting surveillance or pursuit operations have the right of way and will not divert for you
- Do not position near a pursuit, tactical operation, or crime scene from the air — you could compromise the operation and will be ordered out of the area
- If instructed by police aviation to leave an area, comply immediately

**Air ambulances (HEMS):**
- HEMS helicopters are on priority missions with lives at stake
- Give way to any aircraft showing HEMS markings or any helicopter in an obvious emergency approach
- If you are at an uncontrolled LZ and a HEMS aircraft is inbound, clear the area

**News and media helicopters:**
- Media helicopters have no special airspace rights. Give way only per the standard right-of-way rules.
- Do not position your aircraft next to a news helicopter to see what they are filming — you are creating a mid-air collision hazard.

### 9.2 Aerial Photography and Filming

Civilian pilots may conduct aerial photography from their aircraft subject to the following:

- You may not fly below safe minimum altitudes to get a better shot
- You may not hover or orbit at low altitude over people, crowds, or private property in a manner that creates a hazard or constitutes harassment
- Any aerial photography over restricted areas (government buildings, military installations, crime scenes) requires prior authorization
- If you are commissioned to conduct aerial filming, confirm the operation is within your license privileges and the aircraft is loaded within limits with camera equipment aboard

Commercial aerial photography operations require a CPL and may require a specific operating waiver from SADA.

### 9.3 Passenger Charter Operations

If you are flying passengers for compensation — money, goods, or services — you must hold a **Commercial Pilot License (CPL)** at minimum. A PPL does not permit flying for hire under any circumstances.

**Before every charter flight:**
- Confirm all passengers are manifested — name and weight
- Complete weight and balance calculations with actual passenger and baggage weights
- Brief all passengers on emergency procedures: seatbelts, emergency exits, what to do if you call an emergency
- Ensure you have the required documentation aboard: your license, medical certificate, aircraft airworthiness certificate, aircraft registration, and operating limitations

**You are responsible for your passengers.** They trust you because of the license you hold. If something goes wrong, you are the professional who is accountable. Fly that charter flight with exactly the same discipline you used on your check ride.

### 9.4 Flight Instruction

Only a Certified Flight Instructor (CFI) may charge for flight instruction. A PPL holder may fly with a friend in the right seat and point out things of interest — but they may not conduct structured training, assess competency, or sign off student logbooks.

If you want to teach, earn your CFI. The process exists because teaching aviation is not the same as flying aviation. Poor instruction kills students.

## CHAPTER 10: INTERCEPTION PROCEDURES

### 10.1 If You Are Intercepted

An interception means a military or law enforcement aircraft has been dispatched to escort you out of restricted airspace or to investigate your presence in an area. It is not immediately hostile — it is a signal to comply.

**Standard interception signals from the intercepting aircraft:**

| Signal | Meaning | Your Response |
|---|---|---|
| Aircraft pulls alongside you and rocks wings | Follow me — comply and follow | Rock your wings in response, then follow the aircraft |
| Aircraft breaks away and circles back | Not complying — escorting you anyway | Follow the aircraft to the designated landing area |
| Aircraft positions ahead and lowers landing gear | Land at this airfield | Comply — lower your gear in response and follow to land |

**What to do immediately when intercepted:**

1. Do not panic — an interception means they want you to comply, not that they are immediately hostile
2. Rock your wings to acknowledge you have seen them
3. Tune Guard frequency immediately: *"Military aircraft, this is [Callsign], I am acknowledging your intercept, please advise."*
4. Squawk 7777 if instructed to do so by the intercepting aircraft
5. Follow the intercepting aircraft to the designated airfield
6. Do not make any sudden maneuvers, climbs, or descents without signaling your intentions

**What you must never do when intercepted:**

- Ignore the intercepting aircraft
- Accelerate or attempt to flee
- Make aggressive maneuvers toward the military aircraft
- Shut off your transponder
- Fly toward the military base

Non-compliance during an interception is a provocation. Military pilots are authorized to escalate. Compliance costs you time and an uncomfortable conversation on the ground. Non-compliance costs significantly more.

### 10.2 After the Interception Landing

You will be directed to land at Sandy Shores Airfield or another designated field. Military or law enforcement personnel will meet you at the aircraft.

- Comply with all instructions from responding personnel
- Provide your pilot license, medical certificate, and aircraft documentation without argument
- Be honest about your route, intentions, and how you came to be in restricted airspace

An honest pilot who accidentally entered restricted airspace and complied with every instruction will typically leave with a warning and a record of the incident. A pilot who tried to flee, argued, or provided false information to investigators will not.

## CHAPTER 11: DOCUMENTATION AND RECORD-KEEPING

### 11.1 Required Documents — Aircraft

The following must be aboard the aircraft on every flight. The acronym **ARROW** covers the required documents:

| Letter | Document | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | Airworthiness Certificate | Issued when the aircraft passed its certification. Must be displayed in the cockpit. |
| **R** | Registration Certificate | The aircraft's equivalent of a vehicle title. |
| **R** | Radio Station License | Required for aircraft with radios operating over international waters. Recommended for all aircraft. |
| **O** | Operating Limitations | The POH (Pilot's Operating Handbook) or placard limitations for the aircraft. |
| **W** | Weight and Balance | The current weight and balance documentation for the aircraft's configuration. |

If any of these are missing, the aircraft is not legal to fly. Do not depart without them.

### 11.2 Your Personal Documents

Before every flight, verify you have:

- Your current pilot license (correct rating for the flight you are conducting)
- Your current medical certificate (correct class for your license level)
- Your logbook or access to your electronic flight log

Your license must be on your person — not at home, not in your car, not "somewhere in your bag." If you are intercepted, land at a controlled airport, or have an incident, you will be asked to produce it immediately.

### 11.3 The Flight Log

Your flight log is your personal record of every hour you have flown, every aircraft, every flight type, and every qualification. It is the proof that you are current to fly.

**What to log after every flight:**

```
DATE:
AIRCRAFT REGISTRATION:
AIRCRAFT TYPE:
DEPARTURE AIRPORT:
DESTINATION AIRPORT:
FLIGHT TIME (Hobbs):
PILOT IN COMMAND TIME:
DUAL RECEIVED (if with instructor):
REMARKS: (Conditions, notable events, training completed)
```

**Your log is a legal document.** Falsifying it — recording flights that did not happen, inflating hours, signing off training that was not completed — is a criminal offense. It also puts you in a position where you believe you are qualified for something you have not actually trained for. The only person who suffers when a pilot with falsified hours meets an emergency is the pilot.

## CHAPTER 12: VIOLATIONS AND ENFORCEMENT

### 12.1 Civil Aviation Authority Enforcement

The San Andreas Department of Aviation Civil Aviation Authority (SADA-CAA) investigates aviation incidents and violations. Their authority includes:

- Issuing written warnings for first-time minor violations
- Imposing administrative fines
- Suspending licenses — temporarily or until retraining is completed
- Revoking licenses permanently
- Referring cases to law enforcement for criminal charges

### 12.2 Violation Categories and Consequences

| Category | Examples | Range of Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Administrative / Paperwork** | Missing logbook entry, expired documents | Written warning, fine |
| **Operational Violation** | Airspace incursion (accidental), pattern violation | Fine, suspension up to 30 days |
| **Reckless Operation** | Reckless low flying, buzzing, near miss | Suspension 30-180 days, mandatory retraining |
| **Criminal Aviation Conduct** | Flying under influence, deliberate restricted entry, false filings | License revocation, criminal prosecution |
| **Accident Caused by Negligence** | Preventable accident with property damage or injury | Full investigation, suspension or revocation, civil liability |

### 12.3 Your Rights in an Enforcement Action

If SADA-CAA contacts you regarding an incident or investigation:

- You have the right to know what you are being investigated for
- You have the right to provide your account of events
- You are not required to self-incriminate in a criminal context — but civil aviation is a regulated environment, and cooperation is expected for administrative proceedings
- You may have legal representation for any formal hearing

**The best defense in any enforcement action is a clean record and honest documentation.** Pilots who maintain accurate logs, follow procedures, and cooperate with investigations are treated very differently from pilots who do not.

## CHAPTER 13: THE CIVILIAN AVIATION COMMUNITY

### 13.1 What You Represent

You are part of a community. Every civilian pilot in San Andreas shares the same airspace, the same airports, and the same public perception. A single incident — one pilot doing something reckless, one aircraft causing chaos — invites restriction and scrutiny that affects every legitimate operator.

The civilian aviation community's relationship with the San Andreas Department of Aviation, with the military, and with law enforcement depends on the consistent, professional conduct of its members. You earn that relationship — or you destroy it — one flight at a time.

### 13.2 Mentorship

If you are an experienced pilot, help those who are newer to the community. Answer questions on the ground, offer to accompany student pilots on cross-country flights for observation, and correct poor practice when you see it — not to embarrass, but because the standard matters.

If you are a newer pilot, seek out experienced pilots. Ask questions. There is nothing you can encounter in the air that some pilot has not encountered before. Their experience is available to you if you ask.

### 13.3 Reporting Safety Concerns

If you observe unsafe flying — by any pilot, in any aircraft — you are expected to report it. This is not snitching. This is the community policing itself so that SADA-CAA does not have to.

Report unsafe operations to:
- LSIA ATC if the unsafe operation is occurring in the airspace you are monitoring
- SADA-CAA if you observed an incident and want it on record
- Your flight school if a student or instructor is the concern

Reporting an unsafe pilot may prevent an accident. The accident they would have caused cannot be un-caused once it happens.

## CLOSING STATEMENT

You hold a civilian pilot license. That is not a small thing.

Every pilot who came before you navigated the same ground school, the same check rides, the same nervous moments in the pattern when the instructor pulled the throttle and said "engine failure — you have the aircraft." You made it through that. You earned this.

What this handbook asks of you now is simple: respect the standard that got you here. Fly with the same care and discipline every time you climb into the cockpit, whether it is the first flight of your career or the five hundredth. The aircraft does not know how experienced you are. The weather does not care about your logbook hours. The mountain does not move because you are having a good day.

Stay current. Stay safe. And when you are standing on the ramp watching another new pilot earn their wings — remember what it felt like, and be the kind of pilot they deserve to learn from.

**Clear skies.**
