# Pilot Licensing Guide — Earning Your Wings in Los Santos

Every pilot in Los Santos started on the ground. The licensing system exists to ensure that anyone who takes the controls of an aircraft — whether it's a Mammatus trainer at Sandy Shores or a Luxor full of passengers at LSIA — has demonstrated the knowledge, skill, and discipline to fly safely. Licensing isn't a formality. It's the foundation of aviation RP credibility.

This guide walks you through every license type, the training pipeline, practical flight tests, medical requirements, and what happens when you break the rules.

---

## Why Licensing Matters

Without a licensing system, anyone with access to an aircraft could take off and create chaos. Licensing creates:

- **Progression** — You earn your way from student pilot to airline captain over time.
- **Accountability** — Your license can be suspended or revoked for violations.
- **RP depth** — Flight school, check rides, and examiner interactions create rich RP moments.
- **Trust** — Other players know a licensed pilot has been vetted and trained.

---

## License Types

Aviation licensing follows a tiered structure. Each license builds on the previous one and unlocks new privileges.

| License | Full Name | Privileges | Requirements |
|---|---|---|---|
| **SPL** | Student Pilot License | Fly only with a certified instructor aboard | Enrollment in flight school |
| **PPL** | Private Pilot License | Fly solo, carry passengers (no compensation) | Ground school + 10 flight hours + check ride |
| **CPL** | Commercial Pilot License | Fly for hire — charter, cargo, tours | PPL + 25 total flight hours + commercial check ride |
| **ATPL** | Airline Transport Pilot License | Command scheduled airline flights (Luxor, Miljet) | CPL + 50 total flight hours + ATPL written + check ride |
| **RR** | Rotorcraft Rating | Operate helicopters (Maverick, Frogger, Buzzard, etc.) | Separate training and check ride for helicopter operations |
| **MER** | Multi-Engine Rating | Operate multi-engine aircraft (Luxor, Shamal, Miljet) | Additional training beyond single-engine certification |
| **IR** | Instrument Rating | Fly in reduced visibility / IMC conditions | Additional ground school + instrument flight training |
| **CFI** | Certified Flight Instructor | Train and certify student pilots | CPL or ATPL + instructor course + examiner approval |

### License Progression Path

The typical career path looks like this:

```
SPL → PPL → CPL → ATPL
              ↓
         Add Ratings: RR, MER, IR
              ↓
         CFI (if pursuing instruction)
```

## Flight School Enrollment

To begin training, you need to enroll in a recognized flight school. This is an IC process:

1. **Find a flight school** — Most servers have a designated flight school at LSIA or Sandy Shores Airfield.
2. **Meet with an instructor** — Introduce your character and express interest in flying.
3. **Complete paperwork** — IC registration forms, emergency contact, background check.
4. **Receive your SPL** — Once enrolled, you're issued a Student Pilot License.
5. **Begin ground school** — Classroom instruction before you touch an aircraft.

## Ground School Topics

Ground school covers the theoretical knowledge every pilot needs. You don't need to be an expert in real-world aerodynamics, but you should understand the basics well enough to RP them convincingly.

| Topic | What You Learn |
|---|---|
| **Aerodynamics** | Lift, drag, thrust, weight — why planes fly |
| **Aircraft Systems** | Engine, fuel, electrical, flight controls |
| **Navigation** | VFR landmark navigation, compass headings, altimeter use |
| **Weather** | Wind effects, weather minimums, when to stay grounded |
| **Radio Communication** | Proper phraseology, frequency management, emergency calls |
| **Regulations** | Airspace classes, no-fly zones, right-of-way rules |
| **Emergency Procedures** | Engine failure, fire, forced landing, autorotation (rotorcraft) |

Ground school is typically delivered through IC lectures, handouts, or one-on-one sessions.

## Practical Flight Training

After ground school, you begin flying with an instructor.

### Phase 1 — Pre-Flight and Basics

- Aircraft walkaround and pre-flight inspection
- Engine start procedures
- Taxi and runway operations
- Straight-and-level flight
- Gentle turns, climbs, and descents

> **Instructor:** "Alright, before we start the engine, tell me what you're checking on the walkaround."
> **Student:** "Control surfaces — ailerons, elevator, rudder — all free and correct. Fuel quantity, oil level, tire condition, no visible damage."
> **Instructor:** "Good. Walk me through it."
> `/me crouches under the left wing of the Mammatus and checks the fuel drain, catching a small sample in a clear cup.`

### Phase 2 — Core Maneuvers

- Steep turns (45-degree bank)
- Slow flight and stall recognition
- Stall recovery
- Emergency descent procedures
- Pattern work — takeoff, crosswind, downwind, base, final, landing

### Phase 3 — Navigation and Cross-Country

- VFR navigation from LSIA to Sandy Shores
- Checkpoint identification using landmarks (Vinewood Sign, Alamo Sea, Mount Chiliad)
- Fuel planning for cross-country flights
- Diversion to alternate airports

### Phase 4 — Emergency Procedures

- Simulated engine failure after takeoff
- Forced landing in an open field
- Autorotation practice (rotorcraft students)
- Emergency radio calls — `MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY`

> `/me pulls the throttle to idle and announces: "Simulated engine failure."  He scans the terrain below for a suitable field.`
> "I've got a flat area at my one o'clock, about half a mile. I'm turning toward it and establishing best glide speed."

## Practical Flight Tests (Check Rides)

The check ride is your final exam. A Designated Pilot Examiner (DPE) conducts the test in two parts:

### Oral Examination

The examiner will ask you questions on the ground before you fly:

- "What are the weather minimums for VFR flight?"
- "Describe the traffic pattern at an uncontrolled field."
- "What do you do if you lose engine power at 500 feet after takeoff?"
- "What are the airspace restrictions around Fort Zancudo?"

### Flight Examination

You will fly with the examiner and demonstrate:

| Maneuver | Standard |
|---|---|
| Pre-flight inspection | Thorough, systematic, no items missed |
| Takeoff | Centerline tracking, proper rotation speed |
| Steep turns | Maintain altitude within ±100 feet |
| Stall recovery | Prompt recognition, correct recovery technique |
| Navigation | Arrive at assigned checkpoint within 2 minutes of ETA |
| Emergency procedures | Appropriate response, maintain aircraft control |
| Landing | Stable approach, touchdown in the first third of the runway |
| Radio communication | Correct phraseology, timely calls |

If you pass, you receive your license. If you fail, you'll need additional training and a re-test.

> **Examiner:** "Congratulations. You demonstrated solid airmanship today. I'm signing you off for your Private Pilot License."
> `/me shakes the examiner's hand and grins, tucking the signed certificate into his flight bag.`
> "Thank you, sir. That stall recovery had me sweating, but the training kicked in."

## License Restrictions

Not all licenses grant unlimited privileges.

| License | Restrictions |
|---|---|
| **SPL** | Must have instructor aboard. No passengers. Day VFR only. |
| **PPL** | No flying for compensation. Day VFR only unless instrument-rated. |
| **CPL** | May fly for hire. Cannot command airline flights without ATPL. |
| **ATPL** | Full privileges. Must maintain currency. |
| **RR** | Only valid for rotorcraft. Does not authorize fixed-wing flight. |
| **Night Rating** | Required for any flight after sunset. Added to PPL or higher. |

---

## Medical Clearance

All pilots must hold a valid medical certificate, issued through an IC medical exam.

- **Class 1 Medical** — Required for ATPL holders. Most stringent.
- **Class 2 Medical** — Required for CPL holders. Moderate standards.
- **Class 3 Medical** — Required for PPL holders. Basic health check.

Medical certificates must be renewed periodically. A pilot who loses medical clearance is grounded until cleared by a physician.

## Instructor Certification (CFI)

To become a Certified Flight Instructor, you must:

1. Hold a CPL or ATPL.
2. Have a minimum of 50 total flight hours.
3. Complete an instructor training course with a senior CFI.
4. Pass an instructor check ride with a DPE.
5. Demonstrate the ability to teach — not just fly, but explain and correct.

CFIs are the backbone of the aviation community. Without them, no new pilots get trained.

---

## Common Violations and Suspension

| Violation | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Flying without a valid license | Immediate grounding, fine, potential arrest |
| Reckless flying (buzzing buildings, low passes) | License suspension (7-30 days) |
| Entering restricted airspace (Fort Zancudo) | License suspension, investigation, possible arrest |
| Flying under the influence | Immediate revocation, criminal charges |
| Falsifying flight logs | License revocation |
| Ignoring ATC instructions | Warning on first offense, suspension on repeat |
| Operating an aircraft beyond your rating | License suspension, retraining required |

## Final Thoughts

Earning your wings is one of the most satisfying achievements in aviation RP. It's not instant — it's a journey from nervous student pilot to confident captain, filled with training flights, failed approaches, nervous check rides, and finally that moment when an examiner shakes your hand and says you've earned it.

Respect the process. Log your hours. Keep your skills sharp. The license in your pocket isn't just a piece of paper — it's proof that you've put in the work.
