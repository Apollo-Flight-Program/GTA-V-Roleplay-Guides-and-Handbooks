# Air Ambulance Guide — HEMS Operations in Los Santos

When a patient is bleeding out on a mountainside above Paleto Bay and the nearest ground ambulance is twenty minutes away, the air ambulance is the difference between life and death. Helicopter Emergency Medical Services — HEMS — is one of the most demanding and rewarding roles in aviation RP. You're not just a pilot. You're part of a medical team operating in high-pressure, time-critical situations where every decision matters.

This guide covers activation criteria, crew roles, dispatch procedures, landing zones, in-flight medical care, coordination with ground EMS, and the operational discipline that keeps HEMS running safely.

---

## What Is HEMS?

HEMS stands for Helicopter Emergency Medical Services. It's the deployment of a medically equipped helicopter to reach patients who are either too far from a hospital, too critically injured for ground transport, or in locations that ground units can't reach.

In Los Santos RP, HEMS typically operates out of Pillbox Medical Center or LSIA using a Maverick or Frogger configured for medical operations. The helicopter carries a pilot, a flight medic, and sometimes a flight paramedic — a crew of two to three who must work seamlessly under pressure.

---

## When to Activate HEMS

Not every medical call warrants a helicopter. Air ambulances are expensive, limited in number, and carry risk. HEMS should be activated when:

| Criteria | Example Scenario |
|---|---|
| **Patient is in a remote location** | Hiker injured on Mount Chiliad, no road access |
| **Critical time sensitivity** | Gunshot victim losing blood, hospital is 15+ minutes by ground |
| **Ground units are unavailable or delayed** | All ambulances committed to a multi-vehicle pileup on Route 68 |
| **Terrain prevents ground access** | Patient on a rooftop, cliff edge, or island |
| **Multi-casualty incident** | Mass shooting, building collapse — additional transport needed |
| **Specialized facility required** | Patient needs a trauma center, nearest one is across the map |
| **Inter-facility transfer** | Moving a critical patient from Sandy Shores clinic to Pillbox |

### When NOT to Activate HEMS

- Minor injuries (broken arm, lacerations) with ground units available
- Weather below flight minimums (heavy fog, thunderstorms)
- Landing zone cannot be secured
- Patient is in a location with adequate ground access and time is not critical

---

## Crew Roles

A HEMS crew is a tight unit. Each member has a specific role, and they depend on each other absolutely.

| Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|
| **HEMS Pilot** | Flies the aircraft, selects landing zones, manages fuel, communicates with ATC and dispatch. Does NOT leave the aircraft unless secured. |
| **Flight Medic** | Senior medical crew member. Assesses the patient, directs treatment, makes medical decisions in-flight. Typically a paramedic or doctor. |
| **Flight Paramedic** | Assists the flight medic with patient care, manages equipment, helps load/unload the patient. |

The pilot's only job is to fly safely. They do not participate in patient care. The medical crew's only job is the patient. They do not interfere with flight operations. This separation is critical.

> **Pilot:** "Two minutes to the LZ. It's a parking lot off Route 68 — I can see the flares. I'll put us down facing east."
> **Flight Medic:** "Copy. We'll be ready to offload as soon as the skids are down. Patient is intubated and on a pressure infuser."

## Dispatch Procedures

HEMS activation follows a structured dispatch process:

### Step 1 — Request Received

A ground EMS unit, dispatch, or hospital requests HEMS. The request must include:

- **Patient location** (as specific as possible)
- **Nature of the emergency** (trauma, medical, entrapment)
- **Number of patients**
- **Hazards at the scene** (fire, active shooter, power lines, rough terrain)
- **Landing zone status** (secured, needs setup, unknown)

### Step 2 — Crew Assessment

The HEMS pilot and flight medic evaluate: Is the weather flyable? Is the aircraft fueled? Can we reach the patient faster than ground? Is there a safe landing zone?

### Step 3 — Launch

If approved, the crew launches. The pilot files a rapid departure with ATC (or self-announces at uncontrolled fields) and proceeds direct to the scene.

> `/me flips the battery switch and begins the startup sequence, watching the rotor blades begin to spin.`
> "Air Med One is spinning up. Dispatch, confirm LZ is at the intersection of Route 68 and Zancudo Road. We're airborne in ninety seconds."

### Step 4 — En Route

The pilot navigates to the scene while the flight medic prepares equipment. The medic contacts the ground crew by radio for a patient update.

### Step 5 — On Scene

The pilot lands at the designated LZ. The medical crew disembarks and moves to the patient.

## Landing Zone Setup

A landing zone (LZ) is the area where the helicopter will land. Ground EMS or fire units typically set up the LZ before the helicopter arrives.

### LZ Requirements

| Requirement | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Size** | Minimum 60x60 feet (roughly the size of a parking lot) |
| **Surface** | Flat, firm surface — pavement, grass, dirt |
| **Obstructions** | Clear of power lines, trees, poles, fences within 100 feet |
| **Lighting** | At night, mark corners with flares, vehicle headlights, or chem lights |
| **Debris** | Clear loose debris that could be blown by rotor wash |
| **Security** | Keep bystanders and vehicles at least 200 feet away |

### LZ Communication

The ground unit securing the LZ becomes the **LZ Coordinator** and communicates directly with the pilot:

> **LZ Coordinator:** "Air Med One, LZ is a parking lot at the Harmony gas station. Surface is asphalt, no obstructions, winds from the south. I'm standing at the north end with a green chem light."
> **Pilot:** "Copy, I have the chem light in sight. Landing to the south. Keep personnel to the north."

### Helicopter Safety Around the LZ

| Rule | Reason |
|---|---|
| **Never approach from the rear** | The tail rotor is invisible and lethal |
| **Approach from the front or sides only** | The pilot can see you and signal |
| **Stay low — never reach above your head** | The rotor blades are lower than you think |
| **Secure all loose items** | Rotor wash will send hats, papers, and debris flying |
| **Wait for the pilot's signal** | Do not approach until the pilot waves you in |

## Patient Loading

Once the medical crew reaches the patient, they stabilize and prepare for transport.

### Loading Sequence

1. **Assessment** — Flight medic performs a rapid assessment. Is the patient stable enough for flight?
2. **Stabilization** — Airway management, bleeding control, IV access, splinting.
3. **Packaging** — Patient is secured on a backboard or stretcher.
4. **Loading** — Patient is loaded into the cabin and secured with straps.
5. **Departure** — Medical crew signals the pilot. Pilot departs.

> `/me and the flight paramedic lift the stretcher toward the idling Maverick, ducking under the rotor arc.`
> "Patient is loaded and secured. We're good to go — let's get to Pillbox."

---

## In-Flight Medical Care

Medical care doesn't stop when the helicopter lifts off. The flight medic continues treatment en route:

- **Monitoring vitals** — Heart rate, blood pressure, oxygen saturation
- **Medication administration** — Pain management, sedation, cardiac drugs
- **Airway management** — Maintaining intubation, suctioning
- **Bleeding control** — Maintaining pressure, monitoring bandages
- **Communication** — Radio ahead to the receiving hospital with a patient report

> **Flight Medic (to hospital):** "Pillbox, Air Med One inbound, ETA four minutes. We have a thirty-two-year-old male, single GSW to the right chest, intubated in the field, BP eighty over fifty, heart rate one-twenty. Request trauma team standing by on the helipad."
> **Pillbox:** "Copy, Air Med One. Trauma team is en route to the roof."

---

## Hospital Helipads

The primary receiving facility is **Pillbox Medical Center**, which has a rooftop helipad. The secondary option is **Central Los Santos Medical Center**.

### Helipad Operations

- The pilot contacts the hospital on approach to confirm the helipad is clear.
- The hospital trauma team meets the helicopter on the pad.
- The patient is transferred from the helicopter stretcher to a hospital gurney.
- The helicopter does NOT shut down during a hot offload — the pilot keeps the rotors turning for rapid departure.

> `/me keeps the collective steady as the trauma team approaches from the stairwell, crouching under the rotor arc and moving to the cabin doors.`
> "Hot offload — rotors are live. Take the patient and clear the pad. I'll reposition to the LSIA ramp."

---

## Night Operations

Night HEMS is significantly more dangerous than daytime operations. Reduced visibility, difficulty identifying landing zones, and the risk of spatial disorientation all increase at night.

### Night Operations Requirements

- Pilot must hold a **Night Rating** in addition to their standard license.
- LZ must be illuminated — flares, vehicle lights, or chem lights at all four corners.
- The pilot relies on instruments more heavily and may fly a more conservative approach.
- Crew uses NVG (Night Vision Goggle) RP where applicable.

> `/me scans the darkness below, looking for the green chem lights marking the LZ corners. He spots them at his two o'clock and begins a slow, stabilized approach.`
> "I have the LZ in sight. Four lights, good square. Coming in steep to clear those trees on short final."

---

## Weather Minimums

HEMS does not fly in all conditions. The pilot has final authority to refuse a mission based on weather.

| Condition | Day Minimum | Night Minimum | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Visibility** | 1.5 miles | 3 miles | Below minimums: mission declined |
| **Cloud ceiling** | 500 feet AGL | 800 feet AGL | Below minimums: mission declined |
| **Wind** | 40 knots sustained | 30 knots sustained | Above limits: mission declined |
| **Thunderstorms** | Not within 10 miles | Not within 20 miles | Ground stop |
| **Fog** | Scattered acceptable | Not acceptable | Mission declined in fog at night |
| **Icing** | Not acceptable | Not acceptable | Mission declined |

The pilot never faces pressure to fly in unsafe weather. "We don't save anyone if we crash on the way there."

## Coordination with Ground EMS

HEMS doesn't replace ground EMS — it supplements it. Effective coordination between air and ground units is essential.

### Communication Flow

1. **Dispatch** contacts HEMS with the mission.
2. **Ground EMS** provides patient updates and LZ information.
3. **HEMS crew** provides ETA and requests.
4. **On scene**, the flight medic takes over patient care from ground medics.
5. **Ground medics** brief the flight medic on treatments administered.

> **Ground Medic:** "Air Med One, ground medic on scene. Patient is a forty-five-year-old female, fall from approximately thirty feet, suspected spinal injury, GCS twelve. We've got her c-spined and on high-flow O2. LZ is the dirt lot south of the ranger station — you'll see our rig with the lights on."
> **Flight Medic:** "Copy all. ETA three minutes. We'll take her to Pillbox. Keep her packaged and ready for us."

## Common Mistakes in HEMS RP

| Mistake | Why It's a Problem | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Activating HEMS for a sprained ankle | Wastes the resource, breaks immersion | Reserve HEMS for critical patients and remote locations |
| Pilot leaving the aircraft on scene | The aircraft is unsecured and vulnerable | Pilot stays in the cockpit, keeps rotors turning |
| Approaching the helicopter from the rear | Tail rotor danger — this kills people IRL | Always approach from the front or sides |
| Flying in thunderstorms to "save" a patient | Unrealistic and dangerous RP | Decline the mission and send ground units |
| Skipping the patient handoff RP | Loses a critical RP interaction | Brief the hospital team, transfer care formally |

---

## Final Thoughts

HEMS RP is not for everyone. It demands discipline, teamwork, and the willingness to say no when conditions aren't safe. But when it works — when you lift a critical patient off a mountainside, keep them alive in the back of a Maverick, and land on Pillbox with a trauma team waiting — it's some of the best roleplay you'll ever experience.

Fly safe. Save lives. And never forget: the crew that takes care of each other takes care of the patient.
