# Police Aviation Guide — Air Support Operations for Law Enforcement

When a suspect flees south on the Great Ocean Highway at 140 mph and ground units are falling behind, one voice on the radio changes everything: "Air One has eyes on the vehicle." Police aviation is the force multiplier that turns chaotic pursuits into controlled operations, that finds a missing person in the Tongva Hills at night, and that gives SWAT the tactical advantage to end a standoff safely.

This guide covers everything about law enforcement air support — from pursuit tactics and FLIR operations to observer protocols, communication standards, and the discipline required to operate a police helicopter effectively.

---

## Air Support Unit Overview

The Air Support Unit (ASU) is a specialized division within law enforcement, typically operating under LSPD or BCSO. The unit provides aerial assistance to ground units across Los Santos and Blaine County.

### Mission Types

| Mission | Description | Priority |
|---|---|---|
| **Pursuit Support** | Tracking fleeing vehicles and suspects from the air | High |
| **Search and Rescue** | Locating missing persons, hikers, drowning victims | High |
| **Surveillance** | Monitoring gang activity, drug operations, stakeouts | Medium |
| **Perimeter Security** | Aerial overwatch for crime scene perimeters | Medium |
| **SWAT Support** | Insertion, extraction, overwatch for tactical operations | High |
| **VIP Transport** | Moving officials, witnesses, or high-value detainees | Variable |
| **Traffic Management** | Monitoring major incidents, directing ground units | Low |
| **Crime Scene Photography** | Aerial documentation of large crime scenes | Low |
| **Community Patrol** | Routine aerial patrol, deterrence presence | Low |

## Aircraft

The ASU operates specific aircraft equipped for law enforcement operations.

| Aircraft | Role | Equipment | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Police Maverick** | Primary patrol and pursuit | Searchlight, FLIR camera, radio suite | The standard police helicopter |
| **Buzzard** | Tactical operations | Light armament capability, speed advantage | Used for high-threat operations |
| **Maverick (unmarked)** | Surveillance | No external markings, standard equipment | Covert observation missions |
| **Annihilator** | SWAT transport | Capacity for tactical team, fast rope points | Heavy tactical insertion |

The Police Maverick is the workhorse. Its searchlight and FLIR (Forward Looking Infrared) camera make it ideal for nighttime pursuits and search operations.

---

## Crew Positions

Every ASU flight operates with a minimum two-person crew.

| Position | Responsibilities |
|---|---|
| **Pilot (Air One Pilot)** | Flies the aircraft, maintains safe altitude and positioning, manages fuel, communicates with ATC if required |
| **Tactical Flight Observer (TFO)** | Operates the FLIR/camera, provides commentary to ground units, directs the pursuit or search, maintains radio contact with dispatch and ground supervisors |

The TFO is the voice that ground units hear. While the pilot focuses on flying, the TFO provides the running commentary that guides the entire operation.

## Pursuit Support

Pursuit support is the most common ASU mission. The helicopter provides continuous overhead surveillance of a fleeing suspect while ground units maneuver to intercept.

### Pursuit Communication Protocol

The TFO provides a structured commentary over the radio. Effective pursuit commentary includes:

1. **Vehicle description** — Color, make, model, damage, plate if visible
2. **Direction of travel** — Compass direction and street name
3. **Speed estimate** — Slow, moderate, high rate of speed
4. **Driving behavior** — Running lights, wrong way, swerving, hitting vehicles
5. **Turns** — Announced BEFORE they happen when possible
6. **Stops** — "Vehicle is stopping" or "Suspect is bailing on foot"
7. **Foot bail details** — Description of suspect, direction of travel, clothing

> **TFO:** "Air One has the eye. We're overhead a black four-door sedan, southbound on the Los Santos Freeway from the Strawberry on-ramp, high rate of speed, weaving through traffic. Suspect ran the red at Innocence and Forum. Two occupants visible."

> **TFO (foot bail):** "Air One, suspect has bailed on foot! White male, dark hoodie, jeans, running westbound through the alley south of Integrity Way. He just jumped a fence into the parking structure. Ground units, he's on the second level of the parking structure, moving toward the north stairwell."

### Pursuit Tactics

| Tactic | When to Use | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **Parallel tracking** | Open highways, high-speed | Helicopter tracks alongside the pursuit at altitude |
| **Overhead orbit** | Urban streets, low-speed | Helicopter circles directly above the suspect |
| **Offset orbit** | Surveillance mode | Helicopter orbits at a distance to avoid detection |
| **High orbit** | Suspect is aware of helicopter | Higher altitude to maintain visual while being less obvious |
| **Lead positioning** | Predicting suspect's route | Helicopter moves ahead to cover intersections |

## FLIR / Thermal Camera Operations

The FLIR camera is the TFO's primary tool. It detects heat signatures for night pursuits, foot chases, search and rescue, building surveillance, and evidence detection.

> `/me toggles the FLIR to white-hot mode and scans the tree line behind the suspect's last known position.`
> "Air One, I've got a single heat signature in the drainage ditch, approximately fifty meters east of the bail point. He's prone — not moving. Ground units, approach from the west side of the drainage channel."

## Search Patterns

When searching for a missing person, wanted suspect, or evidence over a large area, the ASU uses structured search patterns.

| Pattern | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **Expanding square** | Start at last known position, fly expanding squares outward | Missing persons, foot bail with unknown direction |
| **Parallel track** | Fly back and forth in parallel lines across the search area | Large open areas (desert, fields, shoreline) |
| **Sector search** | Divide the area into sectors, clear each one systematically | Urban areas with defined blocks |
| **Contour search** | Follow terrain features (rivers, roads, ridgelines) | Suspects following natural paths of travel |
| **Creeping line ahead** | Advance line-by-line in one direction | Shoreline or river searches |

> **TFO:** "Ground Supervisor, Air One is initiating an expanding square search from the suspect's last known position at the intersection of Sinner Street and Vespucci Boulevard. Each leg will be approximately two hundred meters. Request ground units hold the perimeter at the four corners."

## Communication Standards

The ASU operates on specific radio channels and follows strict communication discipline.

### Radio Channels

| Channel | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Primary Tactical** | Direct communication with ground units during pursuits and operations |
| **Air Ops** | Air-to-air communication between multiple ASU aircraft |
| **Dispatch** | Standard dispatch channel for routine requests |
| **ATC** | Communication with air traffic control when operating near LSIA |
| **SWAT Tactical** | Encrypted channel for tactical operations |

### Communication Discipline

- **Be concise** — Ground units need clear, brief updates during pursuits.
- **Use cardinal directions** — "Northbound" not "going up the screen."
- **Name streets** — "Turning right on Alta Street" not "turning right."
- **Confirm when you lose visual** — "Air One has lost the eye" is critical information.
- **Announce fuel status** — "Air One is bingo fuel in fifteen minutes" gives ground units time to plan.

## Surveillance Operations

Not all ASU missions involve pursuits. Surveillance is equally important.

### Covert Surveillance

When tasked with monitoring a target, the ASU operates differently:

- Use an **unmarked Maverick** if available
- Maintain a **high orbit** (offset, not directly overhead)
- Minimize radio traffic — use encrypted channels
- Document activity with the camera system
- Coordinate with detective units on the ground

> `/me adjusts the camera zoom to 20x and focuses on the parking lot behind the warehouse on Elgin Avenue.`
> "Detective Reyes, Air One. I've got three vehicles in the rear lot. Two subjects just exited the gray van and entered through the back door. One is carrying a duffel bag. Want us to hold and observe?"

## SWAT Support

The ASU provides critical support during tactical operations.

### Insertion and Extraction

The Annihilator or Buzzard can insert SWAT operators on rooftops or areas inaccessible by ground.

| Phase | ASU Role |
|---|---|
| **Pre-operation** | Aerial reconnaissance, identifying entry points, escape routes, and obstacles |
| **Insertion** | Deliver tactical team to rooftop or landing zone near the target |
| **Overwatch** | Maintain aerial surveillance during the operation, report suspect movement |
| **Extraction** | Pick up the team and any detainees after the operation |
| **Post-operation** | Perimeter surveillance to catch fleeing suspects |

> **SWAT Commander:** "Air One, we're going in sixty seconds. I need eyes on the north side of the building — confirm no movement."
> **TFO:** "Air One confirms north side is clear. No movement on the roof. Two windows on the third floor are open. Thermals show one heat signature on the second floor, center of the building."

## Pilot Requirements

ASU pilots must meet higher standards than civilian pilots.

| Requirement | Standard |
|---|---|
| **License** | CPL with Rotorcraft Rating minimum |
| **Flight hours** | Minimum 100 hours total, 50 hours rotorcraft |
| **Law enforcement** | Must be a sworn officer |
| **Night rating** | Required |
| **Annual proficiency check** | Required |
| **Background** | Clean disciplinary record, no excessive force complaints |

## Fuel Management

Fuel awareness is critical. A helicopter that runs out of fuel during a pursuit is a disaster.

| Status | Meaning | Action Required |
|---|---|---|
| **Full** | Maximum fuel load, 1.5-2 hours of flight | Normal operations |
| **Half** | Approximately 45-60 minutes remaining | Begin planning for refuel |
| **Bingo** | 15-20 minutes of fuel remaining | Announce bingo, return to base or nearest fuel point |
| **Emergency** | Less than 10 minutes remaining | Declare fuel emergency, land immediately |

> **Pilot:** "Air One is bingo fuel. I've got about fifteen minutes. We need to hand off the pursuit or I'm heading back to the ramp."
> **Supervisor:** "Copy, Air One. Air Two is twenty minutes out. Can you hold for five more?"
> **Pilot:** "I can give you five. After that, I'm committed to LSIA."

## No-Fly Zones and Restrictions

| Zone | Restriction | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Fort Zancudo** | No entry without military coordination | Even police aircraft need clearance |
| **Above active shootings** | Minimum 500 feet AGL unless directly supporting | Avoid being a target |
| **Crowd events** | Minimum 1000 feet AGL unless authorized | Safety for people on the ground |
| **Other agency operations** | Coordinate before entering their airspace | Avoid interference |
| **Hospital helipads** | Do not block EMS helicopter operations | Medical priority |

## Common Mistakesin Police Aviation RP

| Mistake | Why It's a Problem | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Flying too low over pursuits | Endangers the crew, unrealistic | Maintain 500+ feet AGL minimum |
| TFO giving unclear directions | Ground units can't act on vague info | Use street names, compass directions, specific descriptions |
| Ignoring fuel state | Running out of fuel mid-pursuit | Monitor fuel constantly, announce bingo early |
| Pilot also doing TFO duties | Divides attention, degrades both roles | Always fly with a dedicated TFO |
| Landing in active crime scenes | Compromises evidence, endangers crew | Land at a safe distance and walk in if needed |
| Using lethal force from the air | Extremely restricted — almost never appropriate | Observe and report, let ground units engage |

---

## Final Thoughts

Police aviation is about patience, precision, and communication. You're not the one making the arrest — you're the one making sure the arrest happens. The best ASU crews are invisible to the suspect and indispensable to the ground units. You are the eye in the sky that sees everything, the voice on the radio that never loses the thread, and the team that brings everyone home safe.

Fly disciplined. Communicate clearly. Keep the eye.
