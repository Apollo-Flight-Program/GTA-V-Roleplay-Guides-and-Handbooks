# Journalist Roleplay Guide — Covering the Stories of Los Santos

Every city needs its press. Without journalists, the crimes go unreported, the corruption stays hidden, and the citizens of Los Santos never learn the truth. Journalist RP is one of the most socially powerful roles on a server — you shape public opinion, hold officials accountable, and create narrative that other players react to for days or weeks after your story drops.

This guide covers everything from building a media character to covering crime scenes, conducting interviews, navigating press freedom, and running a news operation that the entire server pays attention to.

---

## Why Journalist RP Matters

Journalism creates **consequences**. When a police officer uses excessive force and nobody reports it, nothing changes. When a journalist publishes the story with witness statements and bodycam descriptions, suddenly Internal Affairs is investigating, the police chief is holding a press conference, and the officer's career is on the line.

Journalists also create **narrative bridges** between factions that never interact directly. Gang members don't talk to politicians. But a journalist who covers both worlds can create storylines that connect them — a city council campaign promise to "clean up the Southside" collides with a feature story about the human cost of gang life.

Good journalism RP makes the server feel like a living world with memory and accountability.

## Creating a Media Character

Your journalist character needs more than a notepad. Think about:

| Element | Questions to Consider |
|---|---|
| **Background** | Where did they study? What's their career history? Are they a veteran reporter or fresh out of college? |
| **Specialty** | Crime reporter? Political correspondent? Investigative journalist? Entertainment/gossip? |
| **Motivation** | Truth-seeker? Career climber? Idealist? Cynical veteran? Adrenaline junkie covering crime scenes? |
| **Ethics** | Will they protect sources at all costs? Would they publish something that could get someone hurt? |
| **Employer** | Staff reporter at a news station? Freelance? Independent blogger? |
| **Personality on camera** | Calm and professional? Aggressive and confrontational? Empathetic storyteller? |

A crime reporter who's been doing this for fifteen years plays very differently from a twenty-two-year-old intern covering her first gang shooting.

> `/me adjusts the press lanyard around her neck and flips open the notepad, scanning the police tape line and counting the squad cars.`
> "This is the third shooting on Forum Drive this month. Someone at the precinct owes me a comment."

## Press Credentials

Press credentials establish your legitimacy. In most RP servers, credentials are issued by:

- **A recognized news organization** — Your employer provides a press badge and ID.
- **The city government** — Some servers require city-issued press passes for access to government buildings, press conferences, and crime scene press areas.
- **Self-issued (freelance)** — Freelancers may carry their own identification, but it carries less weight with law enforcement.

Credentials grant you **access**, not **immunity**. A press badge lets you into the press area at a crime scene. It does not let you cross police tape, interfere with an arrest, or refuse lawful orders from an officer.

## Covering Crime Scenes

Crime scenes are where most journalist RP happens. Here's how to do it well:

### Arrival

1. Park your vehicle away from emergency vehicles — don't block access.
2. Identify yourself to the nearest officer: "I'm [Name], press, with [Outlet]. Is there a PIO or scene commander I can speak to?"
3. Stay behind police tape. Do not cross it unless explicitly invited.
4. Begin observing — note the number of units, ambulances, detectives present.

### Information Gathering

- **Official statements** — Ask for the Public Information Officer (PIO). They're trained to give statements.
- **Witness interviews** — Talk to bystanders. "Did you see what happened? Can I quote you?"
- **Visual documentation** — Describe what you see. RP taking photographs.
- **Scanner monitoring** — Listen to police radio traffic for additional context.

> `/me holds up the camera and takes several photographs of the scene from behind the police tape, capturing the ambulance loading a stretcher.`
> "Excuse me, ma'am — I'm with Weazel News. Were you here when the shooting happened? Can you tell me what you saw?"

### What NOT to Do

| Don't | Why |
|---|---|
| Cross police tape without permission | You'll be arrested for obstruction or trespassing |
| Interfere with EMS treating a patient | You'll be removed and possibly charged |
| Publish a victim's name before family notification | Ethical violation — extremely poor form |
| Broadcast officer positions during an active standoff | You could get someone killed |
| Touch or move evidence | Tampering with evidence is a crime |

## Interviewing Officials

Interviewing police commanders, mayors, council members, and department heads is a core journalism skill.

### Types of Interviews

| Type | Description | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| **On the record** | Everything said can be published with attribution | Official statements, public figures |
| **Off the record** | Nothing said can be published | Building trust, getting background |
| **On background** | Information can be used but not attributed by name | "A source within the department said..." |
| **Deep background** | Information shapes your understanding but is never published | Understanding context |

### Interview Technique

- **Prepare your questions** — Know what you want to ask before you approach.
- **Start soft** — Build rapport before asking hard questions.
- **Listen more than you talk** — Let them fill the silence.
- **Follow up** — "Can you elaborate on that?" or "What do you mean by that?"
- **Pin them down** — "So you're saying the department has no comment on the allegations?"

> **Journalist:** "Chief, three officers have been involved in use-of-force incidents this month. Is there a pattern here that concerns you?"
> **Police Chief:** "Each incident is being reviewed independently. I have full confidence in our officers."
> **Journalist:** "With respect, Chief, that's what you said last month. Has Internal Affairs opened any investigations?"
> `/me watches the Chief's expression carefully, pen hovering over the notepad.`

## Writing News Articles IC

In-character news articles are typically posted on server forums, Discord channels, or in-game websites. A good IC article follows standard journalism structure:

### Article Structure

1. **Headline** — Punchy, informative, attention-grabbing
2. **Byline** — "By [Character Name], Weazel News"
3. **Lead paragraph** — Who, what, when, where, why in the first two sentences
4. **Body** — Details, quotes, context, background
5. **Closing** — Ongoing investigation, community reaction, what happens next

### Example Article Lead

> *SHOOTING IN STRAWBERRY LEAVES ONE DEAD, TWO INJURED*
> *By Maria Santos, Weazel News*
>
> A shooting in the 400 block of Strawberry Avenue late Thursday night left one man dead and two others hospitalized with gunshot wounds. LSPD officers responded to reports of gunfire at approximately 11:45 PM and found three victims in a parking lot adjacent to a convenience store. The deceased has not been identified pending family notification.

## Broadcasting Live

Live broadcasts — standing in front of a crime scene with a camera, reporting to the audience — are powerful RP moments.

### Live Broadcast Protocol

1. **Establish your shot** — Position yourself with the scene visible behind you.
2. **Introduce yourself** — "This is [Name] reporting live from [Location] for Weazel News."
3. **Report what you know** — Stick to confirmed facts.
4. **Mention what's unconfirmed** — "We're hearing reports that..." or "This has not been confirmed by police."
5. **Sign off** — "Reporting live from Strawberry, I'm Maria Santos, Weazel News."

> `/me faces the camera operator and smooths her jacket, waiting for the red light. She takes a breath and begins.`
> "Good evening, Los Santos. I'm Maria Santos reporting live from Forum Drive where a deadly shooting has left one person dead and two others in critical condition at Pillbox Medical Center. Police have cordoned off a two-block area and detectives are on scene. Witnesses tell us they heard at least six gunshots. No arrests have been made at this time."

## Press Freedom vs. Obstruction

This is the line every journalist must understand:

| Your Rights | Your Limits |
|---|---|
| Film and photograph from public areas | Cannot cross police tape or enter restricted areas |
| Ask questions of officials and witnesses | Cannot interfere with police operations |
| Refuse to reveal confidential sources (varies by server) | Cannot withhold evidence of a crime in progress |
| Publish factual reporting without prior restraint | Cannot publish classified law enforcement operations in progress |
| Attend public government meetings | Cannot disrupt proceedings |
| Request public records | Cannot hack, steal, or bribe for documents |

> **Officer:** "You need to move back behind the tape, ma'am."
> **Journalist:** "I'm press — I have a right to be here."
> **Officer:** "You have a right to be behind that tape. Not in front of it. Move back or I'll arrest you for obstruction."
> `/me steps back behind the tape, holding up her press badge for the officer to see, but not arguing further.`

## Investigative Journalism

Investigative journalism is the deep end — long-term stories that uncover corruption, fraud, or systemic problems.

### Investigative Process

1. **Receive a tip** — A source contacts you with information.
2. **Verify independently** — Never publish based on a single source.
3. **Document everything** — Records, photographs, statements, dates.
4. **Seek comment from the accused** — Always give them a chance to respond before publishing.
5. **Consult with your editor** — If you have one, get a second opinion on the story.
6. **Publish** — With full documentation and attribution.

### Source Protection

Protecting sources is a cornerstone of journalism ethics. If a police officer leaks information about corruption in the department, revealing their identity could end their career or endanger them.

> **Detective:** "Who told you about the evidence tampering?"
> **Journalist:** "I can't reveal my sources, Detective. You know that."
> **Detective:** "This is an active investigation. I could subpoena you."
> **Journalist:** "Then subpoena me. I still won't reveal my source."

## Ride-Alongs with Police

Some departments allow journalists to ride along with officers on patrol. This creates excellent RP opportunities.

- **Request through the PIO** — Don't just hop in a cruiser.
- **Follow officer instructions** — You're a guest, not a partner.
- **Stay in the vehicle during dangerous situations** unless told otherwise.
- **Don't publish officer tactics or identities** without permission.
- **Write about the experience** — "A Night on Patrol in South Los Santos" makes for compelling content.

## News Station Operations

Running a news station is a management RP role that supports all the journalists on the team.

| Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|
| **Station Manager** | Oversees operations, hires reporters, sets editorial direction |
| **Editor** | Reviews stories before publication, fact-checks, assigns stories |
| **Reporter** | Covers stories in the field, writes articles, does live broadcasts |
| **Camera Operator** | Accompanies reporters, RP's filming live shots and interviews |
| **Anchor** | Presents news from the studio, reads stories on-air |
| **Intern** | Assists reporters, learns the craft, does research |

## Common Mistakes in Journalist RP

| Mistake | Why It's a Problem | What to Do Instead |
|---|---|---|
| Making up stories with no RP basis | Destroys credibility and immersion | Only report what you've witnessed or verified |
| Ignoring police commands at crime scenes | Gets you arrested and ruins the RP | Comply first, challenge later through IC legal channels |
| Publishing without giving subjects a chance to comment | Unfair and lazy journalism | Always seek comment before publication |
| Treating journalism as a combat role | You're not a cop — you don't chase suspects | Observe and report, stay safe |
| Never publishing anything | You're a journalist who never writes — that's not RP | Write and publish IC articles regularly |

---

## Final Thoughts

Journalism RP is about telling the city's story. You're the witness, the recorder, and sometimes the conscience of Los Santos. The best journalist characters don't just report the news — they create ripple effects that shape the server's narrative for weeks.

Pick up the pen. Turn on the camera. The story won't tell itself.
