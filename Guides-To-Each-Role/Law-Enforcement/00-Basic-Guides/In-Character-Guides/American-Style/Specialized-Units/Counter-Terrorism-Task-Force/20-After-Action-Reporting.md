# San Andreas Counter-Terrorism Task Force
## After-Action Reporting and Lessons Learned

**Issuing Authority:** Deputy Director — Operations

## Part I: The Purpose of After-Action Review

Every operation SACTF conducts is a learning opportunity. The after-action review (AAR) is the mechanism by which that learning is captured, analyzed, and integrated into future operations and training.

AARs are not blame exercises. They are professional reviews of what happened versus what was planned, and why the difference occurred. Personnel who participated in an operation contribute to the AAR without fear that honest reporting will be used against them. The culture of honest AARs is more valuable than any individual finding.

When an organization stops doing honest AARs, it repeats its mistakes. SACTF will not repeat its mistakes.

## Part II: AAR Requirements

### 2.1 Mandatory AAR Events

The following operations require a formal written AAR:

| Operation Type | AAR Timeline | Approving Authority |
|---|---|---|
| Tactical warrant service | 72 hours | TOD Commander |
| Crisis/hostage incident | 72 hours | Operations Deputy Director |
| Arrest operations (more than 3 subjects) | 5 business days | Investigations SAC |
| Surveillance operations (significant complexity) | 5 business days | Surveillance Unit Supervisor |
| Any use of force | 48 hours | Division Commander |
| Any officer safety incident | 48 hours | Operations Deputy Director |
| Full-scale operational deployment | 7 business days | Director |
| Training exercises (quarterly events) | 10 business days | Training Division |

**Minor operations:** Operations not meeting the above thresholds complete an abbreviated AAR (the Operations Debrief Form) within 5 business days.

### 2.2 Immediate Verbal Debrief

Before any written AAR, a verbal debrief is conducted with all participants immediately (within 4 hours) of operation completion, while memories are fresh:
- What was the objective?
- What happened?
- What went well?
- What needs improvement?
- Any immediate safety or legal follow-up actions needed?

Verbal debrief notes are recorded and form the basis of the written AAR.

## Part III: AAR Content Requirements

### 3.1 Standard AAR Format

**Section 1: Operation Overview**
- Operation name/identifier and date
- Mission statement — what was the objective?
- Participating elements and numbers
- Geographic area of operation
- Operation type (deliberate entry, surveillance, arrest, etc.)

**Section 2: Intelligence Picture**
- What was the intelligence available before the operation?
- Was the intelligence accurate?
- Where were intelligence gaps, and what was the consequence of those gaps?
- Any new intelligence developed during the operation?

**Section 3: Planning Assessment**
- Was the operational plan adequate for the mission?
- Were rehearsals conducted? Were they sufficient?
- Were legal authorizations complete and appropriate?
- Were resources appropriate to the mission?
- Identified planning shortfalls and their consequences

**Section 4: Execution Assessment**
- Chronological narrative of the operation
- For each significant phase, assessment of execution versus plan
- Communications effectiveness
- Decision points — what decisions were made, by whom, and were they right?
- Subject and community interactions

**Section 5: Use of Force (if applicable)**
- Complete description of each use of force incident
- Context and perceived threat
- Force technique or weapon used
- Assessment of policy compliance
- Any injury and medical response
- Referral for formal review if warranted

**Section 6: Evidence and Documentation**
- Was evidence handling conducted correctly?
- Any chain of custody concerns?
- Documentation completeness assessment

**Section 7: Identified Strengths**
- What did the team do well?
- Specific examples of effective performance
- Individual recognition for exceptional performance (noted here; formal recognition processed separately)

**Section 8: Identified Issues and Recommendations**
- Specific, actionable issues identified
- For each issue: description, root cause analysis, specific recommendation
- Priority classification: Safety-Critical / Significant / Minor

**Section 9: Follow-Up Actions**
- Specific actions required
- Owner of each action
- Due date for each action

### 3.2 Sensitive Information in AARs

AARs that involve classified collection, undercover operations, or sensitive source information are written in a manner that protects that information. Classified versions of AARs are stored appropriately. Where a sanitized version is needed for broader distribution, the sanitized version is clearly labeled.

## Part IV: Lessons Learned Integration

### 4.1 From AAR to Lesson

An identified issue in an AAR becomes a lesson when it is:
- Documented with enough specificity to be actionable
- Analyzed for root cause (not just described)
- Converted into a specific recommendation
- Assigned to an owner for resolution

Issues that are documented but not converted into actionable recommendations are wasted. The purpose of the AAR is change, not documentation.

### 4.2 Training Division Review

Every AAR is reviewed by the Training Division within 10 business days of completion. The Training Division:
- Identifies lessons with training implications
- Logs training-relevant lessons in the Training Issues Database
- Integrates recurring issues into the training curriculum
- Requests AAR briefings from the operation team for significant training-relevant incidents

### 4.3 Recurring Issue Analysis

Quarterly, the Training Division and Operations section jointly review the accumulated AAR lessons from the prior quarter. Recurring issues — the same problem appearing in multiple AARs — are treated as systemic issues requiring systemic solutions, not just individual corrections.

---

## Part V: After-Action Culture

The AAR process only works in an organization where it is safe to be honest. SACTF supervisors and command are responsible for maintaining the culture that makes honest AARs possible:

- Personnel are not penalized for honest reporting of operational failures in AARs
- Personnel are not singled out by name in AARs for individual mistakes, except where individual misconduct is at issue (misconduct is handled through the disciplinary process, separately from the AAR)
- Command uses AAR findings for improvement, not for targeting personnel
- Command participates in AARs of their own decisions with the same honesty they expect from subordinates

An organization that does honest AARs is, over time, substantially safer and more effective than one that does not. The short-term discomfort of honest self-assessment is the cost of long-term performance.
