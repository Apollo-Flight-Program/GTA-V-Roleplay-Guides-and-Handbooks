## Part I: Training Philosophy

### 1.1 Why Training Matters Here More Than Anywhere Else

Every law enforcement agency trains its personnel. SACTF training is different in two important ways.

First, the consequences of a training failure in counterterrorism are of a different magnitude than in conventional law enforcement. An investigator who fails to follow evidence protocol on a robbery case may lose the prosecution. An investigator who makes the same failure in a terrorism case may lose the prosecution, allow a dangerous individual to remain at large, and compromise a years-long intelligence effort. The cost of getting it wrong scales with the significance of the threat.

Second, SACTF personnel routinely operate in conditions of greater ambiguity, higher stakes, and more complex legal frameworks than most law enforcement personnel ever encounter. Their training must address that complexity, not just the foundational mechanics of law enforcement practice.

Training in SACTF is not a periodic qualification event. It is a continuous professional development program that keeps personnel at the edge of their capabilities throughout their assignment.

### 1.2 Training Principles

**Train as you fight:** Training scenarios replicate operational conditions as closely as possible. Personnel do not practice clean, cooperative scenarios that do not reflect what operations actually look like.

**Stress inoculation:** Particularly for tactical personnel, training deliberately introduces stress — time pressure, physical fatigue, unexpected complications, conflicting information — to build the physiological and psychological resilience to perform under operational conditions.

**Legal integration:** Legal and procedural knowledge is integrated into practical training, not taught separately. When operators clear a building in training, they are also practicing the documentation and reporting protocols that will be required after an actual operation.

**Failure analysis:** When training exercises go wrong — and in well-designed training, some of them should — the failure is analyzed, not hidden. Understanding what failed and why is more valuable than a successful run-through of a scenario that was too easy.

## Part II: New Personnel Training

### 2.1 SACTF Foundations Course

All new personnel attend the SACTF Foundations Course during their first month of assignment. The course is two weeks, conducted at the SACTF Training Facility.

**Week One: Legal and Analytical Foundations**

*Day 1: Constitutional Framework and Civil Liberties*
- Fourth Amendment (unreasonable search and seizure) in the counterterrorism context
- First Amendment considerations for investigations of expressive or associational activity
- Fifth and Sixth Amendment rights in arrest, interrogation, and prosecution
- The legal landscape that distinguishes counterterrorism from conventional law enforcement

*Day 2: Surveillance Law and Warrant Requirements*
- General warrant requirements and the affidavit process
- Electronic surveillance authorization — telephone intercept, digital monitoring
- Physical surveillance — what requires authorization and what does not
- Reporting and monitoring requirements for authorized surveillance
- Common legal errors and how to avoid them

*Day 3: Intelligence Law and Information Sharing*
- The legal framework for intelligence collection and retention
- Information sharing limitations and authorities
- Classified information handling
- The overlap between criminal investigation and intelligence collection — and why it matters

*Day 4: Terrorism Statutes and Charges*
- Applicable San Andreas and federal terrorism statutes
- Material support elements and prosecution requirements
- Conspiracy charges in terrorism investigations
- Evidence standards specific to terrorism prosecution

*Day 5: Case Studies in Legal Compliance and Failure*
- Review of documented cases where legal compliance failures destroyed investigations
- Discussion of pressure situations and how to handle them
- Ethics scenarios — write-your-own outcome

*Day 6–7: Intelligence Collection, Analysis, and Product Development*
- Intelligence cycle fundamentals
- Source reliability and information credibility assessment
- Writing intelligence products that are analytically sound and useful
- Collection planning workshop

*Day 8: Operational Security*
- Counterintelligence fundamentals
- OPSEC practices for SACTF personnel
- Social media, personal conduct, and professional discretion

*Day 9: CBRN Awareness (All Personnel)*
- Introduction to chemical, biological, radiological, and nuclear threats
- Recognition indicators — signs of CBRN hazard
- Immediate response — what to do before the specialists arrive
- Personal protective actions at the non-specialist level

*Day 10: Course Review and Assessment*
- Written examination covering all Week One material
- Minimum passing score required; personnel who do not pass are given one retest; failure on retest initiates additional training

**Week Two: Role-Specific Foundations**

Week Two is divided by assignment type. Personnel attend the track appropriate to their assignment.

**Track A — Investigations:**
- Case management systems and documentation requirements
- Affidavit preparation — standard and complex warrant applications
- Interview and interrogation in the terrorism context — legal framework and technique
- Witness management in sensitive cases
- Coordination with prosecution
- Practical exercise: full simulated investigation from initial intelligence to warrant application

**Track B — Intelligence Analysis:**
- SACTF intelligence systems — hands-on orientation
- Analytical tradecraft — structured analytic techniques
- Link analysis methods and software
- Geospatial analysis fundamentals
- Writing for operational consumers — brevity, precision, and appropriate confidence language
- Practical exercise: complete target package development from provided source material

**Track C — Tactical (Foundations Only — Full Tactical Qualification Separate):**
- SACTF tactical doctrine overview
- Legal authority for tactical operations
- Operational planning process — the eight-step process in practice
- Documentation requirements for tactical operations
- Communication protocols — SACTF radio procedures
- Orientation to the full tactical qualification program

### 2.2 Division Integration Phase

Following the Foundations Course, new personnel complete a 30-day Division Integration Phase under the supervision of a designated mentor:
- Shadow experienced personnel on 3–5 operational activities (appropriate to clearance level and security of the operation)
- Complete division-specific SOPs review and acknowledgment
- Establish familiarity with current active cases and their intelligence context
- Complete mentor evaluation at days 15 and 30
- Final integration review with Division Commander

## Part III: Tactical Qualification Program

### 3.1 Initial Tactical Qualification

All TOD personnel complete the Initial Tactical Qualification Program before independent duty. The program is 8 weeks and operates at the SACTF Training Facility and authorized external training sites.

**Weeks 1–2: Foundations**
- Fitness pre-assessment and baseline
- Weapons safety and SACTF-specific weapons handling standards
- Individual movement techniques — cover, concealment, movement between positions
- Basic communications — radio procedures, brevity codes, communications under stress
- Medical: Tactical Casualty Care (TCCC equivalent) — tourniquet application, wound packing, airway management, casualty movement

**Weeks 3–4: Individual Skills**
- Pistol qualification — Combat Qualification Course (CQC) at all standards
- Rifle/long gun qualification
- Night operations fundamentals — movement, communications, and weapons employment in low-light conditions
- Less-lethal systems qualification — authorization, deployment, and limitations
- Defensive tactics — control techniques, handcuffing under stress, ground defense

**Weeks 5–6: Team Tactics**
- Team movement formations — stack, wedge, line, modified
- Building entry fundamentals — fatal funnel, buttonhook, cross-through
- Room clearing — individual room, L-shaped room, multiple-room sequences
- Hallway and staircase clearance
- Perimeter establishment and operation
- Vehicle assaults — approach, containment, and controlled subject removal

**Week 7: Integrated Scenarios**
- Full scenario exercises integrating all individual and team skills
- Stress inoculation scenarios — elevated heart rate prior to entry, time pressure, role players providing realistic resistance
- Force-on-force training (simulated ammunition) — experience the target's perspective; experience making decisions under return fire
- Night integrated scenario

**Week 8: Qualification**
- Weapons qualification: all platforms
- Tactical fitness qualification
- Scenario qualification: two full team scenarios evaluated by Training Division cadre
- Written examination: legal framework for tactical operations, ROE application, documentation requirements
- Oral board: scenario-based questions before a three-officer board

**Pass standards:** All components must be passed. Failure of any component results in remediation and retest. Personnel who cannot pass after remediation are not assigned to tactical roles.

### 3.2 Annual Tactical Recertification

All TOD personnel complete annual recertification covering:
- Weapons qualification (all platforms)
- Tactical fitness standard
- One integrated team scenario evaluated by Training Division
- Legal update — any changes in applicable law or department policy

### 3.3 Quarterly Training Events

TOD teams conduct quarterly training events to maintain perishable skills. Training Division provides the curriculum; team leaders are responsible for execution. Quarterly training covers topics selected on a rotating basis:
- Vehicle interdiction and felony stops
- Maritime operations (boat-based target approach)
- Elevated structure operations
- Underground/confined space operations
- Explosive/IED environment awareness
- CBRN environment operations
- Low-light and night operations
- Long-range surveillance and reconnaissance

## Part IV: Specialized Training Programs

### 4.1 Surveillance Operator Course

Personnel assigned to the Surveillance Unit complete the eight-week Surveillance Operator Course:

*Weeks 1–2: Physical Surveillance Fundamentals*
- Surveillance ethics and legal requirements
- Foot surveillance — single operator, two-operator, multi-person team
- Maintaining coverage without compromise — distance, angle, pace
- Recognition of target counter-surveillance

*Weeks 3–4: Vehicular Surveillance*
- Vehicle surveillance team composition and roles
- Box formation and rolling coverage
- Urban terrain vs. rural terrain challenges
- Helicopter-supported surveillance integration

*Weeks 5–6: Technical Surveillance*
- Electronic monitoring legal requirements review
- Monitoring equipment operations
- GPS and tracking device deployment (court-authorized)
- Technical surveillance vulnerability assessment

*Weeks 7–8: Integrated Scenarios*
- Multi-day surveillance exercises — real-world urban environment
- Scenario evaluation by Training Division

### 4.2 Crisis Negotiator Training

Personnel assigned to the Crisis Negotiation Unit complete a specialized program:

*Initial Training (40 hours minimum):*
- Crisis negotiation theory and research
- Communication techniques for high-stress situations
- Behavioral analysis — assessment of subjects in crisis
- Communication with individuals in acute psychological distress
- Tactical patience — the negotiator's role in time management
- Documentation and contemporaneous note-taking during negotiations

*Annual Recertification (16 hours minimum):*
- Scenario-based exercises
- Legal update
- Behavioral assessment skills review

### 4.3 Intelligence Analysis Professional Development

IAC personnel participate in a continuous professional development program:
- Structured Analytic Techniques training (initial and refresher)
- Subject-matter expertise development — personnel are assigned to specific threat domain portfolios and receive relevant specialized training
- Writing workshop (annual)
- Tool proficiency — analytical software, database access, geospatial tools
- External courses — analyst-focused programs provided by federal partners

### 4.4 CBRN Specialist Training

Personnel designated as CBRN specialists (WMD Squad, HAZMAT Integration Team) receive:
- Hazardous Materials Operations Level (initial)
- CBRN Awareness and Response (initial, 40 hours)
- Annual CBRN scenario exercise
- Technical updates from the applicable specialty (chemical, biological, radiological, or nuclear)

## Part V: Continuing Education Requirements

### 5.1 Mandatory Annual Training

All SACTF personnel complete the following every year:

| Training | Hours |
|---|---|
| Legal update — applicable law and policy changes | 4 |
| Use of force policy review and scenario | 4 |
| Information security and OPSEC | 2 |
| Implicit bias and professional conduct | 4 |
| Crisis recognition and response (mental health first aid) | 4 |

### 5.2 External Training Opportunities

SACTF personnel are encouraged to pursue external training that deepens their professional capabilities. The Training Division maintains a catalog of approved external courses, including:

- Federal law enforcement training programs relevant to counterterrorism
- Academic programs in security studies, criminology, or intelligence
- Specialized skills courses (language training, technical certifications, etc.)
- Inter-agency exchange programs

Requests for external training are submitted to the Training Division. Approval is based on relevance, availability, and budget. SACTF typically funds travel and tuition for approved external training.

### 5.3 After-Action Learning Integration

The Training Division reviews all significant operational AARs and integrates lessons learned into the training curriculum. This process ensures that the training program evolves with operational experience rather than remaining static.

## Part VI: Training Documentation

### 6.1 Training Records

All training completed by SACTF personnel is documented in the SACTF Training Records System. Records include:
- Course name and content
- Date completed
- Pass/fail result for qualifying courses
- Instructor certification or course completion documentation

Training records are maintained for the duration of personnel's SACTF assignment plus five years.

### 6.2 Qualification Currency

The Training Division maintains a qualification currency tracking system that identifies personnel whose qualifications are approaching expiration. Supervisors receive 30-day and 7-day advance notice for any personnel under their supervision with expiring qualifications.

Personnel whose qualifications lapse are placed in a restricted duty status — they may not perform the duty functions covered by the lapsed qualification — until the qualification is renewed.
