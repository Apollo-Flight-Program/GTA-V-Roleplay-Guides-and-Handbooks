## Foreword

Modern terrorist and extremist organizations depend on two arteries: information and money. The movement of data and the movement of funds are the skeletal structure of any organized threat network. Disrupt either, and the network begins to fail. Map both, and the network becomes fully understood.

Cyber and Financial Intelligence — CYBINT and FININT in operational shorthand — represents one of the most technically demanding and most consequential disciplines in the SACTF collection portfolio. Unlike physical surveillance, which requires personnel on the ground and produces results in real time, CYBINT and FININT often require weeks of quiet, methodical work that suddenly produces an insight that collapses an entire investigation into clarity.

This directive establishes SACTF's approach to both disciplines, their legal basis, their operational procedures, and their integration into the broader intelligence effort.

## Part I: Cyber Intelligence (CYBINT)

### 1.1 Definition and Scope

Cyber Intelligence is the collection, processing, and analysis of intelligence derived from digital environments — internet-based communications, network infrastructure, digital devices, and online behavioral patterns.

SACTF CYBINT encompasses:

- **Open Source Intelligence from digital environments (OSINT-D)** — Monitoring of publicly accessible digital spaces including websites, forums, social media, news services, and public records
- **Digital forensic examination** — Extraction and analysis of information from seized devices
- **Network analysis** — Mapping of communications infrastructure, IP relationships, and online identity clusters
- **Dark web monitoring** — Passive monitoring of anonymized networks for extremist activity, weapons and material procurement, and recruitment
- **Communications pattern analysis** — Analysis of metadata and communications patterns without content interception

### 1.2 Legal Framework for Cyber Collection

#### Open Source Collection (OSINT-D)
SACTF analysts may freely monitor any information that is publicly available without authorization — open social media profiles, public forums, news coverage, public records, and comparable sources. No warrant or court authorization is required.

**Caveat:** If a subject has set privacy settings on an account (private Instagram, closed Facebook group), accessing that information without the subject's consent requires a warrant or similar judicial authorization. "Open to the public" is the threshold for free monitoring.

#### Device Examination
Examination of a seized device — telephone, tablet, computer — requires:
- A valid search warrant specifically authorizing electronic device examination; **or**
- The subject's informed, voluntary written consent; **or**
- Exigent circumstances with documented justification

Analysts should note that **a general search warrant does not automatically authorize device examination.** The warrant must specifically identify electronic devices or categories of electronic information being sought.

#### Network Investigative Techniques
Monitoring of network traffic, use of investigative software that communicates with target devices, or any active probing of digital infrastructure requires:
- Court-authorized technical warrant; and
- SACTF Intelligence Division Director approval; and
- Legal Division review prior to any deployment

### 1.3 Open Source Intelligence — Digital Operations (OSINT-D)

#### 1.3.1 Collection Platforms

SACTF OSINT analysts monitor the following categories:

**Social Media and Public Forums**
Platforms used by extremist communities to recruit, radicalize, plan, and communicate. Analysts monitor:
- Public extremist-adjacent communities
- Accounts affiliated with known threat actors
- Event pages, group memberships, and organizational affiliations
- Imagery and geographic location metadata where accessible

**Dark Web and Anonymized Networks**
The dark web refers to internet content hosted on networks requiring specific software (e.g., anonymizing browsers) to access. This includes:
- Marketplaces trading weapons, materials, documents
- Forums discussing planned or desired attacks
- Recruitment pipelines for extremist organizations
- Communications boards for networks operating in anonymized environments

*Note:* SACTF analysts who monitor dark web environments are conducting **passive monitoring** — reading, recording, and reporting. Active participation (posting, responding, buying) requires Undercover Operations approval and documented legal authorization. See `07-Undercover-Operations.md`.

**Public Records and Database Resources**
- Property registration and ownership records
- Business registration and corporate filings
- Court records (civil, criminal, public filings)
- Publicly accessible financial disclosures
- News and media archives
- Public utility applications where accessible

#### 1.3.2 OSINT Collection Protocols

**Documentation Requirement**
Every OSINT collection event must be documented at the time of collection:
- Date and time of observation
- Platform and specific location (URL, forum name, etc.)
- Content (verbatim copy, screenshot, or descriptive summary)
- Analyst identity
- Any associated metadata captured (username, account identifiers, timestamp of original posting)

The most common OSINT failure in court is the inability to establish when and where information was observed. Real-time documentation prevents this.

**Screenshot and Preservation**
Digital content changes and disappears. Analysts must capture and preserve content at the time of observation:
- Screenshots must include the URL, timestamp, and any relevant context visible on screen
- Screenshots are date-stamped and logged in the case file
- Where possible, full-page captures are preferred over cropped images
- Archived copies (web archive services) should be created for significant online content

**Attribution and Identity Verification**
Online identities require verification before information is attributed to a known subject:
- A single username or profile should not be attributed to a known subject based solely on circumstantial similarity
- Multiple independent corroborating factors (photographs, personal detail references, cross-platform handle matching, geographic references, association with known associates) are required before a definitive attribution
- Analyst confidence in attribution is rated using the standard SACTF A-F / 1-6 reliability matrix (see `05-Intelligence-Operations.md`)

#### 1.3.3 Radicalization Indicators in Digital Monitoring

When monitoring an individual for radicalization, analysts track the following behavioral indicators:

**Content Consumption Shift**
- Change from mainstream to extremist content over time
- Increasing engagement with violent imagery or glorification of attacks
- Migration from open platforms to encrypted or anonymized communications

**Network Changes**
- Disengagement from prior associations
- New associations with known extremist accounts
- Creation of new accounts or abandonment of established accounts (operational security shift)

**Behavioral Signaling**
- Expression of intention to act
- Requests for information on acquiring materials, weapons, or targets
- "Farewell" behavior — disposing of property, resolving personal affairs
- Direct or implied threats

**Operational Indicators**
- Research into specific targets
- Communication with operational facilitators
- Material procurement activity
- Casing behavior (geographic research, travel to target areas)

### 1.4 Digital Forensics

#### 1.4.1 Purpose

Digital forensic examination produces intelligence value from seized devices. Primary objectives:
- Recovery of communications and plans
- Identification of associates and network members
- Documentation of transactions and procurement
- Recovery of preparatory materials

#### 1.4.2 Chain of Custody for Digital Evidence

Digital evidence is uniquely vulnerable to claims of tampering or contamination. Strict chain of custody and processing protocols are required.

**Seizure**
- Seized devices are photographed in place before any movement
- Device power state is noted (on/off/locked)
- Devices are immediately placed in Faraday bags at the point of seizure to prevent remote wipe
- No officer touches or activates a device other than the designated evidence handler

**Transport**
- All devices travel in Faraday bags until delivered to the lab
- Transport is documented with the evidence chain of custody form
- Devices are never left unattended in vehicles

**Examination**
- Examination is performed only on forensic copies, never on original devices
- Forensic imaging process is documented (tool used, hash values before and after)
- The original device remains in secure evidence storage throughout examination
- Hash verification confirms no alteration of the forensic image

**Reporting**
- Forensic examination reports document all methods used
- Findings are reported verbatim where text is involved; analyst interpretation is clearly labeled as interpretation
- Full device contents are logged, even if not directly relevant to the investigation (full content is visible to defense in discovery)

## Part II: Financial Intelligence (FININT)

### 2.1 Definition and Scope

Financial Intelligence is the collection, processing, and analysis of intelligence derived from financial records, transactions, and economic activity. In the counter-terrorism context, FININT encompasses:

- **Terrorism financing investigation** — Identifying how an organization raises, moves, and uses money
- **Asset mapping** — Identifying all financial assets and accounts associated with targets
- **Money laundering analysis** — Identifying the mechanisms used to conceal the origin of funds
- **Sanctions and designated entity research** — Determining whether subjects or their associates are subject to existing financial restrictions

### 2.2 Why Money Matters

Every organized terror network requires resources:
- Weapons and materiel procurement
- Transportation and logistics
- Safe houses and operational support
- Recruitment, payments to members
- Communications infrastructure
- Support for families of imprisoned or killed members

Follow the money, and you find the network. Disrupt the money, and the operation fails regardless of whether you can prove the target crime.

FININT is also often the most legally durable evidence. Financial records are objective, authenticated by institutions, and difficult to explain away. A wire transfer or withdrawal pattern may be more powerful in court than a witness.

### 2.3 Legal Framework for Financial Investigation

#### Authorized Financial Record Access

SACTF may access financial records through:

**Grand Jury Subpoena**
A subpoena issued by a grand jury compels a financial institution to produce records. This is the standard method for bank account records, transaction histories, and related documentation.

**Court-Authorized Warrant**
For specific financial records not producible by subpoena or requiring real-time access (live account monitoring).

**Voluntary Disclosure**
Financial institutions may voluntarily report suspicious activity (see Section 2.4 below). This information is usable without further process.

**National Security Letter (NSL) Equivalent**
In national security investigations, SACTF, in coordination with FIB, may use legal process equivalent to an NSL to obtain financial records without initial court disclosure to the target. NSL equivalents are subject to rigorous legal oversight and require Intelligence Division Director and Legal Division approval.

#### International Financial Records
SACTF does not have unilateral authority to compel foreign financial institutions. International financial investigation requires:
- FIB coordination
- Mutual Legal Assistance Treaty (MLAT) process through prosecutors
- Intelligence coordination with federal-level partners

### 2.4 Suspicious Activity Reporting

Financial institutions are required under law to file Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) when transactions exhibit indicators of illicit activity. SACTF analysts routinely review SARs associated with investigation targets.

**Primary SAR Indicators Relevant to Terrorism Financing**

| Indicator | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| Structured Transactions | Multiple transactions just below reporting thresholds ("structuring") |
| Cash-Intensive Activity | Large cash transactions inconsistent with stated business or lifestyle |
| Third-Party Funding | Receipt of funds from parties with no apparent relationship |
| Charitable Funneling | Donations to organizations with suspected extremist ties |
| Informal Value Transfer | Hawala-type transfers bypassing formal financial system |
| Unexplained Foreign Transfers | Wire transfers to/from foreign accounts inconsistent with lifestyle |
| Dormant Account Activation | Long-dormant account suddenly active with large transactions |
| Shell Entity Transactions | Payments to or from entities with no apparent legitimate business activity |

### 2.5 Financial Network Mapping

Financial network analysis maps the flow of money through a threat network to identify:
- **Funders:** Who provides financial resources and from where
- **Facilitators:** Who moves money between nodes (money service businesses, individuals, shell companies)
- **Recipients:** Who receives funds and for what apparent purpose
- **End-use points:** What the money ultimately purchases (weapons, travel, materials)

#### 2.5.1 Mapping Methodology

**Step 1: Anchor Accounts**
Identify confirmed accounts associated with known subjects. These are the starting points of the financial map.

**Step 2: Transaction Analysis**
Obtain full transaction histories for anchor accounts. Categorize:
- Regular, explainable transactions (salary, rent, utilities)
- Irregular or unexplained transactions
- Cash transactions
- Transfers to/from other accounts

**Step 3: Branch Identification**
Every account that sends to or receives from an anchor account becomes a potential branch. Each branch is researched for its holder and purpose.

**Step 4: Link Expansion**
Repeat the process for identified branches, expanding the network map outward. Apply analytic judgment about which branches represent meaningful connections versus incidental transactions.

**Step 5: Pattern Analysis**
Assess the network for:
- Timing patterns (activity before or after known operational events)
- Geographic clustering
- Cyclic funding patterns suggesting regular operational support
- Anomalies suggesting concealment

**Step 6: Intelligence Product**
Produce a financial network assessment, including:
- Network map diagram
- Identified accounts, holders, and purposes
- Assessment of funding volumes and sources
- Analytic conclusions about network structure

### 2.6 Cryptocurrency and Alternative Finance

#### 2.6.1 The Challenge of Cryptocurrency

Cryptocurrency — digital currency operating on distributed ledger systems — presents investigative challenges because:
- Many cryptocurrencies offer partial or complete anonymity
- Transactions are irreversible
- Exchanges may be located in jurisdictions outside SACTF reach
- Conversion between currencies and to cash may be difficult to trace

#### 2.6.2 Cryptocurrency Investigation Techniques

Despite these challenges, cryptocurrency is not untraceable. FININT analysts use:

**Public Ledger Analysis**
Most cryptocurrency transactions are recorded on a public ledger (blockchain). SACTF analysts use specialized blockchain analysis tools to:
- Trace transaction histories from a known address
- Identify clusters of addresses belonging to the same controller
- Map the flow of funds from source wallets through intermediaries to destination

**Exchange Records**
Most users eventually convert cryptocurrency to cash through exchanges. Court process can compel registered exchanges to produce:
- Account registration information
- Transaction records
- KYC (Know Your Customer) documentation
- IP address logs

**Mixing Service Detection**
"Mixers" or "tumblers" are services that obscure cryptocurrency origins by pooling and redistributing coins. The use of a mixer is itself an indicator of intent to conceal, and mixer usage can be identified through blockchain analysis.

**Wallet Seizure**
A seized device may contain cryptocurrency wallet software or private keys. This requires:
- Device forensic examination (see 1.4)
- Cryptocurrency preservation specialist consultation to avoid accidental transaction

#### 2.6.3 Other Alternative Finance

**Informal Value Transfer Systems**
Hawala and similar systems transfer value through trusted broker networks without formal financial paper trail. These systems are investigated through:
- Human sources inside hawala communities
- Physical surveillance of brokers
- Documented patterns of associated cash activity

**Precious Metals and Physical Commodities**
Some networks store and transfer value through gold, jewelry, and physical commodities that avoid financial system monitoring. Investigation includes surveillance, source reporting, and tracking of related procurement activity.

## Part III: CYBINT/FININT Integration

### 3.1 Why Integration Matters

CYBINT and FININT are most powerful when analyzed together. Patterns that appear ambiguous in one domain often become clear when cross-referenced with the other:

- A suspicious financial transfer that aligns with a series of encrypted communications — both previously unexplained — suddenly becomes a logistics payment
- A new online username that appears in extremist forums, cross-referenced with a new phone number purchased in the same location, confirms that a suspected individual has created a new operational profile
- Dark web procurement of materials, correlated with a recent large cash withdrawal, establishes that the subject is in an active preparation phase

### 3.2 Integrated Analysis Protocol

When CYBINT and FININT collection are both active on a subject:
1. All collection feeds to the IAC Cell Chief
2. A designated analyst is responsible for cross-domain correlation
3. Intelligence summaries include a "Correlation Notes" section where analysts flag potential cross-domain connections
4. Major correlations are briefed to the Case Agent immediately, regardless of reporting cycle

### 3.3 Indicators of Operational Preparation

The integration of CYBINT and FININT produces a set of consolidated indicators that suggest a subject is entering an operational (attack-preparation) phase:

| Domain | Indicator |
|--------|-----------|
| CYBINT | Communications security increase (move to encrypted apps) |
| CYBINT | Research into specific targets (online searches, mapping) |
| FININT | Large unexplained cash withdrawal |
| FININT | Procurement payments (new or unusual) |
| CYBINT | Farewell or testament-type online posts |
| FININT | Disposition of assets or accounts |
| CYBINT | Contact with known operational facilitators |
| FININT | Funding receipts with no legitimate explanation |

Two or more of these indicators appearing simultaneously substantially elevates threat assessment and may justify escalation to active surveillance or pre-arrest consultation with the Legal Division.

## Part IV: Intelligence Products — CYBINT/FININT

### 4.1 Standard CYBINT Products

**Digital Profile Summary**
A synthesis of all collected CYBINT on a subject. Includes: online identities, platforms used, communications patterns, network associations, content consumption, and analyst assessment.

**Radicalization Assessment**
An assessment of whether and to what degree a subject exhibits indicators of radicalization. Includes behavioral trajectory analysis.

**Network Map — Online**
A visual and narrative mapping of the subject's online network — associates, organizations, platforms — with source documentation for each connection.

**Dark Web Activity Report**
Reporting on specific dark web activity relevant to an investigation, including marketplace activity, forum posts, or communications.

### 4.2 Standard FININT Products

**Financial Profile**
A comprehensive overview of all known financial accounts, assets, income sources, and liabilities associated with a subject.

**Transaction Pattern Analysis**
Analysis of financial transaction history identifying anomalies, unexplained activity, and patterns indicative of illicit finance.

**Financial Network Map**
Visual and narrative mapping of the financial network — accounts, flows, actors — with confidence assessments for each element.

**Terrorism Financing Assessment**
An analytic product specifically addressing the subject's role in terrorism financing: whether they are a funder, facilitator, or recipient; the estimated volumes involved; and the assessed purpose.

## Part V: Analyst Standards and Responsibilities

### 5.1 Handling Sensitive Technical Information

CYBINT and FININT collection often relies on capabilities that, if disclosed, would be permanently compromised. Analysts are required to:
- Not disclose technical collection methods in court filings without Legal Division consultation
- Not discuss technical methods with personnel not cleared for this information
- Route all court-related use of CYBINT/FININT evidence through the Legal Division for parallel construction guidance where applicable

### 5.2 Analytic Integrity

CYBINT and FININT analysis depends on strict analytic discipline:
- Distinguish clearly between fact and interpretation in all products
- Label assessments with confidence level
- Document all source materials
- Do not adjust analytical conclusions to match investigative preference
- Report information that contradicts prevailing theory alongside information that supports it

### 5.3 Consultation Requirements

CYBINT/FININT analysts consult the Legal Division before:
- Using any information from a source whose reliability or acquisition method may be challenged
- Producing an intelligence product that will be used in a warrant affidavit
- Any contact with external financial institutions or digital platforms
