## Part I: Crisis Incident Framework

### 1.1 Definition of a Crisis Incident

A crisis incident, for SACTF purposes, is any situation involving:
- A subject who has taken, is holding, or is threatening to harm one or more hostages
- A subject who has barricaded themselves and is presenting an ongoing threat to themselves or others
- An active assailant situation where the immediate threat cannot be resolved by standard patrol response
- A terrorism-related event involving a standoff, siege, or hostage-taking with terroristic dimensions

Not all crisis incidents involve terrorism. SACTF provides crisis response capability in the state; its involvement in non-terrorism crises is at the request of the jurisdictional authority.

### 1.2 The Two Tracks: Negotiation and Tactical

Crisis incidents are resolved through two parallel tracks that operate simultaneously:

**Negotiation Track:** The Crisis Negotiation Unit (CNU) engages the subject verbally, builds rapport, assesses the subject's psychological state and motivation, works toward voluntary compliance, and — in hostage situations — works toward the release of hostages without harm.

**Tactical Track:** The Tactical Operations Division maintains the perimeter, positions for an immediate assault if necessary, and prepares a tactical resolution option that can be executed at any moment. The tactical option is the last resort, available if negotiation fails or if the subject takes action that makes immediate tactical response necessary.

**Critical principle:** These tracks are parallel, not sequential. Negotiation is always occurring while tactical preparation is ongoing. The two element leaders — the Lead Negotiator and the TOD Team Leader — coordinate continuously. Each provides information to the other; neither unilaterally determines the crisis response.

### 1.3 Scene Command

**On-Scene Commander (OSC):** A designated senior SACTF officer who has overall command of the incident. The OSC:
- Makes the decision to initiate tactical action
- Approves significant commitments by the negotiation team
- Coordinates with law enforcement leadership outside the SACTF structure
- Manages logistics, resources, and support elements
- Maintains communication with SACTF headquarters

The OSC does not negotiate and does not directly command the tactical team. The OSC makes the big decisions; the Lead Negotiator and the TOD Team Leader execute within their domains.

## Part II: First Response and Perimeter Establishment

### 2.1 First Responding Officer Actions

SACTF TOD personnel are typically not first on scene to a crisis incident. The first responding units are LSPD patrol. First responders who establish initial containment perform a service that directly enables the SACTF response. LSPD first response protocol:

1. **Contain the scene.** Establish an initial perimeter that prevents the subject from exiting and prevents uninvolved parties from entering. The initial perimeter may not be ideal — it will be refined by SACTF when they arrive.

2. **Establish contact if possible and safe.** If the subject is reachable by voice without unsafe exposure, maintain basic communication — not negotiation, but basic human contact — until CNU arrives. "We hear you. We want to resolve this peacefully. Help is coming."

3. **Gather information.** Subject description, number of hostages if known, subject's location within the structure, weapons observed, subject's apparent emotional state. This information is relayed to arriving SACTF units immediately.

4. **Do not make promises.** First responders do not make commitments about charges, release, safe passage, or any other demand. All commitments in crisis incidents are made by the negotiation team, under specified authorization.

### 2.2 SACTF Arrival and Scene Transition

When SACTF TOD and CNU arrive:

1. **Tactical assessment:** TOD Team Leader receives briefing from first responders, assesses current perimeter, identifies tactical entry options, and positions TOD elements.

2. **Negotiation assessment:** Lead Negotiator receives briefing from first responders and any communications that have occurred, reviews any available information on the subject, and establishes communication.

3. **Command post establishment:** The OSC establishes a command post outside the inner perimeter in a location with good communications access.

4. **Relief of first responders from negotiation:** If first responders have established communication with the subject, the CNU Lead Negotiator takes primary communication responsibility. This transition is managed carefully — an abrupt change in voice can destabilize an ongoing communication.

5. **Intelligence request:** The IAC is tasked to develop all available intelligence on the subject — identity (if known), background, mental health history, criminal history, relationships, known concerns, any prior contacts with law enforcement.

## Part III: Negotiation Principles and Techniques

### 3.1 The Behavioral Change Staircase

Negotiation in crisis situations is not about argument or persuasion. It is about building a relationship that creates the conditions under which the subject makes a voluntary choice to comply. This process is understood through the Behavioral Change Staircase:

**Active Listening:** Foundation of everything else. The negotiator is not formulating the next argument while the subject speaks. The negotiator is genuinely listening and demonstrating that they are listening.

**Empathy:** The negotiator communicates understanding of the subject's situation and feelings. This does not mean agreement — it means acknowledgment. "I can hear that you're furious about what happened. That matters to me."

**Rapport:** Through active listening and empathy, a personal connection develops. The subject begins to experience the negotiator as a real person who understands them, rather than as an institutional adversary.

**Influence:** From a base of rapport, the negotiator begins to shape the subject's thinking — introducing considerations, building toward the idea that voluntary compliance is the path that serves the subject's own interests.

**Behavioral Change:** The subject makes the choice the negotiator has been working toward. They release a hostage. They come to the door. They put down the weapon.

This is not a one-way staircase. A subject can descend — from influence back to simple rapport, from rapport back to empathy work — if something disrupts the process. The negotiator must be attentive to these shifts and respond to them rather than rigidly continuing a script.

### 3.2 Active Listening Techniques

**Minimal encouragers:** Brief vocalizations that communicate attention — "mm-hmm," "I see," "yes" — without redirecting or interrupting.

**Paraphrasing:** Restating the subject's words back to them in slightly different form. Demonstrates listening; also creates an opportunity for the subject to correct any misunderstanding.

**Emotional labeling:** Identifying and naming the emotion the subject seems to be feeling. "It sounds like you're feeling betrayed." This demonstrates emotional attunement and often produces a meaningful response from the subject.

**Open-ended questions:** Questions that cannot be answered with a single word. "Help me understand what happened." "What's most important to you right now?" Open-ended questions give the subject the floor and produce information.

**"I" messages:** Statements about the negotiator's own reactions rather than about the subject's behavior. "I'm worried about you in there" is more connecting than "You're going to get hurt."

**Silence:** Sometimes the most powerful tool is to say nothing and allow the subject to fill the silence. Subjects under stress often volunteer information in the silence that they would not provide in response to a question.

### 3.3 Establishing Communication

**Initial contact:** The first words of a negotiation matter. The opening should:
- Introduce the negotiator by first name (not rank or organization, unless essential)
- Communicate the purpose: we want to resolve this peacefully
- Invite the subject to respond
- Be calm, unhurried, and genuine

**Communication channel:** Telephone communication is strongly preferred over shouted communication when feasible. A phone creates a more intimate, personal channel. If a throwphone (a dedicated negotiation handset that can be delivered to the subject) is available, it is preferable to a shared landline.

**Frequency:** Regular contact is maintained throughout the incident. Extended silence from the negotiator is experienced by subjects as abandonment. Even if there is nothing to discuss, periodic contact — "We're still here. How are you doing?" — maintains the relationship.

### 3.4 Demand Management

**Subjects in crisis make demands.** The negotiation team's approach to demands:

**Substantive demands (weapons, vehicles, release of other subjects, public statements):** These are never agreed to. The negotiation team acknowledges hearing the demand, responds with empathy to the underlying concern that the demand reflects, and works to redirect the subject's focus. "I hear that you want to leave the state. Tell me more about what you need to feel safe — let's see if we can work toward that."

**Basic needs demands (food, water, medication, phone calls to family):** These are often possible to meet and are used as negotiation tools — providing basic needs in exchange for small concessions (releasing a hostage, removing a weapon from view). Every concession from the subject, however small, is acknowledged and reciprocated. The pattern of reciprocal exchange is the foundation of a working negotiation.

**Never:** 
- Trade weapons for anything
- Promise safe passage or reduced charges without prosecutive authority authorization
- Lie about tactical readiness or intentions in ways that could endanger hostages or personnel if discovered

### 3.5 Behavioral Assessment

The CNU secondary negotiator (and the IAC intelligence analyst if assigned) conduct continuous behavioral assessment:

**Suicide risk indicators:** Expressions of hopelessness, reference to the situation as final, past suicide history, statements suggesting the subject does not expect to survive.

**Harm to others indicators:** Expressions of anger toward specific hostages, statements suggesting intent to harm regardless of outcome, sounds of physical conflict.

**Deception indicators:** Inconsistencies in what the subject says across conversations, information from other sources that contradicts the subject's account.

**Progress indicators:** Increasing engagement with the negotiator, reduced agitation over time, voluntary disclosure of information, requests that suggest future-orientation.

Behavioral assessment is reported to the OSC at regular intervals and immediately when significant changes occur.

## Part IV: Hostage Management

### 4.1 Intelligence on Hostages

The IAC develops intelligence on hostages where possible — identity, relationship to the subject, any relevant personal history, medical conditions. This information assists the negotiation team in understanding the dynamics of the situation and supports planning for hostage condition upon release.

### 4.2 Proof of Life

In kidnapping or extended hostage situations, proof of life — confirmation that hostages are alive — is an early negotiation objective. Proof of life may be:
- Visual (subject brings hostage to a window briefly)
- Verbal (hostage speaks to negotiators or family)
- Written (note from hostage with confirmed identifying information)

Every proof of life attempt is documented and evaluated for signs of coercion, injury, or distress.

### 4.3 Hostage Release Procedures

When the subject agrees to release a hostage:
1. Negotiator confirms the release, keeps subject talking
2. OSC notifies TOD that a release is occurring — TOD adjusts tactical posture to receive the hostage without creating a confusion/threat situation
3. Hostage exits through a designated path (negotiated with subject during release discussion)
4. Designated reception personnel guide hostage to medical assessment
5. Hostage is provided with immediate medical evaluation
6. After initial welfare check, hostage is interviewed by investigators — information from hostages is critical real-time intelligence

### 4.4 Hostage Medical Assessment

Every released hostage receives a medical assessment. In addition to physical injuries, medical personnel assess for signs of stress-induced physical effects. Hostages are not released to their families before medical assessment.

## Part V: Tactical Decision Point

### 5.1 When Tactical Action is Required

The On-Scene Commander authorizes tactical action when:

- **Imminent threat to life:** The subject takes action indicating immediate threat to hostages or other persons that cannot wait for negotiation to resolve — sounds of violence, visible preparation for harm, weapon discharge into hostage area.

- **Negotiation-created opportunity:** The negotiation process has produced a specific tactical opportunity — a hostage is in a known safe location, the subject's position is confirmed, the subject is distracted or incapacitated.

- **Negotiation conclusion:** Negotiations have been exhausted; the subject has demonstrated they will not comply voluntarily and the prolonged standoff is unsustainable.

**Tactical action is never taken** simply because the situation is frustrating or slow. Patience is a tactical asset. The OSC makes the tactical decision; it is not made by individual tactical operators.

### 5.2 Negotiator Role During Tactical Action

When tactical action is authorized:
- The negotiator continues communicating with the subject if safe to do so — maintaining the subject's attention and potentially preventing escalation
- The negotiator does not announce or hint at the tactical action
- The negotiator follows the communication plan specified for the transition to tactical action

## Part VI: Post-Incident Procedures

### 6.1 Immediate Post-Incident

- All subjects and hostages accounted for
- Medical assessment for all persons, including subjects
- Scene secured for evidence processing
- TOD weapons safed and debrief conducted
- CNU debrief — negotiators provide contemporaneous notes
- OSC conducts situational assessment and preliminary reporting to SACTF command

### 6.2 Negotiator Welfare

Crisis negotiations are psychologically demanding. Lead and secondary negotiators receive a psychological debrief following every significant incident. This is mandatory, not optional.

### 6.3 Full After-Action Review

Within 72 hours of incident conclusion, a full AARis conducted covering:
- Incident timeline and key decisions
- Negotiation track — what worked, what didn't
- Tactical track — what worked, what didn't
- Inter-element coordination
- Identified lessons
- Recommendations for training integration
