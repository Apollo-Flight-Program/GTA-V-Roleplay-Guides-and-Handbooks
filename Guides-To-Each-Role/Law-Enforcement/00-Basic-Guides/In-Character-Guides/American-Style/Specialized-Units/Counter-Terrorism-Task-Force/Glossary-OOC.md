# SACTF Out-of-Character (OOC) Roleplay Guide
## Counter-Terrorism Task Force — Player & Staff Reference

*This document is written for the players and server staff, not the characters. Everything here is OOC guidance for how to engage with CTTF/SACTF roleplay effectively, fairly, and in a way that creates great storytelling.*

---

## What Is CTTF Roleplay?

Counter-Terrorism Task Force (CTTF) roleplay — called SACTF on this server — is an **investigative and tactical law enforcement roleplay** track that operates differently from patrol cop or detective roleplay.

CTTF roleplay is:
- **Long-arc and investigative** — cases develop over days or weeks, not single sessions
- **Small-team and elite** — CTTF units are small (4–12 players), not large patrol departments
- **High-stakes** — when CTTF activates, it means something big is happening
- **Document-heavy** — warrants, affidavits, reports, and intel summaries are part of the fun
- **Collaborative** — CTTF players work with patrol, detective, and other divisions constantly

CTTF roleplay is *not*:
- A way to get "better guns" or "more authority" than patrol
- An excuse to bypass rules about consent or RP quality
- A free pass to run high-speed tactical operations without planning
- A solo pursuit — CTTF scenes require teams and coordination

---

## Why CTTF Roleplay Is Challenging

Most cops deal with robbery, traffic stops, and patrol calls. CTTF deals with **networks** — organized crime, extremist cells, trafficking operations, terrorism plots. The challenge is that:

1. **The story must build.** You don't arrest a terrorist cell on day one. You build intelligence, develop sources, obtain warrants, and run surveillance over time.
2. **You often can't show your hand.** CTTF operators sometimes watch crimes happen because intervening would blow the investigation.
3. **Legal standards matter more.** CTTF evidence ends up in very high-profile cases. A bad search ruins everything.
4. **Coordination is everything.** CTTF works with patrol, detectives, FIB equivalents, and outside agencies — roleplay with all of them matters.

## Roleplay Archetypes in CTTF

### The Investigator / Case Agent
- Builds the investigation from start to finish
- Manages informants, runs analysis, prepares warrants
- Best for players who enjoy procedural, detective-style roleplay
- Documents more than they shoot
- Key question: *"Do I have enough to move forward, and is my evidence clean?"*

### The Intelligence Analyst
- Processes and interprets intelligence
- Identifies patterns, maps networks, produces assessments
- Excellent for players who enjoy writing, puzzles, and background work
- Works behind the scenes but shapes the entire operation
- Key question: *"What does the pattern tell us, and what are we missing?"*

### The Undercover Operator
- Infiltrates criminal organizations using a false identity
- Lives and breathes the legend (assumed identity)
- Must balance the cover story while maintaining officer safety
- One of the most psychologically demanding RP roles
- Key question: *"Is this still safe, and am I maintaining cover?"*

### The Tactical Operator
- Executes warrant service, high-risk arrests, and active threat response
- Disciplined — does not kick in doors without authorization
- Physical but not reckless; process-driven even in dynamic situations
- Key question: *"Do I have the authority, and is the team ready?"*

### The Crisis Negotiator
- De-escalates barricades, hostage situations, and standoffs
- Patient — negotiations can take hours (or multiple sessions)
- Uses psychology and communication, not force
- Key question: *"What does this person need to feel safe enough to surrender?"*

### The Source Handler
- Manages informants and cooperating witnesses
- Builds trust while managing risk on both sides
- Responsible for source safety and operational security
- Key question: *"Is my source safe, and is the information reliable?"*

## How SACTF Operations Work In Roleplay

### Step 1: Intelligence Development
Someone identifies a potential threat. This could come from:
- A patrol officer who noticed something odd
- A tip from another player's character
- An informant passed to CTTF from another division
- OSINT gathered during an RP session

A case agent is assigned. Intelligence collection begins. This is all character work — phone calls, surveillance sessions, source meetings, document requests.

### Step 2: Case Building
Over multiple sessions, the case agent accumulates evidence. Each piece requires documentation:
- Surveillance reports (written during or after the session)
- Contact reports for informant meetings
- Intelligence summaries
- Database checks

### Step 3: Warrant / Authorization
To take action, CTTF needs judicial authorization. This means:
- Writing an affidavit (a written document or Discord submission describing the facts)
- Getting server-side approval (judge character, court system, or admin review depending on server rules)
- Not skipping this step — even if it slows things down

### Step 4: Operation
The planned tactical or investigative action. Entry, arrest, surveillance placement, undercover introduction, etc.

### Step 5: After-Action
Everything that happened gets documented. Evidence is logged. The AAR is written. The investigation continues or closes.

## Common OOC Questions

### "Can I join CTTF?"
CTTF is a whitelisted/application-based role on most servers. Check the server's application process. Generally, you need:
- Prior time as a patrol officer or detective
- Demonstrated RP quality
- Familiarity with basic LEO procedures
- Application, interview, or review by CTTF command

### "Does my CTTF character have more authority than patrol?"
In terms of arrest authority: largely yes, CTTF has statewide and federal-adjacent jurisdiction.
In terms of RP rules: **no.** CTTF players follow the same server RP rules as everyone else. CTTF doesn't allow you to bypass consent rules, fail RP, or be reckless.

### "How do I handle an undercover OP with other players?"
Before going undercover with other players (civilians, criminals), you must:
- Have OOC coordination with the players involved (or server staff, if NPCing is involved)
- Follow server rules on consent for undercover infiltration
- Never use OOC knowledge your character would not have
- Not use the cover to accumulate OOC advantages (e.g., learning player secrets you shouldn't know)

### "Do I have to write documents?"
Writing in-character documents (reports, affidavits, surveillance logs) is highly encouraged and considered premium RP. Most CTTF servers reward it. However, server staff determine what's required — check your server rules.

### "What happens when my undercover officer gets 'burned'?"
If your cover is blown:
- The character should be extracted (IC) and deactivated from the case
- The operation ends for that character's legend
- OOC, you and staff document it and determine next steps for the character
- This is a legitimate, interesting story outcome — not a failure

### "Can a CTTF player shoot at a suspect without warning?"
Only under the same rules as any other LEO character — plus CTTF-specific ROE. CTTF is *not* a license to use lethal force freely. Check server-specific rules, but expect the same or higher scrutiny on use-of-force decisions.

## CTTF RP Tips for Players

### For Investigators
- **Slow down.** The investigation is the story. The arrest is just the ending.
- **Talk to patrol.** The best cases start with a traffic stop someone told you about.
- **Build the file.** Writing up a surveillance log or intelligence summary between sessions makes the next session richer.
- **Use your sources.** A good CI relationship with another player is one of the best RP arcs available.

### For Tactical Operators
- **You are not a one-man army.** CTTF tactical operates in teams. If you find yourself clearing buildings alone, stop and ask if you should be doing this.
- **Plan before you act.** The OPORD (operations order) is the fun, not a formality.
- **Control yourself post-entry.** What happens after the shooting is often more important RP than the shooting itself.
- **Mentor patrol.** If patrol officers are at your scene, explain what's happening and bring them in.

### For Undercover Officers
- **Your character is paranoid.** Professionally trained undercovers operate with extreme awareness. React to what your character would notice.
- **Gradual trust.** Real infiltration takes time. Your character shouldn't be trusted on day one.
- **Check in OOC.** A quick message to the other player: "Hey, I'm running an undercover RP here, cool if I interact with your character?" prevents issues.
- **Know the exit.** Have an IC reason to withdraw from any scene if it gets too far.

### For Negotiators
- **Let scenes breathe.** A 15-minute negotiation scene is rushed. Good crisis RP takes time.
- **Use active listening language.** Words like "I hear that," "It sounds like you're feeling..." are genuinely good negotiation technique and make great RP.
- **Coordinate with tactical OOC.** The negotiator and tactical team leads should agree OOC on what the decision point is.
- **Let it fail sometimes.** Not every negotiation ends peacefully. Tragic outcomes are valid RP.

## Roleplay Scenarios and Ideas

### Scenario: The Long Game
**Setup:** An informant tells CTTF that a local organization is stockpiling weapons.
**RP arc:** Intelligence collection → source development → surveillance → warrant → entry → arrest/disruption
**What makes it great:** Every step creates scenes. The informant has their own story. The surveillance creates tension. The warrant application is a mini-arc. The entry is the climax.
**Duration:** This can run for 2–6 sessions.

### Scenario: The Burned Source
**Setup:** CTTF's primary informant inside an extremist cell is discovered.
**RP arc:** Emergency extraction → source debrief → harm assessment → operation to recover what was lost → new approach
**What makes it great:** High stakes, multiple departments involved, forces creative problem-solving
**Duration:** 1–2 sessions of intense RP

### Scenario: WMD Discovery
**Setup:** Patrol finds a suspicious package at a public event.
**RP arc:** Patrol secures → CTTF activates → EOD assesses → CBRN if confirmed → evacuation → investigation → threat network identification
**What makes it great:** Multi-department, high-intensity, multiple expertise areas all necessary
**Duration:** 1 session of acute response + follow-on investigation

### Scenario: Undercover Introduction
**Setup:** CTTF needs to get an officer inside a criminal organization to gather evidence.
**RP arc:** Legend development → initial contact → test assignment → escalating trust → collection → extraction
**What makes it great:** The criminal player characters know the UO is a cop, but their characters don't. Dramatic irony. Great tension.
**Duration:** Can run for many sessions

### Scenario: Hostage Standoff
**Setup:** Armed subject takes hostages demanding something — release of a prisoner, money, public announcement.
**RP arc:** CTTF activates → scene command established → negotiation begins → tactical prepares → decision point → resolution
**What makes it great:** Two tracks running simultaneously (negotiation and tactical) create dynamic pacing
**Duration:** Single session, 2–3 hours

### Scenario: The Rogue Operator
**Setup:** Evidence suggests a current or former CTTF member is compromised — leaking to criminals or directly involved.
**RP arc:** Internal investigation → surveillance of own team member → confrontation → disciplinary/legal outcome
**What makes it great:** Internal affairs story, creates genuine tension and moral complexity
**Duration:** 2–4 sessions

## For Server Staff: Running CTTF Events

### What CTTF Needs from Server Staff
1. **A court/warrant system.** Even simple (Discord DM to a staff member playing a judge) is better than nothing. CTTF RP falls flat when there's no warrant process.
2. **Opposition that investigates well.** Good CTTF RP requires criminal organization players who think ahead, leave evidence, and conduct themselves realistically.
3. **Session patience.** Don't rush CTTF investigations. Let them run for multiple sessions.
4. **Acknowledgment of documents.** When players write reports and intelligence summaries, recognize them — even just an IC acknowledgment or progression reward.

### Designing CTTF Events
A good CTTF event:
- **Has layers.** The first piece of evidence leads to the second, which raises new questions.
- **Has a credible threat.** The antagonist organization should have a plausible motive, method, and resource base.
- **Involves other departments.** CTTF shouldn't be operating in a vacuum.
- **Has no single solution.** Disruption, prosecution, and negotiation should all be viable approaches.

### Scaling for Server Size
| Server Size | CTTF Recommendation |
|---|---|
| 10–30 players | CTTF as a narrative event, not a standing division. Run as special scenarios. |
| 30–80 players | Small CTTF cell (2–4 officers). Focus on investigations, light on tactical. |
| 80–200 players | Full CTTF division possible. Separate investigators from operators. |
| 200–500 players | Full division with all specialties. Dedicated court/warrant system needed. |
| 500+ players | Multiple CTTF cells/squads, shift-based, with formal application and training pipeline. |

## OOC Glossary

**AAR** — After-Action Review. OOC: The debrief players and staff do after a major event to discuss what worked and what didn't.

**Affidavit (OOC context)** — A document a player writes (usually in Discord) to request server-side warrant approval. Should include: what they believe, why they believe it, what they want to search/seize.

**Burnout (RP)** — The real risk of playing high-intensity roles like CTTF for too long without variety. Long-arc RP can feel unrewarding if there's no resolution. Server staff should build in milestones and wins.

**Continuity** — The principle that events in one session carry forward to the next. CTTF depends on continuity. If a player was surveilled last session, their character should notice the consequences this session.

**CQC (OOC)** — Close-Quarters Combat. In RP, this means combat inside buildings at short range. Requires clear rules about combat RP, consent, and outcome.

**Dead Air** — OOC term for a lull in RP when nothing is happening. Long investigations can create dead air. Staff can inject new intelligence or tips to keep things moving.

**Downtime** — OOC term for periods between active scenes. CTTF players can use downtime to write reports, update intel files, and develop character backstory.

**ERP** — Emotional Roleplay. Important in CTTF — especially for undercover operators, negotiators, and investigators dealing with heavy material.

**Flowthrough** — The process by which information from one department reaches CTTF. A patrol officer reports something; it flows through to detectives, then to CTTF. This chain should happen in RP, not by skipping steps.

**God Mode / Powergaming** — Playing your character as invincible or forcing outcomes on other players. CTTF players are not immune to being caught, compromised, or killed.

**IC/OOC Separation** — The discipline of keeping in-character knowledge and out-of-character knowledge separate. Critical for undercover RP: you may know OOC that the criminal player is planning something, but your UC character doesn't.

**Lore Friendly** — Content that fits within the server's established fictional universe. CTTF documents should use the server's agency names (LSPD, BCSO, SASP, FIB) not real-world names.

**Meta-gaming** — Using OOC information in IC decisions. In CTTF context: if your character's informant texts you (OOC Discord) that a raid is coming, and your character reacts to that information — that's meta-gaming.

**Minmax** — Optimizing character build for mechanical power at the expense of story quality. CTTF is a story role, not a combat power role.

**NPC** — Non-Player Character. When a key character in a CTTF scenario (e.g., an informant, a judge, a witness) isn't being played by a real player, staff may NPC them.

**Off-Screen** — Events that happen between sessions, referenced in documentation but not directly played. CTTF cases accumulate off-screen developments (surveillance logs, database checks) that are documented even if not enacted in a live session.

**One-Shot** — A single-session scenario. Most CTTF RP is not one-shot; it builds over time.

**Operator (OOC)** — Term for a CTTF tactical player. Not to be confused with real-world military use.

**Permadeath (PD)** — When a character is killed and cannot be recovered. CTTF operations have genuine risk; some servers require permadeath consent before high-risk operations.

**Progression** — Character development over time. CTTF provides natural progression: promotion through ranks, specialization in a division, reputation built through successful cases.

**Soft Opening** — When a new CTTF division or event starts small before expanding. Recommended approach for smaller servers.

**Whitelist** — Server access restriction. CTTF is typically a whitelisted role, meaning players must apply and be approved before playing.
