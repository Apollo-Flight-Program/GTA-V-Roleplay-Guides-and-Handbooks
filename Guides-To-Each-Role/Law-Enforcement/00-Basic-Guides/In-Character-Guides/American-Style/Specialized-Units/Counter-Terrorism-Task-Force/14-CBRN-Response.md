## Part I: CBRN Threat Overview

### 1.1 Why CBRN Is Different

CBRN threats represent a category of hazard that is fundamentally different from conventional weapons in two respects. First, the harm they cause is not immediately visible — contamination, infection, and radiation exposure may be present without any observable sign. Second, responders who approach without proper protection become casualties themselves, removing the capacity to help others and potentially spreading the hazard.

Every SACTF officer must understand the foundational principle: **Do not approach. Recognize. Isolate. Deny entry. Notify specialists.**

Non-specialist SACTF personnel are not expected to operate in contaminated environments. They are expected to recognize the indicators of a CBRN event, take immediate protective actions, establish appropriate perimeters, and call in the right resources. Doing only that — and not doing more than that — is what the situation requires.

### 1.2 CBRN Attack Types

**Chemical:** Release of toxic chemical agents in quantities sufficient to cause casualties. Agents range from industrial chemicals (chlorine, ammonia) to purpose-synthesized nerve agents and blister agents. Chemical attacks produce rapid onset effects, are visible (or detectable by odor) in some cases, and affect concentrated populations in enclosed spaces most severely.

**Biological:** Release of pathogenic organisms (bacteria, viruses, toxins) designed to cause disease. Biological attacks may not be immediately apparent — symptoms may not appear for days or weeks after exposure. The initial indicator may be an unusual pattern of illness identified by public health systems.

**Radiological:** Dispersal of radioactive material ("dirty bomb") — typically a conventional explosive combined with radioactive material, designed to disperse contamination over an area. A radiological dispersal device is not a nuclear weapon and does not produce a nuclear explosion. The primary threat is contamination and long-term radiation exposure hazard.

**Nuclear:** Actual nuclear detonation or critical accident. The most catastrophic CBRN event. The law enforcement response to a nuclear event is primarily about post-detonation consequence management and perimeter control; prevention is the primary focus.

## Part II: Chemical Threats

### 2.1 Recognition

**Sensory indicators:**
- Unusual odors — certain agents have characteristic smells (nerve agents: sometimes reported as fruity or similar to almonds; mustard: garlic-like; chlorine: pool/bleach; hydrogen cyanide: bitter almonds)
- However: many toxic agents have no odor at all. Absence of odor does not mean absence of hazard.
- Unexplained liquid on surfaces, particularly oily or colored residue
- Unusual mist, vapor, or smoke not consistent with normal activity

**Victim indicators:**
- Multiple casualties with similar unexplained symptoms
- Symptoms: eye irritation, runny nose, excessive secretions, difficulty breathing, muscle twitching or seizures, loss of consciousness — symptom combination depends on agent type
- Casualties collapsing without apparent cause
- Casualties who tried to help first victims and then became victims themselves (characteristic of nerve agent incidents)

**Symptomatic profiles by agent class:**
| Agent Type | Key Symptoms |
|---|---|
| Nerve agents | Pinpoint pupils, runny nose, excessive secretions, muscle twitching, seizure, loss of consciousness |
| Blister agents (Mustard) | Delayed onset (hours); blistering skin, eye damage |
| Blood agents (Cyanide) | Rapid unconsciousness, cherry-red skin, rapid breathing then apnea |
| Choking agents (Chlorine/Phosgene) | Coughing, chest tightness, pulmonary edema (fluid in lungs) |

### 2.2 Immediate Response — Chemical

**Self-protection first:**
- Do not approach the hazard area without appropriate PPE
- Move upwind and uphill from the suspected release
- If you may have been exposed, do not touch your face; move to fresh air immediately

**If exposure has occurred:**
- Remove contaminated clothing (removing clothing removes approximately 80% of chemical contamination)
- Flush skin and eyes with large quantities of water
- Seek medical attention immediately

**Protective actions:**
- If indoors during a suspected outdoor release: shelter in place, seal air gaps, turn off HVAC
- If outdoors near suspected release: evacuate upwind at right angles to wind direction

**Evacuation zones:**
- Minimum initial isolation: 300 meters from suspected source
- Downwind evacuation: up to 1,000 meters in first 30 minutes; expand as situation develops

### 2.3 Notifications

Immediately upon recognition of a potential chemical incident:
1. LSFD HAZMAT requested
2. SACTF WMD Squad notified
3. Public health emergency notification (for mass casualty incidents)
4. FIB WMD coordinator notified if terrorism nexus

## Part III: Biological Threats

### 3.1 Recognition

Biological incidents are the hardest CBRN category to identify because:
- The initial incident is invisible — no observable release event
- Effects are delayed — symptoms appear days to weeks after exposure
- Initial presentations mimic common illness

**Primary detection pathway for biological attacks:** Public health surveillance — an unusual cluster of illness identified by emergency departments, hospitals, or epidemiological monitoring. Law enforcement is typically notified by public health, not the other way around.

**Visible release events:** Some biological incidents involve an observable release — a white powder in a letter, a suspicious aerosolization device, a container with unusual biological material.

**Indicators of white powder / suspicious letter:**
- Unexpected package or letter, particularly to a high-profile target
- Powder or granular material visible inside packaging
- Unusual odor
- Letter or package claiming to contain dangerous biological material

### 3.2 Immediate Response — Suspicious Powder / Package

**Do not disturb the item.** Do not shake it, open it, or sniff it.

**Isolate the area.** Anyone who has touched or been near the item remains in the area — do not allow them to spread potential contamination through the building.

**Evacuate the affected room.** All persons in the room move to an adjacent area (not through the entire building).

**Close doors.** Reduce air movement into and out of the affected room.

**Prevent hand-to-face contact.** Everyone who was in the affected area avoids touching their face until they have washed their hands.

**Wash hands.** Everyone who may have touched the item or been in the area washes hands thoroughly with soap and water.

**Notify:** Emergency services requested. Building management notified to shut down HVAC. SACTF WMD Squad and public health notified immediately.

**Do not use the fire alarm.** Triggering the fire alarm causes building evacuation — persons who may be contaminated then spread through the building and to the outdoors.

### 3.3 Biological Agent Response Priorities

**Containment:** Identifying who was exposed (population at risk) and preventing spread. Public health leads this function; SACTF supports with scene security and investigation.

**Medical countermeasures:** For certain biological agents, prophylactic treatment (antibiotics, antivirals) is available and effective if administered early. Public health coordinates distribution.

**Investigation:** Biological attacks require investigation to determine the source of the agent, the exposure pathway, and the criminal responsible. SACTF coordinates with public health to gather forensic samples from the incident scene.

## Part IV: Radiological Threats

### 4.1 Recognition

Radiation cannot be seen, smelled, or felt in amounts that do not produce immediate medical effects. The primary detection tool is a radiation detection meter.

**Physical indicators of a radiological incident:**
- Unexplained conventional explosion in an area associated with medical, industrial, or research facilities (these locations store radiological sources)
- Radiological material container or packaging found in unusual circumstances
- Electronic radiation detectors alarming in the absence of known source
- Sick individuals who were in a specific location — radiation sickness symptoms include nausea, vomiting, hair loss (symptoms are delayed hours to days)

**Dirty bomb (Radiological Dispersal Device):**
- A conventional explosion followed by a radiological hazard
- Responders approaching a blast scene where radiological material may have been used face combined blast and contamination hazard
- Any unusual explosion near a location associated with radiological material should trigger radiological precautions

### 4.2 Immediate Response — Radiological

**Distance and shielding:** Distance from the source reduces radiation dose dramatically. Moving away is the most important protective action. Dense materials (concrete, earth, water) provide shielding.

**Detection:** Personnel equipped with radiation meters sweep the area to confirm presence of elevated radiation.

**Evacuation:** Evacuate in all directions to minimum safe distance. Unlike chemical incidents (where wind direction matters), radiation exposure is dose-dependent and distance is the critical factor.

**Contamination control:** If individuals may be contaminated by radiological material:
- Do not transport contaminated individuals through non-contaminated areas without decontamination
- Decontamination: remove outer clothing, flush skin with water (same as chemical)
- Medical evaluation for all persons in the affected area

### 4.3 Nuclear Threat

**Pre-detonation:**
- Any credible intelligence about a nuclear device is the highest-priority threat possible
- Immediately escalated to the Director, FIB, and all federal nuclear response assets
- No SACTF field action for actual nuclear device until federal nuclear emergency response teams are on scene

**Post-detonation:**
- Blast zone (immediate detonation area): do not approach under any circumstances
- Fallout zone: determined by yield, altitude of detonation, and wind. Evacuation or shelter-in-place based on radiation levels and proximity to fallout
- Outer area: contain, support evacuation, facilitate medical response
- Federal consequence management agencies assume overall response coordination

## Part V: CBRN Scene Management

### 5.1 Hot Zone / Warm Zone / Cold Zone

CBRN incidents are managed using a three-zone approach:

**Hot Zone:** The contaminated area. Only properly equipped HAZMAT specialists operate in the hot zone. SACTF personnel do not enter the hot zone without HAZMAT-level PPE and specific authorization.

**Warm Zone:** The area where decontamination occurs and where personnel transitioning out of the hot zone are processed. Limited access.

**Cold Zone:** The safe area. SACTF command post, medical staging, and investigation are based in the cold zone.

Zones are established by HAZMAT personnel based on environmental assessment. SACTF respects zone designations and does not enter higher zones without authorization.

### 5.2 Personal Protective Equipment Levels

| Level | Protection | When Used |
|---|---|---|
| Level A | Fully encapsulating suit, SCBA | Highest chemical hazard; unknown agent |
| Level B | Chemical-resistant suit, SCBA | High hazard, less skin exposure risk |
| Level C | Chemical-resistant suit, air-purifying respirator | Identified agent; appropriate respirator available |
| Level D | Standard work clothes | No chemical exposure risk |

Non-specialist SACTF personnel do not typically have Level A or B equipment available. **This is why non-specialists do not enter the hazard zone.** The response is: recognize, isolate, deny entry, notify specialists.

### 5.3 Decontamination

Personnel who may have been exposed to CBRN hazards before the zone was established are decontaminated before receiving medical evaluation:

**Emergency decontamination:**
1. Remove all clothing (removes majority of contamination for all CBRN types except internal biological exposure)
2. Flush with large quantities of water
3. Move to medical assessment

**Technical decontamination (HAZMAT-conducted):** More thorough process conducted by HAZMAT personnel for confirmed exposures.

## Part VI: CBRN Investigation

### 6.1 Scene Processing Under CBRN Conditions

Evidence at a CBRN scene is processed only after:
- The scene has been declared safe by HAZMAT
- Evidence Response Team personnel are equipped with appropriate PPE
- A sampling protocol has been established by HAZMAT and forensic specialists

### 6.2 Forensic Samples

For deliberate CBRN releases, forensic samples establish:
- The specific agent (identification, concentration)
- Source characteristics (manufacture, origin indicators where determinable)
- Delivery mechanism
- Evidence of criminal intent

Samples are collected by qualified personnel under chain of custody protocols appropriate for both the hazardous material and the evidentiary requirements.

### 6.3 Attribution Investigation

Identifying who is responsible for a CBRN attack is a priority intelligence mission:
- The specific agent often reflects manufacturing capability that limits the suspect population
- The delivery mechanism reflects technical knowledge and construction capability
- Acquisition of the agent or its precursors leaves potential forensic traces
- Intelligence-developed information about organizations with the capability and intent is integrated from the outset
