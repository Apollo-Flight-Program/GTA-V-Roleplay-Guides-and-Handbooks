## Part I: The Value and Risk of Human Sources

### 1.1 Why Human Sources Matter

No technical collection system — no surveillance platform, no intercepted communication, no financial analysis tool — can provide what a human source inside a target organization can provide: direct access to the intentions, plans, capabilities, and internal dynamics of the threat.

A well-placed confidential informant can tell investigators what the target's leadership is thinking. They can provide advance warning of planned operations. They can identify unknown members of the network. They can provide context for technical collection that makes it interpretable. They are, in many respects, irreplaceable.

This value is precisely why human source management is one of the most carefully regulated, most closely supervised, and most ethically demanding aspects of SACTF operations.

### 1.2 The Risks

The same qualities that make human sources valuable make them dangerous:

**Information quality risk:** A source who tells investigators what they want to hear, rather than what is true, produces bad intelligence that can drive bad operations. A source who is themselves deceptive — feeding SACTF false information at the direction of the target organization — is an active threat to the investigation and to officer safety.

**Safety risk:** Sources who are discovered by target organizations face severe consequences. SACTF has a moral and practical obligation to protect sources from the risks that their cooperation creates.

**Legal and ethical risk:** Improper relationships with sources — unauthorized payments, unauthorized promises about prosecution, personal relationships that compromise objectivity, failure to control source criminal activity — create legal vulnerabilities and genuine harm.

**Entrapment risk:** A source who, at agent direction or on their own initiative, induces target individuals to commit crimes they would not otherwise commit creates entrapment defenses that can destroy cases.

## Part II: Source Categories

### 2.1 Confidential Informant (CI)

An individual, who is not a law enforcement officer, who provides information to SACTF on an ongoing, registered basis, typically in exchange for compensation or benefit.

**Key characteristics:**
- Registered in the SACTF source management system
- Has a designated handling agent
- Has a documented motivation for cooperating
- Is subject to regular evaluation of reliability and continued utility
- Has a defined relationship agreement specifying what the source may and may not do

### 2.2 Cooperating Defendant (CD)

An individual who has been charged with a criminal offense and who cooperates with SACTF investigations in exchange for prosecutive consideration. Cooperating defendants are managed jointly with the prosecutive authority.

**Special considerations:**
- Cooperating defendants have defense counsel; all contact with CDs must comply with attorney contact rules
- The terms of cooperation are set in a formal cooperation agreement, reviewed by the Legal Division
- Prosecutive consideration is promised by the prosecutor, not by SACTF agents; agents do not make promises about charges or sentences

### 2.3 Community Source

An individual who provides information on a non-systematic, non-compensated basis through a relationship with a SACTF investigator. Community sources are not formally registered as CIs but are documented.

### 2.4 Anonymous Tip

Information provided without source identification. Tips are logged, assessed for plausibility, and followed up through other collection means. Anonymous tips are not acted on directly — they are used as a pointer for further collection, not as the basis for operations.

## Part III: Source Development and Registration

### 3.1 Initial Assessment

When an investigator identifies a potential source, the following initial assessment is conducted before any source relationship is established:

**Motivation assessment:** Why is this person willing to provide information? Motivations include: financial compensation, personal grievance against the target, fear (coerced cooperation is ethically problematic and legally risky), genuine public safety concern, desire for prosecutive benefit, or seeking leverage in an unrelated matter. Understanding motivation is essential for assessing reliability and managing the relationship.

**Access assessment:** What access does this person actually have? A source who claims direct access to senior leadership of a target organization but whose story doesn't support that level of access is either lying about access or is being deliberately run by the target. Access must be evaluated critically.

**Background check:** The source's criminal history, known associations, and background are assessed. Prior relationships with law enforcement — have they worked with other agencies? What was their reliability? — are verified.

**Risk assessment:** What is the risk to the source if the cooperation is discovered? What is the risk to the investigation if the source is unreliable or working for the target?

### 3.2 Registration

No source relationship is formalized without registration in the SACTF source management system. Registration requires:

- Source biographical information (maintained in secure system)
- Unique source identifier (used instead of name in operational documentation)
- Motivation assessment
- Access assessment
- Risk assessment
- Signed source relationship agreement (reviewed by Legal Division)
- Supervisor approval
- Division Commander notification for high-risk or high-value source registrations

### 3.3 Source Relationship Agreement

All registered sources sign a source relationship agreement that specifies:

- The nature of the information the source will provide
- What the source is authorized to do in connection with SACTF operations (this is the "scope" — sources may not take independent action outside the defined scope)
- What the source is specifically prohibited from doing
- The compensation or benefit, if any
- The source's obligation to provide accurate information
- The SACTF's obligation to protect the source's identity to the extent lawful
- The conditions under which the relationship may be terminated

The agreement is explained to the source in plain language. The source's acknowledgment is documented. The agreement is maintained in the source management system.

## Part IV: Source Handling

### 4.1 The Handler Relationship

Every source is assigned a specific handling agent. The handler is responsible for:
- Regular contact with the source (frequency appropriate to source activity level)
- Receiving and documenting intelligence reports from the source
- Assessing source reliability on an ongoing basis
- Managing source safety — ensuring that source activity does not place them at unacceptable risk
- Ensuring source compliance with the scope of the relationship
- Escalating concerns about source reliability, safety, or conduct

### 4.2 Source Meetings

**Location:** Source meetings are conducted in discreet locations that do not expose the source to target observation. Meeting locations are varied — the same location repeatedly is a counter-surveillance vulnerability. Locations are pre-assessed for safety and observation risk.

**Two-agent rule:** Meetings with sources are conducted by two SACTF agents — the primary handler and a second agent. A single agent meeting alone with a source is never authorized. This protects both the source (from later claims of inducement or coercion) and the agent (from claims about what was said or promised).

**Documentation:** Every source meeting is documented in a contemporaneous report (Contact Report) that covers:
- Date, time, and location of meeting
- Agents present
- Source identifier
- Information provided by the source
- Tasking given to the source
- Payments or benefits provided
- Source's physical and emotional condition
- Handler's assessment of information reliability

Contact Reports are completed the same day as the meeting and filed in the source management system.

### 4.3 Source Tasking

Sources are tasked by the handling agent to collect specific information responding to current investigative requirements. Tasking must:
- Be within the scope of the source's authorized activity
- Not require the source to commit crimes
- Not place the source in unreasonable danger
- Be realistic given the source's actual access

Tasking is documented. Agents do not verbally task a source without documentation.

### 4.4 Payments and Benefits

All payments to registered sources are:
- Authorized by the Division Commander (up to threshold) or the Deputy Director (above threshold)
- Documented with the source's signature (under source identifier) acknowledging receipt
- Based on a payment schedule that is consistent across comparable sources
- Proportionate to the value and risk of the information provided

Agents do not promise payments or benefits that have not been authorized. Agents do not provide personal gifts or money outside the authorized payment system.

### 4.5 Controlling Source Criminal Activity

Sources who are members of criminal organizations may continue to engage in criminal activity as part of their normal role in the organization. SACTF has limited ability to prevent this. The rules governing what activity is and is not permitted are critical:

**Permitted with advance authorization:** Sources may participate in certain criminal activities that are part of their normal role in the target organization, where the activity is authorized in advance by the Division Commander and the Legal Division, and the authorization is documented.

**Never permitted:**
- Acts of violence against persons
- Recruitment of others into the criminal organization
- Providing weapons or materials for terrorist acts
- Actions that create immediate danger to uninvolved persons
- Criminal acts against other law enforcement personnel

If a source is about to engage in unauthorized criminal activity, the handling agent must take reasonable steps to stop it. If immediate contact with the source is not possible, the handling agent escalates to the supervisor immediately.

## Part V: Source Safety and Protection

### 5.1 Identity Protection

The source's identity is protected at the highest applicable level of security throughout the investigation and, where legally possible, after prosecution. SACTF goes to significant lengths to protect source identities in prosecution:

**Non-disclosure in charging documents:** To the extent legally permitted, charging documents do not identify cooperating witnesses.

**Pseudonyms in reports:** Source identifiers (not real names) are used in all operational documentation.

**Controlled disclosure:** In prosecution, the legal requirement to disclose the identity of witnesses who will testify is managed by coordinating testimony to minimize pre-trial disclosure, and by providing security support to identified witnesses.

**Source protection assessment:** Before the operation's conclusion, a source protection assessment is conducted — what is the risk to the source from the prosecution, and what measures are appropriate?

### 5.2 Physical Safety

If a source's physical safety is threatened as a result of their cooperation, SACTF:
- Provides temporary relocation if immediately necessary
- Coordinates with prosecution for formal witness protection consideration in appropriate cases
- Documents all threats and provides law enforcement protection response

SACTF does not make promises about witness protection that are outside its authority. Formal witness protection programs are administered by prosecutive and correctional authorities.

## Part VI: Source Evaluation and Termination

### 6.1 Ongoing Reliability Assessment

Source reliability is assessed on an ongoing basis:

**Accuracy verification:** Information provided by the source is verified through other collection means wherever possible. A source whose reports consistently cannot be corroborated is assessed as low reliability.

**Consistency:** The source provides consistent information — different meetings produce compatible, not contradictory, accounts.

**Access confirmation:** The source demonstrates access to the information they claim to have. They know things that a person with their claimed access would know, and they do not know things that someone without that access would know.

**Indicator of compromise:** The source is not being used to feed false information to SACTF. This is assessed by looking for consistent patterns of error — where the source is consistently wrong in ways that benefit the target organization.

### 6.2 Termination

Source relationships are terminated when:
- The investigative purpose has been achieved
- The source is no longer productive
- The source has provided false information (immediate termination; consideration of prosecution)
- The source has conducted unauthorized criminal activity (immediate termination; prosecution decision)
- The source's safety risk has become unacceptable
- The source requests termination

Termination procedures are documented. The source's system record is marked terminated with the reason. Sources who were cooperative and productive receive a formal acknowledgment of their contribution in their file (not communicated to the source, but maintained as part of the record).
