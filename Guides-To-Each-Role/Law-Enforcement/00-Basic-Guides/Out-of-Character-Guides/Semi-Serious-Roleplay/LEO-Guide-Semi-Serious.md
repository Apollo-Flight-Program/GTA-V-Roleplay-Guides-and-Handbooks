# LEO Guide — Semi-Serious Roleplay

Playing a police officer in a semi-serious RP server is a balancing act. You need enough realism to make interactions feel legitimate, but enough flexibility to keep things fun when civilians are not following a script. Semi-serious does not mean low quality — it means the server recognizes that not every player wants to spend forty-five minutes on a single traffic stop. The expectation is competence without perfection, immersion without rigidity.

This guide covers how to approach law enforcement RP in semi-serious environments — what is expected of you, how to handle common situations, and the mistakes that will get you removed from the department faster than a failed polygraph.

---

## Semi-Serious vs. Serious — Understanding the Difference

The line between semi-serious and serious RP is not about caring less. It is about the depth of simulation expected in every interaction.

| Aspect | Semi-Serious | Serious |
|---|---|---|
| Traffic stops | Identify yourself, state reason, run plates, issue citation or warning | Full approach protocol, body camera RP, detailed report, supervisor review |
| Radio comms | Mix of 10-codes and plain language, keep it clear | Strict 10-code or signal system, formal callsigns, structured format |
| Use of force | Reasonable escalation, common sense application | Documented force continuum, post-incident reports, IA review |
| Pursuits | Initiate, call it out, attempt to end it efficiently | Supervisor authorization, PIT policy, spike strip protocols, air unit coordination |
| Reports | Basic incident summaries, sometimes optional | Mandatory detailed reports for every significant interaction |
| Character depth | Consistent personality, basic backstory | Deep character history, emotional arcs, off-duty life RP |
| Uniform standards | Correct uniform, appropriate vehicle | Exact loadout, proper patches, vehicle inspected before shift |

**The key takeaway:** Semi-serious expects you to know what you are doing and play the role convincingly. You are not expected to have memorized every SOP or write a novel after each arrest. But you are expected to take the role seriously while you are in it.

## Getting Accepted into LEO

Most semi-serious servers still require an application or interview before handing you a badge. The bar is lower than serious servers, but it exists.

**What servers look for:**
- Basic understanding of law enforcement procedures — you do not need academy training, but you should know what Miranda rights are and when a traffic stop requires backup
- Server rule knowledge — especially rules about use of force, vehicle pursuits, and engagement
- Maturity — can you handle being disrespected IC without losing your temper OOC?
- Activity — are you going to log in regularly or disappear after three days?
- Prior RP experience — not always required, but it helps

**Common application mistakes:**
- Writing "I watched a lot of cop shows" as your qualification
- Saying you want to be LEO because you want to chase people and shoot
- Submitting a one-paragraph application when the form has ten questions
- Listing every server you have been banned from (or failing to disclose bans)

> **Tip:** Even in semi-serious servers, showing that you understand the *service* aspect of policing — helping people, de-escalating situations, protecting civilians — goes further than showcasing tactical knowledge.

## Your First Shift

You have been accepted. You have your uniform. You are sitting in a cruiser at Mission Row Station. Now what?

**Before you go 10-8 (in service):**
1. Know your callsign — typically assigned by dispatch or department structure (e.g., Lincoln-14, Adam-7)
2. Know your patrol zone — most servers divide Los Santos into sectors or beats
3. Know who is on shift — check the radio, identify the supervisor, know your backup
4. Know the server's current rules on pursuits, weapons, and engagement — these change more often than you think

**Your first calls will probably be:**
- Traffic stops along the Great Ocean Highway or Del Perro Freeway
- Noise complaints in Vinewood or Mirror Park
- Suspicious persons near convenience stores in Strawberry or Davis
- Stolen vehicle reports around the LS Customs near Burton

Do not try to be a hero on your first shift. Handle basic calls. Get comfortable with the radio. Learn the map. Build a reputation for showing up and doing solid work.

## Radio Communication

Semi-serious servers typically use a blend of 10-codes and plain language. The expectation is clarity over formality.

**Acceptable semi-serious radio example:**
> "Lincoln-12 to dispatch, I'm making a traffic stop on a red Dominator, northbound Innocence Boulevard near the gas station. One occupant visible. Plate is running now."

**What you should NOT sound like:**
> "Uhh, hey dispatch, I'm pulling over this guy... he's in a red car, I think? Somewhere near, uh, the city?"

**Common codes you should know:**

| Code | Meaning | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| 10-4 | Acknowledged | Confirming you received information |
| 10-8 | In service | Going on patrol, available for calls |
| 10-7 | Out of service | Taking a break, going off duty |
| 10-20 | Location | Requesting or providing your position |
| 10-80 | Pursuit in progress | When a vehicle flees from a stop |
| 10-99 | Officer emergency | Life-threatening situation, all units respond |
| Code 4 | Scene secure | No further assistance needed |

**Plain language is fine for:** describing suspects, explaining situations, coordinating tactics during active scenes.

**10-codes are expected for:** status updates, standard calls, and anything that needs to be communicated quickly and clearly.

## Traffic Stops — How Detailed Do They Need to Be?

Traffic stops are the bread and butter of patrol RP. In semi-serious, you do not need a twenty-minute production for every stop, but you need more than pulling someone over and typing "license and registration."

**A good semi-serious traffic stop:**

1. **Observe the violation** — speeding, running a red light, reckless driving, equipment violation
2. **Call it in** — notify dispatch with location, vehicle description, and plate if visible
3. **Approach the vehicle** — walk up, identify yourself, state the reason for the stop
4. **Request documents** — license, registration, insurance
5. **Run the information** — check for warrants, stolen vehicle, suspended license
6. **Make your decision** — warning, citation, or arrest based on what you find
7. **Close the stop** — release the driver or proceed with enforcement action

> `/me approaches the driver's side window, resting his left hand on the door frame.`
> "Afternoon. I'm Officer Mitchell with the LSPD. I pulled you over because you ran the red light back at the intersection of Vespucci Boulevard and San Andreas Avenue. Can I see your license and registration, please?"

**What you can skip in semi-serious:**
- You do not need to RP checking your body camera
- You do not need to write a detailed report for a simple citation
- You do not need supervisor approval for a warning

**What you should NOT skip:**
- Stating the reason for the stop
- Giving the driver a chance to respond and interact
- Running their information before deciding on enforcement action
- Being professional — even if the civilian is being difficult

## Handling Civilians Who Do Not RP Back

This is the reality of semi-serious servers. Not every civilian will give you quality RP. Some will comply silently. Some will be ridiculous. Some will ignore you entirely.

**Scenario responses:**

| Civilian Behavior | Your Response |
|---|---|
| Completely silent, does not respond | Give them time. Use /ooc to ask if they need help with the interaction. If still nothing, process them quickly and move on. |
| Immediately runs for no reason | Call the pursuit. Do not get frustrated. Some players just panic. |
| Gives you attitude IC | Stay in character. This is great RP. Play off it. |
| Clearly trolling (flying cars, impossible actions) | Do not engage in RP. Report via server admin tools. Document what happened. |
| Does not know RP mechanics | Help them OOC. Guide them through the interaction. Be patient. |
| Uses fail RP to escape (combat logging, exploiting) | Report it. Do not chase the mechanics. RP only works when both sides play fair. |

**Golden rule:** You cannot control the quality of other people's RP. You can only control yours. Stay professional, stay in character, and let admins handle rule-breakers.

## Dealing with RDM and VDM as an Officer

Random Death Match (RDM) and Vehicle Death Match (VDM) are common problems in semi-serious servers. As an officer, you are a frequent target.

**When you get RDMed:**
- Do not rage in OOC chat
- Use /report or the server's admin system to report the incident
- If the server requires you to RP injuries, do so — call for EMS
- Do not respawn and hunt the player down for revenge

**When you witness VDM:**
- Note the vehicle, the player, and the situation
- If it is clearly intentional (someone driving through a crowd for no reason), report it
- If it could be a legitimate RP scenario (someone fleeing police and hitting a pedestrian), treat it as an IC event

**As LEO, you are held to a higher standard.** If you retaliate against RDM with your own rule-breaking, you will be removed from the department. Server staff expect LEO players to be examples of good RP behavior.

## Game Mechanics vs. RP Mechanics

Semi-serious servers live in the gap between game mechanics and roleplay. Understanding where each applies is critical.

- **Lockpicking a car** — Use the game mechanic, but RP the action: `/me pulls out a slim jim and works it between the window and door seal`
- **Cuffing a suspect** — Use the cuff mechanic, but say something: "Turn around. Hands behind your back."
- **Searching a player** — Use the server's search mechanic, but RP it: `/me pats down the suspect, checking waistband, pockets, and ankles`
- **Spike strips** — Deploy the item and call it on radio: "Spikes are down on Route 68, eastbound lanes"
- **Tasing** — Use the taser mechanic, but RP the warning first: "Stop or you will be tased!"

The mechanic makes it happen. The RP makes it feel real. In semi-serious, you are expected to do both, but the RP can be brief rather than exhaustive.

## Common Scenarios and How to Handle Them

### Store Robbery
Respond to the alarm. Set a perimeter. Do not rush in alone. Coordinate with any other units on scene. Negotiate if possible. If the suspect exits, order them to the ground. Use force only if they are armed and threatening.

### High-Speed Pursuit
Call it out immediately. Provide direction of travel, vehicle description, and speed. Do not PIT on the highway unless authorized. Use spike strips at safe locations. If the pursuit enters Blaine County, coordinate with BCSO jurisdiction. End the pursuit if it becomes too dangerous for civilians.

### Shots Fired Call
Respond with backup. Do not go alone. Approach from cover. Identify the threat before engaging. If the shooter is down, secure the weapon before rendering aid. Call EMS. Preserve the scene for detectives.

### Domestic Disturbance
Respond with a partner. Separate the involved parties. Get both sides of the story independently. Do not take sides. If there is evidence of violence, make an arrest. If it is a verbal dispute, mediate and move on.

### DUI Checkpoint
Set up at locations like the intersection near Pillbox Medical Center or the Route 68 overpass. Have at least two officers. Stop vehicles at a reasonable interval. Ask for license and registration. If you suspect intoxication, conduct field sobriety RP.

## Uniform and Equipment Expectations

Semi-serious servers are generally flexible, but you should still look the part.

**Minimum expectations:**
- Correct department uniform (LSPD blue, BCSO tan, SASP gray — varies by server)
- Appropriate duty belt loadout — pistol, taser, flashlight, radio
- Marked patrol vehicle in proper livery
- Badge visible on character model

**What most servers do NOT require in semi-serious:**
- Specific boot types or undershirt colors
- Body camera equipment modeled on your character
- Exact real-world patch placement
- Vehicle inspection RP before every shift

## What NOT to Do

These are career-ending moves in any semi-serious LEO department:

1. **Do not go off duty and commit crimes on the same character** — most servers prohibit this
2. **Do not use your badge to win OOC arguments** — your rank means nothing outside of RP
3. **Do not shoot first in every situation** — you will be removed for excessive force
4. **Do not ignore dispatch** — if dispatch calls you, you respond
5. **Do not metagame** — knowing a player is a criminal OOC does not give you probable cause IC
6. **Do not solo everything** — call for backup. Coordinate. Work as a team.
7. **Do not take RP personally** — a civilian cursing at your character is not cursing at you
8. **Do not speed to every call with lights and sirens** — not every call is an emergency
9. **Do not arrest everyone for every minor offense** — use discretion. Warnings exist.
10. **Do not ignore internal department RP** — attend briefings, talk to your fellow officers, build relationships within the department

---

## Tips for Long-Term Success

- **Be consistent.** Show up regularly. The officers who last are the ones who are reliable.
- **Build relationships.** Get to know civilian characters, EMS players, and other officers. The best RP comes from recurring interactions.
- **Accept losses.** You will not win every pursuit. You will not catch every criminal. That is fine. The story matters more than the outcome.
- **Learn from mistakes.** If you mess up a procedure, ask a senior officer IC how to do it right. That is good RP and good learning.
- **Have fun.** If you are not enjoying it, step back. LEO RP is demanding. Take breaks when you need them.

Semi-serious LEO RP is about creating a believable law enforcement presence without turning every interaction into a documentary. Be professional, be fair, be human, and give every civilian the RP they deserve — even when they do not give it back.
