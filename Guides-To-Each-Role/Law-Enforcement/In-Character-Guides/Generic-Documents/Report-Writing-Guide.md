# Report Writing Guide — If It Is Not Written Down, It Did Not Happen

Reports are the backbone of every investigation, every prosecution, and every internal review in law enforcement. A well-written report turns a chaotic scene into a clear, factual account that can be understood by anyone who reads it — a detective picking up the case, a prosecutor preparing charges, a defence attorney looking for inconsistencies, or a judge reviewing the evidence. A poorly written report lets criminals go free, generates appeals, and exposes officers and departments to liability.

This guide covers the structure, style, and standards for law enforcement incident reports. It applies to any department, any jurisdiction, and any policing style. Whether you are documenting a traffic collision or a homicide, the principles are the same: be thorough, be factual, be clear.

---

## Why Reports Matter

Every time you respond to a call, make an arrest, use force, recover evidence, or conduct an investigation, a report is generated. That report may be read by:

- **Detectives** who are continuing the investigation
- **Prosecutors** who are deciding whether to file charges
- **Defence attorneys** who are looking for mistakes, contradictions, or procedural errors
- **Judges** who are evaluating warrants, bail decisions, and trial evidence
- **Internal affairs investigators** reviewing officer conduct
- **Other officers** who respond to the same location or suspect in the future

A report that is incomplete, inaccurate, or unclear undermines every person on that list. Write every report as if it will be read aloud in a courtroom — because it might be.

---

## Report Structure

Every incident report follows a standard structure. Individual departments may use slightly different forms or templates, but the core elements are universal.

| Section | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Header / Administrative Data** | Case number, date, time, location, reporting officer's name and badge number, department, type of incident, and classification. |
| **Involved Parties** | Full identification of all persons involved — suspects, victims, witnesses. Include names, dates of birth, physical descriptions, addresses, and contact information where available. |
| **Narrative** | The body of the report. A chronological, first-person account of what happened — from the officer's perspective. This is the most important section. |
| **Evidence** | A list of all physical evidence recovered, including descriptions, locations found, and chain-of-custody documentation. |
| **Disposition** | The current status of the case — arrest made, suspect at large, case referred to detectives, closed with no further action, etc. |
| **Supplemental Reports** | Any additional reports filed by other officers, follow-up reports by detectives, or updated information added after the initial report. |

---

## The Header

The header is the first thing anyone sees when they open the report. It must be accurate and complete.

> **Example — Report Header:**
>
> ```
> INCIDENT REPORT
>
> Case Number:        2024-04781
> Date of Incident:   15 March 2024
> Time of Incident:   Approximately 2245 hours
> Location:           400 block of Main Street, outside the liquor store
> Reporting Officer:  Officer J. Martinez, Badge #4471
> Department:         [Department Name]
> Incident Type:      Assault — Bodily Harm
> Report Date:        15 March 2024
> ```

Every field filled in. No blanks. No guesses — if the exact time is unknown, write "approximately." If the exact address is unknown, describe the location as precisely as possible.

---

## Involved Parties

Every person mentioned in the report is identified as clearly as possible.

| Role | Information Required |
|---|---|
| **Suspect** | Full name (if known), date of birth, physical description (gender, approximate age, height, build, hair colour, eye colour, distinguishing marks), clothing worn at the time of the incident, last known address. If the suspect is unidentified, provide the most detailed description available. |
| **Victim** | Full name, date of birth, contact information, relationship to the suspect (if any), injuries sustained, medical treatment received. |
| **Witness** | Full name, contact information, relationship to the incident, summary of what they reported seeing or hearing. |

> **Example — Suspect Description (Unknown Suspect):**
>
> ```
> Suspect: Unknown male, approximately 25-30 years of age, 5'10" to 6'0" tall,
> medium build, short dark hair, clean-shaven. Last seen wearing a dark-coloured
> hooded jumper, blue jeans, and dark trainers. Fled on foot heading eastbound
> through the alley behind the liquor store. No vehicle description available.
> ```

> **Example — Victim Description:**
>
> ```
> Victim: Michael Torres, DOB 14/07/1989. Male, 35 years of age.
> Address: 1245 Elm Street, Apt. 3B.
> Contact: (555) 472-3381.
> Injuries: Laceration above the left eye, approximately 2cm in length, actively
> bleeding upon officer arrival. Victim reported pain in the left jaw and ribs.
> EMS responded and transported the victim to the hospital for further evaluation.
> ```

## The Narrative — How to Write It

The narrative is the heart of the report. It is a chronological, factual account of the incident written in **first person, past tense**.

### Writing Style Rules

| Rule | Correct | Incorrect |
|---|---|---|
| **First person** | "I responded to the location." | "The reporting officer responded." |
| **Past tense** | "I observed the suspect fleeing on foot." | "I observe the suspect fleeing on foot." |
| **Factual, not opinion** | "The suspect's speech was slurred and he swayed while standing." | "The suspect was obviously drunk." |
| **Specific, not vague** | "I arrived at 2247 hours." | "I arrived shortly after." |
| **Plain language** | "I saw the suspect hit the victim." | "I perceived the suspect deliver a kinetic impact to the victim's facial region." |
| **No assumptions** | "The victim stated the suspect struck him." | "The suspect assaulted the victim." (Unless you witnessed it.) |

### Factual vs. Opinion

This distinction is critical. A report is a factual record. Your personal conclusions, assumptions, and opinions do not belong in the narrative unless they are clearly identified as such.

- **Fact:** "The suspect's eyes were bloodshot and watery. A moderate odour of alcohol emanated from his breath."
- **Opinion:** "The suspect was intoxicated." (This is a conclusion drawn from the facts. Present the facts and let the reader draw the conclusion, or clearly state: "Based on these observations, I formed the opinion that the suspect was impaired.")

### Timeline Construction

The narrative must follow a clear chronological timeline. The reader should be able to follow the sequence of events without confusion.

> **Example — Narrative:**
>
> ```
> On 15 March 2024, at approximately 2243 hours, I was on patrol in the
> 400 block of Main Street when Dispatch advised of a disturbance outside
> the liquor store at that location. The caller reported two males
> fighting, with one male possibly armed with a bottle.
>
> I arrived on scene at approximately 2247 hours. Upon arrival, I observed
> a male — later identified as the victim, Michael Torres — seated on
> the kerb in front of the liquor store. Torres was holding a cloth to
> his forehead, which was soaked with blood. A second male was not present
> upon my arrival.
>
> I approached Torres and identified myself. I asked Torres what happened.
> Torres stated that he had been standing outside the liquor store when
> an unknown male approached him and demanded his wallet. Torres stated
> that he refused, and the male struck him in the face with a closed fist,
> knocking him to the ground. Torres stated the male then kicked him in
> the ribs while he was on the ground before fleeing eastbound through
> the alley behind the store.
>
> I observed a laceration above Torres's left eye, approximately 2cm in
> length, actively bleeding. Torres reported pain in his left jaw and ribs.
> I requested EMS to the location.
>
> I checked the alley behind the liquor store but did not locate the
> suspect. I noted a security camera mounted on the rear wall of the
> adjacent business, pointed toward the alley. I documented the camera's
> location for follow-up by detectives.
>
> EMS arrived at approximately 2255 hours and transported Torres to the
> hospital for treatment. I obtained Torres's contact information and
> advised him that a detective would follow up regarding the investigation.
> ```

Notice the structure: dispatch information, arrival observations, witness statements (attributed with "stated that"), officer actions, evidence noted, and EMS response. Every piece of information is timestamped where possible. No jargon. No embellishment.

## Evidence Documentation

Every piece of evidence mentioned in the report must be logged with sufficient detail for identification and chain-of-custody tracking.

> **Example — Evidence Section:**
>
> ```
> EVIDENCE LOG
>
> Item 1: One (1) security camera recording from [business name],
>         rear-facing camera covering the alley behind the liquor store.
>         Recording requested for the period of 2230-2300 hours on
>         15 March 2024. Pending retrieval by detective unit.
>
> Item 2: One (1) blood-stained cloth, white, recovered from the scene
>         where the victim had been seated. Collected by Officer Martinez
>         at 2252 hours. Placed in a paper evidence bag, sealed, and
>         labelled with case number 2024-04781. Submitted to the evidence
>         locker — locker #17.
>
> Item 3: Photographs — twelve (12) digital photographs taken of the
>         scene, the victim's injuries, and the alley where the suspect
>         fled. Stored on department camera, memory card #09. Card
>         submitted to evidence with the case file.
> ```

## Supplemental Reports

When new information is obtained after the initial report is filed, a supplemental report is generated. Supplemental reports are linked to the original case number and document the additional findings.

> **Example — Supplemental Report Opening:**
>
> ```
> SUPPLEMENTAL REPORT
>
> Case Number:     2024-04781
> Original Report: Assault — Bodily Harm, 15 March 2024
> Supplement By:   Detective R. Okafor, Badge #2219
> Date:            17 March 2024
>
> This supplemental report documents the retrieval and review of security
> camera footage from [business name], and the subsequent identification
> of a suspect in connection with the assault on Michael Torres.
> ```

Supplemental reports follow the same writing standards as the original — first person, past tense, factual, chronological.

## Case Numbering

Every case receives a unique number when it is opened. The numbering system varies by department, but common formats include:

| Format | Example | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| Year-Sequence | 2024-04781 | The year followed by a sequential case number. |
| Department-Year-Sequence | PD-24-0781 | Department abbreviation, two-digit year, and sequence number. |
| Division-Year-Sequence | DET-2024-112 | Division code, full year, and sequence number. |

The case number appears on every document, every evidence tag, every supplemental report, and every court filing related to that case. It is the thread that ties everything together.

## Chain of Custody

The chain of custody documents every person who handles a piece of evidence from the moment it is collected to the moment it is presented in court or destroyed.

| Chain of Custody Entry | Information |
|---|---|
| **Date and Time** | When the evidence changed hands. |
| **From** | Name and badge number of the person releasing the evidence. |
| **To** | Name and badge number of the person receiving the evidence. |
| **Reason** | Why the transfer occurred — submitted to lab, returned from analysis, presented at trial, etc. |
| **Condition** | The condition of the evidence at the time of transfer — sealed, intact, opened for analysis, etc. |

A break in the chain of custody — an unaccounted transfer, a missing signature, an evidence bag that was opened and not resealed properly — compromises the evidence. Defence attorneys will challenge it, and judges may exclude it. Document every handoff.

## Common Report Writing Mistakes

| Mistake | Problem |
|---|---|
| **Writing in third person** | "The officer observed..." distances you from the report. You were there. Write "I observed." |
| **Using present tense** | The incident already happened. Write in past tense. |
| **Including opinions as facts** | "The suspect was lying" is an opinion. "The suspect's account contradicted the witness statement" is a fact. |
| **Being vague** | "I arrived and handled the situation" tells the reader nothing. What did you see? What did you do? What did people say? |
| **Omitting timestamps** | The reader needs to understand the sequence of events. Approximate times are better than no times. |
| **Leaving sections blank** | If a section does not apply, write "N/A" — not nothing. A blank section looks like an oversight. |
| **Waiting too long to write** | Write the report as soon as possible after the incident. Details fade from memory within hours. A report written the next day is a report with gaps. |

Write your reports with the understanding that they will outlast your memory of the event. Six months from now, when you are called to testify about this incident, your report will be the only record of what happened. Make it count.
