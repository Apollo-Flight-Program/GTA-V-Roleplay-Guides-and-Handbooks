# Interview and Interrogation Guide — Getting the Truth

The difference between a good investigation and a great one often comes down to what happens in the interview room. Physical evidence tells you what happened. Witnesses tell you what they saw. But the suspect — the person who was there, who made the decisions, who pulled the trigger or drove the car or broke the window — the suspect can tell you why. A skilled interviewer extracts that information through preparation, rapport, and technique, not through intimidation, threats, or coercion.

This guide covers interview and interrogation techniques for law enforcement roleplay. It applies to any department, any policing style, and any jurisdiction. Whether you are sitting across from a witness who is nervous about cooperating or a suspect who has lawyered up and is staring at the wall, the principles here will guide the conversation. The goal is always the same: obtain accurate, admissible information that advances the investigation.

---

## Interview vs. Interrogation

These terms are not interchangeable. They describe fundamentally different processes, and confusing them will compromise your investigation and your roleplay.

| Aspect | Interview | Interrogation |
|---|---|---|
| **Purpose** | Gather information. Learn what the person knows. | Obtain an admission or confession from a person believed to have committed a crime. |
| **Tone** | Conversational, non-confrontational, open. | More direct, focused, strategically confrontational. |
| **Subject** | Witnesses, victims, persons of interest, cooperative suspects. | Suspects against whom evidence already exists. |
| **Setting** | Can occur anywhere — the scene, a hospital, a person's home, the station. | Typically conducted in a controlled environment — the interview room at the station. |
| **Legal Requirements** | The subject is generally free to leave. Rights advisement may not be required unless the person is in custody. | The suspect must be advised of their rights before custodial interrogation. |
| **Approach** | Open-ended questions, active listening, patience. | Strategic questioning, presentation of evidence, psychological pressure within legal and ethical limits. |

A common mistake is treating a witness like a suspect or interrogating someone before you have enough evidence to justify it. Interview first. Build the case. Then, if the evidence points to a specific individual, transition to interrogation — with rights advisement and a clear strategy.

## Preparing for the Interview

Preparation is everything. Walking into an interview room without knowing the case inside and out is like walking into a courtroom without reading the file. You will miss opportunities, ask the wrong questions, and let critical details slip by.

**Before the interview, review:**

- All reports and statements filed to date
- Physical evidence recovered
- The subject's background — criminal history, known associates, prior contacts with law enforcement
- The timeline of events
- What you need to learn from this specific interview
- What information you are willing to share and what you are holding back

> `/me sits at the desk in the detective bureau, reviewing the case file for Case #2024-04781. Reads through the initial patrol report, the witness statement from the bartender, and the forensic report on the recovered shell casing. Notes three key questions that remain unanswered: where the suspect obtained the weapon, why the suspect was at the location, and whether anyone else was involved. Opens a blank notepad and writes the questions in order of priority.`

**Prepare the room:**

- The interview room should be private, quiet, and free from distractions.
- Remove unnecessary items. A cluttered room is a distracted room.
- Recording equipment — audio and video — should be tested and activated before the subject enters the room.
- Have water available. Small gestures of comfort build rapport.

## Rights Advisement

If the subject is in custody and you intend to ask questions about the crime, you must advise them of their rights before the interrogation begins. Failure to do so renders any statements obtained inadmissible.

> **Officer:** "Before we begin, I need to advise you of your rights. You have the right to remain silent. Anything you say may be used as evidence. You have the right to legal representation. If you cannot afford representation, one will be provided for you. Do you understand these rights?"
>
> `/me notes the suspect's response — verbal acknowledgment of understanding — on the interview form. Records the time: 1435 hours.`

If the suspect invokes their right to remain silent or requests a lawyer, the interrogation stops. Immediately. You do not continue asking questions, you do not try to change their mind, and you do not make statements designed to elicit a response. The invocation is absolute.

> **Suspect:** "I want a lawyer."
>
> **Officer:** "Understood. I'm stopping the interview now. We'll get you access to a phone to contact your legal representative."
>
> `/me stops the recording and notes on the interview form: "Interview terminated at 1437 hours. Suspect invoked right to legal counsel."`

## Building Rapport

People talk to people they trust — or at least people they feel comfortable with. Rapport is not manipulation; it is the creation of a human connection that makes the subject willing to communicate.

**Rapport-building techniques:**

- **Use their name.** "Michael, tell me what happened" is more personal than "Tell me what happened."
- **Start with easy topics.** Ask about their day, their job, their family — anything that is non-threatening and gets them talking. Once a person starts talking, it is psychologically harder to stop.
- **Mirror body language.** If the subject leans forward, lean forward slightly. If they cross their arms, adopt a similarly relaxed but closed posture. Mirroring creates subconscious familiarity.
- **Show empathy.** "That sounds like it was a really difficult situation" is not an endorsement of criminal behaviour — it is an acknowledgment of a human experience.
- **Offer comfort.** Water, a chance to use the facilities, an adjustment of room temperature. Small concessions demonstrate that you see the subject as a person.

> **Example — Building Rapport:**
>
> `/me enters the interview room carrying two cups of water. Places one in front of the suspect and sits across the table, setting the case file to the side — closed, not open. Makes eye contact and offers a slight nod.`
>
> **Officer:** "Thanks for sitting down with me. I know this isn't how anyone wants to spend their evening. Before we get into anything, do you need anything? Water's right there. Need to use the toilet?"
>
> **Suspect:** "No. I'm fine."
>
> **Officer:** "Alright. I'm [name]. I've been doing this a while, so I'll try to keep things straightforward. I'm not here to trick you or put words in your mouth. I just want to hear your side of things."
>
> `/me maintains a relaxed posture, hands visible on the table. Does not open the case file yet. Allows a brief silence to settle.`

## Questioning Techniques

### Open-Ended Questions

Open-ended questions encourage the subject to talk — to provide their narrative in their own words. These are your primary tool during interviews.

- "Tell me what happened last night."
- "Walk me through your evening, starting from when you left your house."
- "What did you see when you arrived at the location?"
- "How did you feel when that happened?"

Open-ended questions produce information you did not know to ask for. Let the subject talk. Do not interrupt. Take notes.

### Closed Questions

Closed questions are used to confirm specific details or pin down facts.

- "What time did you leave the bar?"
- "Was anyone else in the car with you?"
- "Did you see a weapon?"
- "Is this your jacket?"

Use closed questions to fill gaps after the subject has given their open narrative.

### Funnel Technique

Start broad and narrow down. Begin with open-ended questions to get the full picture, then use progressively more specific questions to clarify details and identify inconsistencies.

> **Officer:** "Tell me about last night." *(broad)*
>
> **Suspect:** "I went out with some friends. We went to a bar downtown. Had a few drinks. Came home."
>
> **Officer:** "Which bar did you go to?" *(narrowing)*
>
> **Suspect:** "The one on Main Street. I don't remember the name."
>
> **Officer:** "What time did you arrive at the bar?" *(specific)*
>
> **Suspect:** "Maybe nine, nine-thirty."
>
> **Officer:** "And what time did you leave?" *(specific)*
>
> **Suspect:** "I don't know. Midnight? Maybe later."
>
> **Officer:** "Who were you with?" *(specific)*
>
> **Suspect:** "Just my buddy Marcus."
>
> **Officer:** "Where was Marcus when you left the bar?" *(pinpointing)*

Each question drills deeper. By the end, you have a timeline, locations, names, and a story that can be corroborated or contradicted.

## Reading Body Language in RP

In a roleplay setting, body language is communicated through /me descriptions. Skilled interviewers use these cues to guide their questioning — and skilled roleplayers use them to add depth to the scene.

| Body Language Cue (/me) | Possible Interpretation | Officer Response |
|---|---|---|
| `/me fidgets with their hands under the table, avoiding eye contact.` | Nervousness, discomfort, possible deception. | Note the behaviour. Probe the topic that triggered it. |
| `/me leans back in the chair and crosses arms tightly across the chest.` | Defensiveness, withdrawal, resistance to the line of questioning. | Soften the approach. Return to rapport-building before pressing further. |
| `/me maintains steady eye contact and speaks calmly.` | Confidence. Could indicate truthfulness or a rehearsed story. | Test the details. Ask the same question in a different way later and compare answers. |
| `/me pauses for several seconds before answering, looking at the ceiling.` | Processing the question. Could be recalling the truth or constructing a lie. | Note the delay and the specific question that caused it. Follow up on that topic. |
| `/me taps the table rapidly with their fingers and shifts in the seat.` | Agitation, impatience, or anxiety about a specific topic. | Acknowledge the tension. "I can see this is bothering you. Let's talk about it." |

> **Example — Observing Body Language:**
>
> `/me watches the suspect carefully as the question about the weapon is asked. Notes that the suspect breaks eye contact for the first time in the interview, glancing down and to the left. The suspect's right hand moves to the back of their neck — a self-soothing gesture.`
>
> **Officer:** "Take your time. I can tell this part is hard to talk about. But I need you to be honest with me. Where is the weapon now?"

## Handling Silence

Silence is uncomfortable — and that is exactly why it works. After asking a difficult question, resist the urge to fill the silence. Let the suspect sit with the question. Many people will start talking simply to end the discomfort.

> **Officer:** "Michael, we found your fingerprints on the doorknob of the flat that was broken into. How do you explain that?"
>
> `/me leans back slightly and waits. Does not speak. Maintains calm, steady eye contact with the suspect. Ten seconds pass. Twenty seconds. The silence is deliberate.`
>
> `/me the suspect shifts uncomfortably, uncrosses and recrosses his arms, then finally speaks.`
>
> **Suspect:** "I was... I was there earlier. But not when it happened. I swear."

Silence gave you that. A follow-up question would have given the suspect an easy redirect. Let the pressure of the unanswered question do the work.

## Detecting Deception in RP Context

In roleplay, detecting deception is a collaborative storytelling exercise. The suspect's player may drop subtle hints through /me descriptions, inconsistent dialogue, or nervous behaviour. A good interviewing officer picks up on these cues and presses them — creating engaging, realistic scenes for both sides.

**Common deception indicators to roleplay:**

- Changing the subject or deflecting when a sensitive topic is raised
- Providing excessive detail on irrelevant points while being vague on critical ones
- Contradicting an earlier statement when the same question is rephrased
- Overuse of qualifiers — "honestly," "to tell you the truth," "I swear"
- Delayed or rehearsed-sounding responses to unexpected questions
- Volunteering information that was not asked for — attempting to control the narrative

> **Example — Catching an Inconsistency:**
>
> **Officer:** "Earlier you said you left the bar at midnight. But the bartender's statement says you were still there at one-thirty. Which is it?"
>
> `/me watches the suspect's reaction closely. Notes a brief flash of surprise — the suspect did not expect the officer to have the bartender's statement.`
>
> **Suspect:** "I... might have stayed a bit later than I thought. Time was hard to keep track of."
>
> **Officer:** "That's a ninety-minute difference, Michael. Help me understand."

## Confession Validity and Coercion Avoidance

A confession obtained through coercion — threats, physical force, prolonged deprivation of food or water, denial of legal representation, false promises of leniency, or psychological manipulation that overbears the suspect's will — is inadmissible and may constitute misconduct.

**Rules for valid confessions:**

| Requirement | Standard |
|---|---|
| **Voluntary** | The suspect must confess of their own free will. No threats, no promises, no physical or psychological coercion. |
| **Knowing** | The suspect must understand their rights and the consequences of waiving them. |
| **Intelligent** | The suspect must have the mental capacity to understand the situation. Consider age, mental health, intoxication level, and language barriers. |
| **Recorded** | The interview should be recorded — audio and video — to demonstrate the conditions under which the confession was obtained. |
| **Corroborated** | A confession alone is not sufficient. It must be corroborated by independent evidence — facts only the perpetrator would know, physical evidence, witness testimony. |

**What you must NOT do:**

- "Confess and I'll make sure the judge goes easy on you." — You cannot promise outcomes you do not control.
- "If you don't talk, things are going to get a lot worse for you." — Implied threats of additional punishment.
- "Your buddy already told us everything." — If untrue, this is a deceptive tactic that may render a confession involuntary depending on jurisdiction and context.
- Physically intimidate, strike, or restrain the suspect beyond what is necessary for security.
- Deny the suspect access to a lawyer after they have requested one.
- Continue questioning after the suspect has invoked their right to remain silent.

> **Example — Obtaining a Voluntary Statement:**
>
> **Officer:** "Michael, I'm going to be straight with you. The evidence we have is strong. But I want to hear your side. This is your chance to tell me what happened in your own words. Nobody is forcing you to talk — you can stop at any time. But if there are things that explain what happened, now is the time to tell me."
>
> `/me sits quietly, hands folded on the table, and waits for the suspect to respond. No pressure. No threats. No ultimatums. Just an open invitation.`

## Recording and Documentation

Every interview and interrogation must be documented.

| Documentation | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Audio/Video Recording** | Captures the entire interaction — tone, body language, and exact words. Protects both the officer and the suspect from false claims about what was said. |
| **Written Notes** | The interviewing officer's notes, taken during or immediately after the interview. Include key statements, observations about demeanour, and any inconsistencies noted. |
| **Interview Summary Report** | A formal written report summarising the interview — who was present, what rights were given, what questions were asked, and what information was obtained. Filed with the case. |
| **Witness Present** | In some departments, a second officer is present during interrogations as a witness. Their observations are documented separately. |

> `/me concludes the interview and stops the recording device. Notes the time on the interview form: "Interview concluded at 1523 hours. Duration: 48 minutes." Opens the notepad and immediately writes a summary of the key statements obtained, while the details are fresh.`

## Summary — The Interviewer's Mindset

A good interviewer is patient, prepared, and human. You are not there to win an argument or extract a confession at any cost. You are there to find the truth — and the truth does not always look the way you expected it to.

| Principle | Application |
|---|---|
| **Prepare thoroughly** | Know the case before you enter the room. Know what you need to learn. |
| **Build rapport** | The subject is more likely to talk to someone they feel comfortable with. |
| **Listen more than you speak** | The subject should be doing most of the talking. Every word you say is a word they are not saying. |
| **Be patient** | Silence is a tool. Rushing produces incomplete answers and missed opportunities. |
| **Respect rights** | A confession obtained through coercion is worthless — legally and ethically. |
| **Document everything** | Record it. Write it down. File it. If it is not documented, it did not happen. |
| **Stay ethical** | Your job is to find the truth, not to manufacture a confession. An innocent person who confesses under pressure is a greater injustice than a guilty person who walks free. |

The interview room is where cases are made or broken. Treat every interview with the preparation and respect it deserves, and the truth — whatever it is — will emerge.
