# Patrol Guide — The Foundation of Law Enforcement Roleplay

Patrol is where every law enforcement career begins, and for many officers, it is where they will spend the majority of their service. Patrol officers are the most visible representatives of any department. They are the first badge a citizen sees during the worst moment of their life, the first responder to a robbery in progress, and the officer standing on a street corner at two in the morning making sure the neighborhood is safe. Everything else — detectives, tactical units, traffic divisions — exists because patrol holds the line every single shift.

This guide covers general patrol operations in a way that applies to any law enforcement department, any rank structure, and any server style. Whether your department uses American terminology or British policing conventions, the fundamentals of effective patrol are universal. Know your beat. Know your equipment. Know the people you serve. And above all, stay alive long enough to go home at the end of your shift.

---

## Beginning of Shift

Every patrol shift starts before you ever leave the station. How you prepare in the first fifteen minutes determines how effective you will be for the next eight hours.

### Roll Call

Roll call is conducted by the shift supervisor at the start of every watch. Officers gather in the briefing room to receive critical information before hitting the streets.

| Roll Call Element | Details |
|---|---|
| **Attendance** | All officers assigned to the shift are present and accounted for. Late arrivals are noted and addressed by the supervisor. |
| **Assignments** | Patrol zones, beat assignments, and callsigns are distributed. Know your zone before you leave the room. |
| **BOLOs** | Be On the Lookout notices for wanted suspects, stolen vehicles, missing persons, and any active threats. Write these down — do not rely on memory. |
| **Briefing Notes** | Recent crime trends, ongoing investigations, community events, road closures, or special operations that may affect patrol. |
| **Equipment & Uniform Inspection** | The supervisor may conduct a visual inspection of uniforms, duty belts, and body armor. Present yourself professionally. |

> **Example — Roll Call:**
>
> **Shift Supervisor:** "Alright, listen up. We've got six units on the road tonight. Zone assignments are on the board. We've had a string of armed robberies hitting convenience stores along the main commercial corridor — three in the last week, same description each time: male, dark hoodie, bandana mask, handgun. Keep your eyes on gas stations and late-night shops. BOLO is posted. Additionally, there's a community event at the park in the central district tonight — expect increased foot traffic until around 2200. Questions? No? Kit up and get out there."

### Vehicle Inspection

Before you take a patrol vehicle onto the road, you inspect it. Every single shift. No exceptions.

> **Example — Vehicle Inspection:**
>
> `/me approaches the assigned patrol vehicle and begins a walk-around inspection. Checks all four tires for proper inflation and tread condition. Inspects the exterior for damage, noting any pre-existing dents or scratches on the inspection form. Opens the trunk to verify the presence of road flares, a first aid kit, traffic cones, and a spare tire.`
>
> `/me slides into the driver's seat and adjusts mirrors. Turns the ignition and checks the dashboard — fuel gauge reads three-quarters full, no warning lights active. Tests the emergency lights, cycling through red and blue, then activates the siren briefly to confirm function. Tests the spotlight and alley lights. Checks the in-car computer and confirms the radio is receiving on the primary dispatch channel.`
>
> `/me checks the rear seat and prisoner transport area for contraband, weapons, or items left by a previous detainee. Runs a hand along the seat and floor, finding nothing. Logs the vehicle's starting mileage on the shift log.`

If the vehicle has damage, low fuel, or a mechanical issue, report it immediately. Do not take a broken vehicle onto the road and hope it holds together during a pursuit.

### Equipment Check

Your duty belt and personal equipment must be inspected and accounted for before you step outside the station.

| Equipment Item | Check |
|---|---|
| **Firearm** | Loaded, holstered, retention strap secured. Function check at the start of every shift. |
| **Spare Magazines** | Full and properly seated in the magazine pouch. |
| **Radio** | Powered on, correct channel, battery fully charged. Carry a spare battery if available. |
| **Handcuffs** | Two pairs minimum. Hinged or chain-link — confirm the key is on your person. |
| **Body Armour** | Worn and properly fitted. Not optional. |
| **Torch / Flashlight** | Working. Carry a backup light. Dead batteries in a dark building get people hurt. |
| **Notebook & Pen** | You will write things down tonight. Be ready. |
| **Less-Lethal Options** | Taser charged, OC spray accessible, baton in its ring — whatever your department issues. |

### Radio Check

Before leaving the station, confirm your radio is functional.

> `/me keys the radio: "Dispatch, [callsign], radio check on primary."`
>
> **Dispatch:** "[Callsign], loud and clear. You are 10-8."

You are now in service.

## Patrol Methods

Patrol is not aimless driving. Effective patrol uses structured methods to maximize coverage, visibility, and crime deterrence.

### Random Patrol

Random patrol is the default method — moving through your assigned zone without a fixed route or pattern. The goal is unpredictability. Criminals study patrol patterns. If you drive the same route at the same time every night, the people you are trying to catch will simply wait until you pass.

Vary your route. Double back. Sit stationary in an unexpected location for ten minutes. Take side streets. The less predictable you are, the more effective your presence becomes.

### Directed Patrol

Directed patrol targets specific locations, times, or crime types based on intelligence and crime data. If the briefing noted a string of burglaries in the warehouse district between 0200 and 0400, directed patrol means you spend extra time in that area during those hours.

> `/me parks the patrol vehicle in a concealed position near the warehouse loading docks on the industrial strip. Dims the headlights and monitors the area, noting the time as 0215. Observes a white panel van parked near Warehouse 7 that was not present during the previous pass thirty minutes ago.`

### Community Patrol

Community patrol prioritises visibility and interaction. Walk through a neighbourhood. Talk to shop owners. Stop at a park and speak to residents. Community patrol builds trust, and trust produces information.

> `/me parks the vehicle and exits, walking into the commercial area on foot. Stops at a local shop and greets the owner through the door.`
>
> **Officer:** "Evening. How's business tonight? Any problems I should know about?"
>
> **Shop Owner:** "Couple of kids were hanging around the alley out back earlier. Didn't cause trouble, but it made my customers nervous."
>
> **Officer:** "I'll swing by and take a look. If they come back, call dispatch and we'll get someone out here."

That two-minute conversation might prevent a burglary. It might produce a witness to something that happened last week. At a minimum, it reminds the community that their officers are present and approachable.

## Responding to Calls for Service

When dispatch assigns you a call, your response is dictated by the priority level.

| Priority | Description | Response |
|---|---|---|
| **Priority 1 — Emergency** | Imminent threat to life. Active crimes in progress, shots fired, officer down, serious traffic collisions with injuries. | Immediate response, lights and sirens. All available units may be dispatched. |
| **Priority 2 — Urgent** | Crimes recently occurred, suspects may still be in the area, domestic disturbances, alarms at commercial properties. | Prompt response, lights and sirens as needed based on traffic conditions. |
| **Priority 3 — Routine** | Non-emergency reports, property crimes with no suspect on scene, noise complaints, civil matters, welfare checks. | Respond in due course without emergency equipment. |
| **Priority 4 — Low** | Follow-up reports, administrative tasks, non-urgent requests for contact. | Handle when available, no emergency response warranted. |

When responding to any call, obtain as much information from dispatch as possible before arriving. Who called? What is the nature of the incident? Are there weapons involved? How many people are on scene? Is the suspect still there? Information keeps you alive.

> **Example — Responding to a Call:**
>
> **Dispatch:** "[Callsign], respond to a reported disturbance at [location]. Caller reports two males fighting in the car park. One may have a knife. Priority 2."
>
> `/me acknowledges the call, activates emergency lights, and begins responding. Keys the radio: "Copy, Dispatch. Show me en route. Any further description on the individuals?"`
>
> **Dispatch:** "[Callsign], caller describes one male in a red jacket, second male in dark clothing. Caller is inside the building and unable to provide further detail."
>
> `/me approaches the location, slowing two blocks out and scanning the car park for the described individuals. Parks the vehicle at an angle to provide cover and exits, placing a hand near the duty weapon while assessing the scene from a distance.`

## Traffic Observations During Patrol

While on patrol, you will observe traffic violations constantly. Not every violation warrants a stop, but pattern recognition is key.

Watch for:
- Vehicles operating without headlights after dark
- Erratic driving patterns — swerving, abrupt lane changes, driving significantly below the speed limit
- Expired or missing registration plates
- Equipment violations — broken tail lights, cracked windscreens, excessive window tint
- Vehicles matching BOLO descriptions
- Drivers using mobile phones while operating a vehicle

When you decide to initiate a traffic stop, refer to the department's traffic stop procedures for proper technique.

## Foot Patrol and Building Checks

Foot patrol and building security checks are among the most important — and most frequently neglected — patrol duties.

### Foot Patrol

Get out of the car. Walk through alleys. Check behind businesses. Walk through parks and open areas. You see and hear things on foot that you will never notice from inside a vehicle moving at thirty miles per hour.

> `/me exits the vehicle at the edge of the commercial district and proceeds on foot through the alley running behind the row of shops. Checks door handles on rear entrances — all secure. Notes a ground-floor window slightly ajar at the accounting office, no lights visible inside. Shines the torch through the window and observes a tidy office, no signs of disturbance. Notes the open window on the patrol log for follow-up.`

### Building Checks

Building checks are conducted on commercial properties, particularly during overnight hours when businesses are closed. Rattling door handles, checking windows, and shining a light inside takes thirty seconds and may be the difference between catching a burglary in progress and finding out about it from the owner the next morning.

> `/me approaches the front door of the closed electronics shop. Tests the handle — locked. Shines the torch through the front glass, scanning the interior. Display cases appear intact. No signs of forced entry. Notes the check in the patrol log: "[Time] — Building check, [business name], all secure."`

## Situational Awareness and De-Escalation

Situational awareness is the single most important survival skill in law enforcement. Know what is happening around you at all times. Scan rooftops, alleys, windows, and vehicles. Watch people's hands — hands kill, not faces.

**Contact and cover principle:** When two officers respond to a scene, one officer makes contact (talks, conducts the investigation) while the other provides cover (watches the surroundings, monitors other individuals, maintains tactical readiness). Never let both officers become absorbed in the same task.

### De-Escalation During Patrol

Not every agitated person needs to be arrested. Not every argument needs to become a use-of-force incident. De-escalation is a tool, and skilled officers use it before the situation reaches the point of no return.

- **Use calm, measured speech.** Lower your volume. Speak slowly. An officer who is screaming commands at a confused, frightened person is escalating the situation, not controlling it.
- **Create space.** Physical distance creates time, and time allows thinking. Step back if it is safe to do so.
- **Acknowledge the person's emotional state.** "I can see you're upset. I want to help you, but I need you to talk to me." Validation is not agreement — it is a bridge to communication.
- **Offer options, not ultimatums.** "You can come with me voluntarily and we'll sort this out at the station, or I can place you under arrest right here. I'd rather do this the easy way."

> **Example — De-Escalation:**
>
> `/me approaches a visibly agitated male pacing in front of a block of flats, shouting and gesturing at the building. Stops approximately ten feet away and holds both hands visible at waist height.`
>
> **Officer:** "Hey. I'm [name] with [department]. I'm not here to cause you any problems. Can you tell me what's going on?"
>
> **Agitated Male:** "She locked me out! That's MY flat and she locked me out!"
>
> **Officer:** "That sounds frustrating. I understand you're upset. Let's talk about it. What's your name?"

That opening has accomplished three things: identification, rapport, and de-escalation through calm engagement. No commands were barked. No hands went to weapons. The officer took control of the situation through conversation.

## Break Procedures

Officers are entitled to meal and rest breaks during their shift. Breaks should be coordinated with dispatch and with other units to ensure zone coverage is maintained.

> `/me keys the radio: "Dispatch, [callsign], show me 10-7 for meal at [location]. Approximately thirty minutes."`

Remain available for emergency calls during your break. Keep your radio on and your vehicle accessible. If a Priority 1 call drops in your area during your lunch, the sandwich can wait.

---

## End of Shift

The last hour of your shift is not free time. End-of-shift responsibilities include:

1. **Complete all outstanding reports.** Do not leave paperwork for the next shift to finish. If you took the call, you write the report.
2. **Brief the incoming shift.** Pass along anything relevant — ongoing situations, BOLOs, problem areas, anything the next officer needs to know about your zone.
3. **Vehicle return.** Return the patrol vehicle in the same condition you received it. Refuel if below half a tank. Remove your personal equipment. Report any new damage.
4. **Log off.** Notify dispatch that you are going off duty.

> `/me keys the radio: "Dispatch, [callsign], show me 10-42, end of watch. All reports submitted. Vehicle returned to the yard."`
>
> **Dispatch:** "Copy, [callsign]. 10-42 logged at [time]. Good shift."

---

## Documentation

If it is not in writing, it did not happen. Every stop, every contact, every use of force, every call you respond to generates documentation. Write your reports before the details fade from memory. A report written four hours after the event is far more accurate than one written the next day.

**Daily patrol log:** Record your activities throughout the shift — traffic stops conducted, calls responded to, building checks performed, notable observations, and any citizen contacts. The patrol log is your shift in writing.

**Incident reports:** Any call that involves a crime, an arrest, a use of force, or a significant event requires a formal incident report. See the department's report-writing guidelines for format and submission requirements.

## Summary — The Officer's Patrol Checklist

| Phase | Key Actions |
|---|---|
| **Start of Shift** | Attend roll call, inspect vehicle, check equipment, radio check, go in-service. |
| **On Patrol** | Vary patrol routes, conduct community contacts, perform building checks, monitor traffic, respond to dispatched calls. |
| **Responding to Calls** | Gather information en route, approach tactically, assess before acting, request backup when needed. |
| **Throughout Shift** | Maintain situational awareness, use de-escalation, document everything, coordinate with other units. |
| **End of Shift** | Complete reports, brief incoming shift, return vehicle, log off with dispatch. |

Patrol is not glamorous. It does not come with a detective's intrigue or a tactical operator's adrenaline. But patrol is the foundation. Everything else in law enforcement is built on the work that patrol officers do every single shift, in every single zone, in every single city. Do it well.
