# Traffic Stop Guide — Conducting Vehicle Stops Properly

Traffic stops are the most common interaction between law enforcement and the public. They are also among the most dangerous. An officer standing beside a vehicle on the roadside is exposed, vulnerable, and operating in an uncontrolled environment where the occupants' intentions are unknown. Every traffic stop, from a broken tail light to a felony vehicle stop, must be conducted with precision, professionalism, and tactical awareness.

This guide provides a universal framework for traffic stops applicable to any law enforcement roleplay department regardless of style, jurisdiction, or rank structure. Whether you pull over a vehicle for a moving violation or initiate a high-risk stop on a fleeing suspect, the procedures outlined here will keep your character alive and your roleplay realistic.

---

## Initiating the Stop

Before you activate your emergency equipment, you should already have a plan.

**Pre-stop considerations:**

- **Identify the violation or reason for the stop.** You must be able to articulate why you are stopping this vehicle. "It looked suspicious" is not reasonable suspicion. "The vehicle failed to stop at a red traffic signal at the intersection of Main and Fifth" is.
- **Run the registration plate.** If possible, query the plate through your in-car computer or dispatch before initiating the stop. The result may change your approach — a stolen vehicle return turns a routine stop into a felony stop.
- **Choose your location.** Guide the vehicle to a safe stopping point. Well-lit areas, wide shoulders, and car parks are preferable. Avoid stopping vehicles on narrow roads, blind curves, hills, or bridges.
- **Notify dispatch.** Before you activate lights, radio your location, the vehicle description, and the plate number.

> `/me observes a dark-coloured saloon travelling eastbound on the main road with a broken rear offside tail light. Pulls behind the vehicle and queries the registration plate through the in-car computer. Confirms the vehicle is registered, no wants or warrants on the plate. Keys the radio.`
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "Dispatch, [callsign]. I'll be conducting a traffic stop eastbound on [road], near [landmark]. Dark-coloured saloon, registration [plate]. One occupant visible."
>
> `/me activates the emergency lights and gives a brief chirp of the siren to signal the driver to pull over.`

### Positioning Your Vehicle

Park your patrol vehicle offset — angled slightly to the left of the stopped vehicle. This creates a barrier between you and oncoming traffic and provides cover if the situation escalates. Leave your emergency lights activated to alert other motorists and to illuminate the scene.

At night, activate your spotlight and direct it at the driver's side mirror of the stopped vehicle. This blinds the driver to your approach while giving you illumination of the vehicle interior.

> `/me positions the patrol vehicle approximately one car length behind the stopped saloon, offset to the left. Leaves emergency lights active. Activates the spotlight, directing it toward the driver's side mirror. Pauses for a moment to observe the vehicle — checks for occupant movement, hands visible or reaching, any immediate signs of a threat.`

## The Approach

You have two options: driver-side approach and passenger-side approach. Both are valid; both have advantages.

| Approach | Advantages | Considerations |
|---|---|---|
| **Driver-Side** | Direct contact with the driver. Easier communication. Most commonly expected by civilians. | Exposes you to passing traffic. The driver can track your approach in the mirror. |
| **Passenger-Side** | Unexpected angle — driver is focused on the driver-side mirror. Provides a better view of the vehicle interior. Keeps you away from traffic. | Less common. May confuse the driver. Communication may be more difficult through the passenger window. |

Regardless of which side you choose, follow these principles:

1. **Touch the rear of the vehicle.** As you pass the boot of the car, place your hand briefly on the rear panel or boot lid. This leaves your fingerprints on the vehicle — a forensic link between you and this car should something happen to you during the stop.
2. **Watch the occupants' hands.** Hands are what will hurt you. If you cannot see hands, you cannot assess the threat.
3. **Maintain a position behind the driver.** Do not stand directly beside the driver's window. Stand slightly behind the B-pillar so the driver must turn to face you. This gives you a reaction advantage.

> `/me exits the patrol vehicle, adjusts the duty belt, and begins the approach along the driver's side. As passing the rear of the stopped vehicle, places a hand briefly on the boot lid. Continues forward, scanning the interior through the rear window — one occupant visible in the driver's seat, hands on the steering wheel. Stops just behind the driver's door, standing slightly offset from the B-pillar.`

## Initial Contact

Your first words set the tone for the entire interaction. Be professional. Be clear. Be human.

> **Officer:** "Good evening. I'm [name] with [department]. The reason I've stopped you tonight is that your nearside rear tail light is out. May I see your driving licence and vehicle registration, please?"

Note the structure: greeting, identification, reason for the stop, request for documents. This is not optional — the driver has a right to know who you are and why they have been stopped.

**What you are watching for during initial contact:**

- Nervousness disproportionate to the situation
- Fumbling, excessive movement, or reaching toward the glovebox or centre console without prompting
- The smell of alcohol or narcotics
- Visible contraband, weapons, or drug paraphernalia in plain view
- Conflicting statements or inability to answer basic questions
- Passengers who are overly quiet, overly talkative, or who avoid eye contact

> `/me observes the driver reaching toward the glovebox. Places a hand near the duty weapon — not drawing, but ready.`
>
> **Officer:** "Before you open that, could you tell me what you're reaching for?"
>
> **Driver:** "My registration. It's in the glovebox."
>
> **Officer:** "Go ahead — slowly, please."
>
> `/me watches the driver's hands as they open the glovebox and retrieve a registration document. No weapons or contraband visible inside the compartment.`

## Running Checks

Once you have the driver's documents, return to your patrol vehicle to verify the information.

> `/me returns to the patrol vehicle with the driver's licence and registration. Enters the licence details into the in-car computer and runs a check for wants, warrants, and licence status through dispatch.`
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "Dispatch, [callsign]. Running a licence check. [Provides driver's name and date of birth]. Standing by."
>
> **Dispatch:** "[Callsign], that licence comes back valid. No wants, no warrants. Registered owner matches the name on the vehicle registration."

| Check | What You Are Looking For |
|---|---|
| **Licence Status** | Valid, suspended, revoked, expired, or restricted. A suspended licence changes the nature of the stop. |
| **Wants and Warrants** | Outstanding arrest warrants, failure to appear, or other legal holds on the individual. |
| **Vehicle Registration** | Registered to the driver? Expired? Reported stolen? |
| **Insurance Status** | Valid insurance on the vehicle, if applicable to your jurisdiction. |
| **Criminal History** | Prior offences that may inform officer safety — previous weapons charges, violence, or flight risk. |

## Issuing a Citation vs. a Warning

This is a judgement call. Not every violation requires a citation, and not every driver deserves a warning.

**Consider:**

- The severity of the violation
- The driver's attitude and cooperation
- Whether the violation poses an immediate safety risk
- The driver's history — first offence or a pattern of non-compliance
- Department policy and enforcement priorities

> **Example — Issuing a Citation:**
>
> `/me returns to the driver's window with the citation book.`
>
> **Officer:** "Alright, I've written you a citation for the defective tail light. This is not a criminal matter — it's an equipment violation. You'll have [timeframe] to get it repaired. If you fix it and bring proof to [location], the fine may be waived. Sign here — your signature is not an admission of guilt; it's an acknowledgment that you received the citation."
>
> `/me hands the driver the citation and a pen. Waits for the signature, then provides the driver's copy.`

> **Example — Issuing a Warning:**
>
> **Officer:** "I'm going to let you off with a verbal warning tonight. Get that tail light fixed as soon as you can — it's a safety issue for you and other drivers. Drive safe."

## Handling Non-Compliance

Not everyone will comply with a lawful traffic stop. Your response must be measured and proportional.

**Driver refuses to stop:**
If a driver does not pull over after you activate emergency lights, give them reasonable time and distance. Some drivers may be looking for a safe place to stop. If the vehicle continues to flee after a reasonable period, the situation transitions to a pursuit — refer to your department's pursuit policy.

**Driver refuses to provide identification:**
Remain calm. Explain the legal requirement to produce identification during a lawful traffic stop. If the driver continues to refuse, a supervisor may need to be called to the scene. Forcibly removing a driver from a vehicle over an equipment violation is rarely proportionate.

> **Officer:** "I understand you're frustrated. However, you are legally required to identify yourself during a traffic stop. I'd like to resolve this without any further issues. Can we work together on this?"

**Driver becomes hostile or aggressive:**
Create distance. Return to your patrol vehicle if necessary. Request backup. Do not engage in a verbal confrontation. You are a professional — act like one, even when the other person does not.

## Felony / High-Risk Traffic Stops

When the vehicle or occupants return with indicators of serious criminal activity — stolen vehicle, armed suspect, felony warrant — the stop transitions to a high-risk procedure.

**High-risk stop procedure:**

1. **Request backup.** Do not conduct a felony stop alone.
2. **Position your vehicle farther back** than a routine stop — at least two car lengths. Use the vehicle as cover.
3. **Use the public address system.** All commands are given from behind cover through the PA or by shouting.
4. **Order the driver and passengers out one at a time.**

> **Example — Felony Stop:**
>
> `/me positions the patrol vehicle approximately two car lengths behind the stopped vehicle, angled for maximum cover. Draws the service weapon and takes a position behind the driver-side door, using it as a barricade. Activates the PA system.`
>
> **Officer (PA):** "Driver! Turn off the engine and drop the keys out of the window! Do it now!"
>
> `/do The driver complies, dropping a set of keys onto the roadway.`
>
> **Officer (PA):** "Driver! Place both hands out of the window where I can see them! With your left hand, open the door from the outside! Step out of the vehicle slowly! Face away from me and walk backwards toward the sound of my voice!"
>
> `/me watches the driver exit the vehicle with hands raised, walking backwards. Directs the driver to kneel, then to lie face down on the ground with arms extended. Waits for backup to arrive before approaching to handcuff.`
>
> `/me once backup is in position, approaches the prone driver. Holsters the weapon momentarily, retrieves handcuffs, and applies them behind the back — double-locked. Conducts a pat-down search for weapons.`

## Multiple Occupants

Multiple occupants multiply the risk. You cannot watch everyone simultaneously. Request a second unit any time a vehicle has more than one occupant and you have reason for heightened concern.

- Address passengers directly. "Everyone in the vehicle — keep your hands where I can see them."
- If a passenger is behaving suspiciously, ask them to step out of the vehicle and stand at a safe location where a cover officer can monitor them.
- Separate occupants for questioning if their stories need to be compared.

## DUI / Impairment Detection

If you suspect the driver is impaired, your observations at the window are critical evidence.

**Indicators of impairment:**

- Bloodshot or glassy eyes
- Slurred speech
- The odour of alcohol on the breath
- Delayed responses to questions
- Fumbling with documents
- Inability to maintain a fixed gaze

> `/me observes the driver fumbling with the licence, which is dropped once before being handed over. Notes a moderate smell of alcohol coming from the vehicle interior. The driver's eyes appear glassy and slightly unfocused.`
>
> **Officer:** "Have you had anything to drink tonight?"
>
> **Driver:** "Just a couple with dinner."
>
> **Officer:** "I'd like you to step out of the vehicle for me, please. Watch your step."

Field sobriety assessments and further impairment procedures follow department protocol and local law.

## Search Authority During a Traffic Stop

You cannot search a vehicle simply because you stopped it. Searches during traffic stops generally require one of the following:

| Authority | Description |
|---|---|
| **Consent** | The driver voluntarily agrees to a search. Consent can be withdrawn at any time. |
| **Plain View** | Contraband or evidence of a crime is visible without searching — a bag of drugs on the passenger seat, a firearm on the floorboard. |
| **Probable Cause** | Specific, articulable facts that lead a reasonable officer to believe the vehicle contains evidence of a crime — the strong smell of narcotics, for example. |
| **Search Incident to Arrest** | If the driver is arrested, the immediate area within the driver's reach may be searched for weapons and evidence. |
| **Inventory Search** | If the vehicle is being impounded, an inventory of its contents is conducted for liability purposes. |

> **Example — Requesting Consent:**
>
> **Officer:** "Based on what I'm observing, I'd like to ask your permission to search the vehicle. You have the right to refuse — this is entirely voluntary. Do you consent to a search?"

If the driver refuses consent and you do not have another legal basis, the search does not happen. Respect the refusal.

---

## Concluding the Stop

Once the purpose of the stop is complete — citation issued, warning given, documents returned — wrap it up professionally.

> **Officer:** "You're free to go. Drive safely tonight, and please get that light taken care of."
>
> `/me returns to the patrol vehicle and waits for the driver to pull away before deactivating emergency lights and returning to patrol. Keys the radio: "Dispatch, [callsign]. Traffic stop concluded. Back in service."`

Every traffic stop is an opportunity to demonstrate professionalism, build public trust, and practise the tactical skills that will keep you alive when a routine stop turns into the worst night of your career. Treat every stop like it matters — because it does.
