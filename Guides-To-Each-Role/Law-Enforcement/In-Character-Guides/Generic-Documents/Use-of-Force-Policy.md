# Use of Force Policy — Measured Response, Maximum Restraint

The authority to use force is the most serious responsibility given to any law enforcement officer. It is the power to physically compel compliance, to cause pain, to injure, and in the most extreme circumstances, to take a human life. Every officer who carries a badge and a weapon must understand when force is justified, how much force is appropriate, and what happens after force is used. There is no room for ambiguity.

This policy establishes a universal use-of-force framework applicable to any law enforcement department in a roleplay setting. It is not tied to any specific agency, rank structure, or policing style. The principles are universal: force must be reasonable, proportional, necessary, and documented. Officers who cannot exercise restraint under pressure have no business wearing the badge.

---

## The Use-of-Force Continuum

Force is not binary — it is not "no force" or "lethal force" with nothing in between. The use-of-force continuum is a structured escalation model that provides officers with a range of responses proportional to the threat they face. Officers should always attempt to resolve situations at the lowest level of force practicable, escalating only when the subject's resistance or threat level demands it.

| Level | Force Option | Subject Behaviour | Description |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | Officer Presence | Compliance / Cooperative | The mere presence of a uniformed officer is often enough to deter criminal activity or gain cooperation. No force is used. The officer's authority is communicated through their uniform, badge, marked vehicle, and professional demeanour. |
| **2** | Verbal Commands | Verbal Non-Compliance | The officer issues clear, direct verbal commands to direct the subject's behaviour. "Stop." "Put your hands where I can see them." "Step away from the vehicle." Commands are firm but not aggressive. |
| **3** | Soft Hand Techniques | Passive Resistance | The subject is not complying but is not actively fighting. Soft techniques include guiding holds, joint locks, escort positions, and controlled takedowns designed to gain compliance without striking the subject. |
| **4** | Hard Hand Techniques | Active Resistance | The subject is physically resisting — pulling away, pushing, bracing against officers. Hard techniques include strikes (open-hand, closed-fist), knee strikes, and takedowns intended to overcome active physical resistance. |
| **5** | Less-Lethal Weapons | Aggressive Resistance / Assault | The subject is assaulting the officer or others, or poses an imminent physical threat. Less-lethal options include conducted energy weapons (Taser), oleoresin capsicum spray (OC / pepper spray), impact weapons (baton), and specialty impact munitions (beanbag rounds, rubber bullets). |
| **6** | Lethal Force | Imminent Threat of Death or Serious Harm | The subject poses an immediate, unavoidable threat of death or serious bodily harm to the officer or another person. Lethal force — typically a firearm — is used as a last resort when no other option can reasonably stop the threat. |

**The continuum is not a ladder.** Officers do not need to start at Level 1 and climb sequentially through each level. If a subject draws a firearm, the officer does not need to attempt verbal commands, then a joint lock, then a Taser before responding with lethal force. The response must match the threat. However, officers must also be prepared to de-escalate — to step back down the continuum when the threat diminishes.

---

## De-Escalation at Every Level

De-escalation is not weakness. It is a tactical skill that resolves situations without the risks, injuries, and consequences that accompany force. At every level of the continuum, officers should consider whether de-escalation can reduce the need for force.

**De-escalation techniques:**

- **Create time and distance.** Step back. Put a barrier between you and the subject. Time is your ally — the longer a situation continues without violence, the more likely it resolves peacefully.
- **Communicate clearly and calmly.** Explain what you need the subject to do and why. "I need you to put the knife down so we can talk. Nobody needs to get hurt today."
- **Listen.** Sometimes a subject is escalating because they feel unheard. Acknowledge their frustration without surrendering your authority.
- **Offer choices.** "You can put the weapon down and walk out of here, or this is going to end in a way that nobody wants. The choice is yours."
- **Call for resources.** A crisis negotiator, a mental health professional, a supervisor — additional resources give you more options.

> **Example — De-Escalation Before Force:**
>
> `/me observes a male subject standing in the middle of the roadway, holding a kitchen knife at his side, shouting incoherently. Positions behind the open door of the patrol vehicle, approximately twenty feet from the subject. Does not draw a weapon. Raises both hands, palms open.`
>
> **Officer:** "Hey — I can see you're going through something right now. I'm not here to hurt you. I just need you to put the knife on the ground so we can talk. Can you do that for me?"
>
> `/me maintains distance, watching the subject's grip on the knife and body posture for signs of escalation or compliance. Keys the radio with the off-hand: "Dispatch, [callsign]. I've got a male with a knife, appears to be in crisis. Request a second unit and mental health resources if available."`

## Level 1 — Officer Presence

Sometimes showing up is enough. A marked patrol vehicle parked outside a location where a disturbance has been reported may resolve the situation before the officer ever exits the vehicle. A uniformed officer walking into a shop where two people are arguing often ends the argument simply by being there.

Officer presence is the lowest level of force and the most desirable outcome. No one is hurt. No reports are required beyond the standard call documentation. The situation is resolved through authority and visibility alone.

> `/me pulls the marked patrol vehicle to the kerb outside the address where a noise complaint has been called in. The music volume from the property drops noticeably within seconds of the vehicle stopping. The officer waits a moment, observing. The noise does not resume.`

---

## Level 2 — Verbal Commands

When presence alone is insufficient, officers use clear, authoritative verbal commands to direct the subject's behaviour.

**Effective verbal commands are:**

- **Clear:** "Stop. Do not move." Not "Hey, you should probably stop doing that."
- **Direct:** One instruction at a time. Do not overload a stressed subject with a sequence of five commands.
- **Firm but controlled:** Shouting creates panic. A raised voice can convey authority; screaming conveys a loss of control.
- **Repeated if necessary:** If the first command is not followed, repeat it. Give the subject time to process and comply.

> **Officer:** "Put your hands on top of your head. Interlace your fingers. Do it now."
>
> `/me watches the subject's hands. The subject hesitates, looking left and right, but slowly raises both hands and places them on top of their head.`
>
> **Officer:** "Good. Stay just like that. Don't move."

---

## Level 3 — Soft Hand Techniques

When a subject is passively resisting — refusing to move, going limp, pulling away without fighting — soft hand techniques are appropriate.

> **Example — Escort Hold:**
>
> `/me steps to the subject's left side and takes hold of the subject's left arm above the elbow with a firm grip. Places the right hand on the subject's shoulder blade and guides the subject forward toward the patrol vehicle using steady, controlled pressure. Does not strike or twist the arm.`

> **Example — Controlled Takedown:**
>
> `/me places a hand behind the subject's right arm and applies downward pressure on the shoulder while simultaneously sweeping the subject's right foot. Guides the subject to the ground in a controlled manner, keeping hold of the arm throughout the takedown to prevent the subject from landing on their face.`
>
> `/do The subject is brought to the ground without injury. They continue to resist passively by tensing their arms against their body.`

---

## Level 4 — Hard Hand Techniques

Active resistance — pushing officers, pulling away forcefully, bracing against walls or door frames, swinging arms — may justify hard hand techniques.

> **Example — Knee Strike:**
>
> `/me delivers a single knee strike to the subject's right thigh — a large muscle group — to disrupt the subject's balance and overcome resistance. Does not strike the head, neck, spine, or groin.`
>
> `/do The strike causes the subject to buckle slightly, allowing the officer to gain control of the right arm and bring it behind the subject's back.`

Hard hand techniques carry a higher risk of injury than soft techniques. They must be justified by the subject's level of resistance and must stop the moment compliance is achieved.

## Level 5 — Less-Lethal Weapons

When a subject is assaulting officers, threatening serious harm, or cannot be controlled through hands-on techniques, less-lethal weapons provide an intermediate option before lethal force.

### Conducted Energy Weapon (Taser)

| Consideration | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Effective Range** | Typically 15–25 feet depending on the model. |
| **Target Area** | Centre mass, below the neck. Avoid the face, head, neck, chest of thin individuals, and groin. |
| **Duration** | A standard cycle lasts five seconds. Additional cycles must be justified individually. |
| **Limitations** | Thick clothing may prevent probe contact. The subject may pull out probes. Effectiveness is not guaranteed. |
| **Contraindications** | Do not deploy near flammable substances. Use caution on elevated surfaces — an incapacitated subject will fall. Avoid use on visibly pregnant individuals, elderly persons, or small children except in extreme circumstances. |

> `/me draws the Taser from the duty belt and points it at the advancing subject, aiming at centre mass.`
>
> **Officer:** "Stop! Taser! Taser! Taser!"
>
> `/me deploys the Taser. Both probes make contact with the subject's torso. The subject's muscles lock and they fall to the ground, incapacitated for the duration of the five-second cycle.`
>
> `/me immediately moves in with a second officer to secure the subject in handcuffs while the Taser cycle completes. Once cuffed, the Taser probes are left in place for medical personnel to remove.`

### OC Spray (Pepper Spray)

Effective for subjects who are combative at close range. Deploy in short bursts directed at the subject's face. Be prepared for cross-contamination — wind and enclosed spaces affect the officer as well.

> `/me draws the OC canister and deploys a one-second burst toward the subject's face from approximately four feet away.`
>
> `/do The subject recoils, hands going to their eyes. They stop advancing and begin coughing.`
>
> **Officer:** "Get on the ground! Hands behind your back!"

### Impact Weapons (Baton / Expandable Baton)

Target large muscle groups — thighs, upper arms, calves. Never strike the head, neck, spine, collarbone, or joints unless lethal force would be justified.

> `/me extends the collapsible baton with a sharp flick and delivers a single strike to the subject's outer left thigh.`
>
> `/do The strike causes immediate pain and the subject drops to one knee, loosening their grip on the officer's vest.`

### Specialty Impact Munitions (Beanbag Rounds)

Deployed from a dedicated shotgun. Effective at range when a subject is armed with a non-firearm weapon and closing distance.

> `/me shoulders the less-lethal shotgun and fires a single beanbag round at the subject's lower abdomen from approximately fifteen feet.`

## Level 6 — Lethal Force

Lethal force is the last resort. It is authorised only when the officer reasonably believes that the subject poses an imminent threat of death or serious bodily harm to the officer or another person, and no lesser force option can reasonably stop that threat.

**Lethal force is justified when:**

- The subject is armed with a firearm and pointing it at the officer or another person.
- The subject is armed with an edged weapon and actively attacking or closing distance in a manner that prevents retreat.
- The subject is using or attempting to use a vehicle as a weapon against an officer or civilian.
- The subject is in the act of killing or inflicting serious bodily harm on another person and cannot be stopped by lesser means.

**Lethal force is NOT justified when:**

- The subject is fleeing on foot and poses no immediate threat to anyone.
- The subject is verbally threatening but unarmed and at a safe distance.
- The officer is angry, frustrated, or acting on emotion rather than assessed threat.
- The use of lethal force would endanger bystanders disproportionate to the threat posed by the subject.

> **Example — Lethal Force:**
>
> `/me observes the subject raise a handgun and level it toward a group of bystanders at the intersection. There is no cover available for the civilians. The officer draws the service weapon and fires two rounds at centre mass from approximately ten feet.`
>
> `/do The subject is struck and falls. The handgun drops from their grip and slides across the pavement.`
>
> `/me immediately scans the area for additional threats. Kicks the dropped firearm away from the downed subject. Keys the radio: "Shots fired, shots fired! [Location]. One subject down. Requesting EMS to my location immediately. Scene is not yet secure — one weapon recovered."`

## After Force Is Used

Every use of force — from a wrist lock to a gunshot — triggers reporting and review requirements.

### Immediate Actions

1. **Secure the scene.** Ensure there are no additional threats.
2. **Render aid.** Once the scene is safe, provide or request medical attention for any injured party — including the subject. An officer's duty to preserve life does not end when the handcuffs go on.
3. **Preserve evidence.** Do not move weapons, casings, or other evidence unless necessary for safety. If a weapon must be moved, document its original position first.
4. **Notify your supervisor.** A supervisor must respond to the scene of any use of force.

### Documentation Requirements

| Report Element | Details |
|---|---|
| **Use-of-Force Report** | A detailed written account of the incident — what the subject did, what the officer perceived, what force was used, and why lesser options were insufficient. |
| **Witness Statements** | Statements from any officers, civilians, or other witnesses who observed the use of force. |
| **Medical Documentation** | Records of any medical treatment provided to the subject or the officer. |
| **Body Camera / CCTV** | All available video and audio recordings are preserved as evidence. |
| **Supervisor Review** | The responding supervisor documents their observations and conducts an initial review of the force used. |

### Internal Review Process

All use-of-force incidents are subject to review. The depth of the review depends on the level of force used.

| Force Level | Review Authority |
|---|---|
| Soft / Hard Hand Techniques | Immediate supervisor reviews the use-of-force report and either approves or flags the incident for further review. |
| Less-Lethal Weapons | Supervisory review plus review by the division commander. The officer's training records are checked to confirm certification with the weapon used. |
| Lethal Force | Full internal investigation. The officer is placed on administrative leave pending the investigation. The case is reviewed by internal affairs, command staff, and potentially an independent oversight body. |

## Proportionality and Legal Justification

Force must be proportional to the threat. An officer who uses a baton on a handcuffed, compliant subject has used excessive force. An officer who shoots an unarmed person running away has used unjustified lethal force. Proportionality is assessed based on what a reasonable officer, with similar training and experience, would have done under the same circumstances.

**The reasonableness standard asks:**

- Was force necessary at all?
- Was the amount of force proportional to the threat?
- Did the officer attempt de-escalation before resorting to force?
- Did the officer stop using force once compliance was achieved?
- Were there less forceful alternatives available?

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Matters |
|---|---|
| **Escalating too quickly** | Jumping from verbal commands to lethal force without attempting intermediate options. |
| **Continuing force after compliance** | Once the subject stops resisting, force must stop. Continuing to strike a compliant subject is excessive force. |
| **Failing to document** | If the force is not documented in a report, it cannot be justified after the fact. |
| **Using force out of frustration** | Force is a response to a threat, not to disrespect, insults, or inconvenience. |
| **Tunnel vision** | Focusing entirely on the subject and ignoring the surrounding environment — bystanders, other threats, escape routes. |
| **Failing to render aid** | Once force has been used and the scene is secure, medical attention must be provided. This is non-negotiable. |
| **Not announcing force options** | Warn before deploying — "Taser!" or "Stop or I will use force!" Warnings give the subject a final chance to comply and demonstrate the officer's intent to use the minimum force necessary. |

Force is not punishment. It is not retribution. It is a tool of last resort used to protect life and establish lawful control when all other options have failed. Every officer must approach force with the gravity it deserves — because every use of force will be scrutinised, reviewed, and judged. Make sure your actions can withstand that scrutiny.
