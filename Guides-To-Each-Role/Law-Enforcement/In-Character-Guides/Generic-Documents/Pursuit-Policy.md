# Pursuit Policy — The Decision to Chase

Vehicle pursuits are among the most dangerous activities in law enforcement. A pursuit puts officers, suspects, and innocent bystanders at extreme risk. Every year, pursuits result in fatalities — and not just the suspect's. Bystanders who are in the wrong place at the wrong time, passengers who had no say in the driver's decision to flee, and officers whose training could not overcome physics at triple-digit speeds. The decision to initiate, continue, or terminate a pursuit is one of the most consequential judgement calls an officer will ever make.

This guide establishes pursuit procedures applicable to any law enforcement department regardless of jurisdiction, style, or rank structure. A pursuit policy is not a suggestion — it is a framework that balances the need to apprehend fleeing suspects against the duty to protect public safety. Know this policy. Follow it. Your career — and someone's life — may depend on it.

---

## When to Initiate a Pursuit

Not every fleeing vehicle justifies a pursuit. The decision to chase must be weighed against the risk.

**A pursuit may be initiated when:**

- The suspect has committed or is suspected of a violent felony — homicide, armed robbery, aggravated assault, kidnapping.
- The suspect poses an ongoing, immediate threat to public safety if not apprehended.
- The suspect's identity is unknown and there is no other means of apprehension.

**A pursuit should NOT be initiated when:**

- The offence is a minor traffic violation, misdemeanour, or non-violent crime.
- The suspect's identity is known and they can be located later through investigative means.
- Road, weather, or traffic conditions make pursuit unreasonably dangerous.
- The officer's vehicle is not equipped for pursuit operations (unmarked vehicle with no emergency equipment, vehicle carrying unsecured passengers or prisoners).
- The pursuit would pass through a school zone, pedestrian-heavy area, or densely populated neighbourhood during peak hours.

| Factor | Pursue | Do Not Pursue |
|---|---|---|
| **Offence Severity** | Violent felony, immediate threat to life | Minor traffic offence, non-violent misdemeanour |
| **Suspect Identity** | Unknown — cannot be apprehended later | Known — can be located through investigation |
| **Road Conditions** | Open road, good visibility, light traffic | School zone, heavy traffic, poor weather, residential area |
| **Vehicle Condition** | Fully equipped patrol vehicle | Unmarked vehicle, mechanical issues, transporting a prisoner |
| **Public Risk** | Low pedestrian / vehicle density in the area | Crowded streets, events, high pedestrian activity |

> **Example — Decision NOT to Pursue:**
>
> `/me observes a vehicle run a red traffic signal at the intersection and accelerate away. Activates emergency lights. The vehicle does not stop and increases speed. The officer notes the registration plate, vehicle description, and direction of travel. Assesses the conditions — heavy traffic, pedestrian crossings ahead, multiple cars at the next intersection.`
>
> `/me deactivates emergency lights and keys the radio: "Dispatch, [callsign]. Vehicle failed to stop for a traffic signal at [intersection] and fled eastbound on [road]. I am not pursuing due to traffic density. Plate is [number], dark SUV, single occupant. Requesting a BOLO."`

The hardest part of a pursuit is not the driving. It is the decision to let someone go when chasing them would put innocent people at risk.

## Requesting Permission to Pursue

Before initiating a pursuit — or as soon as practicable after one begins — the pursuing officer must notify dispatch and request authorisation from a supervisor.

> `/me activates emergency lights and siren. The suspect vehicle accelerates and begins to flee. Keys the radio immediately.`
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "Dispatch, [callsign]! I'm in pursuit of a dark-coloured saloon, eastbound on [road] from [cross street]. Vehicle fled a traffic stop. Plate comes back stolen. Speed is approximately [speed]. Requesting permission to continue pursuit."
>
> **Supervisor (Radio):** "[Callsign], what is the traffic and road condition?"
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "Moderate traffic, two-lane road, no pedestrians visible at this time."
>
> **Supervisor (Radio):** "[Callsign], pursuit is authorised. Keep me updated on speed, direction, and conditions. If conditions deteriorate, terminate immediately."

If a supervisor orders the pursuit terminated, it is terminated. No debate. No "just a little further." The order to stop is absolute.

## Radio Communication During Pursuit

The pursuing officer must provide constant updates over the radio. Other units, dispatch, and supervisors cannot make informed decisions if they do not know what is happening.

**Required updates:**

| Update | Example |
|---|---|
| **Location** | "Eastbound on [road], passing [landmark/cross street]." |
| **Speed** | "Speed approximately 80 miles per hour." |
| **Traffic conditions** | "Light traffic. Moving into a residential area." |
| **Suspect behaviour** | "Suspect is running red lights. Driving into oncoming traffic." |
| **Direction changes** | "Suspect turning northbound on [road]." |
| **Hazards** | "Suspect just sideswiped a parked vehicle. Debris in the roadway." |
| **Termination factors** | "Entering a school zone — recommending termination." |

> **Example — Continuous Radio Updates:**
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "[Callsign], still in pursuit. Now northbound on [road], speed approximately 90. Suspect ran the traffic signal at [intersection]. Light traffic, no collisions. Approaching the motorway on-ramp."
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "[Callsign], suspect has entered the motorway, now westbound. Speed increasing to approximately 110. Three lanes, moderate traffic. Requesting additional units to parallel on surface streets."
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "[Callsign], suspect is weaving between lanes. Nearly struck a civilian vehicle in lane two. Traffic is getting heavier ahead."

If the pursuing officer becomes too focused on driving to provide updates, a secondary unit should take over radio communication while the primary unit continues the pursuit.

## Speed and Safety Considerations

There is no specific speed at which a pursuit automatically becomes too dangerous — the assessment is based on the totality of conditions. However, the following guidelines apply:

- **The pursuing officer must be able to maintain control of their vehicle at all times.** If you are driving beyond your ability or the vehicle's capability, you are a danger to everyone on the road.
- **Excessive speeds in residential or commercial areas dramatically increase the risk to bystanders.** A pursuit through a neighbourhood at 100 mph is almost never justifiable regardless of the offence.
- **Emergency lights and sirens must remain active throughout the pursuit.** Other motorists need warning that high-speed vehicles are approaching.
- **Only authorised units participate in the pursuit.** A convoy of six patrol vehicles chasing one suspect multiplies the risk without proportional benefit. Typically, one primary unit and one secondary unit are sufficient, with additional units deploying to parallel routes or ahead of the pursuit.

## Pursuit Termination

A pursuit must be terminated when the risk to public safety outweighs the need to apprehend the suspect. Termination is not failure — it is sound judgement.

**Mandatory termination criteria:**

- A supervisor orders the pursuit terminated.
- The pursuing officer loses visual contact with the suspect vehicle for an extended period.
- Road, weather, or traffic conditions have deteriorated to the point where continued pursuit creates unreasonable danger.
- The pursuit enters an area with high pedestrian density — school zones, event venues, crowded commercial districts.
- The officer's vehicle develops a mechanical problem — tyre failure, engine warning, brake fade.
- The suspect's identity has been confirmed and they can be apprehended through other means.
- A collision occurs involving the pursuing officer or an uninvolved civilian.

> **Example — Terminating a Pursuit:**
>
> **Officer (Radio):** "[Callsign], I'm terminating the pursuit. We've entered the downtown area — heavy pedestrian traffic and cross streets every block. Speed is too high for these conditions. Last known direction was westbound on [road]. Suspect vehicle is a dark SUV, plate [number]. Requesting air support and a BOLO."
>
> `/me deactivates the siren, reduces speed, and pulls to the side of the road. Takes a moment to note the last known location, direction, and speed of the suspect vehicle for the pursuit report.`

When a pursuit is terminated, the officer must immediately reduce speed and resume normal driving. Do not continue following the suspect at high speed with your lights off — that is still a pursuit without authorisation.

## Pursuit Intervention Tactics

When a pursuit must be concluded but the suspect will not stop voluntarily, intervention tactics may be deployed. Each tactic requires specific authorisation.

### Tyre Deflation Devices

Tyre deflation devices (spike strips, stingers) are deployed ahead of the suspect vehicle to puncture the tyres and gradually reduce speed.

> `/me receives radio notification that a unit has deployed tyre deflation devices on [road] ahead of the pursuit. Maintains pursuit distance and watches for the deployment point.`
>
> `/do The suspect vehicle strikes the tyre deflation device. The front offside tyre begins to deflate. The vehicle's speed decreases gradually as the driver struggles to maintain control. After approximately half a mile, the vehicle pulls to the hard shoulder and stops.`

**Tyre deflation considerations:**

- Deploy on straight stretches of road with clear sight lines.
- The deploying officer must have time to set the device and retreat to a safe position.
- Do not deploy on curves, bridges, or steep grades where loss of tyre pressure could cause the vehicle to roll.
- Do not deploy for motorcycles — loss of tyre pressure on a two-wheeled vehicle is almost certainly fatal.

### PIT Manoeuvre (Precision Immobilisation Technique)

The PIT manoeuvre involves a pursuing vehicle making controlled contact with the rear quarter panel of the fleeing vehicle to cause it to spin and stop.

| PIT Consideration | Guideline |
|---|---|
| **Authorisation** | Supervisor approval required. Some departments restrict PIT to speeds below a certain threshold. |
| **Speed** | Generally authorised only at lower speeds — typically below 40 mph. High-speed PITs are extremely dangerous and may be considered lethal force. |
| **Location** | Open road, clear of bystanders, oncoming traffic, and fixed objects that the spinning vehicle could strike. |
| **Contraindications** | Not authorised on motorcycles, top-heavy vehicles (SUVs, vans) at higher speeds, or in areas with pedestrians. |

> `/me receives authorisation for a PIT manoeuvre. Accelerates alongside the suspect vehicle on the left side. Aligns the front bumper of the patrol vehicle with the rear quarter panel of the suspect vehicle. Applies a sharp steering input to the right, making contact with the suspect's rear panel.`
>
> `/do The suspect vehicle spins clockwise, crosses the opposite lane, and comes to a stop on the grass verge facing the wrong direction. The engine stalls.`
>
> `/me brings the patrol vehicle to a stop, angled to provide cover. Exits and draws the service weapon, approaching the disabled vehicle from the rear. "Driver, turn off the engine! Show me your hands!"`

### Boxing / Rolling Roadblock

Multiple patrol vehicles surround the suspect vehicle — front, rear, and both sides — and gradually reduce speed until the suspect has no choice but to stop. This is a coordinated tactic requiring multiple units and radio discipline.

> **Supervisor (Radio):** "All pursuit units, we are setting up a rolling roadblock. Unit 1, take the front. Unit 2, left flank. Unit 3, right flank. Primary unit, maintain the rear. Reduce speed gradually. Do not make contact with the suspect vehicle."

### Air Support

If air support (helicopter or drone) is available, it is one of the most effective pursuit tools. Aircraft can maintain visual contact with the suspect while ground units reduce speed or disengage entirely, dramatically reducing risk.

> **Officer (Radio):** "Dispatch, requesting air support for this pursuit. Suspect is westbound on the motorway, dark SUV, plate [number]."
>
> **Dispatch:** "Air unit is en route, ETA three minutes. Continue updates on suspect direction and location."
>
> Once air support has visual contact, the ground units may be instructed to back off and allow the aircraft to direct the pursuit from above.

## Post-Pursuit Procedures

When a pursuit concludes — whether by suspect stop, intervention, or termination — immediate steps must be taken.

**If the suspect is stopped:**

1. Conduct a felony / high-risk vehicle stop. The suspect has demonstrated a willingness to flee and may be armed.
2. Secure all occupants. Remove them from the vehicle one at a time.
3. Request EMS if there are injuries — to anyone, including the suspect.
4. Preserve the scene. The suspect's vehicle, any evidence inside, and the route of the pursuit (if collisions occurred along the way) must be documented.

**Regardless of outcome:**

1. **Pursuit report.** Every pursuit generates a written report documenting the reason for initiation, the course of the pursuit, radio communications, intervention tactics used (if any), the reason for termination, and the outcome.
2. **Supervisor review.** The shift supervisor reviews the pursuit and determines whether departmental policy was followed.
3. **Vehicle inspection.** The patrol vehicle used in the pursuit is inspected for damage and mechanical integrity before being returned to service.

## Liability Considerations

Officers and departments can be held liable for injuries and damage caused during a pursuit — not just to the suspect, but to bystanders. The key question in any liability review is whether the decision to pursue, and the manner in which the pursuit was conducted, was reasonable given the circumstances.

**Factors reviewed:**

- Was the initial decision to pursue justified by the severity of the offence?
- Were radio updates maintained throughout the pursuit?
- Was supervisory authorisation obtained?
- Were termination criteria considered as conditions changed?
- Did the officer's driving create unnecessary additional risk?
- Were intervention tactics used appropriately and within policy?

A pursuit that results in a bystander's death because an officer chased a shoplifter through a residential area at high speed will not withstand scrutiny. The offence must justify the risk. If it does not — let them go. Get the plate. Get the description. Find them later. The road will still be there tomorrow. The bystander you might have hit will still be alive.
