# Armed Response Guide — Authorised Firearms Officers

In British policing, the vast majority of officers do not carry firearms. That is not a limitation — it is a deliberate, foundational choice that defines the relationship between police and public. But there are situations where the threat exceeds what an unarmed officer can safely manage. A suspect brandishing a firearm in a populated area. An armed robbery in progress. A terrorist incident. A person with a weapon who poses an immediate threat to life that PAVA, batons, and Tasers cannot resolve.

That is when armed response is called.

Authorised Firearms Officers (AFOs) are specially selected, rigorously trained officers who deploy with firearms only when the situation demands it. They are not SWAT. They are not a military unit. They are police officers who happen to carry guns — and that distinction matters in everything they do. The British armed response model is built on proportionality, accountability, and the absolute minimum use of lethal force. Every round fired will be scrutinised. Every deployment will be reviewed. Every decision you make will be examined against the standard of what was reasonable, necessary, and proportionate in the circumstances you faced.

This guide is written for GTA V FiveM servers operating under the British policing model. If your server uses American-style policing with routine firearms carry, this guide is not for you. This is for servers where armed response is the exception, not the norm — where the arrival of firearms officers signals that the situation has escalated beyond what normal policing can contain.

---

## When Armed Response Is Called

Armed response is not self-deploying. Regular officers encountering a firearms threat do not engage — they withdraw, contain the area, and request armed support through the control room. The decision to deploy firearms officers follows a strict authorisation chain.

**Situations requiring armed response:**

- A suspect is confirmed or believed to be in possession of a firearm
- An edged weapon threat where Taser deployment has failed or is not viable due to distance, clothing, or environment
- Armed robbery in progress where suspects are believed to be carrying firearms
- A terrorist incident or credible terrorist threat involving weapons
- Pre-planned operations against targets known to possess firearms (warrants, arrests of armed suspects)
- Protection of high-risk persons or locations requiring an armed presence
- Incidents where a person poses an immediate threat to life that cannot be resolved by unarmed officers

**Situations that do NOT require armed response:**

- A suspect is non-compliant but not armed
- A suspect is violent but only using fists — unarmed officers handle this
- An officer wants to "show force" or intimidate
- A suspect has fled and is no longer an immediate threat (containment and investigation, not firearms deployment)

> **RP Note:** On a British RP server, the deployment of armed officers should feel significant. It is an escalation. When ARVs roll up, it means the situation is genuinely dangerous. Do not overuse armed response — it cheapens the impact and undermines the British policing model.

## Command Structure — Firearms Commanders

British firearms operations use a three-tier command structure: Strategic, Tactical, and Operational. Each level has distinct responsibilities and authority.

| Commander Level | Rank (Typical) | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| **Strategic Firearms Commander (Gold)** | Superintendent or above | Sets the overall strategy for the firearms operation. Defines the desired outcome, risk parameters, and the limits of authority granted to the Tactical Commander. Does not direct officers on the ground — sets the framework within which they operate. |
| **Tactical Firearms Commander (Silver)** | Inspector or Chief Inspector | Develops the tactical plan to deliver the strategic objective. Decides how firearms officers will be deployed, what containment is needed, what specialist resources to request, and when to escalate or de-escalate. Commands from the control room or tactical command post. |
| **Operational Firearms Commander (Bronze)** | Sergeant (usually the ARV Sergeant) | The on-scene commander. Leads the firearms team at the incident, makes real-time tactical decisions, positions officers, and gives the order to challenge, contain, or withdraw. Communicates directly with Silver. |

In a fast-moving spontaneous incident — an armed suspect encountered during routine patrol — the initial ARV crew may have to act without a formal command structure in place. In those circumstances, the senior AFO on scene assumes the role of Operational Firearms Commander until a supervisor arrives. But even in spontaneous deployments, the principles remain: contain, communicate, and do not engage unless there is an immediate threat to life.

## Authorisation and Deployment

No firearms officer deploys weapons without authorisation. The level of authorisation depends on the circumstances.

**Pre-planned operations:** A Strategic Firearms Commander must authorise the operation. A full tactical plan, risk assessment, and briefing must be completed before any officer draws a weapon. Intelligence is gathered, roles are assigned, and every contingency is planned for. This includes warrant executions on premises where firearms are suspected.

**Spontaneous incidents:** When a firearms threat emerges without warning, the Tactical Firearms Commander (or the Force Duty Officer acting in that capacity) authorises deployment over the radio. If no commander is immediately available, the senior AFO on scene may deploy under the principle of immediate threat to life — but must notify a commander at the earliest opportunity.

> **Example — Spontaneous Armed Deployment:**
>
> **Regular PC (Radio):** "Control, Central 3. I've got reports of a male with a firearm outside the 24/7 on Strawberry Avenue. Multiple witnesses. Male is described as IC1, dark hoodie, jeans, believed to be carrying a handgun. I've withdrawn to the junction with Innocence Boulevard. Requesting ARV, over."
>
> **Control:** "Roger, Central 3. All units, armed incident declared, Strawberry Avenue near the 24/7. Unarmed units withdraw and hold a cordon at the surrounding junctions. ARV Trojan units, deploy to Strawberry Avenue. Silver Commander, are you receiving?"
>
> **Silver Commander:** "Silver receiving. Authorised. Trojan units, I want a containment on all approaches. No one in, no one out until we have eyes on the suspect. Bronze, take command on the ground."
>
> **ARV Sergeant (Bronze):** "Bronze copies. Trojan 1 and Trojan 2, approach from the south via Innocence Boulevard. Trojan 3, cover the northern approach from Forum Drive. Long weapons authorised. Challenge on my command only."

## Equipment

AFOs carry specialist equipment beyond what regular officers are issued. Everything is accounted for, inspected before every shift, and subject to audit.

| Equipment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Glock 17 (sidearm)** | Standard-issue pistol. Carried in a thigh holster. Used for close-quarters engagements and situations where a long weapon is impractical. |
| **SIG MCX / G36C (carbine)** | Primary long weapon for ARV officers. Accurate at range, suitable for urban environments. Deployed from the vehicle's gun safe. |
| **Taser X2** | Less-lethal option. AFOs carry Taser in addition to firearms, providing a force option below lethal. |
| **Ballistic shield** | Carried in ARVs for approach to armed suspects in buildings or confined spaces. Provides cover during a challenge. |
| **Ballistic helmet** | Worn during active firearms deployments. Provides head protection against fragmentation and ricochets. |
| **Body armour (Level IV)** | Enhanced armour worn over standard stab vest. Provides ballistic protection against handgun and some rifle rounds. |
| **MOE kit (Method of Entry)** | Breaching equipment — Enforcer ram, Halligan bar, bolt cutters. Used to gain rapid entry to premises during firearms warrants. |
| **First aid / trauma kit** | Every AFO carries a personal trauma kit including tourniquets, chest seals, and haemostatic dressings. You may be the first person to treat a casualty — suspect, civilian, or colleague. |

> **RP Note:** On FiveM, equip appropriate weapon models and wear tactical gear (plate carrier, helmet) only when deployed to a firearms incident. Walking around in full tactical kit during a routine shift breaks immersion entirely. Your everyday patrol uniform should be standard response officer gear — the tactical equipment comes out of the car when it is needed.

## The Challenge

Before discharging a firearm, officers must issue a challenge — a clear, unambiguous verbal warning that identifies them as armed police and gives the suspect an opportunity to comply. The challenge is a legal and moral requirement. It is not optional.

**Standard armed police challenge:**

> *"ARMED POLICE! STAND STILL!"*

Or, depending on the situation:

> *"ARMED POLICE! DROP THE WEAPON! DO IT NOW!"*
>
> *"ARMED POLICE! PUT THE GUN DOWN AND SHOW ME YOUR HANDS!"*

The challenge must be loud enough to be heard, clear enough to be understood, and delivered with sufficient authority to convey the seriousness of the situation. You are giving the suspect a final opportunity to comply before lethal force becomes necessary.

**When the challenge may be omitted:** If issuing a challenge would place any person at an increased risk of death or serious injury — for example, if a suspect is in the act of shooting at members of the public and a challenge would delay the response — the AFO may fire without challenge. This decision will be scrutinised intensively after the event, and the officer must be able to articulate exactly why a challenge was not possible.

> **Example — Armed Challenge RP:**
>
> `/me moves to a position of cover behind the engine block of the ARV, brings the carbine up to the shoulder, and acquires the suspect in the sights. Takes a breath.`
>
> **AFO:** *shouts* "ARMED POLICE! STAY WHERE YOU ARE! DROP THE WEAPON NOW! DROP IT AND PUT YOUR HANDS WHERE I CAN SEE THEM!"
>
> `/me maintains aim on the suspect's central mass, finger alongside the trigger guard, not on the trigger. Waits for compliance or further threat indicators.`

## Rules of Engagement and Proportionality

British firearms officers operate under strict rules of engagement that are grounded in Article 2 of the European Convention on Human Rights — the right to life. Lethal force is justified **only** when it is absolutely necessary to protect life.

**You may fire when:**

- There is an immediate threat to life — yours, a colleague's, or a member of the public's
- You honestly and genuinely believe that lethal force is the only option available to prevent death or serious injury
- Less lethal options have failed, are not available, or would be ineffective given the circumstances

**You may NOT fire:**

- To prevent escape of an unarmed suspect
- As a warning (warning shots are prohibited in British policing)
- To disable a vehicle (unless the vehicle is being used as a weapon and poses an immediate threat to life)
- Because you are frightened — fear alone does not justify lethal force. Your training exists to manage fear.

**Proportionality assessment (the NDM — National Decision Model):**

Every firearms decision passes through the National Decision Model:

1. **Gather information and intelligence** — What do you know? What do you not know?
2. **Assess threat and risk** — Who is at risk? What is the nature and immediacy of the threat?
3. **Consider powers and policy** — What are you authorised to do? What does the law permit?
4. **Identify options and contingencies** — What are the available responses? What happens if each one fails?
5. **Take action and review** — Act decisively, then immediately review the outcome and reassess.

This model applies in real time. In a spontaneous firearms incident, you may cycle through it in seconds. In a pre-planned operation, you may spend hours on it. But the structure is always the same — think, assess, act, review.

## Difference from American SWAT

Officers coming from American-style RP servers must understand the fundamental differences between British armed response and American SWAT operations. They are not the same thing with different accents.

| Aspect | British Armed Response | American SWAT |
|---|---|---|
| **Routine carry** | No. Firearms are drawn only for specific deployments. | Officers routinely carry sidearms on every shift. |
| **Philosophy** | Minimum force, de-escalation first, lethal force as absolute last resort. | Threat neutralisation, rapid decisive action, domination of the situation. |
| **Accountability** | Every round fired is investigated by the IOPC. Officers are treated as witnesses (or suspects) after any discharge. | Internal affairs review, grand jury in some jurisdictions. |
| **Warning shots** | Prohibited. | Prohibited in most departments but culturally different. |
| **Challenge requirement** | Mandatory unless doing so would increase risk to life. | Verbal commands are standard but the legal framework differs. |
| **Deployment frequency** | Rare and significant. Armed response is an escalation, not a routine tool. | SWAT callouts are more frequent and considered a standard tactical resource. |
| **Post-incident** | Officer is removed from duty, weapon seized, detailed statement taken, IOPC referral automatic. | Varies by department; administrative leave is common. |
| **Command structure** | Gold/Silver/Bronze with strict separation of strategic, tactical, and operational roles. | Incident Commander with team leaders; less formal separation of command tiers. |

The British model prioritises restraint. A successful armed deployment is one where nobody is shot. If you resolve the situation with a challenge and the suspect surrenders, that is the best possible outcome — not a missed opportunity for action.

## Post-Incident Procedures

Every firearms discharge — and every incident where a firearm is pointed at a person — triggers a mandatory post-incident procedure. This process is non-negotiable and exists to protect the public, the officer, and the integrity of the investigation.

**Immediately after a discharge:**

1. **Make the area safe.** Secure the suspect (arrest if alive, preserve the scene if deceased). Ensure no further threat exists.
2. **Request ambulance.** Regardless of the suspect's apparent condition. First aid is rendered immediately.
3. **Notify the control room.** Report shots fired, casualties, and the current status of the scene.
4. **Preserve the scene.** The location where the shot was fired is now a crime scene. Nothing is moved, cleaned, or altered.
5. **Weapon retention.** The officer's weapon is seized by a supervisor and secured as an exhibit. The officer is issued a replacement if they need to remain armed for safety.
6. **Separation.** Officers involved in the shooting are separated from each other to prevent contamination of accounts. They do not discuss what happened until they have provided individual statements.
7. **Post-Incident Manager (PIM).** A senior officer (usually a Federation representative or superintendent) attends to manage the welfare of the involved officers and oversee the handover to investigators.
8. **IOPC referral.** The Independent Office for Police Conduct (formerly IPCC) is notified automatically. They will decide whether to conduct an independent investigation, a managed investigation, or a supervised investigation.

> **Example — Post-Incident RP:**
>
> **ARV Sergeant (Bronze):** "Control, Bronze. Shots fired by Trojan 1 at the Strawberry Avenue location. One suspect down, receiving first aid from Trojan 2. Scene is secure, no further threat. Requesting LAS to the scene immediately. I am commencing post-incident procedures now. All Trojan officers are to remain on scene and not discuss the incident with one another."
>
> `/me secures the discharged officer's carbine, checks the chamber is clear, and places it in an evidence bag. Hands the officer a replacement sidearm from the ARV.`
>
> **ARV Sergeant:** "You alright? Right, I need you to sit in the car. Don't speak to anyone about what happened until the PIM arrives. I'm going to make sure you're looked after, but this process has to happen by the book. Understood?"

## Working with Unarmed Colleagues

Armed response does not operate in isolation. Most incidents involve a combination of armed and unarmed officers, and the relationship between them must be clear and disciplined.

**Unarmed officers' responsibilities during a firearms incident:**

- **Withdraw** from the immediate area of the armed threat. Your safety comes first — you cannot help anyone if you become a casualty.
- **Contain** the area by establishing cordons at a safe distance. Control access points and keep the public away.
- **Communicate** what you saw to the control room and to the arriving AFOs. Your initial observations — suspect description, weapon type, direction of travel, number of suspects — are critical intelligence.
- **Do not** attempt to engage an armed suspect. Do not "back up" the AFOs by approaching the threat. Stay behind the cordon and do your job there.

**AFOs' responsibilities towards unarmed colleagues:**

- Clearly communicate your movements and intentions. Unarmed officers on the cordon need to know where you are going and what you are doing.
- Do not assume unarmed officers understand firearms tactics. Brief them on where it is safe to be and where it is not.
- Respect that unarmed officers got to the scene first and managed it before you arrived. Their initial actions — witness management, public evacuation, containment — are what give you the space to operate.

> **RP Note:** This dynamic creates excellent roleplay opportunities. The tension between unarmed officers holding a cordon, hearing shots fired, and not knowing what is happening inside the containment area is powerful RP. Lean into it. The AFOs resolving the situation and then emerging to debrief the cordon officers is a natural scene that writes itself.

## Adapting to GTA V RP

Armed response on a British RP server should feel weighty and consequential. Here are key points for making it work within the GTA V environment:

- **ARVs should be visually distinct** — marked vehicles with distinct livery, officers in tactical gear only when deployed. When not on a shout, AFOs wear standard patrol uniform.
- **Limit ARV numbers.** A realistic server might have two or three ARV units on duty at any time. The scarcity is the point — if every officer is armed, you are not running British policing.
- **Use the challenge.** Every single time. Even if you know OOC that the suspect is going to shoot. The challenge is what separates a British firearms officer from a gunfighter.
- **Post-incident RP is mandatory.** Do not just shoot and move on. The post-incident process is rich, dramatic roleplay — weapon seizure, separation, the PIM arrival, the IOPC conversation. Play it out.
- **Coordinate with regular units.** Armed response does not just show up and take over. You work with the unarmed officers who are already managing the scene. Respect the chain of command and the cordon.
- **De-escalation is always the preferred outcome.** The best armed deployment is the one where nobody gets shot. If the suspect surrenders to your challenge, that is a win — celebrate it, do not regret it.

British armed response is about discipline, accountability, and the judicious use of lethal force. It is not about being tactical for the sake of being tactical. Every deployment carries the weight of potential consequences — legal, professional, and personal. Carry that weight with the seriousness it deserves.
