# BCSO Handbook — Blaine County Sheriff's Office

The Blaine County Sheriff's Office is the primary law enforcement agency serving the unincorporated communities of Blaine County, San Andreas. While the LSPD handles the gridlocked intersections and neon-lit chaos of Los Santos, the BCSO patrols the vast stretches of desert, mountain, and coastline that make up the rest of this state. Our jurisdiction covers more square miles than every LSPD patrol zone combined — and we do it with a fraction of the personnel.

This is not city policing. There are no backup units staged two blocks away. There are no surveillance cameras on every corner. Out here, a deputy may be the only badge for miles in any direction, handling everything from a bar fight in Sandy Shores to a lost hiker on Mount Chiliad to a armed standoff on a desert homestead with no cell service and no road access. That reality shapes everything about how this office operates.

This handbook is issued to every sworn member of the Blaine County Sheriff's Office. It outlines your duties, your authority, your chain of command, and the standards by which you will be judged. The Sheriff's Office has served this county since before Los Santos had paved roads, and the traditions built by the men and women who wore this star before you are not negotiable. Know this book. Live by it.

---

## Mission Statement

The mission of the Blaine County Sheriff's Office is to preserve the peace, protect the lives and property of the citizens of Blaine County, and enforce the laws of the State of San Andreas throughout our jurisdiction with professionalism, fairness, and respect for the rural communities we serve.

**Core Values:**

- **Self-Reliance** — A deputy in Blaine County cannot depend on immediate backup. You must be capable of handling any situation alone until help arrives, which may take considerable time.
- **Community Trust** — In a small county, your reputation is your most valuable tool. The people you serve today are the same people you will see at the general store tomorrow. Act accordingly.
- **Integrity** — The isolation of rural patrol creates opportunities to cut corners. We do not take them. The badge means the same thing on a deserted highway as it does in front of a courtroom.
- **Adaptability** — No two calls in Blaine County are alike. The terrain changes, the weather changes, and the situation changes. A deputy who cannot think on their feet has no business wearing the star.
- **Service** — We are not occupiers. We are neighbors with badges. Every decision we make must reflect that truth.

## Rank Structure

The Sheriff's Office follows a hierarchical chain of command rooted in the traditions of American county law enforcement. Unlike a police department — where the chief is appointed — the Sheriff is an elected official who answers directly to the voters of Blaine County.

| Rank | Grade | Authority & Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| **Deputy** | D-1 | Entry-level sworn officer. Deputies conduct patrol, respond to calls for service, serve civil papers, transport prisoners, and perform all general law enforcement duties. Every career in the BCSO begins here. |
| **Senior Deputy** | D-2 | Experienced deputy eligible for Field Training Officer duties and specialized assignments. Senior Deputies are expected to mentor junior deputies and may act as shift lead in the absence of a Corporal. |
| **Corporal** | D-3 | First-line supervisor. Corporals oversee small patrol teams, review reports, and serve as the on-scene supervisor for routine incidents. They bridge the gap between deputies in the field and command staff at the station. |
| **Sergeant** | S-1 | Primary field supervisor responsible for an entire patrol shift. Sergeants conduct briefings, approve use-of-force reports, manage personnel issues, authorize tactical decisions, and respond to critical incidents. |
| **Lieutenant** | S-2 | Division commander. Lieutenants manage entire operational divisions — patrol, investigations, or court services — and coordinate staffing, scheduling, and resource allocation. They report directly to the Captain. |
| **Captain** | C-1 | Operations commander. The Captain oversees day-to-day operations of the Sheriff's Office, implements policy directives from the Sheriff, and manages inter-agency coordination. Typically the highest-ranking career law enforcement officer in the office. |
| **Undersheriff** | C-2 | Second-in-command. The Undersheriff is appointed by the Sheriff and assumes full command of the office in the Sheriff's absence. Handles administrative oversight, internal investigations, and serves as the Sheriff's chief advisor. |
| **Sheriff** | E-1 | The chief law enforcement officer of Blaine County. The Sheriff is elected by the citizens of the county and holds constitutional authority over all law enforcement operations within county lines. The Sheriff answers to no one but the voters and the law. |

Promotions through the deputy and supervisory ranks are earned through performance, time in grade, testing, and review by the command staff. The ranks of Undersheriff and Sheriff are not earned through the promotional process — the Undersheriff is appointed, and the Sheriff is elected.

## County Jurisdiction

The Blaine County Sheriff's Office holds primary law enforcement jurisdiction over all unincorporated areas of Blaine County. This includes every community, roadway, waterway, and wilderness area within county boundaries that does not fall under the jurisdiction of an incorporated municipal police department.

**Jurisdictional Boundaries:**

- **BCSO Territory:** Sandy Shores, Grapeseed, Paleto Bay, Mount Chiliad, Chiliad Mountain State Wilderness, Alamo Sea and its shoreline, Raton Canyon, Cassidy Creek, Senora Desert, Mount Gordo, Procopio Beach, Calafia Bridge, Galilee, Cape Catfish, and all county roads and highways within Blaine County lines.
- **LSPD Territory:** The incorporated City of Los Santos and its recognized neighborhoods. BCSO deputies do not conduct enforcement operations within Los Santos city limits without invitation or mutual aid activation.
- **LSSD / Highway Patrol Overlap:** The San Andreas Highway Patrol maintains jurisdiction over state highways and freeways. BCSO deputies may respond to incidents on state routes within county boundaries and regularly coordinate with Highway Patrol on shared calls.

**Mutual Aid Agreements:**

The BCSO maintains mutual aid agreements with the LSPD, the San Andreas Highway Patrol, and the San Andreas Park Rangers. Under mutual aid, any agency may request assistance from another when resources are insufficient to handle an incident. A BCSO deputy responding under mutual aid operates under the authority and policies of their own agency, not the requesting agency. Mutual aid is cooperation, not subordination.

## Patrol Areas

Blaine County is vast. Understanding the geography, population, and common call types for each patrol area is essential for effective policing. The county is divided into the following patrol sectors:

| Patrol Sector | Area | Terrain & Common Calls |
|---|---|---|
| **Sector 1 — Sandy Shores** | Sandy Shores, Trevor's Trailer Park, Sandy Shores Airfield | Desert flatland with scattered trailer parks and low-income housing. High call volume for the county — domestic disturbances, methamphetamine activity, bar fights at the Yellow Jack Inn, public intoxication, and stolen vehicles. The most populated area in the county and the closest thing Blaine County has to a city. |
| **Sector 2 — Grapeseed** | Grapeseed, O'Neil Ranch area, farmlands east of Mount Chiliad | Agricultural community with open farmland and rural homesteads. Calls include trespassing, theft of farming equipment, illegal hunting, and occasional disputes between landowners. Quiet during the day. Dark and isolated at night. |
| **Sector 3 — Paleto Bay** | Paleto Bay, Procopio Beach, Paleto Forest, Paleto Cove | Small coastal town at the northern edge of the county. Tourist traffic in summer months. Calls range from petty theft and shoplifting to boat-related incidents along the coast. The Paleto Bay substation is located here and serves as the northern command post. |
| **Sector 4 — Mount Chiliad Region** | Mount Chiliad, Chiliad Mountain State Wilderness, Mount Gordo | Steep mountain terrain with limited road access. Calls are predominantly search-and-rescue, lost hikers, vehicle accidents on winding mountain roads, and illegal camping. Radio coverage is unreliable at higher elevations. |
| **Sector 5 — Alamo Sea** | Alamo Sea shoreline, Galilee, Sandy Shores Marina | Inland body of water surrounded by desert. Calls include drowning, boating incidents, illegal dumping, trespassing on waterfront properties, and public intoxication along the shore. Water is toxic — deputies responding to water rescue calls must exercise caution. |
| **Sector 6 — Raton Canyon & Senora Desert** | Raton Canyon, Cassidy Creek, Senora Desert, Route 68 corridor | Remote canyon and deep desert. Lowest call volume but highest risk per call — stranded motorists, vehicle accidents in the canyon, illegal weapons activity in the desert, and bodies dumped in remote areas. Response times can exceed twenty minutes. |

Deputies are assigned to a sector at the beginning of each shift. In practice, the small size of the department means deputies regularly cross sector boundaries to back up other units or respond to calls outside their assigned area. Flexibility is the rule, not the exception.

## Rural Policing Challenges

Blaine County is not Los Santos. The challenges deputies face here are fundamentally different from those encountered by city officers, and a failure to understand those differences will get you hurt.

**Response Times:** When a deputy in Los Santos calls for backup, help arrives in minutes. When a BCSO deputy calls for backup on a remote desert road south of Route 68, help may be fifteen to twenty-five minutes away. You must be prepared to manage any situation alone for an extended period. De-escalation is not optional — it is survival.

**Isolation:** Some calls will take you miles from the nearest paved road, into areas with no cell service and intermittent radio coverage. Before leaving your vehicle to hike to a remote location, notify dispatch of your exact position, your intended direction of travel, and your estimated time of return. If you cannot reach dispatch, leave a written note on your dashboard with the same information.

**Terrain:** The county encompasses desert, mountain, canyon, forest, and coastline. Deputies must be proficient in off-road driving, basic wilderness navigation, and vehicle recovery. A patrol vehicle stuck in soft sand or high-centered on a mountain trail is not just an inconvenience — it is an officer safety emergency.

**Armed Residents:** Blaine County has a high per-capita rate of firearm ownership. The presence of a firearm on a property or on a person is common and does not, by itself, constitute a threat. Deputies must exercise sound judgment in distinguishing between a lawful gun owner and a genuine threat. Over-reaction erodes community trust. Under-reaction gets you killed. Balance is everything.

**Limited Infrastructure:** Street lighting is sparse outside of Sandy Shores and Paleto Bay. Many county roads are unpaved. Addresses may be non-existent or unmarked. Deputies must develop a working knowledge of the county's geography that goes beyond GPS — learn the landmarks, the ranch gates, and the dirt roads that do not appear on any map.

## Search and Rescue

Search and rescue operations are a core responsibility of the Blaine County Sheriff's Office. The county's rugged terrain and outdoor recreation activity generate a steady volume of SAR calls, particularly during spring and summer months.

**Common SAR Scenarios:**

- **Lost Hikers — Mount Chiliad / Mount Gordo:** Hikers who leave marked trails, lose daylight, or become injured on steep terrain. Deputies coordinate with Park Rangers to establish search grids and deploy resources based on the subject's last known position.
- **Drowning Calls — Alamo Sea:** Despite posted warnings about water quality, civilians continue to swim in the Alamo Sea. Drowning calls require immediate response. Deputies trained in water rescue may deploy inflatable watercraft. All others establish a perimeter and await rescue-trained personnel.
- **Stranded Motorists — Raton Canyon:** Vehicles that leave the roadway in Raton Canyon often end up at the bottom of steep embankments or in Cassidy Creek. These calls are treated as potential injury accidents until confirmed otherwise.
- **Missing Persons — Senora Desert:** The desert is unforgiving. A missing person in the Senora Desert during summer months becomes a life-threatening emergency within hours due to heat exposure. Time is the enemy.

**SAR Coordination Procedure:**

1. Establish a command post at the nearest accessible staging area.
2. Obtain last known position, physical description, clothing, medical conditions, and equipment of the missing subject.
3. Coordinate with Park Rangers, Fire/EMS, and volunteer search teams as appropriate.
4. Deploy search teams in grid patterns. All searchers must maintain radio contact and check in at regular intervals.
5. Document all areas searched and cleared. Time-stamp everything.

> **Example — SAR Activation:**
>
> `/me arrives at the Mount Chiliad trailhead and establishes a command post at the parking area, spreading a topographic map across the hood of the patrol vehicle.`
>
> **Deputy:** "Dispatch, Unit 4-Charlie, I'm 10-97 at the Chiliad trailhead. We've got a missing hiker — female, mid-twenties, last seen at approximately 1400 hours heading north on the summit trail. Requesting Park Ranger assistance and additional units for a grid search. I'm setting up the CP now."

## Wildlife Encounters

Blaine County's wilderness is home to mountain lions, coyotes, boar, deer, and other wildlife that regularly come into contact with residents and motorists. Deputies must know how to handle animal-related calls safely and lawfully.

**Common Wildlife Calls:**

- **Mountain Lion Sightings:** Usually reported near Mount Chiliad, Mount Gordo, or Raton Canyon. Confirm the sighting, advise residents to bring pets and children indoors, and contact the San Andreas Fish and Wildlife Service. Do not attempt to engage or relocate the animal yourself.
- **Coyotes in Residential Areas:** Common in Sandy Shores and Grapeseed. Coyotes that are simply passing through are not an emergency. Coyotes that display aggressive behavior — approaching humans, acting disoriented, or showing no fear — may be rabid. Establish a perimeter and contact animal control.
- **Deer or Livestock on Roadways:** A vehicle striking a deer at highway speed is a serious accident. Clear the roadway, assist injured motorists, and arrange for removal of the animal carcass. If the animal is alive and suffering, a deputy may humanely dispatch the animal with a firearm. This requires a supervisor's verbal authorization and a use-of-force report.

> **Example — Animal Call:**
>
> `/me pulls the patrol vehicle to the shoulder of Route 68 and activates the emergency lights, illuminating a deer lying in the eastbound lane.`
>
> **Deputy:** "Dispatch, 6-Baker, I've got a 10-91 on Route 68 near mile marker fourteen — deer struck by a vehicle, animal is down in the roadway. I'm going to need traffic control and animal removal. The driver's vehicle has front-end damage. I'll check on the driver first."
>
> `/me approaches the civilian vehicle, scanning for injuries through the windshield before making contact at the driver's window.`

Deputies are not wildlife officers. Our role is public safety — protect the civilians, secure the scene, and hand the animal situation off to the appropriate agency. Do not chase wildlife. Do not attempt to capture wildlife. Do not feed wildlife. This is not a petting zoo.

## Posse Authority

Under San Andreas state law, the Sheriff of any county retains the historic authority to deputize civilian volunteers in times of emergency. This authority — known as posse comitatus — dates to the earliest days of American law enforcement and remains a legal tool available to the Blaine County Sheriff.

**When Posse Authority May Be Invoked:**

- A natural disaster overwhelms the capacity of the Sheriff's Office (wildfire, earthquake, severe flooding).
- A large-scale search-and-rescue operation requires more personnel than the department can field.
- A civil emergency threatens public safety and all sworn personnel are already deployed.

**Posse authority is not invoked lightly.** Deputized civilians are granted limited law enforcement authority for the duration of the emergency only. They operate under the direct supervision of a sworn deputy at all times and are not authorized to carry out arrests, conduct searches, or use force independently. Their role is assistance — extra eyes, extra hands, extra vehicles — not independent enforcement.

Only the Sheriff or, in the Sheriff's absence, the Undersheriff may authorize the activation of posse authority. No other rank in the department holds this power.

> **Example — Posse Activation (Wildfire Emergency):**
>
> **Sheriff:** "We've got a wildfire moving east through Raton Canyon toward Grapeseed. Every sworn deputy is deployed. I'm invoking posse authority — Sergeant Dawson, deputize the volunteer fire auxiliary members at the Grapeseed staging area. They are to assist with road closures and civilian evacuation only. No enforcement action. Keep them supervised."

## Community Relationships

In Los Santos, an officer can work an entire career and never see the same face twice. In Blaine County, you will see the same faces every single day. The rancher whose fence you helped fix on Tuesday is the same man who calls you about trespassers on Friday. The waitress at the Sandy Shores diner who pours your coffee knows your name, your unit number, and whether you take sugar. That is small-town policing.

**Building Trust:**

- **Be present.** Stop at local businesses. Attend community events. Wave from the patrol vehicle. Visibility breeds familiarity, and familiarity breeds trust.
- **Be consistent.** Enforce the law fairly and equally. The moment a resident believes you gave someone else a break because you know them personally, your credibility is gone — and in a small county, word travels fast.
- **Be human.** You are not a robot in a uniform. A brief conversation about the weather, a football game, or someone's new truck goes further than a hundred PR campaigns. People cooperate with deputies they consider neighbors. They resist deputies they consider outsiders.

**The Balance:**

Small-town policing creates an inherent tension. You know these people. Some of them are your friends. And sometimes, you have to arrest your friends. The moment you let personal relationships compromise enforcement decisions, you have failed both the badge and the community. Enforce the law. Be fair about it. Explain your reasoning when appropriate. Most people in Blaine County respect a deputy who is straight with them, even when the news is bad.

> **Example — Community Contact:**
>
> `/me pulls the patrol vehicle into the gas station lot and steps out, adjusting the duty belt before walking inside.`
>
> **Deputy:** "Morning, Earl. How's business? — Listen, I wanted to give you a heads-up. We've had a few reports of somebody breaking into vehicles in this lot overnight. Might want to think about putting up a camera or two. I'll make sure night shift rolls through here more often."

## Courthouse Security

The Blaine County Sheriff's Office is responsible for the security of the Blaine County Courthouse and all judicial proceedings conducted within it. This is a core constitutional duty of the Sheriff's Office — the courts cannot function without our protection.

**Courthouse Security Duties:**

- **Building Security:** Deputies assigned to courthouse duty screen all persons entering the facility. No weapons, contraband, or prohibited items are permitted inside the courthouse without prior authorization from the presiding judge or the Sheriff.
- **Courtroom Security:** At least one armed deputy must be present in the courtroom during all proceedings. The courtroom deputy is responsible for maintaining order, securing the defendant, and responding to any disruption or threat.
- **Prisoner Transport:** Deputies transport inmates from the county detention facility to the courthouse for scheduled appearances. Prisoners are moved in restraints, via secured transport vehicle, and are held in a designated holding area within the courthouse until called before the judge.

**Transport Procedure:**

1. Verify the inmate's identity and court scheduling before departure.
2. Apply restraints — handcuffs and leg shackles for felony defendants. Handcuffs only for misdemeanor defendants unless a risk assessment dictates otherwise.
3. Transport via the most direct secure route. Notify dispatch of departure time, route, and estimated arrival.
4. Deliver the inmate to the courthouse holding area. Transfer custody documentation to the courtroom deputy.

> **Example — Prisoner Transport:**
>
> `/me approaches the holding cell with a set of transport restraints and a court appearance order.`
>
> **Deputy:** "Martinez, you've got court at 0900. Stand up, face the wall, hands behind your back. — Good. We're going to walk to the transport vehicle. You do what I tell you and this goes smooth for both of us."
>
> `/me applies handcuffs, double-locks them, and escorts the inmate down the corridor to the secured transport bay.`

## Standard Procedures

The following procedures are adapted for the unique operational environment of Blaine County. Rural policing demands flexibility, but flexibility does not mean improvisation. Know the standard. Deviate only when the situation demands it, and document your reasoning when you do.

### Traffic Stops — Rural Environment

Traffic stops on rural county roads present unique officer-safety concerns. Lighting is often nonexistent. Shoulders may be narrow, unpaved, or absent. Other motorists may not see your emergency lights until they are dangerously close.

1. Select a location with adequate shoulder space. If no safe location exists, guide the vehicle to the nearest wide spot, intersection, or pull-off.
2. Position your patrol vehicle at an offset angle to create a buffer between you and passing traffic.
3. Activate all emergency lighting. At night, use the patrol vehicle's takedown lights to illuminate the stopped vehicle's interior.
4. Notify dispatch of your location, vehicle description, plate, and number of occupants before approach.

> **Example — Rural Traffic Stop:**
>
> `/me pulls in behind the pickup truck on the shoulder of the dirt road, angling the patrol vehicle slightly left, and activates the emergency lights and spotlight.`
>
> **Deputy:** "Dispatch, 2-Adam, I'm out with a blue Bravado Bison, plate Sam-Adam-Nora-4-7-2, on Alhambra Drive south of Grapeseed. One occupant visible. Running the plate now."
>
> `/me exits the vehicle, flashlight in the support hand, and approaches the driver's side.`

### Welfare Checks — Remote Properties

Welfare checks on remote homesteads are a regular call type in Blaine County. A neighbor has not seen the resident in a week. Mail is piling up. Lights are on but nobody answers the phone. These calls require caution — the resident may be ill, injured, deceased, or they may simply not want visitors and may react poorly to a uniformed deputy on their property.

1. Attempt phone contact before responding, if a number is available.
2. Upon arrival, announce yourself loudly from outside the property. Identify yourself as a deputy.
3. Check the exterior — look for vehicles, signs of activity, open doors, broken windows, or anything unusual.
4. If no contact is made and circumstances suggest the resident may be in distress, force entry may be authorized under exigent circumstances. Contact a supervisor before making that decision if at all possible.

### Handling Armed Residents

As noted above, firearm ownership is common in Blaine County. A resident answering the door with a shotgun is not, by itself, a hostile act — it may simply be how things are done out here. Deputies must read the totality of the circumstances.

- Announce yourself clearly and repeatedly before approaching any rural property.
- If a resident is armed, remain calm, keep your hands visible, and communicate your purpose in plain language.
- Do not issue commands to drop the weapon unless the weapon is pointed at you or another person. An order to disarm a lawful gun owner on their own property without justification will destroy community trust.

## Department Culture

The Blaine County Sheriff's Office is not the LSPD, and we do not pretend to be. City cops have their world — radio cars and skyscrapers and million-dollar budgets. We have ours — patrol trucks and pine trees and making things work with what we have.

There is a pride in rural law enforcement that cannot be manufactured. BCSO deputies are generalists by necessity. A city officer may spend their entire career in one division — patrol, traffic, narcotics. A BCSO deputy handles all of it, often in the same shift. You will clear a domestic call, run radar on the highway, serve a civil summons, rescue a dog from a ravine, and investigate a burglary — all before lunch.

That versatility is our identity. We are not specialists. We are the deputy. We are the detective. We are the traffic unit. We are search and rescue. We are animal control. We are the only law for fifty miles. And we take pride in that.

BCSO deputies do not look down on city officers, and we do not tolerate city officers looking down on us. Mutual respect between agencies is expected. But make no mistake — when an LSPD officer gets lost north of the Tongva Hills and calls for help, it is a BCSO deputy who shows up. Remember that.

## Final Thoughts

Wearing the star in Blaine County is not a stepping stone to something bigger. It is the job. Some deputies build entire careers out here and retire with thirty years of dust on their boots and the respect of every person in the county. That is not a consolation prize. That is the highest achievement in this profession — serving a community so well and so long that they consider you one of their own.

You will work alone more than you work with partners. You will make hard decisions with no supervisor on scene and no policy manual in your hand. You will drive hours of empty highway and then walk into absolute chaos with nothing but your training, your judgment, and your nerve. The county does not care about your academy scores or your résumé. It cares about what you do when the radio crackles and you are the only unit available.

Be worthy of the star. Serve the county. Protect the people. Uphold the law. And when the shift is over, drive home knowing you did it right.

Welcome to the Blaine County Sheriff's Office.
