# Detective Guide — Following the Evidence

Patrol answers the call. Patrol secures the scene. Patrol takes the initial report, strings the yellow tape, and holds the perimeter until the situation is stable. But when the lights stop flashing and the uniforms clear out, someone has to answer the question that remains — who did this, and how do we prove it?

That is the detective's job.

Detective work is the backbone of criminal justice roleplay. Without investigators, crimes happen and nobody is held accountable. Shootings become disconnected events with no storyline. Drug operations run unopposed because nobody builds a case. Murders go unsolved because nobody follows up after the body is transported to the coroner. A good detective transforms isolated incidents into layered, ongoing narratives — connecting suspects, motives, evidence, and witnesses into cases that drive server-wide roleplay for days, weeks, or months.

This guide covers the investigative process from crime scene to courtroom. If you are assigned to a detective bureau, this is your operational handbook. If you are patrol working a scene before detectives arrive, this tells you what we need from you and why the first ten minutes matter more than the next ten hours. Know it. Use it.

---

## What a Detective Does

A detective is not a patrol officer with a suit and an unmarked car. The role is fundamentally different. Patrol is reactive — you respond, you stabilize, you move to the next call. Detective work is proactive and methodical. You receive a case, and that case belongs to you until it is closed, whether that takes two hours or two months.

**The investigative mindset:**

- **Observe everything.** Patrol sees a dead body in an alley behind Innocence Boulevard. A detective sees the blood trail leading east, the shell casing near the dumpster, the security camera on the adjacent liquor store, and the witness peering through the blinds three floors up.
- **Question everything.** The girlfriend says he was home all night. The bartender at the Vanilla Unicorn says he left at midnight with two men she did not recognize. Someone is lying. Find out who and why.
- **Document everything.** If it is not in your report, it did not happen. Every conversation, every piece of evidence, every lead followed or dismissed — it goes on paper.
- **Be patient.** Cases are not solved in a single scene. They are built over time through persistence, follow-up, and attention to details that everyone else overlooked.

Detectives operate out of the detective bureau — typically housed within Mission Row PD or the Blaine County Sheriff's Office, depending on jurisdiction. You carry a badge, a sidearm, and a notebook. Your most important weapon is your mind.

## Case Management

Every investigation begins with a case. Every case gets a number. No exceptions.

**Opening a case:**

When a crime is reported or discovered that requires investigation beyond the initial patrol response, a case is opened. The detective assigned to the case becomes the lead investigator. The case number is the reference point for all reports, evidence logs, warrants, and court filings associated with that investigation.

| Case Element | Description |
|---|---|
| **Case Number** | A unique identifier assigned when the case is opened. Format varies by department — commonly year-sequence (e.g., 2024-0347). Every document, evidence bag, and report references this number. |
| **Lead Investigator** | The detective assigned primary responsibility. This is your case. You own it — the good, the bad, and the cold. |
| **Case Status** | Active, Inactive, Suspended, or Closed. Active means leads are being pursued. Inactive means leads have dried up but the case is not formally closed. Suspended means the investigation is paused pending new information. Closed means an arrest was made and the case was filed with the DA, or the case was closed administratively. |
| **Priority Level** | Homicides and violent crimes take priority. Property crimes are investigated but do not supersede active threat-to-life cases. |
| **Case Board** | A physical or digital board tracking all active cases — suspect photos, evidence summaries, connections between cases, and investigative timelines. The case board is your war room. |

Track your cases. A detective juggling six investigations who loses track of a witness statement or forgets to follow up on a forensic result is a detective who lets criminals walk. Use the case board. Update it daily.

## Crime Scene Processing

The crime scene is where the case is won or lost. Evidence destroyed by careless boots, contaminated by untrained hands, or overlooked because nobody looked closely enough is evidence that never makes it to court. Treat every scene like the case depends on it — because it does.

**Securing the scene:**

1. Establish a perimeter. Nobody enters who does not have a reason to be there.
2. Identify and separate witnesses before they can coordinate stories.
3. Document the scene as found — before anything is touched, moved, or collected.
4. Assign a scene log. Every person who enters and exits the perimeter is recorded with name, badge number, time in, and time out.

**Documentation:**

Before you touch a single piece of evidence, you photograph and sketch the entire scene. Every angle. Every detail. The position of the body. The location of shell casings relative to the victim. The broken window. The overturned furniture. Blood spatter patterns on the wall. You are preserving this scene on paper because the physical scene will be cleaned up, rained on, or walked through by tomorrow.

> **Example — Processing a crime scene:**
>
> `/me arrives at the alley behind Innocence Boulevard, clips on latex gloves, and surveys the scene from the perimeter tape. Notes the position of the victim — male, face down, apparent gunshot wound to the upper back. Begins photographing the scene from the east end of the alley, capturing wide shots before moving in for detail photography of the body, the blood pooling, and the surrounding area.`
>
> `/me places a yellow evidence marker — number one — beside a brass shell casing located six feet northeast of the victim. Photographs the casing in place with the marker visible. Notes the position in the scene sketch: "Marker 1 — spent casing, brass, approx. 9mm, 6 ft NE of victim, adjacent to dumpster."`
>
> `/me sketches the alley layout on a notepad — building walls, dumpster placement, victim position, evidence markers, entry and exit points. Labels compass directions and estimated distances between key items.`

Take your time. A thorough scene takes thirty minutes to an hour of focused roleplay. Rush it and you miss something. Miss something and the defense attorney will make sure a jury hears about it.

## Evidence Collection

Evidence comes in three forms, and a good detective knows how to find, collect, and preserve all of them.

| Evidence Type | Examples | Collection Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Physical** | Shell casings, weapons, blood, fibers, fingerprints, tire marks, clothing, narcotics, spent rounds, broken glass | Collected with gloves, placed in appropriate containers (paper bags for biological, plastic for non-biological), sealed and labeled with case number, date, time, collector's name, and description. |
| **Digital** | Phone records, CCTV footage, text messages, financial records, GPS data | Subpoenaed or obtained via warrant. Devices are seized and secured — never browse a suspect's phone at the scene without a warrant. |
| **Testimonial** | Witness statements, suspect statements, victim accounts, confidential informant tips | Documented in writing or recorded. Witness credibility is assessed and noted. Statements are taken separately — never in a group. |

**Chain of custody** is everything. From the moment evidence is collected to the moment it is presented in court, every hand it passes through is logged. A break in the chain — evidence left unattended, an unsigned transfer form, an unsealed bag — gives the defense grounds to have it thrown out.

> **Example — Collecting evidence:**
>
> `/me kneels beside evidence marker three, photographs the blood droplet pattern on the concrete, then uses a sterile swab to collect a sample. Places the swab in a sealed evidence tube, labels it: "Case 2024-0347, Marker 3, blood sample, east wall base, collected by Det. [Name], [date/time]." Logs the item on the evidence collection sheet.`
>
> `/me carefully lifts the semi-automatic pistol found beneath the dumpster using gloved hands, checks that the weapon is safe — magazine removed, chamber cleared — and places it in a paper evidence bag. Seals the bag with red evidence tape and initials the seal. Notes: "Case 2024-0347, Marker 7, Pistol — semi-auto, black, serial partially filed. Found beneath dumpster, 2 ft west of victim."`

Every piece of evidence tells part of the story. Your job is to collect enough pieces that the story becomes undeniable.

## Witness Interviews

Witnesses are unreliable, emotional, frightened, and sometimes lying. They are also the most valuable source of information you have. A detective who cannot interview a witness effectively is a detective who misses half the case.

**Approach:**

- Introduce yourself calmly. Show your badge. Explain that you are investigating the incident and that their cooperation is important.
- Put the witness at ease. A scared witness gives you nothing. A comfortable witness gives you details they did not even realize they remembered.
- Ask **open-ended questions**. "Tell me what you saw" produces more information than "Did you see the shooter?" One invites a narrative. The other invites a yes or no.
- Do not lead the witness. Do not suggest answers. Do not show them suspect photos before they have described the suspect in their own words.
- Document the statement in writing, including the witness's name, contact information, date, time, and location of the interview.

> **Example — Witness Interview IC Dialogue:**
>
> **Detective:** "Ma'am, I'm Detective [Name] with the LSPD. I understand you may have seen what happened out here tonight. I'd like to hear what you can tell me — in your own words, just start from the beginning."
>
> **Witness:** "I was on my balcony having a cigarette. I heard shouting — two men, sounded like they were arguing. Then I heard three pops. I looked down and saw someone running east toward the main road."
>
> **Detective:** "Can you describe the person you saw running? Anything at all — height, build, clothing, the way they moved?"
>
> **Witness:** "Tall. Hoodie — dark, maybe black or navy. He was fast. I think he got into a car but I couldn't see what kind from my angle."
>
> **Detective:** "That's helpful. Did you notice anything else before the shouting started? Any other people in the area, vehicles parked nearby, anything out of the ordinary?"

Never dismiss a witness because their information seems minor. The woman who noticed a blue sedan parked on the wrong side of the street at 11 PM might be handing you the suspect's vehicle.

## Interrogation Techniques

Interrogation is not an interview. An interview seeks information. An interrogation seeks the truth from someone who has a reason to hide it. You do not interrogate a suspect until you have done your homework — evidence collected, witnesses interviewed, timeline established. Walk into that room prepared, or do not walk in at all.

**Before the interrogation:**

- **Know your case.** Review every piece of evidence, every witness statement, every inconsistency. The suspect will lie. You need to know the truth well enough to catch the lies.
- **Miranda rights.** If the suspect is in custody, they are Mirandized before any questioning. No exceptions. A confession obtained without Miranda is worthless in court and a stain on your investigation.
- **Have a plan.** Know what you need the suspect to confirm or deny. Know which evidence you will reveal and which you will hold back. An interrogation is a conversation you control.

**Techniques:**

| Technique | Description |
|---|---|
| **Building rapport** | Start conversational. Offer water. Use the suspect's first name. Make them comfortable enough to talk before you start pressing. A suspect who sees you as a human being is more likely to talk than one who sees you as the enemy. |
| **The evidence reveal** | Present evidence strategically — not all at once. Let the suspect commit to a story, then introduce the evidence that contradicts it. "You said you were not at the scene. Can you explain why your fingerprints are on the fire escape railing?" |
| **Minimization** | Downplay the severity to encourage admission. "I don't think you went out there planning to kill anyone. Things got out of hand, didn't they? Help me understand what happened." This is not deception — it is a framework that allows the suspect to confess without feeling cornered. |
| **Good cop / bad cop** | Two detectives, two approaches. One is aggressive, impatient, and skeptical. The other is understanding, patient, and sympathetic. The contrast encourages the suspect to bond with the "friendly" detective and open up. Overused in media but still effective when executed with subtlety. |
| **Silence** | Say nothing. Let the suspect sit in the quiet. People are uncomfortable with silence and will fill it — often with information they did not intend to share. |

> **Example — Interrogation IC Dialogue:**
>
> **Detective 1:** "Marcus, I'm going to be straight with you. We've got witnesses. We've got forensics. We've got camera footage from Vespucci Boulevard that puts you three blocks from the scene at 11:42 PM. You told the officers who picked you up that you were home all night. That doesn't line up."
>
> **Suspect:** "I want a lawyer."
>
> **Detective 1:** "That's your right, and we'll get one here for you. But I want you to understand something before you make that call — we're going to file charges tonight either way. The evidence doesn't need your confession. I'm giving you a chance to tell your side of the story. Once the lawyer shows up, that window closes and the DA works with what we've got."
>
> **Detective 2:** "Marcus, look — nobody is saying you're a bad person. People get into situations. Things escalate. If this was self-defense, if there's something we don't know, now is the time to tell us. We can't help you if we don't know what happened."

If the suspect invokes their right to an attorney, the interrogation stops. Immediately. No tricks, no "just one more question," no casual conversation designed to elicit a response. Miranda is not a suggestion.

## Surveillance Operations

Sometimes the case requires watching, not asking. Surveillance is the art of being present without being noticed — gathering information on a suspect's movements, associates, and activities without alerting them that they are under investigation.

**Setting up surveillance:**

- **Location selection.** Find a position with a clear line of sight on the target — their residence, their business, their known hangouts. The position must be concealed. A detective sitting in a Crown Vic across the street from a suspect's house in Davis is not conducting surveillance — he is advertising it.
- **Unmarked vehicles.** Use nondescript vehicles that blend into the neighborhood. No police-package sedans with exempt plates and a spotlight on the A-pillar. A used Bravado or a beat-up Declasse blends in. A polished black Benefactor does not.
- **Photography.** Document who the suspect meets, when, and where. License plates of vehicles that visit the location. Photos are timestamped and logged.
- **Activity log.** Every observation is recorded: time, description of activity, individuals involved, vehicles, direction of travel. The log is evidence.

**Tailing a suspect:**

Follow at a distance. Vary your position. Do not make the same turn as the suspect three times in a row. If you are burned — if the suspect has identified your vehicle or made deliberate counter-surveillance moves — pull off and report. A burned tail compromises the entire investigation. It is better to lose the suspect for a day than to lose the case permanently.

> `/me parks the unmarked Stanier on the east side of Strawberry Avenue, fifty yards south of the target address. Adjusts the rearview mirror for a view of the front entrance. Begins the surveillance log: "1845 hrs — On station. Target residence shows lights on, ground floor. Blue Bravado Buffalo parked in the driveway, plate [number]. No visible activity."`
>
> `/me observes a white four-door sedan pull up to the target address at 1923 hrs. Photographs the vehicle and occupant — male, mid-thirties, dark jacket — as he exits and approaches the front door. Logs: "1923 hrs — White sedan arrives, one male occupant enters residence. Plate photographed."`

## Building Probable Cause

Probable cause is the legal threshold required to obtain search warrants, arrest warrants, and to make warrantless arrests in certain circumstances. Without it, your evidence is inadmissible and your arrest is unlawful. A detective who cannot articulate probable cause has no business requesting a warrant.

**What constitutes probable cause:**

- Facts and circumstances that would lead a reasonable person to believe a crime has been committed and the suspect committed it.
- Direct evidence: eyewitness identification, physical evidence linking the suspect to the scene, surveillance placing the suspect at the location.
- Circumstantial evidence: motive, opportunity, behavior consistent with guilt, statements inconsistent with known facts.
- Informant tips — corroborated by independent investigation. A CI's word alone is not sufficient; their information must be verified.

**Warrant applications:**

When you have established probable cause, you draft an affidavit — a sworn written statement detailing the facts of the investigation, the evidence collected, and the specific items or locations to be searched. The affidavit is presented to a judge, who reviews it and issues the warrant if probable cause is established.

> **Example — Warrant Request IC Scenario:**
>
> **Detective:** "Your Honor, based on the investigation to date — including witness testimony placing the suspect's vehicle at the scene, ballistic evidence matching a firearm registered to the suspect, and surveillance confirming the suspect's presence at the victim's residence within the hour prior to the homicide — I am requesting a search warrant for the suspect's residence at 4232 Amarillo Vista, Davis, to search for the firearm, clothing matching witness descriptions, and any additional evidence related to case 2024-0347."

Be specific. A warrant that says "search for evidence of a crime" is a fishing expedition and no judge worth their robe will sign it.

## Working with Prosecutors

Your investigation means nothing if it cannot survive a courtroom. The District Attorney's office is your partner in bringing a case to trial, and that partnership starts long before the courtroom doors open.

**Preparing cases for trial:**

- **Evidence package.** Compile all evidence — physical, digital, testimonial — in an organized case file. Label everything. Cross-reference evidence with the reports that document its collection. A prosecutor who has to hunt through a disorganized box of reports for a critical witness statement will not be happy, and an unhappy DA is a DA who does not call you for cases.
- **Witness list.** Identify all witnesses and their expected testimony. Note any credibility concerns — prior convictions, inconsistencies in their statements, potential bias.
- **Timeline.** Build a clear, chronological timeline of the crime, the investigation, and the evidence collected. The jury needs a narrative, not a pile of facts.
- **Testimony preparation.** You will take the stand. Know your case inside and out. Anticipate defense questions. Answer truthfully, clearly, and without embellishment. A detective who exaggerates on the stand destroys the case and their career.

**DA coordination:**

Meet with the assigned prosecutor early. Walk them through the case. Identify weaknesses before the defense does. If the prosecutor needs additional evidence or follow-up interviews, handle it promptly. A case that goes to trial with gaps is a case that ends in acquittal.

## Cold Cases

Not every case is solved in a week. Some go cold — leads dry up, witnesses disappear, evidence sits in a box on a shelf. A cold case is not a closed case. It is a case waiting for the break it needs.

**Reopening a cold case:**

- New evidence emerges — a weapon recovered in an unrelated arrest matches ballistics from the cold case.
- A witness comes forward years later — guilt, a falling out with the suspect, or a deathbed confession.
- Advances in forensic technology allow re-examination of evidence that was previously inconclusive.
- A suspect arrested for a different crime is linked through DNA, fingerprints, or witness identification.

> **Example — Cold Case IC Scenario:**
>
> **Detective:** "Sarge, I've been going through the 2022 unsolveds. Case 2022-0118 — the shooting behind the Maze Bank Arena parking structure. We had a 9mm casing at the scene, no match at the time. Narcotics just seized a Browning Hi-Power off a Vagos associate in Rancho last week. I ran the ballistics — it's a match. Same gun. I want to reopen the case and bring this guy in for questioning."
>
> **Sergeant:** "Pull the full case file, re-interview the original witnesses if you can locate them, and coordinate with Narcotics on everything they have on this associate. If the ballistics hold up, draft a warrant and walk it over to the DA. Good catch, detective."

Cold cases reward patience and persistence. The detective who revisits an unsolved file when everyone else has moved on is the detective who brings closure to a family that stopped hoping.

## Undercover Basics

Undercover work is the most dangerous assignment a detective can accept. You operate without backup in arm's reach, without a badge visible, and without the authority that comes with being identified as law enforcement. One mistake — a wrong word, a recognized face, a nervous glance — and you are in a situation that no amount of training can guarantee you walk away from.

**Building a cover identity:**

- Your cover must be simple and believable. A name, a background story, a reason for being where you are. Do not overcomplicate it. The more elaborate the lie, the easier it is to get caught in it.
- Know your cover inside and out. Where you "grew up." What you "do for a living." Why you are "new in town." If someone asks a question you cannot answer, your cover is blown.

**Rules of undercover work:**

- Never commit a violent crime to maintain cover. There are legal and ethical lines that cannot be crossed regardless of the investigation's importance.
- Maintain a check-in schedule with your handler. If you miss a check-in, your team assumes the worst and begins extraction protocols.
- Do not carry your badge, police ID, or anything that identifies you as law enforcement. A pat-down that reveals a badge is a death sentence in certain circles.
- Know your extraction signal and your emergency protocol before you go in. If things go wrong, you need to get out — not figure out how to get out.

**When to pull out:**

- Your cover is compromised or you believe it is compromised.
- You have gathered sufficient evidence to support warrants and arrests.
- The risk to your safety exceeds the investigative value of continued operation.
- Your handler orders you out. That order is not negotiable.

## Forensic Awareness

A detective does not need to be a forensic scientist, but you need to understand what forensics can tell you and how to request it. Knowing what questions to ask the lab is half the battle.

| Forensic Discipline | What It Tells You |
|---|---|
| **Ballistics** | Matches bullets and casings to specific firearms. Determines caliber, weapon type, and whether the same gun was used in multiple incidents. |
| **Blood Spatter Analysis** | Reveals the position of the victim and attacker at the time of injury, the type of weapon used, the number of blows or shots, and the sequence of events. |
| **Fingerprint Analysis** | Places a specific individual at a specific location. Latent prints lifted from surfaces are compared against known prints in the database. |
| **Toxicology** | Identifies substances in a victim's or suspect's system — drugs, alcohol, poisons. Relevant for cause of death and suspect impairment. |
| **DNA Analysis** | Links biological evidence (blood, saliva, hair) to a specific individual. The strongest form of forensic identification. |
| **Digital Forensics** | Recovers data from phones, computers, and other devices — deleted messages, call logs, GPS history, financial transactions. |

**Requesting forensic analysis:**

Submit evidence to the lab with a clear request specifying what you need analyzed and why. "Test this for everything" is not a request — it is laziness. "Requesting ballistic comparison of the recovered 9mm casing (Case 2024-0347, Evidence Item 1) against the Browning Hi-Power seized in Case 2024-0512" is a request that gets results.

## Case Filing and Documentation

Paperwork is not optional. It is not a chore to be done later. It is the record of your investigation, and if your documentation is incomplete, inaccurate, or missing, your case falls apart in court.

**Case file contents:**

- Initial incident report from patrol.
- Supplemental reports from the lead detective documenting every investigative step taken.
- Evidence collection logs with chain of custody documentation.
- Witness statements — written and signed.
- Suspect interview and interrogation records.
- Forensic analysis reports.
- Surveillance logs and photographs.
- Warrant applications and returns.
- Correspondence with the DA's office.
- Case status updates and disposition.

**Closing a case:**

A case is closed when an arrest has been made and the case has been filed with the District Attorney, or when the case has been closed administratively (e.g., victim declines to prosecute, suspect is deceased, statute of limitations has expired). The closing report summarizes the investigation, the outcome, and the disposition of all evidence.

> `/me sits at the desk in the Mission Row detective bureau, opens the case file for 2024-0347, and begins drafting the closing report. Types the summary: "Investigation initiated following the discovery of a deceased male in the alley behind 4500 Innocence Boulevard on [date]. Through witness testimony, forensic evidence, and suspect interrogation, the investigation identified and arrested [suspect name] for Murder in the First Degree. Case filed with the San Andreas District Attorney's Office on [date]. All evidence transferred to DA custody. Case status: Closed — Arrest Made."`

Write your reports as if a stranger will read them in five years and need to understand exactly what happened. Because that is exactly what will happen.

## The Detective's Toolkit

You are not patrol. You do not roll with a duty belt full of less-lethal options and a rifle in the trunk. Your tools are different because your job is different.

| Item | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Badge and ID** | Plain-clothes means your badge is your identification. Carry it on a belt clip or neck chain where it can be displayed immediately when you identify yourself. |
| **Sidearm** | Concealed carry. Shoulder holster, hip holster, or appendix carry — whatever you can draw from reliably under a jacket or shirt. |
| **Notebook** | Your most critical tool. Every detail — names, dates, times, observations, witness statements, suspect descriptions — goes in the notebook. Digital notes are fine as a backup, but the notebook is primary. |
| **Camera** | For crime scene documentation, surveillance photography, and evidence photography. Phone cameras work but a dedicated camera produces better results. |
| **Evidence Kit** | Latex gloves, evidence bags (paper and plastic), evidence tape, markers, swabs, and a Sharpie. You should be able to process a scene with what you carry. |
| **Unmarked Vehicle** | A vehicle that does not scream "police." No light bar, no exempt plates, no push bumper. Something that blends into traffic and neighborhoods. Maintain it — a filthy car with fast-food wrappers on the dash does not project professionalism. |
| **Case Files** | Physical or digital copies of your active cases. You never know when a lead will develop and you need to reference a detail from the file while in the field. |
| **Recorder** | For recording witness statements and suspect interviews with consent or as required by department policy. |

## Final Thoughts

Detective work is not glamorous. It is not the dramatic interrogation scenes or the cinematic foot chase through Vinewood. It is sitting at a desk at 2 AM reviewing phone records for the third time because something does not add up. It is knocking on doors in Strawberry for the fourth day in a row because the witness who saw everything will not answer her phone. It is staring at a case board full of threads that do not connect yet — and trusting that they will if you keep pulling.

The best detectives are not the smartest people in the room. They are the most persistent. They are the ones who refuse to let a case die, who follow the evidence wherever it leads even when it leads somewhere inconvenient, and who understand that justice is not always fast but it must always be thorough.

Carry your notebook. Trust the evidence. Follow up on everything. And when the case breaks — when the last piece of evidence clicks into place and you know who did it and you can prove it — that is why you do this job.
