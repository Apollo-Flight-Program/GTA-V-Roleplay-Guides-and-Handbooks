## Part I: Tactical Philosophy

### 1.1 Mission of the Tactical Operations Division

The TOD is the last resort. Before a tactical deployment, every other tool should have been considered: an arrest during a traffic stop, a controlled meeting, a voluntary surrender negotiated by investigators. Tactical force is employed only when those options are not viable — when the target's location is known and fixed, when the threat to the public requires immediate action, or when the nature of the target's capabilities makes less intrusive approaches insufficient.

Operators are expected to understand this context. The TOD exists to solve problems that cannot be solved any other way. Understanding why tactical force is warranted — rather than treating it as a default option — makes operators more effective and the Task Force more legitimate.

### 1.2 Core Tactical Principles

**Speed, Surprise, and Violence of Action:** High-risk operations succeed when they are conducted faster than the target can react, from angles and at times the target does not anticipate, and with the decisive commitment that denies the target the ability to resist. These are not independent elements — they work together, and weakness in any one element increases risk to personnel and mission.

**Positive Control:** Every operator knows the plan, their position, their sector of responsibility, and the signals that govern movement. Operations conducted without positive control produce fratricide, missed targets, and documentation failures that destroy prosecutions. Discipline and communication are not obstacles to speed — they are what makes speed sustainable.

**Minimize Casualties:** The objective of a CTTF tactical operation is to take the target into custody or neutralize the threat. It is not to achieve maximum force application. Casualties — to operators, to subjects, to civilians — represent failures of planning and execution. Every operational plan must have a casualty minimization assessment and a medical plan.

**Documentation:** Every step of a tactical operation — pre-operation intelligence, the plan, the execution, what was found, what happened — is documented. Documentation is not bureaucratic overhead. It is what turns a successful operation into a successful prosecution, and what protects operators when their actions are reviewed.

### 1.3 Rules of Engagement

All TOD operations are conducted within the SACTF Use of Force Policy (see `18-Use-of-Force-Policy.md`) and applicable law. Rules of Engagement (ROE) are specified in each operational plan and are briefed to all participants before deployment.

**No ROE variation is authorized without command approval.** Operators who receive instructions in the field that conflict with the approved ROE must immediately seek clarification from the on-scene commander.

## Part II: Operational Categories

### 2.1 Deliberate Operations

**Definition:** Pre-planned, intelligence-driven operations with significant preparation time — typically 24 hours or more from decision to execution.

**Characteristics:**
- Full intelligence picture available
- Multiple rehearsals conducted
- Complete operational plan documented and approved
- All required resources in position before initiation
- Legal authorizations (warrants) obtained and reviewed
- Contingency plans developed for foreseeable complications

**Standard:** Most TOD operations should be deliberate. Intelligence-led counterterrorism is inherently deliberate. When operations feel rushed, it is usually because the intelligence drive has been compromised or command pressure is creating inappropriate urgency.

**When Used:**
- Arrest operations where target location is established and stable
- Search warrant executions at known locations
- Pre-planned interdiction at known time and place
- Facility takedowns where entry is required to secure the location

### 2.2 Time-Sensitive Operations (TSO)

**Definition:** Operations that must be executed within a window that does not permit full deliberate planning — typically under 6 hours.

**Characteristics:**
- Intelligence picture may be incomplete
- Planning cycle is abbreviated but not eliminated
- Rehearsals limited by time
- Contingency plans cover primary foreseeable complications only
- Command approval required at higher threshold than deliberate operations

**Risk:** TSOs carry higher operational risk than deliberate operations. Command authority must affirmatively decide that the operational opportunity or threat justification outweighs the increased risk from abbreviated planning.

**When Used:**
- Developing threat intelligence indicating imminent attack
- Target location identified under circumstances suggesting the window will close
- Active pursuit/containment requiring immediate tactical response

### 2.3 No-Notice Operations

**Definition:** Immediate tactical response to a developing critical incident — active shooter, hostage situation, attack in progress.

**Characteristics:**
- No pre-operation planning
- Structured around immediate threat response protocols and trained muscle memory
- Command authority is the on-scene commander, operating under standing authorization
- Operations transition to deliberate character as quickly as the situation permits

**Standing Protocols:** TOD trains extensively on no-notice response scenarios to ensure that the absence of a specific operational plan does not mean the absence of a coherent operational approach. Protocols govern initial actions, communications, perimeter establishment, and transition to deliberate operations.

## Part III: Operation Planning Process

### 3.1 The Eight-Step Planning Process

**Step 1: Receive and Analyze the Mission**  
What is the objective? What are the constraints and limitations? Who has approved the mission and at what authority level? What is the tactical problem to be solved?

**Step 2: Issue Warning Order**  
Notify all elements that a mission is developing. Provide initial guidance on timeline and available information. This step allows subordinate elements to begin preparation without waiting for the completed plan.

**Step 3: Make a Tentative Plan**  
The Team Leader and senior operators develop the initial course of action — entry points, movement routes, roles, contingencies. This is a working document, not the final plan.

**Step 4: Initiate Movement**  
If reconnaissance, pre-positioning, or forward staging is required, begin those activities while planning continues. Do not allow planning to delay necessary preliminary movement.

**Step 5: Conduct Reconnaissance**  
Confirm the intelligence picture with as much direct observation as feasible. Validate entry points. Confirm target presence where required. Identify hazards that intelligence may not have captured.

**Step 6: Complete the Plan**  
Finalize the operational plan incorporating reconnaissance results. Address contingencies. Prepare the intelligence summary for briefing. Prepare documentation packages for post-operation reporting.

**Step 7: Issue the Operations Order (OPORD)**  
Brief all participating personnel. The standard OPORD format covers:
- *Situation:* What we know about the target environment
- *Mission:* What we are doing, in a single clear sentence
- *Execution:* How each element accomplishes the mission
- *Administration and Logistics:* Resources, medical plan, prisoner handling
- *Command and Signal:* Command relationships, communications plan, signals

**Step 8: Supervise and Refine**  
Conduct rehearsals. Review the plan against current intelligence. Make final adjustments. Confirm all personnel understand their assignments.

### 3.2 Intelligence Requirements for Operational Planning

An operational plan is only as good as the intelligence it is built on. Before approving a tactical operation, command must confirm that the following intelligence requirements have been addressed:

**Target confirmation:** Is the target present at the specified location? What is the basis for that assessment and how current is it?

**Physical environment:** What is the layout of the target location? How many entry and exit points? What are the interior configurations? What are the fields of observation and fire?

**Threat assessment:** How many individuals are expected? What weapons or capabilities do they likely have? What is the expected response to law enforcement presence?

**Civilian presence:** Are there individuals at the target location who are not subjects — family members, employees, uninvolved occupants? What is the expected response of those individuals?

**Adjacent considerations:** What is the environment around the target location? Adjacent buildings, public spaces, traffic patterns? What are the implications for perimeter establishment and civilian safety?

## Part IV: Team Tactics and Formations

### 4.1 Movement Formations

**Stack:** Primary tactical formation for approach to and entry of a structure. Operators move in a single-file column, typically with 1-2 meter spacing. Each operator covers a designated sector. The stack is oriented to respond immediately to threats from any direction.

**Standard stack order for structure entry:**
1. *Breacher:* Responsible for defeating entry obstacles (locks, doors, barriers)
2. *Point:* First operator through the entry point; clears immediate threat area
3. *Cover:* Second operator through; provides immediate support to Point
4. *Additional operators:* Enter in sequence; each assigned to expand the cleared area
5. *Rear security:* Last operator in stack; maintains rear security and manages the entry point

**Modified Wedge:** Used during approach in open areas where 360-degree security is required. Operators are positioned at angles from the lead operator, providing overlapping sectors of coverage.

**Line:** Used when a broad front is tactically advantageous — clearing a long exterior wall, covering a large open space. Provides maximum forward coverage with reduced depth security.

**File with Security:** Standard movement formation in urban terrain. Lead operator and rear security provide movement security; internal operators maintain intervals and cover assigned sectors.

### 4.2 Building Entry — Dynamic Entry

**Definition:** High-speed, low-warning entry designed to overwhelm the target's ability to react before the tactical team gains control of the entry point and initial spaces.

**When Used:**
- Targets assessed to have sufficient warning and weapons to resist a deliberate approach
- Time-critical situations where delay creates unacceptable threat escalation
- When element of surprise is the primary tactical advantage

**Entry Sequence:**
1. **Breach:** The breacher defeats the entry obstacle. Mechanical breach (battering ram, Halligan tool) is preferred when noise discipline is not critical. Ballistic breach is authorized when mechanical breach is not feasible and is documented.
2. **Flash-bang deployment** (when authorized in ROE): Distraction device deployed immediately before or concurrent with entry to degrade target awareness and reaction capability.
3. **Initial entry:** Point operator enters immediately following breach/distraction, moving to assigned coverage position.
4. **Stack flow:** Remaining operators enter in rapid succession. Each moves to a designated position; no operator stops in the fatal funnel (the immediate entry area).
5. **Announcement:** "POLICE — SEARCH WARRANT" or equivalent announcement is made at entry. Announcement timing is specified in the operational plan.
6. **Immediate control:** The initial entry team secures the immediate entry space and establishes the control point for subsequent clearing.

**Fatal Funnel:** The doorway and immediate threshold area. Movement through the fatal funnel is done at maximum speed. Operators do not pause, hesitate, or crowd in the fatal funnel. Training is designed to build the reflex to move through the fatal funnel, not stand in it.

### 4.3 Building Entry — Deliberate Entry

**Definition:** Controlled, methodical entry used when surprise is not required or not achievable, and the operational priority is thorough control rather than speed.

**When Used:**
- Serving warrant on cooperative or non-violent subject
- Entering abandoned or unoccupied structures for intelligence or evidence purposes
- Transitioning from containment to controlled entry in a barricaded subject situation

**Entry Sequence:**
1. Announcement from covered position
2. Allow reasonable compliance time
3. Controlled approach to entry point
4. Methodical entry with full sector coverage maintained at all points
5. Progressive clearing from entry point through each space

### 4.4 Room Clearing — Standard Procedure

**T-Method for Two-Operator Entry:**

1. Operators position on either side of doorway (the "button-up" position) — outside the fatal funnel
2. On signal, lead operator crosses the doorway threshold at the low ready position, moving laterally to cover the far corner of the room ("buttonhook" — moving into the room on the same side as your entry button-up position)
3. Second operator crosses the threshold simultaneously, moving to cover the opposite far corner (cross-through method)
4. Both operators reach their designated coverage positions; announce "CLEAR" or threat status
5. Room is only declared clear when both operators have visual coverage of all concealment and both confirm no threat

**Corner Clearing:**
Every room has two near corners (beside the entry doorway) and two far corners (across the room). The near corners must be cleared before operators enter through the doorway — operators "slice the pie" from outside the doorway to clear near corners before exposing themselves to entry.

**Closets, bathrooms, and concealed spaces:** Every concealed space is treated as occupied until confirmed empty. No room is declared clear until all concealed spaces have been physically verified.

### 4.5 Multi-Story Structures

**General principle:** Clear from bottom to top in most configurations. Top-down clearing is authorized in specific circumstances (e.g., target known to be on upper floor; lower floor confirmed clear by other means) and must be specified in the operational plan.

**Staircase approach:**
- At least one operator maintains security at the base of the staircase during all clearing operations above.
- Staircase approach uses compressed stack; each landing is confirmed clear before proceeding up.
- Operator on point covers up; next operator covers the prior landing.

**Transition between floors:** The transition from clearing one floor to approaching the next is a high-risk moment. The team briefly pauses to consolidate, communicate, and confirm before committing to the staircase.

## Part V: Perimeter Operations

### 5.1 Inner Perimeter

**Purpose:** Contain the target area. Prevent subjects from leaving the target location.

**Establishment:** The inner perimeter is established by Bravo Team before Alpha Team initiates entry in deliberate operations. In dynamic situations, inner perimeter establishment may be concurrent with entry team movement.

**Coverage:** Inner perimeter positions are established at all viable exit points from the target structure and at coverage positions for windows and secondary access. Positions are specified in the operational plan based on reconnaissance.

**Communication:** All inner perimeter positions maintain continuous radio communication with the on-scene commander. Any movement at the target structure — prior to or during entry — is immediately reported.

### 5.2 Outer Perimeter

**Purpose:** Prevent civilian access to the operational area. Establish a buffer between the tactical operation and the public.

**Establishment:** Outer perimeter is typically established by LSPD patrol officers or SASP troopers working in support of the SACTF operation. The outer perimeter is established before inner perimeter whenever possible.

**Size:** The outer perimeter must be sufficiently large to provide meaningful safety buffer and prevent civilians from observing tactical approach. Minimum distances are specified by the TOD Commander based on the specific environment.

### 5.3 Perimeter Communications

**Radio discipline on perimeter:** Perimeter operators report significant observations immediately and concisely. Non-essential radio traffic is eliminated during active tactical phases. The operational net has one frequency; critical traffic is flagged with the prefix "PRIORITY."

**Subject escape from perimeter:** If a subject exits the target location before or during entry, the perimeter operator who spots the subject immediately announces direction of travel, description, and any observable threat indicators. Pursuit decisions are made by the on-scene commander, not by individual perimeter operators.

## Part VI: Tactical Communications

### 6.1 Frequency Plan

Each operation has a designated frequency plan with the following elements:
- **Tactical Net (TACT):** Primary communication for all tactical operators during the operation
- **Command Net (CMD):** Communication between on-scene commander and supervisory elements
- **Perimeter Net (PERIM):** Communication among perimeter elements
- **Support Net (SUP):** Coordination with supporting elements (outer perimeter, LSPD support, EMS staging)

### 6.2 Communications Discipline

- Report necessary information; do not narrate
- Standard brevity codes are used on the tactical net
- Numbers and locations are given in reference to the briefed grid/designation system
- Operators acknowledge receipt of transmissions with brevity codes (confirmed, negative, etc.)
- "Break, break, break" is the emergency interruption signal; only used for urgent safety or mission-critical information

### 6.3 Standard Brevity Codes

| Code | Meaning |
|---|---|
| EXECUTE | Begin the operation (or designated phase) |
| HOLD | Pause current movement/action; await next instruction |
| ABORT | Terminate the operation; all elements withdraw to designated staging |
| COMPROMISE | Operational security has been breached; assess and report |
| CONTACT | Hostile threat encountered; specify location and nature |
| CLEAR | Designated space confirmed no threat present |
| SECURE | Subject is in custody; location is under control |
| SHOTS FIRED | Discharge of weapon; specify who, where, direction |
| MAN DOWN | Personnel injured; specify location and nature of injury |
| PLUS ONE | Discovered additional subject not in intelligence assessment |
| ECHO | Evidence located |
| CHEMICAL/BIO/RAD | Detected hazardous material; all elements hold; initiate CBRN protocols |

### 6.4 MEDEVAC Communication

If a medical emergency occurs during an operation, the medical officer or most senior trained operator broadcasts on the tactical net:

*"MAN DOWN, [LOCATION], [NATURE OF INJURY IF KNOWN], REQUEST MEDEVAC"*

EMS pre-staged at the support element moves to coordinate extraction. All other operators maintain their positions unless directed by the on-scene commander. Operations do not automatically abort for medical emergencies — the on-scene commander makes the decision to abort based on overall tactical situation.

## Part VII: Detention and Handling of Subjects

### 7.1 Subject Control

**Announcement and verbal commands:** Subjects are addressed with clear, direct commands in a calm but firm voice. Commands are given one at a time. Operators do not issue multiple simultaneous commands.

**Standard control sequence:**
1. "POLICE, DON'T MOVE" (or equivalent, specified in briefing)
2. "SHOW ME YOUR HANDS"
3. "GET DOWN ON THE GROUND / FACE DOWN / HANDS AWAY FROM YOUR BODY"
4. Once subject is compliant and on the ground: approach for handcuffing

**Non-compliant subjects:** If a subject does not comply with verbal commands, operators assess the reason: language barrier, hearing impairment, intoxication, deliberate resistance. The response is calibrated to the reason. Non-compliance does not automatically authorize force escalation — the use of force policy governs escalation decisions.

### 7.2 Handcuffing and Search

**Handcuffing:** Subject is cuffed with hands behind back. Cuffs are double-locked after application. Cuffs are checked for appropriate tightness — two-finger space between cuffs and wrist. Operator announces "SECURE" when handcuffing is complete.

**Initial search:** Following handcuffing, each subject receives a thorough initial search for weapons and immediately dangerous items. Search is conducted systematically from head to toe. All items found are noted and preserved for evidence handling. The operator conducting the search announces "CLEAR" when complete.

**Segregation:** Multiple subjects are separated immediately upon control. Subjects must not be able to communicate with each other before they are individually processed.

### 7.3 Miranda

Following a subject's arrest and before any questioning, Miranda rights are administered by a designated operator. The fact of Miranda administration, the time, and the subject's acknowledgment are documented in the arrest report.

Miranda does not prevent operators from giving subjects safety commands — "sit down," "don't reach into that bag" — that are not interrogation.

## Part VIII: Hostage and Barricaded Subject Integration

### 8.1 Transition to CNU

When an operation encounters a hostage situation or barricaded subject that cannot be immediately resolved through tactical entry, the scene transitions to a CNU-led response. This is not a retreat — it is a reconfiguration to the most appropriate tool for the situation.

CNU assumes operational lead for negotiations. TOD maintains all perimeter positions and tactical readiness. The TOD Commander and the Lead Negotiator coordinate continuously.

Full procedures are in `12-Hostage-and-Crisis-Negotiation.md`.

### 8.2 Tactical Patience

In barricaded subject/hostage situations, time is almost always on the side of law enforcement. Subjects under stress, isolated from support, without sleep and food, under persistent communication from negotiators, are progressively less capable of the decisive resistance they might have mounted in the first minutes.

TOD operators must understand the discipline required to maintain a perimeter for an extended period while the negotiation process unfoys. The tactical option remains available. It is exercised at the moment of maximum tactical advantage and minimum collateral risk — which is rarely the first hour.

## Part IX: Post-Operation Procedures

### 9.1 Initial Secure Phase

After the target location is secured:
- All subjects are accounted for, handcuffed, and segregated
- All spaces in the target location have been cleared and confirmed
- All operators are accounted for
- Medical assessment of all personnel (including subjects — subjects who were injured during the operation receive medical care as a priority)
- Evidence Response Team notified to begin crime scene processing

### 9.2 Evidence Transfer

Tactical operators do not process the crime scene. Their job ends at physical security of the location. The Evidence Response Team or designated Investigations Division personnel take custody of the scene for evidence processing.

If tactical operators discover potential evidence during clearing, they mark the location, do not touch the item, and notify the evidence team.

### 9.3 Debrief

All participants in a tactical operation conduct a debrief within 24 hours of completion. The debrief covers:

- What was planned vs. what occurred
- Tactical execution assessment
- Use of force review (any use of force is documented and reviewed)
- Lessons identified
- Follow-up actions required

Debrief notes become part of the operation file and are reviewed by the Training Division for integration into future training.

### 9.4 Use of Force Documentation

Any use of force — including, but not limited to, weapons discharge, Taser deployment, physical force resulting in injury — is documented in a Use of Force Report within 24 hours. Use of Force Reports are reviewed by the TOD Commander and the Legal Division.
