## Part I: Foundation

### 1.1 The Constitutional Standard

The use of force by law enforcement is governed by the Constitution's prohibition on unreasonable seizures. A use of force against a person is a seizure. The force is lawful if it is objectively reasonable given the totality of the circumstances facing a reasonable officer at the moment the force is used.

**Graham v. Connor standard (three-factor test):**
1. The severity of the crime at issue
2. Whether the suspect poses an immediate threat to the safety of the officers or others
3. Whether the suspect is actively resisting arrest or attempting to evade arrest by flight

These factors are not a checklist. They are considerations that inform the overall reasonableness assessment. Reasonableness is judged from the perspective of a reasonable officer on scene at the time — not with hindsight, not from behind a desk.

### 1.2 SACTF-Specific Context

SACTF officers often operate in threat environments that are more complex than those facing general law enforcement:
- Targets may be assessed to have more dangerous weapons capabilities
- Operations may be based on intelligence about subjects' willingness to use violence
- Tactical operations place personnel in inherently higher-risk environments

These realities do not modify the constitutional standard. They are factors in the objective reasonableness analysis. They can justify higher levels of force when they create genuine, articulable increases in threat — not as a blanket elevation of force authority simply because the assignment is counterterrorism.

### 1.3 Force Proportionality

Force must be proportional to the threat being addressed. Overwhelming force is lawful when overwhelming force is needed to address an overwhelming threat. It is not lawful as a matter of preference or convenience when lesser force would accomplish the objective.

## Part II: Force Continuum

### 2.1 Officer Presence

The presence of a uniformed or identified law enforcement officer — or an identified SACTF team — is itself a form of force application. For most subjects, official presence is sufficient to achieve compliance.

**Application:** All SACTF contacts. Officer presence should always be the starting point; its adequacy is assessed before any additional force is applied.

### 2.2 Verbal Commands

Clear, direct verbal commands, stated in a calm but authoritative voice, directed at the specific behavior sought. Verbal commands are escalated in volume and directiveness if initial commands are not followed.

**Application:** Any situation where the subject can hear and is apparently capable of compliance. Verbal commands are always attempted before physical force unless circumstances (immediate, active threat) make them impractical.

### 2.3 Soft Empty-Hand Control

Physical control techniques that do not involve strikes — escort holds, joint manipulations, body weight control, guided compliance. These techniques are used to guide, restrain, or control a non-combative or minimally resistive subject.

**Application:** Subjects who are non-compliant but not actively assaultive. A subject who will not place their hands behind their back on a verbal command, but who is not fighting.

### 2.4 Hard Empty-Hand Control

Physical techniques involving strikes or pain compliance — pressure points, joint locks applied forcefully, controlled strikes to large muscle groups. These techniques may cause localized pain and occasionally minor injury.

**Application:** Subjects who are actively resisting without a weapon — physically fighting the officer, struggling to escape control, using their body as a weapon.

### 2.5 Less-Lethal Weapons

Intermediate force options that are intended not to cause death or serious bodily harm:
- **Electronic control device (ECD/Taser):** Deploys barbs that deliver an electrical charge, creating temporary muscle incapacitation. Effective against actively resistive subjects.
- **OC spray (pepper spray):** Chemical irritant affecting eyes and respiratory tract. Effective against moderate resistance.
- **Impact munitions:** Less-lethal projectiles for use in specific scenarios where direct physical contact is not feasible and greater standoff is required.
- **Distraction devices (flash-bang grenades):** Create sensory disorientation without producing fragmentation injury when used properly. Authorized for TOD use in authorized tactical entries only.

**Application:** Subject is actively resistive, combative, or presenting a threat that cannot be addressed with empty-hand techniques, where deadly force is not yet justified.

### 2.6 Deadly Force

Force that is likely to cause death or serious bodily harm.

**Authorized when:**
- An officer has an objectively reasonable belief that the subject poses an imminent threat of death or serious bodily harm to the officer or another person

**"Imminent threat" means:**
- The subject has the **ability** to cause death or serious bodily harm (has a weapon, has hands on a victim, displays capacity to harm)
- The subject has the **opportunity** to cause that harm (is within range, can reach the victim)
- The subject's actions indicate **intent** to cause that harm (is taking action, not merely talking)

All three elements — ability, opportunity, intent — must be present. Imminent does not mean future; it means now or in the immediate seconds that follow.

**Warning shots:** Not authorized. Warning shots create unacceptable collateral risk.

**Shooting at moving vehicles:** Not authorized unless the vehicle is being used as a weapon against persons and there is no other means of stopping the threat. Shooting at vehicles does not typically stop them and creates fragmentation and ricochet hazard.

## Part III: Documentation Requirements

### 3.1 Mandatory Reporting

Any use of force above verbal commands is reportable. The following events require a Use of Force Report:

- Any pointing of a firearm at a person
- Any application of physical control techniques resulting in injury or complaint of injury
- ECD or OC spray deployment
- Any weapons discharge (including accidental)
- Any impact munition deployment
- Any other force application that results in injury

### 3.2 Use of Force Report

Use of Force Reports are completed by the officer who applied force within 24 hours of the incident. The report covers:
- Description of the subject's behavior that preceded the force
- The specific force technique or weapon used
- The justification — what threat the officer perceived, why the force was necessary
- The outcome — any injury to the subject, any injury to the officer
- Medical attention provided or offered to the subject
- Witnesses

### 3.3 Supervisor Review

The Use of Force Report is reviewed by the officer's direct supervisor and the Division Commander. The review assesses:
- Whether the force was consistent with policy
- Whether the documentation is complete and accurate
- Whether any training issues are indicated
- Whether referral for formal investigation is warranted

## Part IV: Shooting Incidents

### 4.1 Immediate Post-Shooting Procedures

**Officer who discharged:**
- Once the scene is safe, the officer secures their weapon (but does not field strip or clean it — it is evidence)
- The officer verbally notifies their supervisor immediately
- The officer does not discuss the incident with non-authorized personnel
- The officer is removed from the scene to a safe location pending investigative interview
- The officer is offered immediate medical and psychological support

**First arriving supervisor:**
- Secures the scene
- Ensures medical response for injured persons
- Notifies command
- Identifies and segregates witnesses
- Notifies the Legal Division

### 4.2 Administrative Investigation

All weapons discharges are investigated. The investigation is conducted by personnel independent of the officer's chain of command. The investigation is not a presumption of wrongdoing — it is an assurance that the incident is thoroughly documented.

Personnel under investigation cooperate fully. They provide a complete account of their actions and perceptions. The protections against self-incrimination are preserved; the obligation to cooperate with the administrative investigation (as opposed to criminal investigation) is separately established.

## Part V: Prohibited Uses of Force

The following uses of force are expressly prohibited regardless of circumstances:

- **Neck restraints** that restrict blood flow to the brain (vascular neck restraints) except when deadly force is otherwise authorized
- **Strikes to the head** with a hard instrument except when deadly force is otherwise authorized
- **Force against a subject who is fully restrained** and not presenting an active threat
- **Force as punishment** — striking a subject who has been placed in custody and is compliant
- **Force against persons** in need of medical attention, except as necessary to preserve officer safety
- **Retaliatory force** — force motivated by the subject's prior words or conduct rather than present threat
- **Continued force against** a subject who has become compliant

Violations of these prohibitions are not a use of force policy issue. They are potential criminal violations. SACTF does not internally resolve or minimize such violations.

---

## Part VI: Psychological Aftermath

All officers who have been involved in a weapons discharge or other serious use-of-force incident receive a psychological debrief within 72 hours. This is not optional.

Officers who are involved in incidents resulting in death (of subject, bystander, or colleague) receive extended psychological support. Return to full duty following such incidents follows a structured process that includes psychological clearance.

SACTF takes the psychological consequences of force encounters seriously. Officers who seek psychological support after force incidents are not penalized. They are supported. The failure to seek support, and the accumulated consequences of unaddressed trauma, creates greater long-term risk to the officer and the Task Force than the incident itself.
