## Part I: Undercover Operations Philosophy

### 1.1 What Undercover Operations Are

An undercover operation is the placement of a law enforcement officer in an assumed identity — a "cover" — for the purpose of infiltrating or associating with a target organization or individual to develop intelligence and evidence.

Undercover work is simultaneously the most powerful and the most dangerous investigative tool available to SACTF. It is the most powerful because it provides direct access to what a target organization knows, plans, and does — access that no amount of technical collection or surveillance can fully replicate. It is the most dangerous because the officers who conduct it are physically present in the target environment, operating without immediate backup, dependent entirely on their cover, their training, and their judgment.

UOP operations are authorized only after a rigorous review confirms that:
1. The investigative objective cannot reasonably be achieved through less intrusive means
2. There is adequate legal basis for the operation
3. The operational risk is proportionate to the investigative objective
4. The operation plan includes adequate officer safety protocols
5. Director-level authorization has been obtained

### 1.2 Ethical Boundaries

Undercover operations are subject to firm ethical limits that cannot be waived by operational necessity:

**Entrapment prevention:** SACTF undercover officers do not induce individuals to commit crimes they would not otherwise have committed. The officer may provide opportunity; the target must provide the criminal intent. An undercover officer who repeatedly pressures a reluctant target, offers extraordinary financial inducement, or creates the operational plan that the target merely executes is creating an entrapment defense that will destroy the prosecution.

**Non-participation in violence:** Undercover officers do not commit, facilitate, or approve violent acts in furtherance of their cover. There is no circumstance in which an undercover officer allows harm to come to a person because declining would compromise their cover. Officer safety and human safety always supersede operational continuity.

**Truthful reporting:** Undercover officers report what they observe and hear with complete accuracy. They do not shade reports to support a desired investigative conclusion. They do not omit information that is inconvenient. The integrity of the officer's reporting is the foundation of the operation's evidentiary value.

**No permanent cover relationships:** Undercover officers do not form genuine personal or romantic relationships that they intend to maintain after the operation concludes. If the operational cover requires developing social relationships with target associates, those relationships are maintained only for the duration of the operation and are disclosed to the target's knowledge at case conclusion.

## Part II: Cover Development

### 2.1 Cover Identity (Legend) Requirements

An undercover officer's cover identity must be sustainable — capable of withstanding the scrutiny that a careful target will apply. The development of a sustainable cover is not quick work. A thorough legend takes weeks or months to properly establish.

**Biographical authenticity:** The cover identity must have a coherent life history. The officer must be able to speak fluently and consistently about their legend's background — where they grew up, where they went to school, work history, family situation, criminal history (where relevant). The officer does not rely on notes; the legend must be internalized.

**Documentation:** Government-issued identification documents consistent with the legend are required for most undercover placements. These are provided through the SACTF administrative process. The officer does not create documents independently.

**Digital presence:** Modern targets investigate associates using publicly available online tools. A legend without a digital presence is immediately suspicious in 2020s operations. Legend social media accounts and online presence are developed by Technical Operations Unit support staff and maintained by the undercover officer on a schedule appropriate to the legend.

**Criminal history (if required):** Some undercover placements require a legend with a criminal background that will be verified against law enforcement records. Technical Operations Unit support staff manage this through approved deception protocols. The officer is briefed on exactly what record exists and for what.

**Backstory consistency:** Multiple people who know the officer in their undercover role must receive consistent answers to the same questions. Inconsistency is the single most common way undercover officers are detected.

### 2.2 Cover Maintenance in the Field

**Staying in cover:** Once deployed, the undercover officer is their legend. They speak with the legend's vocabulary. They display the legend's knowledge and ignorance. They react with the legend's emotional register. Breaking cover in the field is a safety emergency, not a protocol choice.

**Cover conversations:** The officer should be prepared for extended, detailed cover conversations on any aspect of the legend. Target members will test the officer — sometimes deliberately, sometimes through casual conversation that probes inconsistencies. The officer practices the legend extensively before deployment.

**Knowledge and ignorance:** One of the most common cover failures is an undercover officer who knows too much. An officer posing as a newly recruited criminal associate should not display the professional knowledge of a 10-year investigator. The legend defines what the officer "knows," and the officer must suppress their real knowledge when it exceeds what the legend would have.

**Physical evidence of cover:** Any physical items associated with the legend — a vehicle registered to the cover identity, housing documents, business records — must be authentic. Target members sometimes verify. Items that cannot withstand verification are not used.

## Part III: Operational Roles

### 3.1 Handler

Every undercover officer is assigned a dedicated Handler who is their primary point of contact throughout the operation. The Handler is a senior Supervisory Special Agent who is not a member of the case team.

**Handler responsibilities:**
- Primary safety contact for the undercover officer
- Receives operational intelligence from the officer
- Manages the communication protocol and schedule
- Monitors officer welfare — psychological status, signs of stress or compromise
- Escalates concerns to the UOP Director
- Coordinates extraction if required

The Handler relationship is the most important professional relationship in the operation. The officer must trust the Handler completely. The Handler must know the officer well enough to recognize distress signals and distinguish normal operational stress from safety emergencies.

### 3.2 Undercover Officer

The UO is the officer deployed in the undercover role. They are responsible for:
- Maintaining cover at all times in the target environment
- Reporting accurately and completely to the Handler through established protocols
- Making real-time judgment calls within the parameters set by the operational plan
- Recognizing and responding appropriately to safety concerns
- Never compromising officer safety for operational continuity

### 3.3 Case Agent

The Case Agent is the Investigations Division agent responsible for the investigation that the undercover operation is supporting. The Case Agent:
- Identifies specific intelligence requirements that the UO should pursue
- Receives processed intelligence reports from the Handler
- Integrates intelligence into the overall investigation
- Does not have direct contact with the undercover officer during the operation (all contact is through the Handler)

### 3.4 Technical Support

The Technical Operations Unit provides:
- Legend documentation and digital presence
- Communication security tools
- Any court-authorized technical collection coordinated through the undercover officer's activities

## Part IV: Communication Protocols

### 4.1 Regular Contact Schedule

Undercover officers maintain a regular contact schedule with their Handler. The schedule is established at operation initiation and must be consistent with the legend (a contact schedule that requires the officer to disappear at specific intervals raises questions if the target organization notices).

**Standard schedule:** Typically daily contact at a minimum. High-risk operations may require more frequent check-ins.

**Contact methods:** Cover-consistent communication. An undercover officer in a criminal organization cannot check in using a government-issued encrypted phone. Communication methods are specified in the operational plan and consistent with the legend.

**Missed check-in protocol:** If an undercover officer misses a scheduled check-in without prior notification, the Handler immediately initiates the emergency protocol. Time thresholds before emergency escalation are specified in each operation's plan.

### 4.2 Coded Communication

When the undercover officer cannot speak freely (target members are present; monitored communication), coded communication is used:

**Safety signal:** A pre-agreed phrase or action indicating that the officer is in good condition. Absence of the safety signal during a check-in is treated as a potential emergency.

**Distress signal:** A pre-agreed phrase or action that the officer can use during a monitored communication to indicate they are in danger. The distress signal triggers immediate extraction protocol.

**Operational codes:** Operation-specific codes for particular status reports — target is planning to move, meeting occurring, target has identified a specific individual, etc. Codes are simple, plausible in context, and briefed to both officer and Handler before deployment.

### 4.3 Documentation of Contact

Every contact between the Handler and the undercover officer is documented:
- Date and time
- Method of contact
- Summary of information reported
- Officer safety status
- Any follow-up actions required

Contact documentation is maintained by the Handler and reviewed by the UOP Director.

## Part V: Officer Safety

### 5.1 Pre-Deployment Safety Planning

Before any undercover deployment, a detailed safety plan is developed and approved by the UOP Director:

- **Cover documentation verification:** All documents have been verified to withstand expected scrutiny
- **Communication plan:** Check-in schedule, methods, codes, and missing-check-in protocol
- **Extraction plan:** How to safely extract the officer if safety is compromised, including planned "cover exit" (a story the officer can use to explain their departure to the target) and emergency extraction (immediate physical extraction regardless of cover impact)
- **Backup presence:** In most operations, backup agents are positioned in proximity to the officer, not in contact but capable of rapid response. The officer does not know the specific location of backup; this knowledge could affect behavior that the target might observe.
- **Legal weapons:** The officer's right to carry a concealed weapon for personal protection, consistent with the cover, is addressed in each operation's plan. The officer always has access to means of self-defense.

### 5.2 Signs of Compromise

The undercover officer must be trained to recognize signs that their cover is under threat:

**Increased skepticism:** Target members are asking more questions about background, asking different members the same questions to compare answers, pressing for details that weren't previously interesting.

**Isolation:** Target members are excluding the officer from activities or conversations that they previously included them in.

**Testing:** Target members present situations or information designed to elicit a response that a genuine criminal would give and a law enforcement officer might not.

**Hostile surveillance:** The officer believes they are being followed or observed when not in the target environment.

**Third-party inquiries:** Someone the officer does not know has been asking questions about the officer in contexts outside the operation.

When any of these signs are observed, the officer reports immediately through the designated protocol. The situation is evaluated jointly by the officer, the Handler, and the UOP Director. An increase in compromise indicators may result in acceleration of the operation's timeline or extraction.

### 5.3 Psychological Support

Undercover work has documented psychological consequences, including:
- Difficulty separating the undercover persona from the officer's authentic identity
- Desensitization to criminal behavior observed or heard about during the operation
- Stress from sustained deception
- Trauma from exposure to violence, criminal planning, or the content of conversations with dangerous individuals
- Social isolation — the inability to discuss work with anyone outside the operation

All undercover officers receive psychological support throughout the operation:
- Regular check-in with the SACTF psychological support staff (at a minimum, before deployment, monthly during, and after conclusion)
- Clear guidance that requesting psychological support does not affect assignment status
- Post-operation debrief with psychological support component
- Return to conventional duties with a managed transition period

Officers who show signs of significant psychological distress during an operation are immediately evaluated. Continuing an operation with a psychologically compromised officer creates unacceptable risk to the officer and the operation.

## Part VI: Termination of Undercover Operations

### 6.1 Planned Termination

Most undercover operations conclude according to a planned exit:

**Investigative objective achieved:** The operation has developed the evidence and intelligence it was designed to develop. The officer is extracted on a timeline that allows for arrest operations to be planned without the target's counter-surveillance interfering.

**Cover exit story:** The officer uses a pre-planned story to explain their departure from the target organization's world — a move, a family situation, a criminal matter that requires them to "go quiet." The cover exit reduces the target's suspicion that law enforcement is involved until arrests are made.

**Evidence preparation:** The officer's handler and the case agent work together to ensure that all intelligence developed during the operation is properly documented, processed, and packaged as evidence.

### 6.2 Emergency Extraction

If the officer's safety is at immediate risk:

1. **Officer activates distress signal** through any available means
2. **Handler immediately alerts operational command** and activates extraction team
3. **Extraction team moves to officer's last known location** — typically from nearby staging
4. **Officer's priority is to physically separate** from the target environment as quickly as possible
5. **If direct threat is imminent**, the officer may identify as law enforcement to stop the threat — cover is abandoned to protect life

After emergency extraction, the operation may be terminated or may transition to an immediate arrest phase, depending on the intelligence picture and the circumstances of the compromise.

### 6.3 Post-Operation Debrief

All undercover operations conclude with a comprehensive debrief:
- Complete operational review with intelligence and evidence documentation
- Psychological debrief component
- Operational lessons — what the operation did well, what it would have done differently
- Legend retirement (digital presence removal, document deactivation)
- Intelligence products from the operation are finalized and filed
