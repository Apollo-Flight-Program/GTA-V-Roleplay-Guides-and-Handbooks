## Part I: Explosive Threat Framework

### 1.1 The IED Threat

Improvised Explosive Devices (IEDs) are among the most dangerous and versatile weapons available to terrorist organizations. Unlike military ordnance, which is standardized and documented, IEDs are built from materials that vary enormously in composition, configuration, and triggering mechanism. This variability makes them uniquely dangerous — even experienced EOD personnel cannot assume that one device will behave like another.

SACTF personnel who are not EOD specialists are not expected to render IEDs safe. They are expected to:
- Recognize indicators of explosive threats
- Take appropriate initial response actions
- Establish appropriate safety perimeters
- Call EOD specialists and brief them effectively
- Conduct post-blast investigation
- Develop intelligence on IED networks

### 1.2 Categories of Explosive Threat

**Vehicle-Borne IED (VBIED):** Explosive device placed in or constructed as part of a vehicle. Range from car bombs designed for remote detonation to vehicle-borne suicide devices. The vehicle provides concealment for large quantities of explosive material.

**Suicide-Borne IED (SBIED):** Explosive device worn on the person of the person intending to detonate it. Variable explosive capacity; primary threat is proximate detonation in public spaces.

**Package / Mail Bomb:** Explosive device delivered in a package or letter. Often designed to detonate on opening; may be designed as shaped charge to project fragmentation.

**Improvised Landmine / Remote Detonation Device:** Devices designed to be activated by a victim's presence or by a remote operator. Common in attack planning against law enforcement and military targets.

**Incendiary Devices:** Devices designed to produce fire rather than or in addition to blast. Not technically explosive in the fragmentary sense but included in IED response planning.

## Part II: Threat Recognition

### 2.1 Suspicious Item Recognition

The mnemonic **RAMP** provides a framework for evaluating whether an item may be a suspicious device:

**R — Recently placed:** Has the item appeared recently? Is it out of place compared to normal conditions at this location?

**A — Apparently abandoned:** Is the item unattended? In a location where unattended items would normally be removed?

**M — Manifestly suspicious:** Does the item have visible characteristics that suggest it may be a device — wiring, antennae, containers with visible components, unusual weight for its apparent size?

**P — Placed deliberately:** Is there evidence that the item was left intentionally? Obscured positioning, positioning near high-value targets, positioning consistent with threat warnings received?

Any item meeting one or more of these criteria is treated as potentially suspicious pending EOD assessment.

### 2.2 VBIED Indicators

Suspicious vehicles may be IEDs. Indicators include:
- Vehicle parked in unusual location relative to surroundings (blocking access, positioned for maximum crowd exposure)
- Vehicle markedly lower than normal on its suspension (consistent with heavy explosive payload)
- Wires, antennae, or components visible through windows
- Vehicle that matches description from a threat communication
- Vehicle from which unusual odors (commercial explosives, accelerants) are detectable
- Rental vehicle or vehicle with questionable registration in a security-sensitive area
- Vehicle left unattended in a restricted area

**VBIED standoff distance:** EOD guidance on minimum evacuation distance varies by estimated device size. For a vehicle of unknown content in a threat context, minimum initial evacuation is 300 meters. For small passenger vehicles, 100 meters minimum. Increase distance when possible.

### 2.3 Suspicious Person Indicators

A person carrying a SBIED may display:
- Unusual bulkiness under clothing inconsistent with season
- Visible wires, clips, or trigger mechanism
- Stiff, unusual gait suggesting concealed rigid device
- Unusual sweating or agitation inconsistent with environment
- Extreme focus on a specific target location
- Failure to respond to normal environmental stimuli (unusual eye contact avoidance, apparent dissociation)

**Critical consideration:** These indicators alone are not sufficient for action. Many innocent people display stress behaviors in law enforcement presence. The totality of circumstances governs. In a known threat environment with specific intelligence, sensitivity is appropriately higher.

## Part III: Response Protocols

### 3.1 Initial Response: The Four Cs

**Confirm:** Assess whether the item is genuinely suspicious. Do not touch it. Do not approach it more closely than needed to confirm the observation.

**Clear:** Immediately evacuate the immediate area. People are cleared as quickly as possible, with minimum explanation.

**Cordon:** Establish a perimeter at appropriate distance (see standoff distances below). Do not allow persons to reenter.

**Control:** Establish command and communications. Call EOD. Brief incoming EOD personnel.

### 3.2 Standoff Distances

| Suspected Device | Minimum Evacuation Distance |
|---|---|
| Pipe bomb equivalent | 70 meters |
| Suicide vest equivalent | 110 meters |
| Briefcase / backpack device | 200 meters |
| Small car (suspect VBIED) | 320 meters |
| Large car / van (suspect VBIED) | 400 meters |
| Small truck (suspect VBIED) | 600 meters |
| Large truck (suspect VBIED) | 900 meters |

These are minimum distances. Increase whenever terrain and resources permit. Clear secondary evacuation areas — blast effects are deflected and channeled by buildings and terrain. Evacuation must account for secondary hazard paths.

### 3.3 Communications Protocol for Explosive Threats

**Radio use near a suspicious device:** Radio transmitters can trigger some radio-frequency IEDs. Until EOD personnel assess the device:
- No radio transmissions within 25 meters of a suspected device
- Mobile phones powered off within 25 meters
- Electronic vehicle keys used at minimum distance from suspected device

**Notification sequence:**
1. Patrol supervisor notified
2. Dispatch notified; bomb squad/EOD requested
3. SACTF WMD Squad notified
4. If SACTF involvement: SACTF command and intelligence cell notified
5. If credible terrorism nexus: FIB WMD coordinator notified

### 3.4 EOD Integration

When EOD personnel arrive, the SACTF agent on scene provides:
- Exact location of suspicious item
- Description of item
- What actions have been taken (approach, touch, movement)
- Information from any threat communications relevant to the device
- Current perimeter status

EOD takes primary authority for the device. SACTF personnel comply with EOD direction regarding safety distance and activity near the device. After EOD renders the device safe, SACTF assumes primary authority for evidence processing and investigation.

## Part IV: Bomb Threats

### 4.1 Receiving a Bomb Threat

Persons who receive bomb threats by phone should:
- Keep the caller on the line as long as possible
- Note exact wording of the threat
- Ask the caller: Where is the device? When will it go off? What does it look like? Why did you place it?
- Listen for background sounds — machine noise, crowd noise, transportation, distinctive audio environment
- Note caller characteristics — voice, accent, gender, apparent age, emotional state
- Do not hang up first; let the caller disconnect

Bomb threat calls are reported immediately to a supervisor and dispatched as a potential explosive threat.

### 4.2 Written Bomb Threats

Written threats are treated as evidence from first contact:
- Handled minimally, holding by edges
- Not carried in a pocket or folded and unfolded
- Immediately placed in a protective sleeve for evidence preservation
- Photographed in place before movement if possible

### 4.3 Bomb Threat Assessment

Not all bomb threats are credible. SACTF WMD personnel assess bomb threats for credibility based on:
- Specificity of threat (specific location, specific time, specific device description — higher credibility)
- Consistency with known threat environment and prior intelligence
- Whether the threat is consistent with any prior intelligence about planned attacks
- Prior threat history from the same source or in the same environment

A non-credible assessment does not eliminate the threat response — it informs the resource allocation. A suspicious search of a threatened building is conducted regardless of credibility assessment. The assessment affects the scale of the response, not whether it occurs.

## Part V: Post-Blast Investigation

### 5.1 Scene Safety

An explosion scene is treated as an active threat environment until:
- Secondary device search is completed
- Active shooter/attacker threat has been assessed
- Structural safety has been assessed

**Secondary device protocol:** Terrorists sometimes place a secondary device designed to detonate against responding personnel — positioned to be detonated after first responders are concentrated at the initial blast site. Every explosion scene receives a secondary device search before rescue and recovery efforts are concentrated. This is not optional. Personnel have been killed by secondary devices when this protocol was not followed.

Secondary device search is conducted by EOD with SACTF support. Search areas include: areas where first responders naturally congregate (triage areas, command post), high-observation locations overlooking the scene, and approach routes to the blast site.

### 5.2 Scene Preservation

The blast scene is a crime scene. As much as is possible given the immediate priorities of rescue and medical response, scene preservation begins immediately:
- Outer perimeter established well beyond the blast area
- No personnel enter the blast zone except for life-saving purposes, until EOD has cleared it
- No debris moved except for life-safety access
- Documentation begins as soon as practicable — photography, video, mapping

### 5.3 Evidence at a Blast Scene

Evidence from a blast scene includes:
**Pre-blast materials:** Items associated with device preparation or positioning — containers, packing materials, components not consumed in the explosion.

**Post-blast debris:** Components of the device itself — fragmentary evidence of the initiation system, main charge container, shrapnel, rendered-safe remnants.

**Trace evidence:** Chemical traces from explosive materials, tool marks on components, biological material (in suicide device attacks), fingerprints on recovered components.

**Environmental evidence:** Surveillance footage, witness observations, access records, debris scatter pattern.

**Crater and blast pattern analysis:** The shape and direction of the blast provides information about device construction and placement. EOD and technical specialists conduct this analysis.

### 5.4 Blast Pattern Analysis

The blast radius, fragmentation pattern, and structural damage provide information about:
- Estimated charge size
- Device construction (shaped charge vs. omnidirectional; fragmentation material)
- Initiation point (ground contact, mid-air, confined vs. open placement)
- Device orientation

These assessments are made by EOD technical specialists and WMD Squad agents with post-blast investigation training.

## Part VI: IED Network Investigations

### 6.1 The Value of Device Intelligence

Every recovered device component is an intelligence lead. IED construction leaves signatures:
- Specific components (electronic components, containers, triggering mechanisms) may be traced to purchase sources
- Construction techniques may link devices to a common builder
- Component sourcing reflects logistical networks
- Trade craft — the level of sophistication — indicates training and experience

IED intelligence is developed through:
- Component trace investigations
- Forensic analysis (fingerprints, DNA, chemical signature)
- Comparison to known device signatures in law enforcement databases
- Comparison to intelligence about target organizations' capabilities and materials

### 6.2 Interdicting the Supply Chain

IED construction requires materials. Even crude devices require precursors — commercial explosive materials, initiators, containers. Supply chain interdiction targets the acquisition of these materials:
- Sales monitoring for explosive precursor materials
- Storage facility surveillance for unusual quantities of relevant materials
- Transportation monitoring for shipments of precursor materials

Individuals who purchase unusual quantities of explosive precursor materials without commercial justification are flagged for investigative follow-up. This is a high-yield investigative technique — many IED incidents could have been interdicted at the material acquisition phase.
