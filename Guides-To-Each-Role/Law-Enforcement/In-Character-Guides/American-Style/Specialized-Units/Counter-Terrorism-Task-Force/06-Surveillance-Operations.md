## Part I: Surveillance Fundamentals

### 1.1 The Purpose of Surveillance

Surveillance is systematic, covert observation of a target. In the SACTF context, surveillance serves several distinct purposes:

**Intelligence Development:** Building the pattern-of-life picture on a target — where they go, who they meet, what they do, how they travel, where they sleep. Pattern-of-life intelligence is foundational for understanding a subject's operational role and for identifying network members.

**Prosecution Evidence:** Observations made by trained surveillance personnel during authorized operations are admissible evidence. A surveillance agent who observes a subject in a meeting with a designated co-conspirator, at a location relevant to the investigation, provides testimonial evidence that is often more compelling than any technical collection.

**Target Location:** Identifying where subjects are at specific times — critical for both arrest operations and for triggering other collection activities.

**Trigger Function:** Surveillance operations can be the trigger for other collection activities — activating a court-authorized technical device when the subject enters a monitored location, alerting investigators when a meeting begins.

### 1.2 The Surveillance Mindset

Surveillance is the most detection-sensitive activity SACTF conducts. A subject who detects surveillance immediately alters their behavior — changes routes, terminates contacts, moves operations, and activates counter-surveillance measures. A detection event does not just compromise the surveillance operation; it may compromise the entire investigation.

Every decision made during surveillance must be evaluated against this risk. Maintaining coverage of a target for one more block is not worth compromising the operation. Abandoning coverage early — letting the target go — is often the right decision.

**Critical principle:** Surveillance is a long game. A single coverage session is not the objective. Building a complete intelligence picture over weeks or months is the objective. Trading the long game for a short-term gain is a consistent surveillance mistake.

## Part II: Physical Surveillance

### 2.1 Team Composition

**Minimum team:** Three surveillance operators for most mobile surveillance. Fewer than three operators conducting mobile surveillance on a target who is actively counter-surveillance aware is not effective — the same operator will be seen too often.

**Standard team:** Four to six operators for urban mobile surveillance. Larger teams provide more flexibility and reduce exposure of any individual operator.

**Roles:**

*Eye:* The operator currently with the closest visual contact on the target. "Has the eye" means has direct observation. The Eye is designated with a specific call sign and calls all significant target activity.

*Containment:* Operators positioned to cover potential escape routes and pick up coverage if the Eye loses contact. Containment operators do not position close to the target.

*Point:* Operator positioned ahead of the target's expected direction of travel — in front, not behind.

*Command (in large operations):* A senior operator who maintains the overall picture, directs team movements, and manages communications without having direct visual contact. Frees the Eye from coordination functions.

### 2.2 Mobile Foot Surveillance

**Spacing:** Surveillance operators maintain distance appropriate to the environment. In dense urban areas (crowded pedestrian areas, shopping districts), closer spacing is acceptable because the target's ability to identify a follower is reduced by the number of people present. In open areas (parks, quiet residential streets), greater distance is required.

**Angle:** Operators do not shadow targets directly from behind. The standard surveillance position is at an angle — behind and to the side — which reduces the visual signature of following.

**Cover:** Operators use available cover for their movement — window shopping, phone use, stopping to consult maps. Cover is natural behavior for the environment. A surveillance operator in a business district does not look like they are stopping to consult a map; they stop in front of a business as if assessing whether to enter.

**Hand-offs:** When an operator is at risk of being detected (has been near the target too long, the route is becoming too consistent), control of the Eye is handed to another operator. The hand-off is announced on the team net: "I have control, you're clear." The former Eye immediately moves away from the target at a natural pace.

### 2.3 Mobile Vehicle Surveillance

**Spacing:** Vehicle surveillance typically maintains two to four vehicles of separation in urban traffic. The spacing varies with traffic density — more vehicles in dense traffic; more distance in light traffic.

**Route selection:** Surveillance vehicles do not maintain constant position behind the target vehicle. The box formation — vehicles positioned in front, behind, and to the sides — provides coverage while reducing the exposure of any single vehicle.

**Committing to turns:** The most detection-sensitive moment in vehicle surveillance is a turn. A vehicle that follows a target through multiple turns is identifiable as surveillance. Teams use the point vehicle (ahead of the target) and a containment vehicle (parallel route) to pick up the target after a turn without following through it.

**Traffic light gaps:** Losing a target at a traffic light is a common and often acceptable outcome. Attempting to run a red light to maintain contact with a target creates multiple problems — it is legally problematic, it is obvious to the target, and it creates public safety risk. Standard procedure: lose the target at the light; use other team members and available intelligence to relocate.

### 2.4 Static Surveillance

**Definition:** Sustained observation of a fixed location — a residence, a meeting place, a business — from a fixed or limited-movement position.

**Covert positions:** Static surveillance positions are selected for concealment, field of view, and duration sustainment. A position that provides an excellent view but requires operators to explain their presence to police responding to a suspicious person call is not a good position.

**Observation posts:**
- *Vehicle-based OP:* Most flexible. Requires a vehicle that is consistent with the neighborhood and a cover reason for extended parking. Operator switches with another operator periodically to avoid extended single-vehicle presence.
- *Building-based OP:* Higher observation angle, more sustained coverage. Requires access to appropriate building under a cover that supports extended presence.
- *Purpose-built OP:* For long-duration sensitive operations, a dedicated location may be established. Requires significant resource investment and is used for high-priority targets only.

### 2.5 Counter-Surveillance Recognition

Targets who are surveillance-aware conduct counter-surveillance (CS) to detect coverage. Surveillance operators must recognize CS activity:

**Surveillance detection routes (SDR):** Target takes apparently random route with multiple turns, doubles back, or makes unexplained stops. The target is looking for consistent coverage.

**Choke points:** Target routes through locations where surveillance is unavoidable — long stretches with no parallel routes, entrances with limited access. Target positions to observe who passes through after them.

**Foot counter-surveillance:** Sudden stops, window shopping with extended observation of reflections, turns that allow the target to see behind themselves, entering and immediately exiting buildings.

**Vehicle counter-surveillance:** Sudden U-turns, lane changes designed to create a gap in traffic and observe who follows, high-speed driving with observation for consistent following vehicles.

**Response to detected counter-surveillance:** If a team determines that a target is actively conducting counter-surveillance, the surveillance is broken off cleanly. "Break, break, break — abort surveillance, all units clear the area by alternate routes." The situation is immediately reported to command and to the Intelligence Division.

## Part III: Technical Surveillance

### 3.1 Legal Requirements Summary

All SACTF technical surveillance operations require prior legal authorization. There are no exceptions. The Legal Division maintains authority over all technical surveillance applications. Personnel who initiate technical collection without authorization are subject to immediate removal from duty.

**Court-authorized technical collection is valid.** SACTF takes aggressive advantage of authorized technical collection. The obligation is to obtain the authorization, not to avoid the collection.

### 3.2 Telephone and Communications Intercept

**Application Process:**
1. Case agent prepares initial factual summary
2. Legal Division reviews and advises on sufficiency of showing
3. Legal Division prepares or reviews the court application (affidavit + order)
4. Application filed with appropriate court
5. Court issues order or requests supplementation
6. Following court authorization, Technical Operations Unit activates the collection
7. Collection proceeds for the authorized period; monitoring is conducted by designated personnel
8. Content reports are prepared and entered into the case file
9. Minimization procedures are followed — collection that is clearly not relevant to the investigation is not recorded; personal calls unrelated to criminal activity are minimized

**Minimization:** Federal and state law require that intercept collection be minimized to collect only communications relevant to the investigation. SACTF monitoring personnel apply minimization in real time. Minimization decisions are documented.

**Reporting:** Monitoring personnel prepare a detailed log of each intercepted communication that is relevant to the investigation. The log includes date, time, parties, call duration, and a summary of content. Verbatim transcripts are prepared for significant communications.

### 3.3 Audio/Video Collection (Microphones and Cameras)

**Court authorization required:** Court orders for audio and video collection in non-public locations require an enhanced showing. Applications must demonstrate:
- Probable cause that the crime is being committed
- That normal investigative procedures have been tried and failed or are unlikely to succeed
- That the location to be monitored is being used for criminal activity
- Specific description of the location and the period for which monitoring is authorized

**Installation:** All installation of court-authorized audio/video devices is conducted by Technical Operations Unit personnel. No other personnel install or service technical collection devices.

**Physical Entry for Installation:** If installation requires entering a location (which it typically does), a separate legal authorization for the entry is required in addition to the monitoring order. SACTF typically obtains a "sneak and peek" warrant authorizing covert entry.

**Termination:** When the authorization period expires, the collection device is deactivated. Retrieval is coordinated to minimize operational security risk.

### 3.4 GPS Tracking Devices

**Court authorization required:** Placement of a GPS device on a vehicle requires a court order.

**Installation:** Devices are installed by Technical Operations Unit personnel during periods of darkness or when the vehicle is in an unsupervised location. Installation is documented with photos of vehicle location and device placement.

**Monitoring:** GPS data is monitored continuously and entered into the operational picture. Significant movements — to locations not previously identified in the investigation, to meetings, to unusual times of day — are immediately reported to the case agent.

**Recovery:** Tracking devices are recovered at the end of the authorized period or when the vehicle is seized as evidence.

## Part IV: Surveillance Documentation

### 4.1 Surveillance Log

Every surveillance operation generates a surveillance log, completed contemporaneously. The log records:

- Operation identifier and date
- Personnel assigned (operator call signs or unit identifier, not full names in operational logs)
- Time surveillance initiated
- Target description at initiation — vehicle description, clothing, direction of travel
- Chronological log of all observations — time, location, target activity, contact with other individuals
- Any counter-surveillance activity observed
- Any loss of coverage — time, location, circumstances, duration
- Handoff times between Eyes
- Time surveillance terminated
- Post-operation assessment

### 4.2 Contact Identification

When surveillance observes the target meeting or associating with an unidentified individual:
- Full physical description is recorded
- Vehicle information if the individual arrived or departed in a vehicle
- Photographs if feasible without compromising coverage
- The contact is assigned a temporary identifier (CONTACT-1, CONTACT-2, etc.) for reference in the log

Contact identification is a priority intelligence requirement — unknown associates are among the most important leads in network mapping.

### 4.3 Photographic and Video Documentation

Where authorized by the operational plan and the legal framework, photographic and video documentation is collected during surveillance operations:

- All photographs and video are logged with date, time, operator identifier, and location
- Technical metadata is preserved
- Files are uploaded to the SACTF evidence management system with full documentation
- Chain of custody is established at the time of collection

## Part V: Surveillance Security

### 5.1 Team Security Practices

Surveillance teams use call signs, not names. Call sign assignments are rotated periodically.

Team members do not know more about the investigation than they need to conduct their surveillance assignment. The surveillance team's job is to observe and document — not to know the full investigative context.

Pre-operation briefings cover the target description, operation area, communication plan, and emergency procedures. They do not include classified investigation details that surveillance personnel do not need.

### 5.2 Cover for Action

Every surveillance operator has a cover story for their presence in the target area. The cover story is:
- Plausible for the environment
- Consistent with the operator's appearance and equipment
- Rehearsed before deployment
- Not the same for every operator (multiple operators with the same cover story is suspicious if counter-surveillance detects multiple people using it)

### 5.3 Emergency Extraction

If a surveillance operator is confronted, detained, or in immediate safety danger, they activate the emergency extraction signal. All other team members break contact with the target and move to designated extraction points. The operation is immediately aborted and the incident is reported.

**Confrontation procedure:** An operator who is confronted by the target or a member of the public (not law enforcement) handles it with their cover story. They do not identify as law enforcement. They do not confirm or deny surveillance activity. They remove themselves from the situation as quickly as naturally possible.

**Law enforcement contact:** If another law enforcement officer (non-SACTF) initiates contact with a surveillance operator, the operator may discreetly identify as law enforcement to the officer. The operator does not reveal the nature of the operation. The supervisor is immediately notified.
