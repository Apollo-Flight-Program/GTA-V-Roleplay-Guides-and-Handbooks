## Foreword

Interdiction is the tactical art of stopping a known or suspected threat before it reaches its target. Where tactical entry is the hammer — used when a threat is located, identified, and actionable — interdiction is the trap: a carefully positioned, legally defensible intervention at a point where a threat must pass.

SACTF interdiction operations range from a single officer conducting an intelligence-driven traffic stop on an interstate to a multi-agency checkpoint established at the border of a major public event. Each requires the same core discipline: clear legal authority, precise operational planning, disciplined execution, and meticulous documentation.

This directive governs all SACTF interdiction activities: vehicle stops, checkpoint operations, subject screening, and behavioral detection. Every principle here is grounded in the constitutional requirements that govern our authority. A poorly executed interdiction — one that cannot withstand legal scrutiny — does not merely fail; it poisons every case that flows from it.

## Part I: Legal Authority for Interdiction

### 1.1 The Fourth Amendment Framework

Interdiction is fundamentally a Fourth Amendment issue. Every stop — every moment we require a person to halt and answer questions — is a seizure under the law, and every seizure must be justified.

**The Three-Tier Framework**

| Tier | Type | Standard Required | Example |
|------|------|-------------------|---------|
| 1 | Consensual Encounter | None | Approaching a pedestrian and asking if they'd like to talk |
| 2 | Investigative Stop (Terry Stop) | Reasonable Articulable Suspicion (RAS) | Detaining a vehicle for a suspected traffic violation or threat indicator |
| 3 | Arrest | Probable Cause | Arresting a subject when we have evidence sufficient for a charging decision |

An interdiction begins at Tier 1 or Tier 2 and must not escalate without the legally required basis.

### 1.2 Reasonable Articulable Suspicion (RAS)

RAS is more than a gut feeling but less than probable cause. To justify a Tier 2 stop, the interdicting officer must be able to articulate specific observable facts that, combined with officer training and experience, created a reasonable belief that criminal activity was afoot.

**What Counts:**
- Specific behaviors consistent with pre-operational surveillance (driving slowly past a location multiple times)
- Matching a description from credible intelligence (vehicle matching a reported VBIED component delivery description)
- Observable equipment or materials not consistent with a legitimate stated purpose
- Reactions to law enforcement presence inconsistent with innocent activity
- Information from a reliable source specifically identifying this person or vehicle

**What Doesn't Count:**
- "He looked nervous" without more
- Race, religion, national origin, or ethnicity alone or as the primary basis
- "Gut feeling" without specific articulable facts
- General knowledge that "a lot of trafficking happens on this road"

### 1.3 Checkpoints

Suspicionless checkpoints — stops that do not require individual reasonable suspicion — are legally permissible in limited circumstances:

**Legally Permissible Checkpoints**
- Sobriety checkpoints
- Border checkpoints (at or near border areas)
- Security checkpoints at special events (courts, government buildings, permitted events)
- Emergency checkpoints established in response to an immediate public safety threat

**Key Requirements for All Checkpoints**
- Non-discretionary operation: Officers cannot select individual vehicles for screening based on personal judgment. The stop pattern must be predetermined (every vehicle, every nth vehicle, etc.)
- Supervisory oversight: A supervisor must be present and accountable for checkpoint operations
- Minimal intrusion: Unless escalating based on findings, initial screening should be brief
- Advance authorization: SACTF checkpoints require Tactical Operations Commander approval and, depending on the scenario, prosecutorial consultation

## Part II: Traffic Stop Interdiction

### 2.1 Intelligence-Driven Traffic Stops

An intelligence-driven stop is a traffic stop executed not because of a traffic violation, but because intelligence information identifies a specific vehicle or occupant as a threat or subject of interest.

**Pre-Stop Requirements**
Before conducting an intelligence-driven stop, the interdicting officer must have:
1. A documented intelligence basis (what is known, from what source, with what confidence level)
2. Supervisor notification (at minimum, advise that you are conducting the stop and on what basis)
3. A legally articulable reason for the stop that can withstand challenge — ideally a legitimate traffic violation observed, or documented RAS from the intelligence basis
4. Backup positioned or on-route

**Note on Pretextual Stops**
A traffic stop made ostensibly for a minor violation, but actually motivated by an intelligence basis, is a "pretextual stop." These are legally permissible as long as a genuine traffic violation was observed. However, if no actual violation was observed, the stop must be based on documented RAS. Officers should not invent traffic violations to justify intelligence-driven stops — if the intelligence alone creates RAS, document the intelligence basis.

### 2.2 Traffic Stop Protocol — Interdiction Level

Standard officer safety protocols apply, with the following additions:

**Positioning**
- The interdicting officer positions the primary unit behind and offset from the target vehicle
- Secondary unit (where available) positions at the right rear to cover the passenger side
- Officers do not position directly behind the target vehicle in line with the driver's door mirror — this is the highest-risk positioning for a vehicle-accelerated attack

**Initial Contact**
- Standard greeting and document request
- Observe: driver demeanor, number of occupants, visible items in the vehicle
- Note any deviations from normal compliant behavior
- Do not reveal the intelligence basis for the stop during initial contact

**Upgrade Indicators**
Any of the following during an initial contact justify escalation from a standard stop to a heightened-caution contact:
- Refusal to produce identification or documentation
- Implausible or inconsistent answers to standard questions
- Observable indicators of concealment (unusual nervousness, blocking view of interior, covering areas)
- Odors associated with chemicals, accelerants, or other materials of concern
- Visible items inconsistent with stated purpose of travel
- Presence of weapons-related items

**Extended Detention**
Detaining a vehicle beyond the time reasonably required for the purpose of the stop requires either:
- Consent to extended contact; or
- Developed RAS for a separate criminal matter; or
- Probable cause developed during the initial stop

Officers may not extend a stop to permit a K-9 unit to arrive on the mere hope of developing cause.

### 2.3 Consent Searches

A subject who consents to a search waives their Fourth Amendment protections for the scope of the consent given. SACTF officers may request consent to search a vehicle.

**Requirements for Valid Consent**
- Consent must be voluntary — not the product of coercion
- Consent may be withdrawn at any time, at which point the search stops
- Consent extends only to the scope actually agreed to ("Can I look in your trunk?" does not authorize search of the passenger compartment)
- The officer must be able to testify that the consent was given freely

**Best Practice**
Always document consent in writing when possible. If consent is verbal, document the exact words used and circumstances in the stop report.

### 2.4 K-9 Operations in Interdiction

SACTF may deploy K-9 units trained for explosive detection, drug detection, or both. K-9 sniffs of the exterior of a lawfully stopped vehicle do not require additional justification. K-9 sniffs of the interior require either consent or a search warrant.

**K-9 Alert**
A K-9 alert provides probable cause to search the vehicle without a warrant for the substance the K-9 is trained to detect.

**Documentation**
K-9 deployment must be documented including: handler identification, specific indication observed (sit, stare, scratch, bark), prior search history and reliability record for the specific animal.

## Part III: Checkpoint Operations

### 3.1 Types of Checkpoints

**Event Security Checkpoint (ESC)**
Established to screen entrants at public events, government facilities, or special venues. Designed to prevent weapons and materials from entering a protected location.

**Emergency Operational Checkpoint (EOC)**
Established in response to an immediate public safety threat — active search for a suspect, response to a credible attack warning, containment of a scene. Has different legal authority and operational urgency from routine checkpoints.

**Intelligence-Based Checkpoint (IBC)**
Established based on specific intelligence indicating a subject will travel a route or pass through a location. Requires the highest level of pre-authorization, the clearest intelligence basis, and the most carefully documented operation.

### 3.2 Checkpoint Planning

Before any checkpoint is activated, the supervisor must complete a Checkpoint Operations Plan addressing:

**Legal Authorization**
- What legal authority authorizes this checkpoint?
- Has prosecutorial consultation occurred where required?
- What is the checkpoint type (ESC / EOC / IBC)?

**Operational Parameters**
- Exact location and time window
- Selection criteria (every vehicle / every nth vehicle / specific trigger)
- Primary and secondary screening procedures
- De-escalation and escalation protocols

**Personnel and Resources**
- Checkpoint commander (supervisor on-site)
- Primary screeners
- Secondary screeners (for vehicles flagged for additional review)
- K-9 (if applicable)
- Tactical element (for elevated-threat checkpoints)
- Medical (for large operations)

**Communications**
- Checkpoint operating frequency
- Notification to dispatch/communications center
- Contact with host agency (LSPD, BCSO, SASP as applicable for jurisdiction)

### 3.3 Checkpoint Layout

**Standard Two-Lane Checkpoint Layout**

```
[BARRIER / CONES]
    |               |
[SCREENER]    [SCREENER]
    |               |
[SECONDARY] [SECONDARY]
    |               |
[SUPERVISOR POSITION]
         |
    [K-9 STAGING]
```

**Key Positions**

*Checkpoint Commander (Supervisor)*
- Maintains overall situational awareness
- Authorizes all escalations
- Communicates with outer security and SACTF command
- Manages pace and queue

*Primary Screener*
- Makes initial contact with vehicle occupants
- Conducts visual inspection and document check (if warranted by checkpoint type)
- Determines whether to clear or flag for secondary

*Secondary Screener*
- Conducts more detailed inspection of flagged vehicles
- Has authority to request consent search or deploy K-9
- Communicates findings to Checkpoint Commander before escalating

*Outer Security*
- Positioned before the primary screening line
- Monitors approaching vehicles for suspicious behavior before arrival at the checkpoint
- Positioned to stop vehicles that attempt to flee or evade

### 3.4 Primary Screening Protocol

At primary screening, the screener conducts:
1. Brief stop — no longer than required for the checkpoint's stated purpose
2. Visual inspection of the vehicle and occupants
3. Any requested documentation check (if authorized for this checkpoint type)
4. Question — consistent across all vehicles, not individually tailored unless cause develops
5. Clearance or secondary referral

**Primary Screening Script (Standardized)**
*"Good [morning/afternoon/evening]. This is a [state the type of checkpoint] checkpoint. [If document check is authorized: May I see your identification and registration?] [If not: We're checking all vehicles in the area. Thank you for your patience.] Are you carrying any weapons, explosives, or hazardous materials today?"*

The scripted protocol ensures non-discrimination in screening — the same questions, every vehicle.

### 3.5 Secondary Screening Protocol

Secondary screening is conducted when primary screening develops reasonable articulable suspicion or when the checkpoint protocol designates random secondary (e.g., every 5th vehicle).

**Secondary Screening Procedure**
1. Direct vehicle to secondary screening area, away from primary flow
2. Explain the basis for secondary screening
3. Conduct document verification and vehicle inspection as authorized
4. Determine whether to: clear, consent-search, K-9 deploy, or detain for investigation

**Time Limits**
Secondary screening should be completed within a reasonable timeframe. If an investigation develops beyond what can be resolved at the checkpoint, the vehicle and subject should be moved to a nearby facility rather than creating prolonged roadway detention.

### 3.6 Use of Behavioral Detection at Checkpoints

Behavioral detection is a structured approach to identifying individuals who exhibit pre-deception or pre-attack behavioral indicators — indicators that go beyond mere nervousness and reflect potential preparatory activity.

**Primary Behavioral Indicators for Checkpoint Context**

| Category | Indicator |
|----------|-----------|
| Approach | Abrupt lane change away from checkpoint when checkpoint becomes visible |
| Approach | Turnoff or U-turn at visible checkpoint (not clearly navigation-related) |
| Vehicle | Heavily loaded vehicle inconsistent with stated purpose |
| Vehicle | Signs of modification (extra weight, unusual geometry, sealed areas) |
| Occupant | Extreme nervousness inconsistent with a routine checkpoint stop |
| Occupant | Grooming inconsistent with stated role (recently shaved beard, fresh haircut combined with travel gear) |
| Occupant | Rehearsed or scripted responses with unusual affect |
| Occupant | Unusual clothing for ambient temperature (obscuring a device) |
| Occupant | Fixed gaze, rigid posture, or stress indicators inconsistent with innocent nervousness |
| Documents | Documents that are inconsistent with each other or with stated purpose |

**Important Caveat**
No single indicator justifies secondary referral or detention. Behavioral detection requires pattern recognition — multiple indicators in combination, in context. Race, religion, national origin, and protected class characteristics are **never** legitimate behavioral indicators and must never factor into screening decisions.

## Part IV: Tactical Interdiction

### 4.1 Felony Traffic Stop

A felony stop (high-risk stop) is used when the interdicting officer has specific reason to believe the vehicle occupants are armed and dangerous — not merely that they may be armed, but that the totality of circumstances justifies the highest-caution approach.

**Indicators Justifying Felony Stop Procedures**
- Specific intelligence indicating subject is armed and has expressed intent to resist
- Subject is wanted for violent crimes
- Observed weapons in the vehicle prior to stop
- Behavior during the stop suggesting immediate threat (reaching, erratic movement, failure to comply)

**Felony Stop Procedure**
1. Position units to create a blocking environment (angled units at the front and rear)
2. Issue commands by PA: "Driver, turn off the vehicle and place your keys on the roof."
3. "Driver, with your left hand, open the door from the outside."
4. "Driver, step out slowly. Keep your hands visible."
5. Direct driver to a controlled position at the rear of the vehicle using PA commands
6. Move to take custody of the driver with a team — covering officers maintain on the vehicle
7. Clear remaining occupants one at a time using the same procedure
8. Only when all occupants are secured does any officer approach the vehicle

**Critical Principle:** The vehicle is approached *after* occupants are secured, not during. No officer voluntarily positions themselves alongside a potentially occupied vehicle while unknown occupants remain inside.

### 4.2 Vehicle Pursuit in Interdiction Context

When a vehicle attempts to flee an interdiction:
1. Immediately notify the supervisor and dispatch
2. Follow standard pursuit protocol — pursuit authorization goes through the Checkpoint Commander or primary unit supervisor
3. Do not initiate pursuit without authorization
4. Roadblock and tactical vehicle intervention (TVI) require supervisor authorization and are not self-authorized

### 4.3 Armed/Violent Resistance at Checkpoint

When armed resistance occurs at a checkpoint:
1. Immediate alert to all checkpoint personnel: "THREAT! THREAT!" and direction
2. Screeners move to covered positions
3. Tactical element activates (from staging) and moves to contain
4. Non-tactical personnel maintain perimeter
5. Checkpoint Commander assumes incident command
6. Civilians and vehicles in queue are directed away
7. All response governed by SACTF Use of Force Policy (see `18-Use-of-Force-Policy.md`)

## Part V: High-Value Target (HVT) Interdiction

### 5.1 Definition

HVT interdiction is the planned stop of a specific, named individual whose intelligence profile indicates that they represent a significant threat, are carrying materials of significance, or whose detention/contact at a specific point would substantially advance an ongoing investigation.

HVT interdiction is not a routine traffic stop or a checkpoint screening. It is a planned, intelligence-driven, targeted operation.

### 5.2 Pre-Operation Requirements

HVT interdiction requires:

**Intelligence Package**
- Full target profile including physical description, vehicle information, route intelligence
- Source reliability assessment for route/timing information
- Associated threat profile (is this individual assessed to be armed? Likely to resist?)
- Known associates and potential for additional occupants

**Legal Basis**
- If an arrest warrant exists: the stop is a warrant arrest
- If no arrest warrant exists: the interdiction basis must independently satisfy RAS or probable cause requirements
- Legal Division review required before all HVT interdictions

**Operational Plan**
- Stop location: selected for tactical advantage (away from crowded areas, good sight lines, controlled environment where possible)
- Team composition: sufficient for the assessed threat
- Containment: backup positioned to prevent vehicle flight
- Contingencies: for resistance, for multiple occupants, for armed confrontation

**Command Authorization**
All HVT interdictions require Tactical Operations Commander written authorization.

### 5.3 Execution

HVT interdiction is executed as a felony stop (see 4.1) regardless of whether the initial RAS suggests the subject is armed. The intelligence profile of an HVT subject justifies elevated caution as a baseline.

### 5.4 Post-Interdiction

Following HVT interdiction:
1. **Scene Documentation:** The stop location is treated as a potential crime scene. Vehicle is photographed before search. Any contraband is photographed in place before removal.
2. **Immediate Debrief:** The interdiction team conducts an immediate verbal debrief before leaving the scene to capture impressions and observations.
3. **Intelligence Exploitation:** Any materials seized are immediately passed to the Intelligence Division for exploitation.
4. **Notification:** FIB liaison and prosecutive authorities are notified as appropriate.

## Part VI: Documentation and Reporting

### 6.1 Traffic Stop Documentation

Every interdiction traffic stop requires:
- **Interdiction Stop Report** documenting: time, location, basis for stop, vehicle and occupant information, questions asked, responses given, observations, any search conducted and its basis, disposition
- **Evidence Documentation** for any items seized
- **Supervisor Review** prior to submission

### 6.2 Checkpoint Documentation

Every checkpoint activation requires:
- **Checkpoint Activation Log** including: authorization, start/end times, vehicles processed, secondaries conducted, results
- **Individual Contact Reports** for any secondary screening that results in detention or search
- **Incident Reports** for any use of force or resistance
- **Post-Checkpoint Summary** submitted to Tactical Operations Commander within 24 hours

### 6.3 After-Action Review

All major interdiction operations (HVT interdiction, armed resistance, large checkpoint operations) require an After-Action Review per `20-After-Action-Reporting.md`.

## Part VII: Special Interdiction Scenarios

### 7.1 Aircraft Interdiction Coordination

When intelligence suggests a threat arriving or departing via aircraft:
- Coordinate with SASP aviation and airport security authority
- SACTF does not have authority to stop aircraft directly — work through appropriate airport and aviation authority
- Intelligence should be shared through the inter-agency coordination process (`16-Inter-Agency-Coordination.md`)
- If the threat is airside, FIB coordination is required

### 7.2 Maritime-Adjacent Interdiction

For threats arriving via coastal or waterway routes:
- SACTF coordinates with FIB marine operations
- SACTF provides intelligence support; maritime enforcement action is through FIB/SASP
- Shoreside interdiction at points of embarkation/disembarkation is within SACTF authority and follows standard vehicle/pedestrian stop procedures

### 7.3 Mass Event Interdiction

For major public events (concerts, sporting events, public gatherings) where intelligence identifies a potential threat:
- SACTF Intelligence provides threat assessment to event security and host agency
- If SACTF activates an interdiction element, it operates embedded with the host agency's event command
- All use of force at mass events is subject to heightened scrutiny — the Tactical Operations Commander must approve any use of force beyond presence/communication
