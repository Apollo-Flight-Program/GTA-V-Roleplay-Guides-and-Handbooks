## Part I: Intelligence Doctrine

### 1.1 The Role of Intelligence in Counterterrorism

Intelligence is not a product. It is a process — a continuous cycle of asking questions, collecting information, analyzing what it means, and acting on what is learned. In counterterrorism, that cycle is the difference between preventing an attack and responding to one.

The SACTF's operational effectiveness is entirely dependent on the quality of its intelligence function. Tactical capability without intelligence is unfocused force. Investigative work without intelligence is reactive rather than anticipatory. The Intelligence Division is not a support function for operations — it is the foundation on which operations are built.

Every person assigned to this Task Force, regardless of their primary role, is a collector. Investigators encounter information. Tactical operators observe environments. Undercover personnel develop sources. All of this information has analytical value, and all of it must flow into the intelligence process.

### 1.2 Intelligence vs. Information

**Information** is raw material — an observation, a report, a communication intercept, a financial record. It has no analytical value until it is processed.

**Intelligence** is processed, evaluated information that provides understanding. It answers questions that matter for decision-making: What is this organization capable of? What is it planning? Who are its members? Where is it most vulnerable?

The transformation from information to intelligence requires analysis — skilled human judgment that places raw material in context, evaluates its reliability, assesses its significance, and integrates it with other information to produce understanding.

All SACTF personnel must understand this distinction. Reporting raw information to the IAC is valuable and required. Drawing unsupported analytical conclusions from that information — particularly when communicating to command — is dangerous.

### 1.3 The Intelligence Cycle

The SACTF operates on the standard intelligence cycle, adapted for the operational tempo and threat environment of a counterterrorism task force:

**1. Planning and Direction**  
Command identifies priority intelligence requirements (PIRs) — the questions most critical to decision-making. The Intelligence Division allocates collection resources to address those requirements. PIRs are reviewed at each daily intelligence briefing and adjusted as the operational picture evolves.

**2. Collection**  
Information is gathered through all authorized means: human sources, physical and technical surveillance, open-source research, partner agency reporting, judicial process (warrants for records and communications), and investigative activity by Investigations Division personnel.

**3. Processing**  
Raw collected material is processed into a form suitable for analysis — translated, transcribed, indexed, cross-referenced, and entered into the case management and intelligence systems.

**4. Analysis and Production**  
Analysts evaluate processed material, assess source reliability, integrate new information with existing knowledge, and produce analytical products — assessments, reports, target packages — that answer the PIRs and support operational decision-making.

**5. Dissemination**  
Intelligence products are distributed to the personnel and agencies that need them. Dissemination is governed by classification, compartmentation, and need-to-know. Not all intelligence products are distributed to all personnel.

**6. Feedback and Evaluation**  
Intelligence consumers — operators, investigators, command — evaluate whether products answered their requirements and provide feedback to the IAC. The cycle then begins again, incorporating what was learned.

## Part II: Collection Disciplines

### 2.1 HUMINT — Human Intelligence

**Definition:** Intelligence derived from human sources — individuals who provide information about threats through direct observation, access, or relationship.

**Source Categories:**

*Confidential Informants (CIs):* Individuals who provide information to the Task Force, typically motivated by financial compensation, legal benefit, or ideological opposition to the target organization. CI management is governed by strict protocols detailed in `08-Informant-and-Source-Management.md`.

*Undercover Personnel:* Task Force personnel operating in assumed identities within or adjacent to target organizations. Governed by the Undercover Operations Program.

*Community Contacts:* Members of the public, community leaders, and other non-source individuals who provide information on a non-systematic basis. Managed by Investigations Division personnel.

*Liaison Sources:* Information provided by partner agencies — LSPD intelligence functions, BCSO intelligence, FIB, and others — through established liaison relationships.

**HUMINT Requirements:**
- All human source relationships must be documented and registered through the approved process
- Source reliability assessments are required for all CIs and undercover placements
- HUMINT from a single uncorroborated source is assessed as low confidence
- Source identity information is protected at the highest applicable classification level

### 2.2 SIGINT — Signals Intelligence

**Definition:** Intelligence derived from the interception of electronic communications and signals.

**SACTF SIGINT Programs:**

*Telephone Intercept:* Court-authorized monitoring of telephone communications. All intercepts require a specific judicial order meeting applicable legal standards. Applications are prepared by the Legal Division and reviewed by the Deputy Director — Intelligence before filing.

*Digital Communications:* Court-authorized monitoring of email, messaging applications, and other digital communications. Same requirements as telephone intercept.

*Radio Monitoring:* Monitoring of radio communications on frequencies associated with target activity. Monitoring of unencrypted communications does not require court authorization. Monitoring of encrypted communications requires legal review.

*Technical Device Operations:* Court-authorized placement of listening devices (audio) or cameras (video) in locations relevant to an investigation. These are the most sensitivity-intensive technical collection methods and require the highest level of authorization.

**Legal Compliance:** The Legal Division reviews all SIGINT collection operations. No SIGINT collection is initiated without a legal review memorandum. Personnel who initiate SIGINT collection without legal authorization face administrative and potentially criminal consequences.

### 2.3 OSINT — Open-Source Intelligence

**Definition:** Intelligence derived from publicly available sources — social media, news reporting, public records, commercial databases, and similar material.

**OSINT Methods:**

*Social Media Monitoring:* Review of publicly accessible social media accounts associated with or relevant to target individuals and organizations. Monitoring of public content does not require legal authorization. Accessing non-public content requires court process.

*Open-Source Research:* Review of news reporting, public financial disclosures, court records, property records, business registrations, and similar public documents.

*Dark Web Monitoring:* Monitoring of forums and communications platforms used by extremist communities. Requires specialized technical capability and legal review for any active interaction.

**OSINT Limitations:** Open-source material is subject to manipulation, disinformation, and deliberate targeting. Assessments based primarily on OSINT require corroboration from other collection disciplines before supporting high-confidence conclusions.

### 2.4 FININT — Financial Intelligence

**Definition:** Intelligence derived from financial records and transaction analysis.

**Legal Access:** Financial records are obtained through judicial subpoenas, grand jury process, bank examiner cooperation, and voluntarily disclosed records. No financial records are obtained without legal authorization.

**Analytical Value:** Financial analysis is among the most valuable intelligence tools available to the Task Force. Financial transactions are objective records — they are created by parties to the transaction without awareness of investigative interest, making them reliable in a way that testimonial evidence often is not. Financial analysis can:

- Map the organizational structure of a terrorist network through payment patterns
- Identify previously unknown network members who receive or send funds
- Establish timelines for planning activities
- Link otherwise separated individuals through common financial relationships
- Identify funding sources and channels

**Terrorism Financing:** Specific financial intelligence collection focused on the identification and disruption of terrorism financing networks is the responsibility of the Financial Investigations Unit. Full procedures are in `09-Cyber-and-Financial-Intelligence.md`.

### 2.5 TECHINT — Technical Intelligence

**Definition:** Intelligence derived from the analysis of technical equipment, materials, and systems associated with target activities.

**Application in CTTF Context:**
- Analysis of recovered explosive devices and IED components
- Technical examination of communications equipment
- Analysis of surveillance or counter-surveillance technology used by targets
- Examination of weapons and weapons systems
- Assessment of CBRN-related materials and equipment

Technical intelligence collection is primarily conducted by the WMD/Critical Infrastructure Squad and the Evidence Response Team, with analytical support from the IAC.

## Part III: Priority Intelligence Requirements

### 3.1 Standing PIRs

The following intelligence requirements are standing — they are always in effect, regardless of specific operational focus:

**PIR-1: Intent and Capability**  
What are identified threat actors planning, and do they have the capability to execute those plans?

**PIR-2: Network Mapping**  
Who are the members and associates of identified threat organizations? What are the command, communications, logistics, and financial relationships within the network?

**PIR-3: Radicalization Indicators**  
What individuals within the state are showing signs of radicalization toward violence? What are the vectors through which radicalization is occurring?

**PIR-4: Financial Networks**  
What financial mechanisms support identified threat organizations? Who are the funders, facilitators, and money movers?

**PIR-5: Safe Houses and Infrastructure**  
Where are threat organizations operating? What physical locations — residences, storage facilities, meeting places — support their activities?

**PIR-6: Counter-Surveillance Activity**  
Are identified targets aware of Task Force interest? What counter-surveillance methods are they employing?

### 3.2 Operation-Specific PIRs

Each active investigation generates its own specific PIRs, approved by the case SSA and logged in the case file. Operation-specific PIRs guide collection allocation for that investigation.

## Part IV: Intelligence Analysis Standards

### 4.1 Analytic Objectivity

Intelligence analysis must reflect the evidence. Analysts do not confirm desired conclusions, shade assessments to please command, or suppress information that contradicts a prevailing assessment. The obligation to deliver accurate intelligence exists regardless of whether that intelligence supports or complicates current operational activity.

**Challenge Obligation:** Analysts who believe a prevailing assessment is wrong have a professional obligation to say so, through the analytic disagreement process. Suppressed disagreement has caused catastrophic intelligence failures. The SACTF analytic culture requires that disagreement be expressed, documented, and given honest evaluation.

### 4.2 Source Reliability and Information Credibility

All intelligence products must evaluate both the **reliability of the source** and the **credibility of the information**.

**Source Reliability** assesses how trustworthy the source is overall:
- **A — Reliable:** Demonstrated history of providing accurate information; no known motivation to deceive
- **B — Usually Reliable:** Mostly accurate historical record; minor reliability concerns
- **C — Fairly Reliable:** Mixed accuracy record; treat with caution
- **D — Not Usually Reliable:** Poor accuracy record or significant motivation concerns
- **E — Unreliable:** Documented history of providing false information
- **F — Cannot Be Judged:** Insufficient information to assess reliability

**Information Credibility** assesses whether this specific item of information is believable:
- **1 — Confirmed:** Corroborated by another reliable source
- **2 — Probably True:** Consistent with other information; no contradictions
- **3 — Possibly True:** Plausible but not corroborated
- **4 — Doubtful:** Inconsistent with other information or implausible given context
- **5 — Improbable:** Contradicts confirmed information or is highly implausible
- **6 — Cannot Be Judged:** Insufficient basis for assessment

All intelligence reports include a reliability/credibility assessment in the format [Letter/Number] — e.g., B/2 (Usually Reliable source, Probably True information).

### 4.3 Confidence Levels

Intelligence assessments express confidence in conclusions using standardized language:

- **HIGH CONFIDENCE:** Assessment is well-supported by multiple reliable sources; the conclusion is robust to alternative interpretations of the evidence.
- **MODERATE CONFIDENCE:** Assessment is supported by available information but there are gaps, source reliability concerns, or alternative interpretations that cannot be fully dismissed.
- **LOW CONFIDENCE:** Assessment is based on limited information, single-source reporting, or information with significant reliability concerns. The conclusion is reasonable but should not be treated as established.

Confidence levels are always stated explicitly in analytical products.

### 4.4 Alternative Analysis

For significant assessments, analysts are required to document alternative interpretations — explanations of the evidence that lead to a different conclusion. The obligation to consider alternatives prevents confirmation bias and produces more honest assessments of analytical uncertainty.

## Part V: Intelligence Products

### 5.1 Daily Intelligence Summary (DIS)

**Purpose:** Current-focus situational awareness for Task Force leadership and relevant partner agencies.  
**Length:** 1–3 pages.  
**Frequency:** Daily, produced by morning.  
**Content:** Significant overnight developments; current threat level assessments for active investigations; items requiring immediate command attention; analyst notes.  
**Distribution:** Director, Deputy Directors, Division Commanders, designated partner agency points of contact.

### 5.2 Threat Assessment

**Purpose:** Evaluated analysis of a specific threat — a credible report of planned attack, a newly identified organization, a significant change in an existing target's activity.  
**Length:** Variable; typically 3–10 pages.  
**Frequency:** As required.  
**Content:** Threat description; source information and reliability; capability assessment; intent assessment; timeline assessment (if determinable); recommended response options.  
**Distribution:** Director and all relevant division commanders. Partner agencies on need-to-know.

### 5.3 Target Package

**Purpose:** Comprehensive intelligence compilation on a specific target individual or organization, used as the foundation for operational planning.  
**Length:** Typically 10–30 pages plus attachments.  
**Frequency:** Produced for each significant investigative target; updated as new information is developed.  
**Content:** Subject identification; organizational affiliation and role; known associates and relationships; financial information; location information; behavioral pattern analysis; threat assessment; collection gaps; recommended collection priorities.  
**Distribution:** Case team, relevant command authority. Portions shared with partner agencies as needed for operational coordination.

### 5.4 Weekly Threat Report

**Purpose:** Extended analysis of the current threat environment for partner agency leadership.  
**Length:** 5–10 pages.  
**Frequency:** Weekly.  
**Content:** Threat trend analysis; significant developments across all active investigative areas; regional threat level summary; emerging threat indicators.  
**Distribution:** Contributing agency heads (executive summary), senior intelligence personnel at partner agencies (full report).

### 5.5 Special Intelligence Reports

As-needed products for specific situations: analysis of recovered material from a crime scene, urgent assessment of a developing situation, specialized products responding to specific command requests.

## Part VI: Intelligence Security

### 6.1 Compartmentation

Not all Task Force personnel have access to all intelligence. Access is governed by role and by the compartment the intelligence is assigned to. Current compartments are documented in the Access Control Registry maintained by the Intelligence Division.

Personnel who receive intelligence material outside their compartment access — because it was sent to them in error, because someone shared it verbally, or for any other reason — must immediately report the over-dissemination to their supervisor and the Intelligence Division.

### 6.2 Information Security Protocols

**Physical security:** Classified material is stored in approved facilities when not in active use. Classified documents are not left unattended. Laptops and devices containing classified material are encrypted.

**Digital security:** SACTF intelligence systems are operated on networks that are physically and logically separated from general agency systems. Personnel do not transmit classified intelligence material on unclassified systems.

**Communication security:** Classified discussion is restricted to secure facilities or secure communications systems. Personnel do not discuss operational details in public spaces, vehicles, or over non-secure phone lines.

### 6.3 Counterintelligence Awareness

Terrorist organizations routinely attempt to identify and penetrate law enforcement operations. SACTF personnel must maintain counterintelligence awareness — recognizing potential penetration attempts and reporting them promptly.

**Signs of potential counterintelligence threat:**
- Unexpected knowledge of Task Force activity by target subjects
- Unexplained compromises of surveillance or undercover operations
- Targeted social contact with SACTF personnel by individuals connected to investigation targets
- Access attempts to SACTF facilities or systems by unauthorized persons

Any potential counterintelligence concern is immediately reported to the Deputy Director — Intelligence.
