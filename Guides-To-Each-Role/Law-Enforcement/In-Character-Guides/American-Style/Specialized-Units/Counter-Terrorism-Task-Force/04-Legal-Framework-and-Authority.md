## Part I: Constitutional Foundation

### 1.1 The Fourth Amendment and Counterterrorism

The Fourth Amendment prohibits unreasonable searches and seizures. It requires that warrants be supported by probable cause, describe with particularity what is to be searched and what is to be seized, and be issued by a neutral and detached magistrate.

In counterterrorism investigations, the Fourth Amendment governs:
- Surveillance of communications
- Physical searches of residences, vehicles, and persons
- Seizure of property
- Placement of tracking or monitoring devices
- Access to stored records

**The Special Needs Doctrine:** Courts have recognized limited exceptions to the warrant requirement for certain government interests that go beyond ordinary law enforcement. In the counterterrorism context, intelligence gathering has sometimes been treated differently from criminal investigation. SACTF personnel must understand that these distinctions are narrow, subject to ongoing legal development, and cannot be invoked to avoid warrant requirements for investigations that are fundamentally criminal in character.

**Plain View:** If SACTF personnel are lawfully present in a location and observe evidence in plain view, they may seize it without an additional warrant. However, plain view seizures must be documented with the same rigor as warrant-authorized seizures, and the basis for lawful presence must be clearly established.

### 1.2 The First Amendment and Investigations of Expressive Activity

The First Amendment protects freedom of speech, association, religion, and press. Terrorism investigations frequently involve individuals whose extremist ideologies are expressed through constitutionally protected activity — speech, writing, attending meetings, associating with like-minded individuals.

**The critical distinction:** Investigating the ideology is not the same as investigating the act. SACTF investigations must be predicated on conduct — specific, articulable facts indicating criminal activity — not on the expression of views, however objectionable those views are. An individual who advocates for extremist political positions, attends extremist meetings, and publishes extremist material is engaging in protected activity. The same individual who also takes steps toward planning or facilitating a violent attack has crossed into criminal conduct that may be investigated.

This distinction must be maintained in all investigative activity. The fact that an investigation was triggered by observing protected activity does not make the investigation unconstitutional — the constitutional obligation is that the investigation then identifies specific criminal conduct as its basis.

### 1.3 Due Process and Equal Protection

**Due Process:** All persons — including suspects, subjects of surveillance, and arrested individuals — retain due process rights. SACTF investigations must be conducted in ways that respect these rights, particularly with respect to:
- The right to contest detention before a neutral authority
- The right to know the charges against you
- The right to prepare a defense

**Equal Protection:** Investigations are not conducted on the basis of race, religion, national origin, gender, sexual orientation, or other protected characteristics. Threat-based investigations must be predicated on specific, articulable facts about individual conduct — not on demographic profiling. Profiling not only violates constitutional rights; it is a documented intelligence failure that wastes resources on the innocent while potentially missing actual threats.

## Part II: Warrant Requirements and Process

### 2.1 Search Warrants

**Probable Cause Standard:** A search warrant must be supported by probable cause — facts and circumstances that would cause a reasonable person to believe that the evidence sought is located in the place to be searched. The standard is not absolute certainty, but it is more than a hunch or suspicion.

**Particularity Requirement:** The warrant must describe with particularity the place to be searched and the items to be seized. General warrants — "search the premises and seize any evidence of a crime" — are not valid. SACTF affidavits must identify specific locations and specific items.

**Affidavit Preparation:** All SACTF search warrant affidavits are reviewed by the Legal Division before filing. The Legal Division reviews:
- Whether the facts stated support probable cause
- Whether the facts are based on reliable sources that are appropriately described
- Whether the particularity requirement is met
- Whether any information in the affidavit requires classification protection

**Timeliness:** Search warrants must be executed within the time specified by the issuing court. In San Andreas, standard search warrants are typically valid for ten days. Time-critical warrant applications note the basis for urgency.

**After-Action Warrant Return:** After execution of a search warrant, a return must be filed with the issuing court confirming execution and describing what was found and seized. This is a legal obligation, not an administrative option. Failure to file a proper return can compromise the prosecution.

### 2.2 Arrest Warrants

**Probable Cause for Arrest:** An arrest warrant requires probable cause to believe that the named individual has committed a specified crime.

**Warrantless Arrest Authority:** Officers may arrest without a warrant when they have probable cause to believe a felony has been committed and there is no time to obtain a warrant. In the counterterrorism context, given the potential scale of harm from terrorist acts, courts have generally accepted a lower threshold for urgency in warranting warrantless arrest decisions. However, warrantless arrests remain the exception — the standard of getting a warrant whenever feasible applies.

**Exigent Circumstances:** Entry into a structure for the purpose of a warrantless arrest based on exigent circumstances (imminent danger to life, hot pursuit, destruction of evidence) is a legally narrow authority. The exigent circumstances must be documented immediately and thoroughly. SACTF does not treat exigent circumstances as a routine workaround for warrant requirements.

### 2.3 Electronic Surveillance Authorization

**General Electronic Surveillance:** Court orders are required for real-time monitoring of electronic communications — telephone calls, text messages, email, and similar communications. Applications for these orders require:
- A sworn affidavit from an officer establishing probable cause
- Identification of the specific facilities or accounts to be monitored
- Description of the offenses being investigated
- Statement that normal investigative procedures have been tried and failed, or are unlikely to succeed, or are too dangerous to employ
- Description of the period for which surveillance is authorized (standard maximum 30 days; extendable with additional showing)

**Pen Register / Trap-and-Trace:** Collection of the fact of communications — who called whom, when, and for how long, without content — has a lower authorization standard than content interception. Court authorization is still required, but the showing needed is less than probable cause.

**Stored Communications:** Access to stored electronic communications (email in a provider's server, stored messages) requires a court order. The specific requirements depend on the age and type of the stored communications.

**Tracking Devices:** Placement of a GPS tracking device on a vehicle requires a court order supported by probable cause. The order must specify the vehicle and the duration of tracking.

### 2.4 Grand Jury Process

The grand jury is a powerful investigative tool that SACTF utilizes through the prosecutive authority of the District Attorney and federal prosecutive partners.

**Grand Jury Subpoenas:** A subpoena issued by a grand jury compels the production of documents, records, or testimony. Grand jury subpoenas are used extensively to obtain:
- Financial records from banks and financial institutions
- Business records
- Communications records
- Testimony from witnesses

**Testimonial Compulsion:** Witnesses who receive a grand jury subpoena for testimony are compelled to appear and answer questions. A witness may assert Fifth Amendment privilege to avoid self-incriminating answers. The prosecution may respond with a grant of immunity, which compels testimony but prevents the testimony from being used against the witness.

**Secrecy:** Grand jury proceedings are secret. Information developed through grand jury process may not be disclosed outside authorized channels. SACTF personnel who receive grand jury material handle it under strict secrecy requirements.

## Part III: Special Authorities in Terrorism Investigations

### 3.1 Material Support Statutes

It is unlawful to provide material support or resources to a designated terrorist organization or for a terrorist act. "Material support" includes:
- Financial contributions
- Training
- Logistics support
- Communications equipment
- Personnel (including oneself)
- Expert advice or assistance

The material support statutes are among the most broadly applicable tools in terrorism prosecution. They extend criminal liability to the broader support network that enables terrorist activity, not just the individuals who commit or plan specific acts.

**Key Distinctions:** Material support charges require that the support be knowing — the individual must know that the organization is a terrorist organization, or that the activity being supported constitutes terrorism. General charitable giving to an organization that was later designated (without prior knowledge of its terroristic character) does not meet this standard.

### 3.2 Conspiracy Charges

Conspiracy to commit a terrorism offense — an agreement between two or more persons to commit such an offense, plus at least one overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy — is criminally prosecutable separately from, and often before, the underlying offense. Conspiracy charges allow prosecution at earlier stages of planning, before the terrorist act occurs.

**Pinkerton Liability:** Under established conspiracy doctrine, a conspirator may be held liable for the reasonably foreseeable crimes committed by co-conspirators in furtherance of the conspiracy. This doctrine has significant application in network-level terrorism prosecution.

### 3.3 Emergency Authorities

In circumstances involving an imminent threat of a terrorist act that is likely to produce serious bodily harm or death, certain emergency authorities allow expedited action:

**Emergency Surveillance:** An emergency wiretap may be initiated before obtaining court authorization when there is an immediate threat and insufficient time to obtain an order. Emergency intercepts must be immediately reported to the appropriate court, which then issues a retroactive authorization or orders termination. Personnel initiating emergency surveillance must document the basis in contemporaneous writing.

**Emergency Entry:** When personnel have an objectively reasonable belief that an immediate threat to life exists within a structure, they may enter without a warrant to address that specific emergency. Emergency entry authority is strictly limited to addressing the specific emergency; it does not authorize a general search of the premises.

**Emergency Arrest:** As noted above, warrantless arrest based on probable cause is available when the time to obtain a warrant is not available. Emergency circumstances must be documented.

All emergency authority uses are immediately reported up the chain of command and to the Legal Division.

## Part IV: Evidence Law in Terrorism Investigations

### 4.1 Chain of Custody

The reliability of physical evidence in court depends on the prosecution's ability to establish an unbroken chain of custody — a documented record of who had the evidence, when, and what happened to it during that time. In terrorism cases, where the volume of evidence may be enormous and the prosecution timeline may extend years from collection to trial, chain of custody documentation is critical.

**SACTF chain of custody requirements:**
- Every item of evidence is tagged with a unique evidence number at the point of collection
- A chain of custody form accompanies every item of evidence through every transfer
- Each person who receives custody signs the chain of custody form, documents the date and time, and describes the condition of the evidence
- Storage conditions are documented and appropriate to the type of evidence
- Electronic evidence is handled under procedures designed to preserve its integrity against alteration claims

### 4.2 The Exclusionary Rule

Evidence obtained in violation of the Fourth Amendment is generally excluded from criminal proceedings. This is the most significant legal consequence of unlawful search and seizure in the counterterrorism context.

**Fruit of the Poisonous Tree:** Evidence derived from an unlawful search or seizure is also typically excluded, even if its collection was itself technically lawful. A warrantless search that produces a name may not allow that name to be used as the basis for a subsequent warrant if the original search was unlawful.

**Good Faith Exception:** Evidence may not be excluded if officers had an objectively reasonable belief that their conduct was lawful — for example, executing a warrant that turns out to have been issued in error, when officers had no reason to know of the error. The good faith exception does not protect knowing or reckless disregard of constitutional requirements.

### 4.3 Classified Evidence in Prosecution

Terrorism prosecutions frequently involve evidence derived from classified intelligence sources and methods. The use of classified evidence in prosecution is governed by a procedure that protects national security while preserving the defendant's right to a fair trial.

**Clearance for Defense Counsel:** In cases involving classified material, defense counsel may be granted security clearance to review classified evidence under specified conditions.

**Substitution:** In some cases, the classified information may be substituted with an unclassified summary or statement of facts that provides the defendant with the essential content without revealing sources and methods.

**Dismissal vs. Disclosure:** If the prosecution cannot provide the defendant with access to classified material that is essential to a fair defense — and substitution is inadequate — the court may require either disclosure or dismissal. SACTF operations are planned with awareness that classified collection must eventually be disclosed or that prosecutions built entirely on classified sources carry legal risk.

## Part V: Oversight and Accountability

### 5.1 Internal Oversight

**Legal Division Review:** The Legal Division reviews all non-routine operations before execution. The review standard: is this operation legally authorized? Is it being conducted in a manner consistent with applicable law? Is it consistent with SACTF policy?

**Inspector General Function:** The IG function reviews completed operations and investigations for compliance, identifies systemic issues, and reports findings to the Director. IG reviews are not adversarial audits of individual performance — they are systemic quality assessments that produce improvement recommendations.

**After-Action Legal Review:** Any operation involving use of force, emergency authority, or any novel legal authority receives a legal review as part of the after-action process.

### 5.2 External Oversight

The SACTF operates under several forms of external oversight:

**Judicial Oversight:** Every warrant, wiretap order, and grand jury process is subject to judicial review. Courts review SACTF applications and may reject them, modify them, or later suppress evidence if they are found to have been issued improperly.

**Legislative Oversight:** The applicable legislative committee receives periodic briefings from SACTF leadership on the Task Force's activities. Personnel are not directed to conceal lawful activities from legislative oversight.

**Contributing Agency Oversight:** Each contributing agency retains oversight responsibility for its personnel assigned to SACTF. Home-agency leadership may review the conduct of assigned personnel. SACTF cooperates with legitimate home-agency oversight requests.

**Civil Litigation:** Individuals who believe their rights were violated by SACTF operations may bring civil claims. SACTF cooperates with the legal defense of civil claims; it does not impede access to records for litigation purposes, subject to applicable classification requirements.

### 5.3 Reporting Obligations

Personnel have the following mandatory reporting obligations:

**Unlawful Orders:** Any order that appears to be unlawful must be reported to the Legal Division immediately. Personnel are not required to obey unlawful orders.

**Witness to Misconduct:** Any witness to potential misconduct by another SACTF member reports to their supervisor. If the supervisor is implicated, the report goes directly to the IG function.

**Security Incidents:** Any potential security incident — unauthorized access, potential disclosure of classified information, potential counterintelligence threat — is immediately reported to the Intelligence Division.

**Use of Force:** All use of force is immediately reported to the direct supervisor, regardless of whether injury resulted.
