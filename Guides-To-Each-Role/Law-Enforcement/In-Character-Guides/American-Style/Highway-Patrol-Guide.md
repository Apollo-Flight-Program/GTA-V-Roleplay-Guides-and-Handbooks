# Highway Patrol Guide — Owning the Asphalt

Highway patrol is not regular policing on a faster road. It is a specialized discipline that demands a different mindset, a different skill set, and a different relationship with the environment you operate in. The highway is your beat — not a neighborhood, not a district, not a precinct grid. It is miles of asphalt moving at lethal speeds, where every traffic stop puts you inches from vehicles doing 70 miles per hour, where a single inattentive driver can turn a fender-bender into a mass casualty event, and where backup may be ten minutes away across open desert.

Dedicated highway patrol officers — whether operating as State Troopers, Highway Patrol Officers, or specialized units within a municipal or county agency — exist because highways are unforgiving environments that cannot be policed as an afterthought. A city patrol officer might handle a traffic stop on a 35 mph residential street between calls. A highway patrol officer conducts that same stop on the shoulder of a freeway with 18-wheelers blasting past at 75 mph. The margin for error is zero.

This guide covers every aspect of dedicated highway patrol operations in the State of San Andreas. It is written for officers who will spend the majority of their shifts on the freeway system — running speed enforcement, inspecting commercial vehicles, managing accident scenes, conducting DUI operations, and keeping the state's highways safe and moving. If you are assigned to highway patrol, this is your operational manual. Know it.

---

## What Highway Patrol Is

Highway patrol is the continuous, dedicated enforcement of traffic law and public safety on the state highway and freeway system. It is not the same as responding to a traffic complaint between other calls. It is a full-time assignment with a singular focus: the highway is your jurisdiction, your responsibility, and your identity.

**Why highways need dedicated enforcement:**

- **Speed kills disproportionately.** A collision at 80 mph is not twice as deadly as one at 40 mph — it is exponentially more destructive. Highways produce the highest-speed crashes and the highest fatality rates in the state.
- **Volume and exposure.** More vehicle-miles are traveled on the freeway system than on any other road type in San Andreas. The sheer volume of traffic means more opportunities for violations, collisions, and criminal activity.
- **Commercial traffic.** The state's economy moves on trucks. Overloaded, uninspected, or improperly secured commercial vehicles are rolling hazards that only trained highway officers are equipped to deal with.
- **Criminal corridors.** Highways are the primary arteries for drug trafficking, human smuggling, stolen vehicle transport, and fugitive flight. Highway officers are the first — and often the only — line of interdiction.
- **Remote stretches.** Large portions of the San Andreas freeway system run through desert, mountains, and rural areas where no other law enforcement agency maintains a regular presence. If you are not there, nobody is.

Your mission is simple: keep the highways safe, keep them moving, and catch the people who use them to do harm. Everything in this guide serves that mission.

## Patrol Zones

San Andreas has an extensive highway and freeway network. Every mile of it falls under highway patrol authority. Knowing your patrol zone — the terrain, the traffic patterns, the problem spots — is foundational. You cannot effectively patrol a road you do not understand.

| Highway / Freeway | Corridor | Description | Common Issues |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Los Santos Freeway** | Urban core | The main interstate artery running through the heart of Los Santos. Multiple interchanges, heavy traffic volume at all hours, elevated and depressed sections through downtown. | Aggressive driving, high-speed weaving, multi-vehicle collisions, road rage incidents, freeway shootings. |
| **Del Perro Freeway** | Western Los Santos | Runs through western Los Santos connecting Del Perro, Rockford Hills, and the interchange to the Great Ocean Highway. Congested during peak hours. | Stop-and-go collisions during rush hour, reckless lane changes, pedestrians on the shoulder near beach exits. |
| **Route 1 (Great Ocean Highway)** | Western coastline | Scenic coastal highway running from Los Santos north through Chumash, past Fort Zancudo, and up to Paleto Bay. Narrow, winding, with blind curves and sheer cliff drops. | Excessive speed on curves, head-on collisions from improper passing, cyclists sharing the roadway, tourist traffic. |
| **Route 68** | Cross-state | East-west route cutting through the Grand Senora Desert from the Zancudo River corridor to the desert interior. Low traffic volume but wide open and flat. | Extreme speeding on open straightaways, commercial vehicle violations, smuggling corridor activity, stranded motorists. |
| **Senora Freeway** | Eastern corridor | Connects Los Santos to Blaine County through the eastern hills and into the Senora Desert. Heavy commercial truck traffic. | Truck rollovers on grades, brake failures on descents, fatigued commercial drivers, speed differentials between trucks and passenger vehicles. |
| **Palomino Freeway** | Southeastern | Runs along the eastern edge of Los Santos toward Palomino Highlands and connects to the Senora Freeway. Suburban to rural transition. | Speeding in transition zones where speed limits change, DUI activity on weekend nights, drag racing in low-traffic hours. |
| **La Puerta Freeway** | Southern Los Santos | Short urban freeway connecting the port area and La Puerta to the Los Santos Freeway interchange. Industrial traffic. | Hazardous material vehicles from the port, heavy truck congestion, tight ramps with limited merge distance. |
| **Elysian Fields Freeway** | Port district | Connects the Port of Los Santos to the freeway network. Primarily commercial and industrial traffic. | Overweight trucks exiting the port, unsecured cargo, container spills, restricted vehicle violations. |

Familiarize yourself with every interchange, every on-ramp, every known trouble spot on your assigned route. Drive it off-duty if you have to. The officer who knows the road always has the advantage over the driver who does not.

## Speed Enforcement

Speed enforcement is the backbone of highway patrol operations. Excessive speed is the single largest contributing factor to fatal highway collisions, and your visible presence running speed enforcement is the most effective deterrent on the road.

**Positioning for speed traps:**

- **Median crossovers and turnarounds** — Position your vehicle in a median crossover facing oncoming traffic. This gives you a clear line of sight for radar or LIDAR and allows you to pull into traffic to pursue a violator in either direction.
- **Overpasses and elevated positions** — Overpasses provide an elevated vantage point for LIDAR operations. You can clock vehicles from above and radio a chase unit positioned ahead to make the stop.
- **Shoulder setbacks** — Park on the right shoulder, slightly recessed behind a bridge abutment or guardrail, facing with the flow of traffic. Vehicles approaching from behind will not see you until they are already in range of your radar.
- **Moving enforcement** — Pace violators from behind by matching their speed and confirming it on your calibrated speedometer over a measured distance. This is the simplest and most defensible method of speed detection.

**Radar and LIDAR roleplay:**

When roleplaying radar or LIDAR use, announce the method and reading during the stop. Specificity builds immersion and credibility.

> **Example — Speed Enforcement Stop:**
>
> `/me is positioned in the median crossover on the Los Santos Freeway near the Strawberry interchange, running stationary radar on westbound traffic. The radar unit locks a return at 94 mph on a white sedan in the number one lane.`
>
> `/me activates emergency lights, pulls out of the median, and accelerates to close the gap on the violator. Keys the radio.`
>
> **Officer:** "Dispatch, 2-Lincoln-14. Traffic stop, westbound Los Santos Freeway at mile marker 12. White sedan, plate 7-Alpha-Bravo-Charlie-4-9-2. One occupant."
>
> `/me conducts a passenger-side approach to stay clear of the travel lanes.`
>
> **Officer:** "Good evening. I'm Officer Danes, Highway Patrol. I had you on stationary radar at 94 in a posted 65 zone back at the Strawberry overpass. That's 29 over the limit. License, registration, and insurance, please."

Do not fabricate impossibly precise readings or claim technology that does not exist. Keep it realistic. State the device, the reading, and the location. That is what a real traffic court would require, and that is the standard your roleplay should meet.

## Commercial Vehicle Inspections

The state highway system carries thousands of commercial vehicles every day. Trucks, tankers, flatbeds, and tractor-trailers move freight across San Andreas — and a significant percentage of them are operating with safety violations that put every other driver on the road at risk.

**What you are checking:**

| Inspection Area | What to Look For |
|---|---|
| **Driver credentials** | Valid CDL with proper endorsements, medical card, hours-of-service log, any out-of-service orders. |
| **Vehicle registration and insurance** | Current registration, proof of insurance, IRP/IFTA credentials for interstate carriers. |
| **Brakes** | Air brake system pressure, slack adjuster travel, brake pad condition, air leaks. |
| **Tires** | Tread depth, sidewall damage, inflation, mismatched tires on the same axle. |
| **Lights and signals** | All required marker lights, brake lights, turn signals, and reflective tape functional. |
| **Cargo securement** | Load properly tied down, tarped, or contained. No shifting cargo, nothing hanging off the trailer. |
| **Hazardous materials** | Proper placarding, shipping papers accessible, driver HAZMAT endorsement on CDL, emergency response information present. |
| **Weight compliance** | Gross vehicle weight and axle weights within legal limits. Overweight trucks destroy road surfaces and are significantly harder to stop. |

> **Example — Roadside Commercial Vehicle Inspection:**
>
> `/me directs a tractor-trailer to the shoulder inspection area on Route 68 and approaches the driver's side.`
>
> **Officer:** "Afternoon, driver. Highway Patrol. This is a routine commercial vehicle inspection. I'll need your CDL, medical card, registration, insurance, and your log book. Are you carrying any hazardous materials today?"
>
> `/me walks the perimeter of the rig, inspecting tires, lights, and cargo securement while the driver retrieves documents. Notes a bald steer tire on the passenger side and two non-functional marker lights on the trailer.`
>
> **Officer:** "Sir, I've got a steer tire below minimum tread depth and two dead marker lights on the trailer. I'm placing this vehicle out of service until those are corrected. You'll need to call your carrier for a mobile repair or a tow. Here's your inspection report."

Commercial vehicle enforcement is not glamorous. It is, however, one of the most impactful things a highway patrol officer does. A single tire blowout on a loaded 80,000-pound truck at freeway speed can kill an entire family in the next lane.

## Accident Investigation

When a serious collision occurs on the highway, the highway patrol officer is the primary investigator, the scene commander, and the traffic manager — all at once. Accident investigation on a highway is one of the most complex and dangerous tasks you will perform.

**Scene management priorities (in order):**

1. **Protect the scene and yourself.** Position your vehicle upstream of the crash with all emergency lighting activated to create a buffer zone. You are the first warning that approaching drivers will see.
2. **Assess for injuries and request EMS/Fire.** Determine the number of patients, severity of injuries, and whether extrication is needed. Get EMS rolling immediately.
3. **Secure the scene.** Establish a perimeter using cones, flares, or your vehicle to keep traffic away from the wreckage, the victims, and the responders.
4. **Preserve evidence.** Skid marks, debris patterns, final rest positions, fluid trails, and vehicle damage all tell the story of the collision. Do not allow anything to be moved until the scene is documented.
5. **Manage traffic.** Close lanes or the entire roadway as needed. Coordinate with additional units for traffic control upstream and downstream.

> **Example — Major Collision Response:**
>
> `/me arrives on scene at the Senora Freeway eastbound, mile marker 47. Two-vehicle head-on collision in the number two lane. One vehicle is on its roof. Debris field covers both eastbound lanes. Activates all emergency lighting and positions the cruiser diagonally across the eastbound lanes 200 feet west of the wreck to block traffic.`
>
> **Officer:** "Dispatch, 2-Lincoln-14 on scene. I've got a two-vehicle TC, head-on, Senora Freeway eastbound at mile 47. One vehicle overturned. At least two patients, one appears entrapped. I need EMS and Fire to my location. I'm shutting down all eastbound lanes. Send me two additional units for traffic control."
>
> `/me grabs the first aid kit and fire extinguisher from the trunk and moves to the overturned vehicle to assess the driver's condition while waiting for EMS.`

**Working with EMS and Fire:**

Fire and EMS have authority over patient care and extrication. Your job is to secure the scene, manage traffic, and preserve evidence. Do not interfere with medical operations. Coordinate with the fire incident commander on lane closures and scene duration. When the patients are clear and the wreckage is ready for removal, you manage the tow and the reopening of the roadway.

## DUI Checkpoints

DUI checkpoints are a high-visibility enforcement tool used on highways and major arterials to detect and remove impaired drivers. They require planning, legal authority, and disciplined execution.

**Setting up a checkpoint:**

- Select a location with adequate visibility, lighting, and space for vehicles to be diverted to a secondary screening area. Freeway off-ramps and major highway intersections work well.
- All checkpoints must be authorized by a supervisor and conducted according to established legal guidelines. Every vehicle is contacted, or a neutral, predetermined pattern is used (every third vehicle, every fifth vehicle, etc.). You do not select vehicles based on driver appearance.
- Signage must be posted in advance warning drivers of the checkpoint ahead. Emergency lighting and cones mark the approach.
- A minimum staffing of four officers is recommended: two at the contact point, one at the secondary screening area, and one managing traffic flow.

**Field sobriety and processing:**

> **Example — DUI Checkpoint Contact:**
>
> `/me stands at the driver's window of the next vehicle in the checkpoint line, flashlight angled to illuminate the interior without blinding the driver.`
>
> **Officer:** "Good evening. Highway Patrol DUI checkpoint. Have you had anything to drink tonight?"
>
> `/me observes the driver's eyes — glassy, slow to focus. Detects a strong odor of alcohol from the vehicle interior. Notes slurred speech as the driver responds.`
>
> **Officer:** "Sir, I'm detecting an odor of alcohol. I'm going to ask you to pull your vehicle over to the secondary screening area to your right and put it in park for me."
>
> `/me directs the driver to the screening area and begins Standardized Field Sobriety Tests. Instructs the driver to exit the vehicle.`
>
> `/me demonstrates the walk-and-turn test: nine heel-to-toe steps forward, pivot, nine steps back. Observes the subject miss heel-to-toe on steps 4, 5, and 7, and use arms for balance throughout. Documents the observations on the SFST form.`
>
> **Officer:** "Based on my observations, I'm placing you under arrest for suspicion of driving under the influence. Turn around and place your hands behind your back."

Every observation — the odor, the slurred speech, the eye movement, the SFST performance — must be articulated and documented. A DUI case lives or dies on the officer's report.

## Construction Zone Enforcement

Construction zones on highways are where workers operate feet from live traffic moving at highway speed. Your presence in these zones is the difference between a safe work site and a tragedy.

**Key enforcement principles:**

- **Fines are doubled in active construction zones.** Make sure drivers know this. Your visible presence at the start of a construction zone is the most effective enforcement tool you have.
- **Reduced speed limits are enforceable regardless of whether workers are physically present** if signage is posted. Do not let drivers argue that "nobody was working" as a defense.
- **Position your vehicle at the entry point of the construction zone** with emergency lights activated. Drivers will reduce speed when they see you before they even read the sign.
- **Enforce aggressively.** A citation in a construction zone sends a message that reaches far beyond the single driver you stopped. Workers' lives depend on compliance.

> **Example — Construction Zone Stop:**
>
> **Officer:** "I clocked you at 71 in a posted 45 construction zone on the Del Perro Freeway. That's 26 over in an active work zone. All fines are doubled. I need your license and registration."

## Pursuit Policy on Highways

Highway pursuits are among the most dangerous events in law enforcement. Speeds escalate rapidly, innocent motorists are placed at extreme risk, and the margin between a successful apprehension and a catastrophic collision is razor thin.

**When to initiate a pursuit:**

- The suspect has committed or is reasonably believed to have committed a violent felony.
- The suspect poses an immediate and ongoing threat to public safety that outweighs the risks of pursuit.
- A supervisor must be notified immediately upon initiation and has the authority to terminate the pursuit at any time.

**When to terminate:**

- Speeds exceed the ability to safely control the patrol vehicle or react to traffic conditions.
- The suspect enters a densely populated area where the risk to bystanders becomes unacceptable.
- The suspect's identity is known and apprehension can be achieved through other means.
- Road conditions, weather, or visibility degrade to the point where continued pursuit is reckless.
- The supervisor orders termination. This is not a suggestion — it is an order.

**Tire deflation devices (spike strips):**

Spike strips are the preferred method for ending a highway pursuit without a collision. They are deployed ahead of the fleeing vehicle at a location with clear sight lines and adequate space for the deploying officer to reach safety.

> **Example — Spike Strip Deployment:**
>
> `/me positions behind the concrete barrier on the Senora Freeway overpass at mile marker 39, spike strip coiled and ready. Monitors the pursuit on the radio.`
>
> **Officer:** "Dispatch, 2-Lincoln-22, I'm set up with spikes at the 39 overpass, eastbound Senora Freeway. Ready on your call."
>
> `/me pulls the spike strip across the number two and three lanes as the suspect vehicle passes the 500-foot marker, then immediately retracts it to prevent spiking pursuing units.`

**PIT maneuvers** are authorized only at speeds below 40 mph and only with supervisor approval. At highway speeds, a PIT maneuver is not a controlled stop — it is a rollover waiting to happen.

## Traffic Flow Management

When a highway is blocked — by an accident, a hazmat spill, a pursuit, or a planned event — traffic does not manage itself. Gridlock develops in minutes. Road rage follows. Secondary collisions happen in the backup. Managing traffic flow is a core highway patrol function.

**Accident scene traffic control:**

- Close only the lanes you need. A full freeway closure should be the last resort.
- Position traffic control units at least 1,000 feet upstream of the scene on freeways. Drivers need distance to slow down and merge.
- Use cones, flares, and emergency vehicle positioning to create a taper that guides traffic around the scene smoothly.
- Assign a dedicated traffic control officer — do not pull investigators off the scene to wave traffic.

**Road closures and detours:**

- When a full closure is necessary, coordinate with dispatch to establish detour routes using surface streets.
- Notify other agencies if detour routes pass through their jurisdiction.
- Station officers at every critical decision point on the detour so drivers do not get lost and re-enter the closed highway.

**Event traffic:**

Large events — races, protests, dignitary motorcades — require pre-planned traffic management. Highway patrol officers are frequently assigned to event traffic control on freeways near venues. Know the plan before the event starts, not when 50,000 vehicles are trying to merge onto the same off-ramp.

## Working at Jurisdictional Boundaries

Highways do not respect city limits or county lines. A pursuit that starts on the Los Santos Freeway within LSPD's city jurisdiction crosses into unincorporated county territory — BCSO's area — within two miles, and onto a state highway under SASP authority a mile after that. Seamless cooperation across jurisdictional boundaries is not optional. It is survival.

| Scenario | Primary Authority | Handoff Protocol |
|---|---|---|
| Pursuit originates in Los Santos, enters freeway | LSPD initiates; highway patrol assumes coordination on the freeway | LSPD advises highway patrol dispatch. Highway patrol takes over radio coordination. LSPD units may continue or break off at agency discretion. |
| Accident on freeway within Los Santos city limits | Highway patrol primary; LSPD assists with traffic control on surface streets | Highway patrol runs the scene. LSPD handles overflow traffic on city streets feeding the freeway. |
| Accident on freeway in Blaine County | Highway patrol primary; BCSO assists | Same as above. BCSO handles county road traffic management. |
| Felony suspect flees from Blaine County onto freeway | BCSO initiates; highway patrol joins and coordinates | BCSO remains primary pursuing agency. Highway patrol coordinates spike strips, air support, and additional units on the freeway. |
| DUI checkpoint on a highway off-ramp in Los Santos | Highway patrol operates the checkpoint | LSPD is notified as a courtesy. Transport to LSPD booking facility may be coordinated if the arrest occurs within city limits. |

**The rule is simple:** communicate early, communicate clearly, and leave the jurisdictional arguments for the after-action report — not the active scene.

## Motorcycle Patrol

Motorcycle units are a force multiplier for highway patrol operations. A motorcycle can get places a full-size cruiser cannot — filtering through gridlocked traffic, positioning on narrow shoulders, and navigating tight construction zones.

**Advantages of motorcycle patrol:**

- Superior maneuverability in congested traffic and at accident scenes.
- Higher visibility — a motorcycle officer is more conspicuous to drivers than a cruiser blending into traffic.
- Faster response through traffic backups to reach accident scenes, disabled motorists, or incidents ahead of the congestion.
- Effective speed enforcement platform — motorcycles can be positioned in locations where a cruiser would be too visible or would not fit.

**Limitations:**

- No prisoner transport capability. A motorcycle officer who makes an arrest must request a caged unit for transport.
- Limited equipment capacity. No long guns, no spike strips, limited emergency supplies.
- Weather-dependent. Motorcycle operations are restricted or suspended in heavy rain, ice, or high winds.

> **Example — Motorcycle Traffic Filtering:**
>
> `/me rides the motorcycle along the right shoulder past the traffic backup on the Del Perro Freeway, emergency lights activated, moving toward the accident scene at the head of the congestion.`
>
> **Officer:** "Dispatch, 2-Motor-7. I'm filtering through the backup on the Del Perro eastbound. I'll be on scene in about 90 seconds. Confirm EMS is en route."
>
> `/me arrives at the accident scene ahead of the cruiser units still stuck in traffic and begins assessing injuries and setting up initial traffic control.`

## Vehicle and Equipment

Highway patrol officers spend their entire shift in or near their vehicle. Your cruiser is your office, your cover, and your toolbox. Know your equipment and keep it ready.

**Patrol vehicle — Interceptor sedan or SUV (Dodge Charger / Vapid Interceptor style):**

| Equipment | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Radar / LIDAR unit** | Speed detection — mounted and handheld. Calibrated at the start and end of every shift. |
| **Emergency lighting package** | Full light bar, rear flashers, and takedown lights. Used for traffic stops, scene protection, and visibility on the shoulder. |
| **PA system** | Vehicle public address for high-risk stops, checkpoint announcements, and directing traffic. |
| **In-car camera / dashcam** | Records all traffic stops, pursuits, and enforcement activity. Ensure it is functioning before you leave the barracks. |
| **Spike strip** | Tire deflation device stored in the trunk. Inspect before shift — tangled or damaged strips cost critical seconds during a pursuit. |
| **Traffic cones and flares** | Scene protection and traffic channeling. Carry a minimum of six cones and a full box of road flares. |
| **First aid kit** | Basic trauma supplies for accident response before EMS arrives. Check expiration dates monthly. |
| **Fire extinguisher** | Vehicle fires at accident scenes. Mounted and accessible — not buried under other equipment. |
| **Reflective vest** | Worn anytime you are on foot on the highway outside of a traffic stop. Visibility is survival. |
| **Measuring wheel / tape** | Accident investigation — measuring skid marks, debris fields, and final rest positions. |

Conduct a thorough vehicle and equipment check at the start of every shift. A radar unit that will not calibrate, a spike strip with missing spikes, or a dead dashcam discovered during a pursuit is an equipment failure caused by your negligence. Check your gear.

## Final Thoughts

Highway patrol is not a stepping stone to a detective bureau or a specialized unit. It is a specialty in its own right — one that demands a unique combination of driving skill, situational awareness, legal knowledge, and the ability to operate alone in a high-speed, high-risk environment for hours on end.

The officer who masters highway patrol knows every mile of their assigned route. They can read traffic patterns and spot the one vehicle moving wrong in a sea of cars. They can run a traffic stop on a narrow shoulder at night with trucks blasting past and stay calm, professional, and thorough. They can manage a multi-vehicle collision scene, coordinate EMS, Fire, tow companies, and traffic control simultaneously, and still produce an investigation report that holds up in court.

The highway does not care about your rank, your seniority, or your reputation. It is indifferent and unforgiving. The only thing that keeps you and the public safe out there is your training, your discipline, and your attention to detail.

Own the asphalt. That is the job.
