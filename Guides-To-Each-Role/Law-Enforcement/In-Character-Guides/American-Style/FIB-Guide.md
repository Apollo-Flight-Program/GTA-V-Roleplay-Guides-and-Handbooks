# FIB Guide — Federal Investigation Bureau

Local police handle the streets. Sheriffs run the counties. State troopers own the highways. But when the crime crosses state lines, threatens national security, or involves the kind of organized enterprise that local agencies cannot dismantle alone — the Federal Investigation Bureau steps in.

The FIB is the premier federal law enforcement agency in the United States. In San Andreas, the FIB operates out of its field office in downtown Los Santos — the imposing federal building on Power Street that most civilians walk past without a second glance. Behind those walls, agents build cases that take months to develop, run surveillance operations spanning entire criminal networks, manage confidential informants embedded in organizations that would kill them on sight, and coordinate inter-agency task forces that bring together local, state, and federal resources under a single operational umbrella.

FIB agents do not respond to 911 calls. They do not run traffic stops. They do not handle bar fights or domestic disturbances. The Bureau investigates federal crimes — terrorism, organized crime, public corruption, cyber crime, kidnapping, bank robbery of federally insured institutions, and violations of federal law that fall outside the jurisdiction of local and state agencies. When an FIB agent shows up at your crime scene, it is because the case just became bigger than your department can handle.

This guide covers FIB operations, jurisdiction, investigative methodology, and inter-agency protocols. If you are joining the Bureau, this is your operational foundation. If you are local law enforcement working alongside FIB, this tells you what to expect, what we need from you, and where the jurisdictional lines are drawn. Know it.

---

## Agency Overview

The Federal Investigation Bureau was established as the primary investigative arm of the federal government. Its mandate is broad — protect the homeland from terrorist threats, dismantle organized criminal enterprises, combat public corruption at every level of government, investigate major cyber crimes, and enforce federal law wherever state and local agencies lack the jurisdiction or resources to act.

The FIB answers to the Department of Justice. It is not a branch of the military, not an intelligence agency in the traditional sense, and not a police department. It is an investigative bureau — agents build cases, develop evidence, and present that evidence for federal prosecution. The Bureau's authority extends across all fifty states, and its agents carry federal credentials that supersede local jurisdiction when federal law is at issue.

In San Andreas, the FIB maintains a field office in downtown Los Santos. The building is a secured federal facility — controlled access, surveillance-monitored perimeter, reinforced construction. The field office houses the Special Agent in Charge and their staff, case management operations, the evidence vault, interview rooms, the Sensitive Compartmented Information Facility, and the joint operations center used for multi-agency coordination.

The FIB has a complicated relationship with the IAA — the International Affairs Agency. Where the FIB focuses on domestic law enforcement and investigation, the IAA operates in the intelligence and foreign affairs sphere. Jurisdictional overlap between the two agencies is a constant source of friction, bureaucratic infighting, and occasional operational conflict. An FIB agent encountering IAA personnel on an operation should expect territorial behavior, information hoarding, and a general unwillingness to share. Handle it professionally. The mission comes first.

## Jurisdiction

Understanding jurisdiction is non-negotiable. The FIB does not investigate every crime — it investigates federal crimes. The distinction between federal and state offenses determines whether the Bureau has authority to act.

| Jurisdiction Category | Federal (FIB) | State / Local (LSPD, BCSO, SASP) |
|---|---|---|
| **Terrorism** | All domestic and international terrorism on U.S. soil | Local agencies assist with perimeter, evacuation, initial response |
| **Organized Crime** | Criminal enterprises operating across jurisdictions, RICO cases | Local gang enforcement, street-level narcotics |
| **Public Corruption** | Corrupt federal officials, corrupt local officials when federal funds are involved | Internal affairs investigations within individual departments |
| **Cyber Crime** | Large-scale hacking, critical infrastructure attacks, federal network intrusions | Local fraud, identity theft at the individual level |
| **Bank Robbery** | Robbery of federally insured institutions | Robbery of non-federal businesses and individuals |
| **Kidnapping** | Kidnapping across state lines, kidnapping of federal officials | Local kidnapping within a single jurisdiction |
| **Weapons Trafficking** | Interstate and international arms dealing | Local illegal weapons possession |
| **Financial Crimes** | Money laundering, securities fraud, large-scale financial enterprise crimes | Local check fraud, petty financial crimes |

**When the FIB takes over a case:**

The Bureau does not "take over" cases on a whim. Federal jurisdiction must be established. This happens when the crime violates a federal statute, crosses state lines, involves federal property or personnel, or when the scale and complexity of the criminal enterprise exceeds what local agencies can prosecute. A single drug dealer on a corner in Davis is an LSPD problem. A cartel moving product through the Port of Los Santos into three states is an FIB problem.

When jurisdiction is shared or contested, the default is cooperation — not confrontation. Joint task forces exist precisely for cases that straddle the line. An FIB agent who walks onto a local crime scene and announces they are taking over without establishing federal jurisdiction is an agent who will find themselves without local cooperation for the rest of their career.

## Rank Structure

The FIB uses a distinct rank structure that differs from local law enforcement. There are no officers, sergeants, or lieutenants. The Bureau operates on a professional hierarchy centered on the Special Agent designation.

| Rank | Duties & Responsibilities |
|---|---|
| **Special Agent** | The foundational rank of the Bureau. Conducts investigations, interviews witnesses and suspects, executes search warrants, manages informants, writes reports, and testifies in federal court. Every agent starts here. |
| **Senior Special Agent** | An experienced agent who has demonstrated investigative excellence over multiple years. Mentors junior agents, handles complex or sensitive cases, and may lead small investigative teams on specific operations. |
| **Supervisory Special Agent (SSA)** | Supervises a squad of agents within the field office. Assigns cases, reviews investigative progress, approves operational plans, and serves as the first line of management. The SSA is responsible for the output and conduct of their squad. |
| **Assistant Special Agent in Charge (ASAC)** | Second in command of a field office division. Oversees multiple squads, coordinates division-level operations, and acts on behalf of the SAC when needed. Handles administrative and strategic planning for their division. |
| **Special Agent in Charge (SAC)** | Commands the entire field office. The SAC is the senior FIB official in the region and is responsible for all operations, investigations, personnel, and inter-agency coordination within the field office's area of responsibility. Reports directly to Bureau headquarters. |
| **Assistant Director** | Oversees a major division at Bureau headquarters — Criminal, Counterterrorism, Cyber, Intelligence, or another national-level program. Sets policy and strategic direction for their division across all field offices. |
| **Deputy Director** | Second in command of the entire Bureau. Manages day-to-day operations at the national level and acts as Director in the Director's absence. |
| **Director** | The head of the Federal Investigation Bureau. Appointed by the President and confirmed by Congress. Sets the Bureau's strategic priorities, represents the FIB to the public and other government agencies, and bears ultimate responsibility for the Bureau's conduct and effectiveness. |

Promotions within the Bureau are based on performance, investigative results, leadership ability, and time in grade. There are no shortcuts. An agent who builds solid cases, manages informants effectively, works well with partner agencies, and demonstrates sound judgment under pressure will advance. An agent who cuts corners, burns sources, or creates inter-agency problems will not.

## Field Office Operations

The Los Santos Field Office is the Bureau's operational hub for all of San Andreas. Every federal investigation in the state is coordinated through this office, and the SAC maintains authority over all Bureau activities in the region.

**Daily operations:**

The field office operates on a squad-based system. Each squad is assigned to a specific program area — organized crime, counterterrorism, cyber crime, public corruption, or violent crime. Agents are assigned to squads based on their expertise and the office's current investigative priorities. Cases are assigned by the Supervisory Special Agent based on workload, agent availability, and case complexity.

**Case assignment and management:**

When a new case is opened, the SSA assigns a lead agent. That agent is responsible for developing the investigative plan, identifying targets, coordinating with other agencies as needed, managing evidence, and driving the case toward prosecution. Complex cases may involve multiple agents, but the lead agent owns the case.

**Task forces:**

The field office regularly participates in multi-agency task forces. These task forces combine FIB resources with state and local law enforcement to address specific threats — narcotics trafficking, gang violence, terrorism, or financial crime. Task force members may include LSPD detectives, BCSO investigators, SASP troopers, and agents from other federal agencies. The FIB typically provides leadership, analytical support, and access to federal tools — wiretap authority, grand jury subpoenas, and federal prosecution.

> **Example — Morning briefing at the field office:**
>
> **SAC:** "Good morning. Let's run the board. Squad One — organized crime. Where are we on the Madrazo network?"
>
> **SSA Torres:** "We have three active wiretaps producing. Our CI inside the operation confirmed a major shipment is scheduled through the port this week. We're coordinating with the Coast Guard and Customs for the intercept."
>
> **SAC:** "Good. Squad Two — public corruption. The city council investigation?"
>
> **SSA Daniels:** "Grand jury subpoenas went out yesterday. We're expecting documents from the City Planning Office by Friday. Agent Chen is prepping the financial analysis."
>
> **SAC:** "Keep it tight. No leaks. If this gets to the press before we're ready, the targets will shred everything. Anything else? Alright — get to work."

## Counter-Terrorism

Counter-terrorism is the Bureau's highest priority. Every field office maintains a dedicated counterterrorism squad, and every agent — regardless of their primary assignment — is expected to be alert to potential terrorist threats.

**Threat assessment:**

The FIB monitors individuals and groups that pose a potential terrorist threat through a combination of intelligence gathering, informant networks, and coordination with federal and international partners. Threat assessments are classified and shared on a need-to-know basis. Local law enforcement receives sanitized intelligence bulletins when a threat is deemed credible and actionable.

**Surveillance and prevention:**

Counterterrorism investigations are proactive. The goal is to identify and disrupt plots before they are executed. This involves long-term surveillance of persons of interest, monitoring communications under court-authorized warrants, infiltrating extremist groups through undercover agents or confidential informants, and analyzing financial transactions for indicators of terrorist financing.

**Response to terrorist incidents:**

When a terrorist attack occurs, the FIB assumes the lead in the investigation. Local agencies maintain perimeter security, manage evacuation, and provide immediate first-responder capabilities, but the investigation itself is federal. The FIB deploys a rapid response team to the scene, establishes a joint operations center, and coordinates with all involved agencies through a unified command structure.

> **Example — Counterterrorism response:**
>
> `/me receives the flash alert on the encrypted terminal — confirmed explosive device detonated at the Los Santos Transit Center. Immediately contacts the SAC and initiates the office's CT response protocol. Grabs the go-bag from the locker, checks the sidearm, and moves to the vehicle bay.`
>
> **Lead Agent (Radio):** "All CT squad agents, this is not a drill. IED confirmed at LS Transit Center. Rally at the field office garage in ten minutes. Full tactical load. We are assuming the lead on this."

## Organized Crime Investigation

Organized crime is the Bureau's bread and butter. The FIB does not target individual criminals — it targets organizations. The goal is not to arrest a single drug dealer or weapons trafficker. The goal is to dismantle the entire enterprise from the top down.

**Long-term investigations:**

Organized crime cases are not built in a day. They are built over weeks, months, sometimes years. Agents develop sources inside the organization, conduct sustained surveillance, analyze financial records, and build a web of evidence connecting individual criminal acts to the leaders who direct them. The endgame is not a single arrest — it is a federal indictment that names every member of the organization and charges them under the Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act.

**Building enterprise cases:**

An enterprise case connects individual crimes — drug sales, extortion, murder, money laundering — to a single criminal organization operating as an ongoing enterprise. The agent must prove that the organization exists, that it engages in a pattern of criminal activity, and that the defendants participated in that activity as part of the enterprise.

> **Example — Organized crime investigation IC scenario:**
>
> `/me sits in the surveillance van parked on Elgin Avenue, monitoring the live feed from the camera concealed across the street from the target's warehouse in Cypress Flats. Notes the time — 0237 hours — as a black Cavalcade pulls into the loading bay. Two males exit. One carries a duffel bag. The other opens the warehouse roll-up door. Logs the vehicle plate and photographs both subjects through the telephoto lens.`
>
> `/me keys the radio to the encrypted channel: "Echo-7, this is Overwatch. Two subjects just entered the Cypress warehouse. Black Cavalcade, plate number Delta-Kilo-4-7-Foxtrot-3. One male carrying a large duffel. Logging and continuing surveillance. How copy?"`
>
> **Echo-7 (Radio):** "Copy, Overwatch. Good copy on the plate. That matches the vehicle registered to our Target Three. Continue observation. Do not approach — repeat, do not approach. We need three more documented meets before we go to the grand jury."

## Cyber Crime

The digital world generates federal cases at an accelerating rate. The FIB's cyber crime program investigates intrusions into critical infrastructure, large-scale financial fraud conducted through digital means, ransomware attacks against major institutions, and criminal enterprises that operate primarily online.

**Digital investigations:**

Cyber investigations require specialized technical skills. Agents assigned to the cyber squad work with digital forensic tools to trace intrusions, analyze malware, recover encrypted data, and identify threat actors who operate behind layers of anonymity. The field office maintains a cyber forensics lab capable of imaging hard drives, recovering deleted files, and analyzing network traffic logs.

**Financial fraud and cryptocurrency:**

Financial crimes conducted through digital platforms — wire fraud, cryptocurrency money laundering, fraudulent investment schemes — fall within the Bureau's cyber crime mandate. Agents track cryptocurrency transactions across blockchain networks, identify wallets associated with criminal activity, and work with financial institutions to freeze assets linked to federal investigations.

**Hacking cases:**

Unauthorized access to computer systems — particularly government networks, financial institutions, and critical infrastructure — is a federal offense investigated by the FIB. Agents work backward from the intrusion point, analyzing attack vectors, identifying command-and-control infrastructure, and building attribution cases that link the attack to specific individuals or groups.

## Public Corruption

Nothing undermines public trust faster than a corrupt public official. The FIB's public corruption program investigates law enforcement officers, elected officials, government employees, and anyone in a position of public trust who abuses that position for personal gain.

**Investigating dirty cops:**

When a police officer or sheriff's deputy is on the take — accepting bribes, providing information to criminal organizations, planting evidence, or engaging in criminal activity under the protection of a badge — the FIB investigates. These cases are handled with extreme discretion. The target's own department may not be informed until the investigation is complete and an arrest warrant is in hand. Trust is limited to those with a verified need to know.

**Corrupt politicians and government fraud:**

Elected officials who accept bribes, steer government contracts to associates, embezzle public funds, or engage in fraud while in office are investigated by the Bureau. These cases often involve financial analysis, undercover operations, and cooperating witnesses who have firsthand knowledge of the corruption.

**Building a corruption case:**

Corruption cases are built on evidence, not allegations. An anonymous tip that a city council member is taking bribes is a starting point — not a case. The agent must document the corrupt acts through surveillance, financial records, recorded conversations, cooperating witnesses, or undercover operations that capture the target in the act.

> **Example — Public corruption investigation:**
>
> `/me reviews the financial records subpoenaed from the San Andreas State Comptroller's Office. Cross-references campaign donations to Councilmember Harwood with city contracts awarded to Pinnacle Construction LLC over the past three years. Notes a pattern — every major donation is followed within sixty days by a no-bid contract. Flags the correlation and begins drafting the affidavit for a wiretap on Harwood's personal phone.`

## Cooperation with Local Agencies

The FIB does not operate in a vacuum. Effective federal law enforcement requires cooperation with local and state agencies — LSPD, BCSO, San Andreas State Police, and others. This cooperation is built on mutual respect, clear communication, and an understanding that every agency brings something to the table.

**Joint task forces:**

Task forces are the primary mechanism for FIB-local cooperation. A joint task force combines federal authority — wiretaps, grand jury subpoenas, federal prosecution — with local knowledge — informants, street intelligence, relationships with the community. Task force officers from local agencies are deputized as federal task force officers, giving them the authority to participate in federal investigations and execute federal warrants.

**Information sharing:**

Intelligence flows in both directions. The FIB provides strategic intelligence to local agencies — threat assessments, suspect profiles, investigative leads that may connect to local cases. Local agencies provide the FIB with street-level intelligence — who is moving what, where, and for whom. An LSPD detective who picks up information about a drug network during a routine arrest may be sitting on the missing piece of a federal investigation.

**Jurisdictional friction:**

It happens. A local detective has been working a case for six months, and an FIB agent shows up and tells them it is now a federal matter. Handle this with diplomacy, not authority. Explain why federal jurisdiction applies. Offer the detective a role on the task force. Acknowledge their work and incorporate their evidence into the federal case. An agent who steamrolls local law enforcement will find that the next time they need local assistance, nobody answers the phone.

> **Example — Jurisdictional coordination:**
>
> **FIB Agent:** "Detective, I understand you've been working this Vagos case for months. I'm not here to take it from you. We've been running a parallel investigation at the federal level — wiretaps, financial analysis, the works. Your street-level work fits perfectly with what we've built. I'd like to bring you onto the task force so we can combine what we have and take the whole organization down, not just the corner dealers."
>
> **LSPD Detective:** "Alright. But my captain needs to know, and I need access to whatever you've got on the financial side. I've got three CIs who won't talk to a fed — they deal with me or nobody."
>
> **FIB Agent:** "Done. Your CIs stay yours. We'll read your captain in tomorrow. Let's set a meeting at the field office for Thursday to sync everything up."

## Undercover Operations

Undercover work is the most dangerous and psychologically demanding assignment in the Bureau. An undercover agent infiltrates a criminal organization, assumes a false identity, and operates within that organization for weeks, months, or in deep-cover assignments, years.

**Building a legend:**

The agent's cover identity — the legend — must be airtight. It includes a backstory, identification documents, a verifiable history, and a persona that fits naturally within the target organization. The legend is constructed by the Bureau's undercover operations unit and is tested against every conceivable background check the target organization might conduct.

**Maintaining cover:**

An undercover agent lives the role. Every conversation, every reaction, every decision must be consistent with the cover identity. A single slip — a wrong name, an unfamiliar reference, a reaction that does not match the persona — can be fatal. The agent must build trust within the organization over time, accepting increasing levels of responsibility and access while gathering evidence.

**Handler relationship:**

Every undercover agent has a handler — a supervising agent who maintains contact through secure communication channels. The handler receives intelligence from the undercover agent, provides guidance, monitors the agent's psychological state, and coordinates extraction if the operation is compromised.

**Extraction protocols:**

When an undercover operation ends — either because the investigation has gathered sufficient evidence for prosecution or because the agent's cover is at risk — the extraction must be planned and executed carefully. A sudden disappearance raises suspicion. The preferred method is a gradual withdrawal that gives the target organization a plausible explanation for the agent's departure. Emergency extraction is reserved for situations where the agent's life is in immediate danger.

> **Example — Undercover check-in:**
>
> `/me dials the pre-arranged number from the burner phone purchased three days ago at a gas station in Sandy Shores. Waits for the second ring, hangs up, dials again. The handler picks up on the first ring.`
>
> **Undercover Agent:** "It's me. I'm in. They trust me enough to let me ride on the next delivery run. Thursday night, out of the warehouse on Innocence. I'll have eyes on the full supply chain."
>
> **Handler:** "Good work. Get what you can, but do not push for more than they offer. If anything feels wrong, you use the exit phrase and we pull you out. No evidence is worth your life."

## RICO Investigations

The Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act is the Bureau's most powerful tool against organized crime. RICO allows federal prosecutors to charge the leaders of a criminal organization with the crimes committed by the organization's members — even if the leaders never personally pulled a trigger, moved a kilo, or laundered a dollar.

**What RICO requires:**

A RICO case must establish four elements: the existence of an enterprise, a pattern of racketeering activity (at least two predicate offenses within ten years), the defendant's association with the enterprise, and the defendant's participation in the enterprise's affairs through a pattern of racketeering activity.

**Building a RICO case:**

RICO investigations are the marathon of federal law enforcement. The agent must document the organizational structure — who gives orders, who carries them out, who handles the money, who enforces discipline. Every predicate crime must be documented with sufficient evidence to sustain a conviction on its own. Financial records must trace the flow of money from criminal activity through the organization. Communications intercepts must capture the leadership directing criminal activity.

**Targeting the organization:**

The objective of a RICO investigation is not to arrest the soldiers — it is to indict the generals. Street-level arrests are avoided when possible because they alert the organization and may compromise the larger investigation. The case is built quietly, methodically, and presented to the grand jury only when the evidence is overwhelming. When the indictment comes down, it names every significant member of the organization and charges them with the full scope of the enterprise's criminal activity.

## Intelligence Gathering

Intelligence is the foundation of every FIB investigation. Without reliable intelligence, agents are operating blind — reacting to events instead of anticipating them.

**HUMINT — Human Intelligence:**

Human sources are the Bureau's most valuable intelligence asset. Confidential informants embedded within criminal organizations provide real-time information about plans, personnel, and operations that no amount of electronic surveillance can replicate. Managing informants requires skill, patience, and constant vigilance — a poorly managed informant can compromise an investigation, endanger lives, or become a liability in court.

**SIGINT — Signals Intelligence:**

Court-authorized interception of communications — phone calls, text messages, emails, encrypted messaging platforms — provides critical intelligence in federal investigations. SIGINT operations require strict legal compliance, and every intercept must be authorized by a federal judge through a Title III wiretap order.

**Informant management:**

Informants are assets, not friends. The agent-informant relationship is transactional and must be carefully documented. Every meeting is logged. Every piece of information is evaluated for reliability. Payments to informants are tracked and audited. An informant who provides false information to manipulate the Bureau is cut loose. An informant whose safety is compromised is relocated or extracted.

**Intelligence databases:**

The Bureau maintains databases that aggregate intelligence from multiple sources — criminal records, financial transactions, travel records, communications metadata, and information shared by partner agencies. Agents query these databases to identify connections between suspects, organizations, and criminal activity that might not be visible at the individual case level.

## Surveillance Authority

The FIB possesses surveillance authorities that local agencies do not. These tools are powerful — and tightly regulated by federal law and judicial oversight.

| Surveillance Type | Description | Authorization Required |
|---|---|---|
| **Title III Wiretap** | Real-time interception of phone calls, text messages, and electronic communications | Federal court order signed by a judge, based on probable cause and a showing that normal investigative techniques have been tried and failed |
| **Pen Register / Trap and Trace** | Records the numbers dialed to and from a target phone without capturing content | Court order, lower standard than Title III |
| **Physical Surveillance** | Agents following and observing a target in public spaces | No warrant required for observation in public; warrant required for GPS tracking devices |
| **Electronic Monitoring** | Concealed recording devices, body wires worn by undercover agents or informants | Court order for non-consensual recording; one-party consent may apply in some jurisdictions |
| **Search Warrants** | Physical search of residences, vehicles, storage units, and other private spaces | Federal warrant signed by a magistrate judge, based on probable cause |
| **National Security Letters** | Administrative subpoenas for specific categories of records from communications providers | Authorized by senior FIB officials for national security investigations; no judicial approval required |

Every surveillance operation is documented, every authorization is filed, and every agent involved understands that exceeding the scope of a surveillance order is a federal crime in itself. The Bureau's authority is broad, but it is not unlimited. An agent who conducts unauthorized surveillance compromises the case, their career, and the Bureau's credibility.

## Standard Procedures

The FIB operates with a level of procedural discipline that exceeds local law enforcement standards. Every investigation, every interview, every operation is conducted according to established protocols.

**Investigative approach:**

FIB investigations begin with intelligence and evidence — not assumptions. Agents build cases from the ground up, starting with the facts and following the evidence wherever it leads. Confirmation bias — the tendency to seek evidence that supports a predetermined conclusion — is the enemy of good investigative work.

**Professionalism:**

FIB agents represent the federal government. Conduct is held to the highest standard at all times — in the field, in the courtroom, and in interactions with other agencies. Agents dress professionally, communicate clearly, and treat everyone — witnesses, suspects, local officers, prosecutors — with respect. The badge carries weight precisely because the Bureau maintains its standards.

**Inter-agency etiquette:**

When working with local agencies, the FIB does not pull rank. Federal authority is exercised when legally required, not as a power play. Agents introduce themselves, explain their purpose, and request cooperation rather than demanding compliance. The most effective FIB agents are the ones who local officers want to work with — not the ones who local officers avoid.

> **Example — FIB agent arriving at a local crime scene:**
>
> `/me approaches the yellow tape perimeter, holds up the federal credentials for the uniformed officer securing the scene. "Special Agent Mercer, FIB. I'm not here to take your scene — we received information that the victim may be connected to an active federal investigation. I'd like to speak with your lead detective and compare notes. Who's running this?"`

## Final Thoughts

Being FIB is not about the suit, the federal badge, or the authority to walk into any police station in the state and demand cooperation. It is about the work — the slow, meticulous, often invisible work of building cases that dismantle criminal organizations, stop terrorist plots before they are executed, root out corruption in public institutions, and hold accountable the people who believe they are above the law.

An FIB agent does not measure success in arrests. Arrests are the final step of a process that begins months or years earlier with an intelligence lead, a suspicious financial transaction, or a confidential informant's whispered tip in a parking garage at two in the morning. Success is the sealed federal indictment that names every member of an organization. Success is the wiretap that captures a corrupt official accepting a bribe. Success is the undercover operation that maps an entire weapons trafficking network from source to street.

The work is long. The hours are brutal. The cases are complex. The bureaucracy is real. And when you do your job right, nobody outside the Bureau will ever know what you did or how close things came to going wrong. That is the nature of federal law enforcement — the best outcomes are the ones the public never hears about.

Carry the badge. Do the work. Protect the mission. And remember that the Bureau's reputation is built on the conduct of every agent who wears it — including you.
