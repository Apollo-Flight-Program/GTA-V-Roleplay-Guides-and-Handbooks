# LEO Guide — Serious Roleplay

Serious roleplay law enforcement is not a casual commitment. It is a second job. You will study procedures, attend academy training sessions, write detailed reports, maintain a character voice, navigate department politics, and invest hours of real time into a fictional career that demands the same discipline as the real thing. The reward is roleplay that feels indistinguishable from a real police drama — slow-burn investigations, tense standoffs, courtroom testimony, and bonds with fellow officers that make your department feel like a real precinct.

This guide is an OOC breakdown of what it takes to be a police officer in a serious or heavy RP server — the application process, the expectations, the daily grind, and the things that will burn you out if you are not ready for them.

---

## The Application Process

Serious RP servers do not hand out badges. You earn them, and the process starts before you ever put on a uniform.

**Typical application stages:**

| Stage | What Happens | What They Are Looking For |
|---|---|---|
| Written application | Multi-question form covering your RP experience, character backstory, scenario responses | Writing quality, understanding of LEO RP, character depth, rule knowledge |
| OOC interview | Voice or text interview with department leadership | Maturity, communication skills, temperament, availability |
| Background check | Review of your server history, prior bans, community reputation | Clean record, no pattern of rule-breaking, good standing |
| IC interview | Your character is interviewed by a department official in-game | Character consistency, ability to stay in character under pressure |
| Academy enrollment | Accepted into the training program | Willingness to learn, attendance commitment |

**Application tips:**
- Write your application like it matters — because it does. Spelling, grammar, and effort all count.
- Your character backstory should explain why this person became a cop. What drives them? What are their flaws?
- Scenario questions are tests of judgment, not knowledge. There is usually no single right answer. They want to see how you think.
- Be honest about your experience level. Pretending to know things you do not will be exposed during training.

**Common reasons applications get denied:**
- Copy-pasted applications from other servers
- Characters with no depth — "John Smith, 28, wanted to help people"
- Inability to articulate basic RP concepts (what is metagaming, what is powergaming)
- Bad reputation on the server or in the broader FiveM community
- Demanding a high rank immediately

## The Training Academy

This is where serious RP separates from everything else. Academy training in heavy RP servers can last days or even weeks of real time.

**Typical academy curriculum:**

| Module | Content | Duration |
|---|---|---|
| Department SOPs | Standard operating procedures, chain of command, department policies | 1–2 sessions |
| Radio communication | Proper callsigns, 10-codes or signal codes, radio etiquette, dispatch interaction | 1 session |
| Traffic enforcement | Stop procedures, approach techniques, DUI protocol, citation writing | 1–2 sessions |
| Use of force | Force continuum, escalation/de-escalation, documentation requirements | 1 session |
| Pursuit policy | Initiation criteria, PIT authorization, spike strip deployment, termination conditions | 1 session |
| Criminal law | Penal codes, probable cause, reasonable suspicion, search and seizure, Miranda | 1–2 sessions |
| Report writing | Narrative format, required elements, MDT documentation, evidence logging | 1 session |
| Firearms qualification | Range RP, weapon handling, rules of engagement | 1 session |
| Scenario training | Simulated calls with instructors acting as civilians — evaluated on response | 2–3 sessions |
| Final evaluation | Written exam and practical assessment | 1 session |

**After academy — Field Training:**
You do not go solo after passing the academy. You ride with a Field Training Officer (FTO) for a set period, typically one to two weeks of active play. The FTO evaluates you on real calls and files daily observation reports. You can be washed out during FTO if you are not meeting standards.

> **Reality check:** Many players apply for LEO, get accepted, attend two academy sessions, and quit. The commitment required for serious RP law enforcement is significant. Know this going in.

## Expected Depth of Knowledge

In serious RP, "I didn't know the procedure" is not an excuse. You are expected to know your department's SOPs and apply them consistently.

**What you must know cold:**
- The penal codes your server uses — or at least know how to look them up quickly
- When you can and cannot search a person, vehicle, or property
- The difference between reasonable suspicion and probable cause
- Your department's use of force continuum and when each level is justified
- How to properly Miranda a suspect and what happens if you do not
- Chain of command — who you report to, who authorizes what
- Your radio callsign structure and proper communication format

**What you should know well:**
- Felony stop procedures vs. routine traffic stop procedures
- Evidence handling and chain of custody
- How to testify in court (serious RP servers with active DOJ run trials)
- Department-specific policies on pursuits, K9 deployment, air unit requests
- How interdepartmental jurisdiction works — LSPD handles the city, BCSO covers Blaine County, SASP has statewide authority

**What you should be learning:**
- Detective and investigative techniques if you want to advance
- SWAT or tactical team procedures if your server has a special units program
- Leadership and mentoring skills for future promotion

## Maintaining Character Voice

Your character is not you in a uniform. They are a person with their own history, opinions, speech patterns, and emotional responses.

**Building a believable LEO character:**
- **Give them a background.** Where did they grow up? Why did they become a cop? Do they have family? Where in Los Santos do they live — an apartment in Alta or a house out in Paleto Bay?
- **Give them flaws.** A perfect cop is boring. Maybe they have a temper they struggle to control. Maybe they drink too much after bad shifts. Maybe they are too idealistic and it keeps getting them hurt.
- **Give them opinions.** Not every officer agrees with every policy. Your character might think the pursuit policy is too restrictive. They might disagree with how the department handles gang task force operations. These opinions create internal department RP.
- **Give them relationships.** A partner they trust. A sergeant they respect. A lieutenant they cannot stand. These connections make the department feel alive.

**Maintaining voice during interactions:**
- Speak the way your character speaks, not the way you speak. If your character is a hardened veteran, they do not use the same language as a nervous rookie.
- React the way your character would react, not how you would react. If your character is hot-headed and someone spits on them, they might lose their composure — and that is good RP, even if it means paperwork.
- Do not break character for convenience. If your character does not know something, play the ignorance. Ask another officer IC. Look it up on the MDT. Do not just magically know because you read it on a wiki.

## Handling Slow Shifts

Not every shift in serious RP is non-stop action. Some shifts are quiet. The city is calm. No calls come in. You have been driving around Vespucci Beach for forty minutes and nothing has happened.

**What to do during slow shifts:**
- **Proactive patrol** — run plates, check on businesses, patrol high-crime areas like Davis or Strawberry, walk foot beats through Vinewood Boulevard
- **Training RP** — grab a junior officer and run them through scenarios in the Mission Row parking lot
- **Vehicle checks** — RP a vehicle inspection, clean your cruiser, organize your gear
- **Reports** — catch up on paperwork from previous shifts
- **Department interaction** — talk to the desk sergeant, chat with dispatch, have a conversation with your partner at a coffee shop on Vespucci Boulevard
- **Community policing** — stop and talk to civilian players. Ask about their day. Build relationships that pay off later.
- **Personal character development** — call a family member, visit a character's apartment, deal with off-duty life that bleeds into the shift

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not go AFK for long periods during a shift
- Do not complain in OOC that nothing is happening — make something happen
- Do not start provoking civilians to generate action — that is not policing, that is harassment

## Building Relationships with Civilians

The best serious RP officers are known by civilians — not feared by them.

- **Be a regular somewhere.** Have a diner or coffee shop your character frequents. Get to know the owner. Become a fixture. When that owner's business gets robbed, you are not just responding to a call — you are protecting a friend.
- **Remember names.** When you pull someone over and recognize them from a previous interaction, reference it. "Didn't I give you a warning on this same stretch of road two weeks ago?" That continuity is what makes serious RP special.
- **Show discretion.** Not every offense needs a citation. Not every warrant needs to be served at the most humiliating moment. How you exercise discretion defines your character and builds your reputation.
- **Attend civilian events.** Car meets, community gatherings, business openings — showing up in uniform builds trust and creates organic interaction.

## Documentation Expectations

In serious RP, if you did not write it down, it did not happen.

**What requires documentation:**
- Every arrest — full narrative report including probable cause, evidence, suspect statements, Miranda advisement
- Use of force incidents — detailed account of what force was used, why, what alternatives were considered
- Pursuits — initiation reason, route, speeds, tactics used, outcome
- Major incidents — shootings, hostage situations, multi-agency operations
- Evidence collection — what was found, where, how it was preserved, chain of custody

**Report writing standards:**
- Write in first person, past tense: "I observed the suspect exit the vehicle and discard a small baggie near the storm drain on Elgin Avenue."
- Be factual. Do not inject opinions or assumptions into reports.
- Include times, locations, and involved parties.
- Reference applicable penal codes for any charges filed.
- Proofread. A sloppy report undermines your credibility if the case goes to court.

> **Court testimony:** In servers with an active Department of Justice, your reports may be entered as evidence in trials. Defense attorneys (other players) will cross-examine you based on what you wrote. Inconsistencies between your report and your testimony will be exploited. Write your reports as if a lawyer will read them — because they will.

## Internal Affairs Awareness

In serious RP, your conduct is monitored. Internal Affairs is not just a department — it is a constant presence.

**Things that trigger IA investigations:**
- Excessive use of force complaints from civilians
- Pattern of civil rights violations (illegal searches, false arrests)
- Failure to activate body camera (if your server uses this mechanic)
- Conduct unbecoming — behavior that embarrasses the department
- Corruption — accepting bribes, leaking information, associating with known criminals off duty
- Insubordination — refusing direct orders from a superior

**How to handle an IA investigation:**
- Cooperate. Fighting IA makes everything worse.
- Know your rights within the department — most servers model Garrity rights (you must answer questions but your statements cannot be used criminally)
- Get a Police Benevolent Association representative if your server has one
- Do not discuss active investigations with other officers unless authorized

**IA is good RP.** Being investigated adds tension, drama, and character development. Do not treat it as punishment — treat it as a storyline.

## Career Progression

Climbing the ranks in a serious RP department is a long road.

| Phase | Timeline (Real Time) | Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Cadet / Probationary | Weeks 1–3 | Survive FTO, learn SOPs, prove reliability |
| Officer I | Months 1–3 | Build patrol skills, handle calls independently, write solid reports |
| Officer II / Senior | Months 3–6 | Mentor juniors, specialize (traffic, K9, detective), demonstrate leadership |
| Corporal / Sergeant | Months 6–12 | Supervise shifts, manage people, handle administrative duties |
| Lieutenant+ | Months 12+ | Command staff, policy decisions, department direction |

**Promotions are not guaranteed.** Time in grade is necessary but not sufficient. You need demonstrated leadership, clean conduct, and often a formal promotion process (written test, oral board, command review).

**Specialization paths:**
- Detective Bureau — investigations, undercover work, case management
- SWAT / Tactical — high-risk warrants, hostage rescue, active shooter response
- Traffic Division — accident reconstruction, DUI enforcement, motorcycle patrol
- Training Division — running the academy, FTO program, scenario development
- K9 Unit — working with a police dog handler script, tracking, narcotics detection
- Air Support — helicopter patrol (if available on the server)

## Department Politics

Serious RP departments have politics. Factions form. People disagree. Leaders have favorites. This is realistic and creates compelling RP.

- **Play the politics IC.** If your character thinks the new pursuit policy is reckless, argue against it through proper channels — bring it up at a briefing, talk to your union rep, write a memo.
- **Do not let IC politics become OOC drama.** Your character can hate the captain. You should not hate the player. This is the hardest line in serious RP and the one that gets crossed most often.
- **Support your chain of command publicly, even when you disagree privately.** Undermining leadership in front of junior officers damages the department. Save your objections for private conversations with peers or through official channels.

## Stress and Burnout — The OOC Reality

Serious RP law enforcement burns people out. This is not a failure — it is a reality of the commitment required.

**Signs of burnout:**
- Dreading logging in for your shift
- Getting frustrated at every civilian interaction
- Taking IC conflicts personally
- Feeling obligated rather than excited to play
- Snapping at other players in OOC channels

**Managing burnout:**
- **Take breaks.** Step away for a few days. Your department will survive without you.
- **Communicate with leadership.** Most departments understand that players need time off. Request a leave of absence IC if needed.
- **Play other characters.** Having a civilian alt lets you enjoy the server without the pressure of LEO expectations.
- **Set boundaries.** You do not have to attend every event, respond to every Discord ping, or be available for every training session. This is a game.
- **Remember why you started.** If you joined LEO because you love the RP, reconnect with that. Do a ride-along with a new cadet. Take a call that reminds you why you like this role.

## What Serious RP Expects That Semi-Serious Does Not

| Expectation | Details |
|---|---|
| Mandatory reports | Every arrest, pursuit, and use of force must be documented — no exceptions |
| Court readiness | Your cases may go to trial. You must be prepared to testify and defend your actions |
| Body camera RP | Many servers require you to RP activating your camera during every interaction |
| Off-duty conduct | Your character's behavior off duty can affect their career. Getting drunk at the Vanilla Unicorn while in uniform is an IA complaint waiting to happen |
| Emotional RP | You are expected to react realistically to traumatic calls — a shooting should affect your character, not just be another call |
| Department meetings | Attending briefings, training sessions, and department meetings is mandatory, not optional |
| SOP compliance | Deviations from standard operating procedures require justification. "I forgot" is not a justification |
| Interdepartmental cooperation | Working with BCSO, SASP, EMS, fire department, and DOJ is part of the job. You do not operate in a vacuum |

---

## Final Thoughts

Serious RP law enforcement is the most demanding role on most servers. It requires more knowledge, more discipline, more time, and more emotional investment than almost any other position. But when it works — when you crack a case that took three weeks, when you testify in a trial and the jury finds the defendant guilty, when a cadet you trained gets promoted and thanks you for making them a better officer — there is nothing else like it in roleplay.

Go in with open eyes. Know the commitment. Respect the craft. And when the burnout comes — because it will — remember that stepping back is not quitting. It is taking care of the person behind the character so that the character can come back stronger.
