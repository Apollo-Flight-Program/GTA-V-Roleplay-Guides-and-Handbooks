# Taxi and Transport Guide

Driving people around Los Santos is one of the most underrated civilian roles in GTA V roleplay. A good taxi or transport driver is not just a chauffeur — they are a storyteller, a therapist, a tour guide, and sometimes the only friendly face someone meets all day. This guide covers everything you need to build a memorable transport character.

---

## Why Transport RP Works

Transport drivers interact with every kind of character on the server — criminals, cops, business owners, newcomers, everyone. No other civilian role gives you this range of exposure. Every fare is a potential story. Every ride is a scene.

You also serve a practical function. New players need rides. Characters without vehicles need transportation. People leaving the hospital, the courthouse, or a crime scene need someone to pick them up. You are the connective tissue of the city.

---

## Choosing Your Role

| Role | Vehicle | Style |
|---|---|---|
| Traditional Taxi | Vapid Stanier (taxi livery) or Vapid Cab | Metered fares, dispatch-based, yellow cab classic |
| Rideshare Driver | Personal vehicle (4-door sedan preferred) | App-based feel, casual conversation, flexible pricing |
| Bus Driver | Dashound or Airport Bus | Fixed routes, multiple passengers, schedule-based |
| Limo / Executive | Dundreary Stretch or Benefactor Schafter | High-end clients, discretion, premium pricing |
| Shuttle Service | Vapid Minivan or Brute Bus | Airport runs, hotel transfers, group transport |

Pick a lane that fits your character. A retired trucker driving a beat-up cab tells a different story than a young hustler running a rideshare operation from his Ubermacht Oracle.

---

## Customer Interactions

The ride is the roleplay. How you interact with passengers defines your entire experience.

**The pickup:**
- Confirm the passenger's name and destination
- Open the door for them if you want to add detail with `/me`
- Ask if they have a preferred route

**During the ride:**
- Make conversation — ask about their day, where they are headed, what they do
- React to what they tell you — a taxi driver hears everything and has opinions
- Comment on traffic, road conditions, or things you pass (the Maze Bank Tower, the Vinewood sign, the construction on Elgin Avenue)
- Respect passengers who want a quiet ride — not everyone wants to talk

**The drop-off:**
- Announce the fare
- Thank them for riding with you
- Offer a business card or tell them how to reach you next time

## Fare Systems

Establish a pricing model and stick to it. Consistency builds trust and makes the RP feel grounded.

| Ride Type | Suggested Fare Range |
|---|---|
| Short trip (same district) | $50 – $150 |
| Cross-city (e.g., Pillbox to Vinewood) | $150 – $350 |
| Long haul (Los Santos to Sandy Shores) | $400 – $800 |
| Long haul (Los Santos to Paleto Bay) | $800 – $1,500 |
| Airport run (LSIA to downtown) | $200 – $400 |
| Wait time (per 5 minutes) | $50 |
| Late night surcharge | +25% |

Adapt these to your server's economy. The actual dollar amounts matter less than having a system that feels fair and consistent.

## Dispatch and Radio Communication

If your server has a taxi company or dispatch system, use it properly.

**Accepting a call:**
> *"Dispatch, this is unit 7. I'll take that fare at Pillbox Medical. ETA three minutes."*

**Reporting status:**
> *"Unit 7, passenger picked up at Pillbox. Heading to Vinewood Boulevard."*

**Completing a ride:**
> *"Unit 7, fare dropped at Vinewood. Back in service, heading south on Alta Street."*

Use 10-codes if your company uses them, or plain language if that is the standard. The key is clear, professional communication that adds immersion without slowing down the RP.

### Common Radio Codes

| Code | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 10-1 | Poor reception |
| 10-4 | Acknowledged / understood |
| 10-6 | Busy with a fare |
| 10-7 | Out of service / off duty |
| 10-8 | In service / available |
| 10-20 | Current location |
| Code 4 | No further assistance needed |

Not every server uses these. Adapt to your company's standards. Even without codes, maintaining professional radio discipline — brief transmissions, clear language, no unnecessary chatter — sets you apart as a serious operator.

## Handling Dangerous Passengers

Not every fare is friendly. Transport drivers encounter drunks, criminals, aggressive passengers, and people running from the law.

**The drunk passenger:**
Slurring, giving wrong addresses, possibly getting sick in your car. Roleplay the frustration. Charge a cleaning fee. Decide whether to drop them off or call someone to pick them up.

**The criminal:**
A passenger gets in and says "Just drive." They are clearly running from something. Do you comply? Do you ask questions? Do you quietly call the police? This is where your character's morality gets tested.

**The armed passenger:**
If someone pulls a weapon in your cab, value your life. Comply with demands. Do not be a hero. After the situation ends, report it to police and roleplay the trauma — shaking hands, considering quitting, being nervous around passengers for a while.

**The non-payer:**
Someone tries to ditch without paying. Chase them down verbally, not physically. Report them. Complain about it to your next passenger. Let it become a story, not a fight.

## Tipping Culture

Tips are a natural part of transport RP and create organic interactions.

- Thank passengers who tip generously
- Remember big tippers and give them priority service next time
- Do not guilt passengers into tipping — let it happen naturally
- Mention to other drivers or at the bar how good or bad tips were today

Tips also create character motivation. A driver saving up for a new cab through tips has a built-in storyline.

---

## Vehicle Maintenance RP

Your vehicle is your livelihood. Treat it that way.

- Visit a mechanic regularly for oil changes, tire rotations, and repairs
- Roleplay breakdowns — overheating on the highway, flat tires, engine trouble
- Keep your cab clean — mention washing it, vacuuming the seats, dealing with a mess a passenger left
- Name your vehicle if it fits your character — truckers and cab drivers often do

When your car is in the shop, you are off duty. Use that downtime for other RP — sit in the mechanic's waiting area, grab food, complain about repair costs.

## Building Regular Clientele

The best transport RP happens when passengers become regulars.

**How to build regulars:**
- Remember their name and details from previous rides
- Greet them warmly when they call again
- Give them a small discount or priority pickup
- Ask follow-up questions about things they mentioned last time

**Why regulars matter:**
- They generate consistent RP without effort
- Their stories evolve over time, and you get to witness the arc
- They recommend you to others, building your reputation
- They become part of your character's social circle

A taxi driver who has five regular clients has five ongoing storylines built into their daily routine.

---

## Driving Etiquette

How you drive matters. Transport drivers should be the best drivers on the server.

| Do | Don't |
|---|---|
| Obey traffic laws (mostly) | Drive on sidewalks or against traffic |
| Use turn signals with `/me` or emotes | Treat every ride like a police chase |
| Drive at a realistic pace | Crash constantly and ignore the damage |
| Pull over safely for pickups and drop-offs | Stop in the middle of intersections |
| React to near-misses and bad drivers around you | Drive in silence like an NPC |

Your driving style is part of your character. A nervous new driver, a reckless speed demon, a careful professional — each one tells a different story.

## Route Knowledge

Know the city. A transport driver who knows the streets is more immersive than one who follows a GPS blindly.

**Key landmarks and routes:**
- **LSIA** — Los Santos International Airport, south of the city. Main terminal access off the highway.
- **Pillbox Medical** — the central hospital near Strawberry. Frequent pickup point.
- **Mission Row PD** — police station. You will pick up people after processing or court.
- **Legion Square** — downtown gathering point. Good spot to idle and wait for fares.
- **Del Perro Pier** — tourist area. Weekend crowds, good tips.
- **Sandy Shores** — long haul territory. Charge accordingly.
- **Paleto Bay** — the longest run. Premium fare, bring snacks for the drive.

Learn shortcuts through Pillbox, the fastest route from the airport to Vinewood, and which roads to avoid during peak hours.

## Running a Transport Business

If you want to take it further, start a company.

- Recruit other drivers and assign shifts
- Create a dispatch system through phone or radio
- Set company policies on fares, dress code, and conduct
- Handle complaints from customers about your drivers
- Compete with other transport companies for territory and reputation

Running a taxi company is a full management RP experience — hiring, firing, training, and dealing with the daily problems of keeping a business running in a city that does not make it easy.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The airport run:**
> Pick up a character who just "arrived in the city" for the first time. They have questions about everything. You become their first impression of Los Santos. Make it count.

**The hospital pickup:**
> Someone calls for a ride from Pillbox. They are limping, bandaged, and do not want to talk about what happened. The silence in the car tells the whole story.

**The late-night confession:**
> A passenger gets in at 2 AM after a rough night. They start talking. About their marriage, their debts, their mistakes. You listen. That is the job.

**The suspicious fare:**
> Two people get in with duffel bags and ask to go to the docks at Elysian Island. They keep checking behind them. Something is wrong. What do you do?

---

## Final Thoughts

A great taxi driver on a roleplay server does more for the community than most people realize. You connect strangers, you witness stories from every corner of the city, and you provide a service that keeps the world moving.

The yellow cab is not glamorous. The money is not great. The passengers are unpredictable.

But the stories are endless.
