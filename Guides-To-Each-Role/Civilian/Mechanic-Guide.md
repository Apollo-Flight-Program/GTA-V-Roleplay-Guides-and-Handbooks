# Mechanic Guide — Auto Repair Roleplay

The mechanic is one of the most socially connected roles on any GTA V RP server. Everyone drives, everyone crashes, and everyone eventually needs their car fixed. A good mechanic shop becomes a social hub — a place where stories cross, deals get made, and characters build trust one oil change at a time.

This guide covers how to roleplay a mechanic with depth, from diagnosing a busted radiator to running a full shop operation.

---

## Why Mechanic RP Matters

Mechanics serve every faction on the server. Police cruisers need servicing. Criminal getaway cars need tuning. Civilians need their daily drivers maintained. Tow truck operators need a shop to deliver vehicles to.

A mechanic is not just a service provider — they are a hub character. The shop becomes a location where people gather, talk, wait, and interact. Some of the best unplanned RP happens in a mechanic's garage while someone waits for an oil change.

---

## Setting Up Your Character

Before you start wrenching, build a character that fits the role.

**Background questions:**
- Where did they learn to work on cars? Trade school? Family garage? Self-taught on YouTube?
- What kind of vehicles do they specialize in? Muscle cars, imports, trucks, motorcycles?
- Are they the owner, a lead tech, or just starting as an apprentice?
- Do they love the work or is it just a paycheck?
- What is their relationship with their tools and workspace?

A mechanic who grew up rebuilding Vapid Dominators in the desert has a very different vibe than one who trained at a European performance shop before moving to Los Santos.

---

## Diagnosing Vehicles

This is where the RP lives. Do not just walk up to a car and say "it's fixed." Take time with the diagnosis.

**The diagnostic process:**
1. Ask the customer what the problem is — listen to their description
2. Pop the hood with `/me` and inspect visually
3. Start the engine and listen for sounds
4. Use `/me` to check specific systems (belts, fluids, wiring, exhaust)
5. Give the customer your assessment — what is wrong and what it will cost

**Common diagnostic scenarios:**

| Symptom | Possible RP Diagnosis | Repair Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Engine overheating | Cracked radiator, coolant leak, bad thermostat | Replace radiator, flush coolant system |
| Strange grinding noise | Worn brake pads, damaged rotor, bad bearing | Replace pads and resurface rotors |
| Won't start | Dead battery, bad starter motor, fuel pump failure | Test electrical system, replace parts |
| Smoke from exhaust | Oil burning, head gasket leak, exhaust damage | Engine teardown or exhaust patch |
| Pulling to one side | Alignment issue, uneven tire wear, bent control arm | Alignment and suspension check |
| Transmission slipping | Low fluid, worn clutch, internal damage | Fluid change or full transmission rebuild |

Use automotive terminology naturally. You do not need to be a real mechanic, but knowing basic terms like alternator, catalytic converter, CV joint, and timing belt makes the RP convincing.

## Pricing Repairs

Have a pricing structure. Post it on a board in the shop or keep it in your head — but be consistent.

| Service | Suggested Price Range |
|---|---|
| Oil change | $100 – $200 |
| Brake pad replacement | $200 – $400 |
| Tire change (per tire) | $75 – $150 |
| Full alignment | $150 – $300 |
| Engine diagnostic | $100 – $200 |
| Radiator replacement | $300 – $600 |
| Transmission repair | $500 – $1,500 |
| Full engine rebuild | $2,000 – $5,000 |
| Body panel repair | $200 – $800 |
| Paint job (full) | $500 – $2,000 |
| Performance tune-up | $300 – $1,000 |
| Custom exhaust | $400 – $1,200 |

Adjust for your server's economy. Offer discounts to regulars. Charge rush fees for emergency work. Haggle with customers who push back on price — that negotiation is roleplay.

### Estimates and Invoices

Always provide an estimate before starting work. A professional mechanic does not surprise the customer with the bill.

**The estimate process:**
1. Diagnose the problem
2. List the parts needed and their cost
3. Add labor (charge by the hour or by the job)
4. Present the total to the customer
5. Get verbal or written approval before touching the car

If additional problems are found during the repair, stop and inform the customer before proceeding. Nothing destroys trust faster than a bill that doubled without warning.

Use `/me writes up an estimate on the shop clipboard` to add detail. Hand it to the customer. Let them review it. Answer their questions.

## Customer Interactions

Every customer interaction is a scene. Approach them all differently.

**The first-timer:**
A new player rolls up and does not know how mechanic RP works. Walk them through it patiently. Explain what you are doing to their car. Make them comfortable. They will come back.

**The regular:**
Greet them by name. Ask about their car. Remember what you worked on last time. Regulars are the lifeblood of a shop.

**The impatient customer:**
They want it done now. They keep asking how much longer. Roleplay the frustration professionally. Set expectations. If they are rude, charge them full price — no discounts for attitude.

**The criminal:**
Someone rolls in with bullet holes in the body panels, blood on the seats, or a car they clearly stole. Do you ask questions? Do you fix it and mind your business? Do you quietly tip off the police? Your character's morality shapes these moments.

**The negotiator:**
They want a deal. They claim they can get it cheaper elsewhere. Negotiate, stand your ground, or offer a compromise. This back-and-forth is pure RP.

## Running a Shop

If you manage or own a mechanic shop, you have a full business to roleplay.

### Location Matters
Popular mechanic shop locations include the Los Santos Customs buildings in Burton, La Mesa, or Harmony. Some servers have custom shop locations in Strawberry, Paleto Bay, or along Route 68.

### Staff Management
- Hire mechanics with different skill levels — apprentices, journeymen, and senior techs
- Assign shifts and ensure coverage during peak hours
- Train new hires on shop procedures and customer service standards
- Handle employee disputes, lateness, and performance issues

### Shop Rules
Establish standards that your employees follow:
- Greet every customer within 30 seconds of arrival
- Always provide an estimate before starting work
- No personal vehicles blocking the bays
- Keep the workspace clean between jobs
- Log every repair in the shop ledger

### Financial Management
- Track income and expenses
- Order parts (RP purchasing from suppliers)
- Pay employees fairly
- Set aside money for equipment upgrades
- Handle slow days — cut hours or run promotions

## Specializations

Not every mechanic does everything. Specializing gives your character depth and creates interdependence between shops.

**Performance Tuning:**
Focus on engine upgrades, turbo kits, suspension tuning, and exhaust systems. Your clients are racers, car enthusiasts, and people who want speed. You know the difference between a stage 1 and stage 3 tune. Work on Annis Elegy RH8s, Pfister Comets, and Karin Sultans all day.

**Body Work and Paint:**
Specialize in dent repair, panel replacement, custom paint jobs, and vinyl wraps. You are the artist of the automotive world. People come to you to make their car look new or to change its appearance entirely — sometimes for reasons they would rather not discuss.

**Electrical and Diagnostics:**
Focus on wiring, computer systems, battery issues, and electronic troubleshooting. You handle the problems other mechanics cannot figure out. Alarm systems, audio installs, lighting modifications.

**Off-Road and Trucks:**
Specialize in lifted trucks, off-road suspension, roll cages, and heavy-duty repairs. Your clients drive Vapid Bobcat XLs, Bravado Bisons, and Canis Mesas. You know Blaine County roads better than anyone.

**Motorcycle Specialist:**
Focus on two-wheeled machines — Dinka Akumas, Western Daemons, Pegassi Batis, and LCC Hexers. Motorcycle work requires a different skill set and attracts a distinct clientele — from commuters to bikers to weekend riders. A motorcycle specialist can carve out a niche that no general shop competes with.

## Tow Truck Partnerships

Build a working relationship with tow truck operators. They bring you business — you give them priority service and referral fees.

- Establish a preferred tow company for your shop
- Offer tow drivers a discount on their personal vehicle maintenance
- Coordinate on accident calls — the tow truck brings the car, you assess the damage
- Share radio frequencies for quick communication

This partnership creates an interconnected network of civilian RP that benefits both roles.

### Police and Emergency Contracts
Some mechanic shops secure contracts with the police department or EMS to maintain fleet vehicles. This means:
- Regular service appointments for patrol cars, undercover vehicles, and ambulances
- Priority repairs when a unit is damaged in the line of duty
- Discretion about modifications you see on police vehicles
- Steady, reliable income for the shop
- A professional relationship with law enforcement that can benefit you in other ways

---

## Inventory Management

Roleplay your parts supply. You do not have unlimited everything.

- Keep a mental or written inventory of common parts
- Tell customers you need to order a part — it will be ready tomorrow
- Run out of something and have to improvise or send them elsewhere
- Negotiate with parts suppliers for bulk discounts
- Deal with backordered or rare parts for exotic vehicles

Inventory limitations create natural delays, and delays create RP. A customer waiting for a part has to come back, check in, and interact with your shop again.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The insurance claim:**
> A customer brings in a wrecked Gallivanter Baller and says insurance is covering it. You document the damage, take photos with `/me`, provide an estimate, and file the paperwork. Three days later the insurance company disputes the claim. Now the customer is stuck with the bill.

**The mystery car:**
> A tow truck drops off a car with no plates, no registration, and modifications that suggest it has been used for racing — or worse. Do you work on it? Do you ask where it came from? Do you keep it in the back and pretend you never saw it?

**The loyalty test:**
> A regular customer finds out your shop charged them for a part that was not actually replaced. Do you come clean? Blame a new employee? Offer a refund and a free service? Your reputation is on the line.

**The competition:**
> A new shop opens across the street with lower prices. Your regulars start going there. Do you lower your prices, differentiate your service, or confront the competition directly?

---

## Final Thoughts

A mechanic shop is more than a place to fix cars. It is a gathering point, a business, a social hub, and a stage for some of the most natural roleplay on any server. The grease on your hands, the tools on your belt, the conversations over an open hood — that is the kind of RP that keeps people coming back.

Fix the car. Build the story.
