# Lawyer Guide — Practicing Law in Los Santos

A lawyer does not just argue in court — they build cases from nothing, navigate a maze of legal strategy, and stand between the state's power and an individual's freedom. Whether you are a prosecutor trying to lock up a dangerous criminal or a defense attorney fighting to keep someone out of prison, legal RP demands preparation, quick thinking, and the ability to create compelling narrative under pressure.

This guide covers both sides of the courtroom — prosecution and defense — along with client management, case-building, courtroom tactics, and the ethical lines every attorney walks.

---

## Defense Attorney vs. Prosecutor

The two sides of criminal law are fundamentally different in philosophy, tools, and obligations.

| Aspect | Prosecutor | Defense Attorney |
|---|---|---|
| **Who they represent** | The State of San Andreas / The People | The individual defendant |
| **Objective** | Prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt | Protect the defendant's rights and challenge the state's case |
| **Burden of proof** | Carries the burden — must prove the case | No burden — does not have to prove innocence |
| **Resources** | Full weight of law enforcement, evidence labs, state funding | Limited to their own investigation, client testimony, and independent evidence |
| **Ethical obligation** | Seek justice, not just convictions — must disclose exculpatory evidence | Zealously advocate for the client, even if personally convinced of guilt |
| **Relationship to police** | Cooperative — works with detectives and officers | Adversarial — challenges police work, procedures, and testimony |
| **Public perception** | Seen as the "good guy" — fighting crime | Often seen negatively — "how can you defend criminals?" |

**Neither side is the hero.** Both are essential. Without prosecutors, criminals face no consequences. Without defense attorneys, the state has unchecked power. The tension between them is what makes the system work — and what makes legal RP compelling.

## Private Practice vs. Public Defender

| Type | Details |
|---|---|
| **Private attorney** | Hired by clients who can pay. Can choose their cases. Sets their own fees. Runs their own firm. |
| **Public defender** | Appointed by the court for defendants who cannot afford an attorney. Cannot choose cases. Often overworked. |
| **District attorney / Prosecutor** | Employed by the state. Prosecutes criminal cases. Works closely with law enforcement. |

**RP implications:**
- A private attorney can build a law firm with other players, creating a business RP ecosystem
- A public defender is always busy, always underpaid, and handles the cases no one else wants — rich character material
- A prosecutor has institutional power but also institutional politics — answering to the DA, navigating office hierarchy, and managing a caseload

> "Yeah, I can take the case. Three thousand retainer, non-refundable. Come by tomorrow morning and bring everything — police reports, witness names, anything you have. And don't talk to anyone else about it until we meet."

## Client Meetings

The attorney-client relationship is the foundation of defense work. Your first meeting with a client sets the tone for the entire case.

**First meeting checklist:**

| Topic | What to Cover |
|---|---|
| Their story | What happened? Get their version of events in full |
| The charges | What are they charged with? Do they understand the charges? |
| Prior record | Any previous arrests, convictions, or pending cases? |
| Evidence against them | What do they know about the prosecution's evidence? |
| Witnesses | Who saw what happened? Who can testify for or against them? |
| Goals | What outcome do they want? Dismissal? Acquittal? Reduced charges? |
| Expectations | Be honest about the strength of the case and likely outcomes |

**Attorney-client privilege:**
Everything your client tells you is confidential. You cannot reveal it to anyone — police, prosecutors, judges, or other players — without your client's permission. This is absolute. Even if your client confesses to you, that confession stays between you.

> "Look, whatever you tell me stays in this room. That's the law. But I need you to be completely honest with me. If I walk into that courtroom and get surprised by something you didn't tell me, I can't protect you. So — what really happened that night?"

## Building a Case

Trial preparation is where cases are won or lost. By the time you stand up in court, you should know every piece of evidence, every witness statement, and every weakness in the other side's argument.

### For the Prosecution

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| Review police reports | Read every report filed by responding and investigating officers |
| Examine physical evidence | Chain of custody, lab results, photographs, surveillance footage |
| Interview witnesses | Speak to every witness, assess their credibility, prepare them for testimony |
| Identify weaknesses | Where is your case vulnerable? Address it before the defense does |
| Develop a theory | What story does the evidence tell? Craft a clear, compelling narrative |
| Anticipate the defense | What will they argue? Prepare to counter every expected defense strategy |

### For the Defense

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| Review discovery | Read everything the prosecution has disclosed — reports, evidence lists, witness statements |
| Interview the client | Multiple times — details change, memories clarify, stories solidify |
| Independent investigation | Visit the scene, interview witnesses the police missed, find inconsistencies |
| Challenge evidence | Was it properly obtained? Is the chain of custody intact? Are lab results reliable? |
| Identify reasonable doubt | You do not need to prove innocence — you need to create doubt |
| Prepare motions | Motions to suppress illegally obtained evidence, dismiss charges, or exclude testimony |

> `/me spreads crime scene photographs across the desk and circles an area near the back door in red marker.`
> "See this? The police report says my client entered through the front. But there's a footprint here at the back door that doesn't match his shoe size. Someone else was there. That's our reasonable doubt."

## Discovery

Discovery is the pre-trial process where both sides share evidence. It is legally required and creates pre-trial RP.

**What the prosecution must disclose:**
- All evidence they intend to use at trial
- Witness lists and statements
- **Exculpatory evidence** (Brady material) — anything that suggests the defendant might be innocent, even if it hurts the prosecution's case
- Expert reports (forensic analysis, autopsies, etc.)

**What the defense may request:**
- Police body camera and dashcam footage
- Internal communications between officers
- Records of prior complaints against testifying officers
- Lab reports and methodology
- 911 call recordings

> **Defense Attorney:** "Your Honor, the prosecution has failed to disclose body camera footage from the arresting officer. We know it exists — it's referenced in the incident report. We move to compel production of this footage and request a continuance until it's provided."
> **Judge:** "Prosecution?"
> **Prosecutor:** "The footage was inadvertently omitted, Your Honor. We'll have it to the defense by end of day."
> **Judge:** "See that you do. Motion to compel is granted."

## Plea Bargaining

Most cases never go to trial. They are resolved through plea deals — negotiations between the prosecution and defense.

**How plea deals work:**

| Component | Details |
|---|---|
| Charge reduction | Defendant pleads guilty to a lesser charge (e.g., murder reduced to manslaughter) |
| Sentence recommendation | Prosecution recommends a lighter sentence in exchange for the plea |
| Cooperation agreement | Defendant provides information or testimony against others in exchange for leniency |
| Nolo contendere | "No contest" — defendant does not admit guilt but accepts the conviction |

**Plea negotiation RP:**

> **Prosecutor:** "Your client is looking at fifteen years for armed robbery. I'll offer second-degree robbery — eight years, eligible for parole in five."
> **Defense Attorney:** "Eight years is still steep for a first-time offender with no prior violent record. How about five years, parole eligible in three? He'll plead today."
> **Prosecutor:** "Six years, parole in four. That's my final offer. Take it or we go to trial — and I'm confident in my case."
> **Defense Attorney:** "Let me talk to my client."

## Courtroom Conduct

How you behave in court matters as much as what you say. Decorum is non-negotiable.

**Rules of conduct:**
- Always address the judge as "Your Honor"
- Stand when speaking unless the judge directs otherwise
- Never speak directly to the opposing attorney during proceedings — address the bench
- Do not argue with the judge's rulings — note your objection for the record and move on
- Dress professionally — suits, ties, professional attire
- Never show visible frustration, anger, or celebration in front of the jury

> `/me stands slowly, buttoning the suit jacket. Approaches the lectern with a legal pad.`
> "Your Honor, the defense calls its next witness — Mr. David Park."

## Objections

Objections are the attorney's tool for enforcing the rules of evidence and procedure during trial.

| Objection | When to Use It |
|---|---|
| Hearsay | Witness is testifying about what someone else said, not what they personally observed |
| Relevance | The question or evidence has no bearing on the case at hand |
| Leading | Attorney is suggesting the answer in their question during direct examination |
| Speculation | Witness is guessing or offering opinions beyond their knowledge |
| Asked and answered | The question has already been asked and answered — attorney is badgering |
| Argumentative | Attorney is arguing with the witness rather than asking questions |
| Foundation | No foundation has been laid to establish the witness's knowledge or the evidence's authenticity |
| Prejudicial | Evidence is more likely to inflame the jury than to prove a relevant fact |
| Beyond the scope | Cross-examination is going beyond topics covered in direct examination |
| Non-responsive | The witness is not answering the question that was asked |

**How to object:**

> **Defense Attorney:** "Objection, Your Honor — hearsay. The witness is testifying about what he was told by a third party, not what he personally observed."
> **Judge:** "Sustained. The jury will disregard the witness's last statement. Prosecution, rephrase your question."

## Evidence Presentation

Presenting evidence effectively is an art.

**Types of evidence:**

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Testimonial | Witness statements given under oath |
| Documentary | Reports, contracts, messages, financial records |
| Physical | Weapons, drugs, clothing, DNA samples |
| Digital | Surveillance footage, phone records, GPS data |
| Demonstrative | Maps, diagrams, timelines used to illustrate testimony |
| Expert | Testimony from qualified experts (forensic analysts, medical examiners, financial auditors) |

**Admitting evidence:**

> **Prosecutor:** "Your Honor, I'd like to mark this as People's Exhibit B — a .45 caliber handgun recovered from the defendant's vehicle during a lawful search."
> **Defense Attorney:** "Objection, Your Honor. The defense moves to suppress Exhibit B. The search of the vehicle was conducted without a warrant and without probable cause."
> **Judge:** "I'll hear arguments on the motion to suppress. Approach the bench."

## Jury Selection (Voir Dire)

Selecting a favorable jury is a strategic exercise that can determine the outcome before a single piece of evidence is presented.

**What to look for:**

| For Prosecution | For Defense |
|---|---|
| Jurors with law enforcement connections | Jurors skeptical of authority |
| Jurors who have been crime victims | Jurors who value presumption of innocence strongly |
| Jurors who express firm views on law and order | Jurors with personal experience with the justice system |
| Decisive, confident personality types | Empathetic, open-minded personality types |

> **Defense Attorney:** "Juror Number 7, you mentioned your father was a police officer. Do you think that might influence how you evaluate police testimony in this case?"
> **Juror 7:** "I'd like to think I can be fair."
> **Defense Attorney:** "Your Honor, the defense thanks and excuses Juror Number 7. Peremptory challenge."

## Legal Ethics and the Bar Association

Attorneys are bound by ethical rules. Violating them can result in sanctions, disbarment, and criminal charges.

| Ethical Rule | What It Means |
|---|---|
| Confidentiality | Never reveal client information without permission |
| Competence | Handle cases you are qualified for — refer out what you cannot handle |
| Candor to the tribunal | Do not lie to the court or present evidence you know is false |
| Conflict of interest | Do not represent clients whose interests conflict with each other |
| Diligence | Pursue your client's case with reasonable promptness and effort |
| Fairness | Do not hide evidence, intimidate witnesses, or obstruct justice |

**Bar Association RP:**
Some servers have a bar association that licenses attorneys, investigates complaints, and disciplines misconduct. This creates oversight RP — a corrupt lawyer can be disbarred, a negligent one can be sanctioned, and bar hearings themselves become courtroom drama.

> **Bar Association Chair:** "Mr. Sullivan, a complaint has been filed alleging that you disclosed privileged client communications to a third party. How do you respond to this allegation?"

## Common Lawyer RP Scenarios

**The guilty client:**
> Your client confesses to you that they committed the murder. Attorney-client privilege means you cannot reveal this. But you also cannot put them on the stand to lie. How do you defend them without suborning perjury?

**The last-minute evidence:**
> The night before trial, your investigator finds a witness who could blow the case open. But you have not disclosed them to the prosecution. Do you ask for a continuance, call them as a surprise witness and risk an objection, or hold them for appeal?

**The plea pressure:**
> Your client is innocent but the evidence looks bad. The prosecution offers a deal — two years instead of a potential twenty. Your client asks you what to do. You believe in their innocence, but you also know juries are unpredictable. What do you advise?

## Final Thoughts

Practicing law in Los Santos is not about winning or losing — it is about the process. The argument you craft, the witness you break on cross-examination, the objection you raise at exactly the right moment, the closing argument that makes the jury pause and think. These are the moments that make legal RP unforgettable.

Whether you wear a prosecutor's badge or carry a defense attorney's briefcase, you are a storyteller. Your stage is the courtroom. Your audience is the jury. And your story is the one that determines someone's future.

Take the case. Build the argument. Stand up and make it count.
