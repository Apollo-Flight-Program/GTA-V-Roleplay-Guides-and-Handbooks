# Judge and Legal System — The Courthouse of San Andreas

The courtroom is where consequences become permanent. A judge's ruling can send a character to prison for years, award millions in damages, or set a legal precedent that reshapes the entire server's criminal landscape. Court RP is methodical, procedural, and dramatic in a way that no other role can replicate — the tension of a jury deliberation, the shock of a verdict, the weight of a sentence handed down from the bench.

This guide covers the full scope of judicial roleplay — courtroom layout and roles, trial procedure from arraignment to appeal, and the authority and responsibility that comes with wearing the robe.

---

## Courtroom Layout

Understanding where everyone sits and stands creates visual immersion and procedural authenticity.

| Position | Location | Who |
|---|---|---|
| The Bench | Elevated desk at the front of the room | The Judge — presides over all proceedings |
| Witness Stand | Adjacent to the bench, facing the courtroom | Witnesses giving testimony under oath |
| Prosecution Table | Table to the judge's right (facing the bench) | Prosecutor and their team |
| Defense Table | Table to the judge's left (facing the bench) | Defense attorney and the defendant |
| Jury Box | Rows of seats to one side of the courtroom | Jury members (if applicable) |
| Gallery | Rows of seating behind the bar | Public observers, family, press |
| Clerk's Desk | Near the bench | Court clerk — manages documents, swears in witnesses |
| Bailiff's Post | Near the entrance or beside the bench | Bailiff — maintains order, escorts defendants |

> `/me takes the bench and adjusts the microphone. Looks over the courtroom — counsel at their tables, the defendant seated, the gallery murmuring. Strikes the gavel once.`
> "This court is now in session. The Honorable Judge Patricia Vance presiding. Please be seated."

## Courtroom Roles

Every person in the courtroom has a defined role. Understanding each one ensures smooth proceedings.

| Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|
| **Judge** | Presides over proceedings, rules on objections, instructs the jury, determines admissibility of evidence, imposes sentences |
| **Prosecutor** | Represents the State of San Andreas, presents the case against the defendant, must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt |
| **Defense Attorney** | Represents the defendant, challenges the prosecution's case, protects the defendant's rights |
| **Defendant** | The person accused of a crime — has the right to remain silent, right to counsel, right to a fair trial |
| **Bailiff** | Maintains courtroom security and order, escorts defendants, announces the judge |
| **Court Clerk** | Manages case files, swears in witnesses, records proceedings |
| **Jury (if applicable)** | Listens to evidence and arguments, deliberates, delivers a verdict of guilty or not guilty |
| **Witnesses** | Provide testimony under oath — can be called by either side |
| **Victim / Complainant** | The person harmed — may testify as a witness |

---

## Types of Court Proceedings

Not every court appearance is a trial. Different proceedings serve different purposes.

| Proceeding | Purpose | What Happens |
|---|---|---|
| Arraignment | First court appearance after arrest | Charges read, defendant enters a plea (guilty, not guilty, no contest) |
| Bail hearing | Determine if defendant can be released before trial | Judge sets bail amount or denies bail based on flight risk and danger |
| Pre-trial conference | Manage case before trial | Discovery exchange, motions heard, plea negotiations discussed |
| Trial | Determine guilt or innocence | Full proceeding with evidence, witnesses, arguments, and verdict |
| Sentencing hearing | Determine punishment after conviction | Both sides argue for appropriate sentence, judge decides |
| Motion hearing | Resolve legal disputes | Motions to suppress evidence, dismiss charges, change venue, etc. |
| Appeal | Review a previous court decision | Appellant argues legal errors were made, appellate judge reviews |

## Trial Procedure — Step by Step

A full trial is the crown jewel of court RP. Follow this procedure for authenticity.

### 1. Jury Selection (Voir Dire)

Both sides question potential jurors to identify bias and select a fair panel.

> **Prosecutor:** "Juror Number 4, have you or anyone in your family ever been the victim of a violent crime?"
> **Juror:** "My brother was mugged last year in Strawberry."
> **Prosecutor:** "Do you feel that experience would affect your ability to be impartial in this case?"

Each side can challenge jurors:
- **For cause** — A specific, stated reason the juror cannot be fair (unlimited)
- **Peremptory** — Remove a juror without stating a reason (limited number)

### 2. Opening Statements

Each side tells the jury what they expect the evidence to show. Opening statements are not arguments — they are roadmaps.

> **Prosecutor:** "Ladies and gentlemen of the jury, the evidence will show that on the night of March 14th, the defendant walked into the Fleeca Bank on Great Ocean Highway, pointed a firearm at the teller, and demanded cash. Security footage, witness testimony, and physical evidence recovered from the defendant's vehicle will prove beyond a reasonable doubt that he committed armed robbery."

> **Defense Attorney:** "Members of the jury, my client was in the wrong place at the wrong time. The prosecution's case relies on grainy footage and a single eyewitness who was fifty feet away in poor lighting. When you see all the evidence, you will have reasonable doubt. And reasonable doubt means not guilty."

### 3. Prosecution's Case-in-Chief

The prosecution presents their evidence and witnesses first. They carry the burden of proof.

**Presenting evidence:**
> **Prosecutor:** "Your Honor, the People move to admit Exhibit A — security camera footage from the Fleeca Bank on the night in question."
> **Judge:** "Any objection from the defense?"
> **Defense:** "No objection, Your Honor."
> **Judge:** "Exhibit A is admitted."

**Examining witnesses (direct examination):**
> **Prosecutor:** "Officer Martinez, can you describe what you observed when you arrived at the Fleeca Bank on March 14th?"
> **Officer:** "When I arrived at approximately 10:47 PM, I observed the front door of the bank open. The teller, Ms. Johnson, was behind the counter visibly shaken. She pointed south down the highway and stated that the suspect had fled in a red four-door sedan."

### 4. Cross-Examination

After each prosecution witness, the defense gets to cross-examine — challenge their testimony, credibility, or observations.

> **Defense Attorney:** "Officer Martinez, you said you arrived at 10:47 PM. How long after the reported robbery was that?"
> **Officer:** "Approximately twelve minutes."
> **Defense Attorney:** "Twelve minutes. And in those twelve minutes, no one at the scene could provide a license plate number, correct?"
> **Officer:** "That's correct."
> **Defense Attorney:** "So the only description you had was a red four-door sedan — one of the most common vehicles in Los Santos?"
> **Officer:** "Yes."

### 5. Defense's Case

The defense may call their own witnesses and present evidence. The defendant may choose to testify but cannot be forced to.

> **Defense Attorney:** "The defense calls Mr. Ricardo Vasquez."
> **Clerk:** `/me raises the book.` "Place your left hand on the book and raise your right hand. Do you swear to tell the truth, the whole truth, and nothing but the truth?"
> **Vasquez:** "I do."
> **Defense Attorney:** "Mr. Vasquez, where was the defendant on the night of March 14th?"
> **Vasquez:** "He was at my house. We were watching the Sharks game. He didn't leave until after midnight."

### 6. Closing Arguments

Both sides summarize their case and argue for their desired verdict. This is where attorneys can be passionate and persuasive.

> **Prosecutor:** "The evidence is clear. The footage shows the defendant. The teller identified him. His clothing matched the description. The dye pack exploded in his car. This is not a coincidence — this is guilt. Find the defendant guilty."

> **Defense Attorney:** "The prosecution has shown you blurry footage, a nervous witness, and circumstantial evidence. That is not proof beyond a reasonable doubt. If there is even a shadow of uncertainty in your mind — and there should be — then you must return a verdict of not guilty. That is your duty."

### 7. Jury Deliberation

The jury retires to discuss the case privately. They must reach a unanimous verdict.

**During deliberation:**
- Jurors review evidence and testimony
- Discuss their interpretations and concerns
- Vote until unanimous agreement or declare a hung jury

### 8. Verdict

The jury returns to the courtroom and the foreperson delivers the verdict.

> **Judge:** "Has the jury reached a verdict?"
> **Foreperson:** "We have, Your Honor."
> **Judge:** "Please hand the verdict form to the bailiff."
> `/me reads the verdict form silently, then hands it to the clerk.`
> **Clerk:** "In the case of the State of San Andreas versus Marcus Cole, on the charge of armed robbery in the first degree, we the jury find the defendant... guilty."

## Sentencing

After a guilty verdict or guilty plea, the judge determines the sentence.

**Factors the judge considers:**
- Severity of the crime
- Defendant's criminal history
- Impact on the victim (victim impact statement)
- Mitigating factors (first offense, cooperation, remorse)
- Aggravating factors (use of a weapon, targeting vulnerable victims, gang enhancement)
- Prosecution and defense sentencing recommendations

| Sentence Type | When Used |
|---|---|
| Fine | Minor offenses, financial crimes |
| Probation | First offenses, low-level crimes, as an alternative to jail |
| Community service | Minor offenses, restorative justice |
| Jail time (short) | Misdemeanors, minor felonies |
| Prison time (long) | Serious felonies, violent crimes, repeat offenders |
| Life imprisonment | Murder, severe violent crimes |
| Suspended sentence | Sentence imposed but not served unless probation is violated |

> **Judge:** "Mr. Cole, you have been found guilty of armed robbery in the first degree. Taking into account the severity of the offense, the fact that a firearm was used, and your prior criminal record — I sentence you to 48 months in state prison, with credit for time served. You will be eligible for parole after 36 months. Court is adjourned."

## Bail Hearings

Bail determines whether a defendant waits for trial in jail or at home.

**Bail decision factors:**

| Factor | What the Judge Considers |
|---|---|
| Flight risk | Is the defendant likely to flee? Do they have ties to the community? |
| Danger to community | Would releasing them endanger the public? |
| Severity of charges | More serious charges = higher bail or no bail |
| Criminal history | Prior failures to appear, outstanding warrants |
| Employment and ties | Stable job, family, property = lower flight risk |

> **Judge:** "The defendant is charged with second-degree murder. Given the severity of the charge, the defendant's lack of stable employment, and the fact that he was found with a passport and $50,000 in cash, bail is denied. The defendant is remanded to custody pending trial."

## Warrants and Court Orders

Judges authorize warrants that empower law enforcement to act.

| Document | What It Authorizes |
|---|---|
| Search warrant | Police may search a specific location for specific evidence |
| Arrest warrant | Police may arrest a named individual |
| Bench warrant | Issued when someone fails to appear in court |
| Subpoena | Compels a person to appear in court or produce documents |
| Restraining order | Prohibits a person from contacting or approaching another |
| Wiretap order | Authorizes interception of communications (high legal standard) |

> **Detective:** "Your Honor, we're requesting a search warrant for 1542 Jamestown Street, Rancho. We have probable cause to believe narcotics and firearms related to the Ortega case are being stored at this location."
> **Judge:** `/me reviews the affidavit carefully, then signs the warrant.`
> "Warrant is granted. It is limited to the premises described — the residence at 1542 Jamestown Street. Execute it within 48 hours."

## Contempt of Court

The judge's ultimate tool for maintaining order. Contempt can be civil or criminal.

| Type | Cause | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| Direct contempt | Disruptive behavior in the courtroom | Immediate fine or jail |
| Indirect contempt | Violating a court order outside the courtroom | Hearing, then fine or jail |

> **Defendant:** *(stands up and shouts)* "This is garbage! You're all corrupt! I didn't do anything!"
> **Judge:** `/me strikes the gavel three times.`
> "Mr. Cole, you will sit down and be silent or I will hold you in contempt. One more outburst and you will spend the night in a holding cell. Bailiff, restrain the defendant if necessary."

## Appeals

A convicted defendant can appeal to a higher court if they believe legal errors were made during their trial.

**Grounds for appeal:**
- Improper admission or exclusion of evidence
- Jury misconduct
- Ineffective assistance of counsel
- Prosecutorial misconduct
- Incorrect jury instructions
- Insufficient evidence to support the verdict

**Appeals are not retrials.** The appellate court reviews the legal record — they do not hear new evidence or new witnesses. They determine whether the trial was conducted properly.

> **Appellate Judge:** "The defense argues that the trial court improperly admitted hearsay testimony from Witness #3 under an exception that does not apply to the facts of this case. After reviewing the trial record, this court agrees. The conviction is vacated and the case is remanded for a new trial."

---

## Final Thoughts

The judge is the most powerful person in the courtroom and bears the heaviest responsibility. Your rulings determine freedom, punishment, and precedent. You must be fair, impartial, and deeply familiar with the law as it exists on your server.

Court RP is slow by design. The tension comes from procedure — the careful questioning, the dramatic objection, the long pause before a verdict is read. Do not rush it. Let every moment breathe. The best trial scenes are the ones where the gallery holds its breath waiting for the foreperson to speak.

Put on the robe. Pick up the gavel. Deliver justice.
