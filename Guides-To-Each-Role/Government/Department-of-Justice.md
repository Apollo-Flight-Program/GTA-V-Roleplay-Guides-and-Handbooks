# Department of Justice — Federal Authority in San Andreas

The Department of Justice is the highest legal authority in the State of San Andreas. While local police handle street crime and local prosecutors take cases to trial, the DOJ operates at a level above — investigating corruption within government agencies, prosecuting federal crimes, managing organized crime task forces, and providing oversight that keeps the entire system honest. DOJ characters do not chase suspects down alleys. They build cases over weeks that dismantle empires.

This guide covers DOJ structure, jurisdiction, operations, and the unique RP opportunities that come with representing the full weight of federal authority.

---

## DOJ Structure

The Department of Justice is a hierarchical organization with clearly defined roles and responsibilities.

| Position | Role | Responsibilities |
|---|---|---|
| Attorney General (AG) | Head of the DOJ | Sets department policy, oversees all DOJ operations, advises the governor on legal matters |
| Deputy Attorney General | Second in command | Manages day-to-day operations, supervises division chiefs |
| US Attorney / State Attorney | Lead prosecutor for federal/state cases | Directs prosecutions, manages assistant attorneys, prioritizes cases |
| Assistant US Attorney (AUSA) | Line prosecutor | Handles individual cases, presents evidence in court, negotiates plea deals |
| Chief Investigator | Head of DOJ investigations | Manages investigative teams, coordinates with law enforcement agencies |
| DOJ Investigator / Special Agent | Field investigator | Conducts investigations, interviews witnesses, gathers evidence |
| Legal Counsel | Internal legal advisor | Advises the AG on legal questions, reviews policy for legality |
| Victim-Witness Coordinator | Victim support | Manages witness protection logistics, supports victims through the legal process |

**The AG answers to the Governor (or the people, if elected).** All other DOJ positions answer up the chain. The DOJ does not answer to local police chiefs, mayors, or city councils — it operates independently, which is both its power and the source of frequent political tension.

## Federal vs. State Jurisdiction

Understanding when the DOJ takes a case versus when it stays local is essential for realistic RP.

| Jurisdiction | Who Handles It | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Local / Municipal | LSPD, City Prosecutor | Street crime, misdemeanors, local ordinance violations |
| County | BCSO, County DA | Crimes in Blaine County, county-level felonies |
| State | State Police, State AG | Crimes crossing county lines, state-level statutes |
| Federal | DOJ, FBI equivalent | Organized crime, public corruption, terrorism, drug trafficking across state lines |

**When the DOJ takes over a case:**

| Trigger | Reason |
|---|---|
| Organized crime operations | Criminal enterprises operating across jurisdictions |
| Public corruption | Elected officials, law enforcement, or government employees abusing their positions |
| Civil rights violations | Law enforcement or government agents violating constitutional rights |
| Large-scale drug trafficking | Operations that cross county or state boundaries |
| Terrorism or domestic extremism | Threats to public safety at a scale beyond local capability |
| RICO cases | Racketeering and organized criminal enterprises |
| Cases involving federal property | Crimes committed at federal buildings, military bases, etc. |

> **AUSA:** "Detective, I appreciate the work your team has done on this case. But what started as a local drug bust in Davis is now connected to a supply network running through Sandy Shores, Paleto Bay, and across the state line. This is federal now. We'll take the lead — but I want your people on the task force."

## Oversight of Law Enforcement

One of the DOJ's most important — and most politically charged — functions is overseeing law enforcement agencies.

**Types of oversight:**

| Oversight Action | What It Involves |
|---|---|
| Pattern-or-practice investigation | Examining whether a department has a systemic pattern of civil rights violations |
| Individual misconduct investigation | Investigating specific officers or officials for criminal behavior |
| Use-of-force review | Analyzing whether a department's use-of-force policies and practices are lawful |
| Consent decree | A court-enforced agreement requiring a department to implement reforms |
| Audit | Financial or operational review of a department's practices |

**RP scenario — DOJ oversight investigation:**

> **AG:** "Chief Morrison, the DOJ has received multiple complaints alleging excessive use of force by officers in the South Los Santos division. I'm directing my investigators to conduct a pattern-or-practice review of the division. Your department will provide full access to use-of-force reports, body camera footage, and internal affairs files for the past twelve months."
> **Chief Morrison:** "Attorney General, my department cooperates with all lawful requests. But I want to be clear — I stand behind my officers."
> **AG:** "Your cooperation is noted and appreciated. Let the evidence speak for itself."

This creates multi-week RP involving DOJ investigators interviewing officers, reviewing footage, documenting incidents, and eventually issuing findings that could reshape the entire police department.

## Corruption Investigations

Corruption cases are the DOJ's signature work — and some of the most complex, rewarding RP on any server.

**Types of corruption the DOJ investigates:**

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Bribery | Officials accepting money or favors in exchange for official actions |
| Embezzlement | Government employees stealing public funds |
| Extortion | Officials using their position to coerce money or compliance |
| Fraud | Falsifying government documents, procurement fraud, insurance fraud |
| Obstruction of justice | Destroying evidence, intimidating witnesses, interfering with investigations |
| Abuse of authority | Using official power for personal gain or to harm individuals without cause |

**Building a corruption case:**

| Phase | Activities |
|---|---|
| 1. Intelligence gathering | Receive tips, analyze financial records, identify suspicious patterns |
| 2. Preliminary investigation | Confirm the tip has merit, identify subjects and potential witnesses |
| 3. Full investigation | Surveillance, financial forensics, witness interviews, undercover operations |
| 4. Grand jury | Present evidence to a grand jury to obtain indictments |
| 5. Arrest and prosecution | Execute arrest warrants, prosecute in court |
| 6. Post-conviction | Sentencing, asset forfeiture, policy recommendations to prevent recurrence |

> `/me opens the file labeled "Operation Clean House" and spreads the documents across the conference table.`
> "We've been tracking Councilmember Reeves for three months. Financial records show deposits totaling $85,000 from shell companies linked to the Ortega organization — coinciding exactly with three zoning votes that benefited Ortega-connected properties. We have enough for the grand jury."

## Grand Jury Proceedings

The grand jury is a powerful investigative tool. Unlike a trial jury, the grand jury does not determine guilt — it determines whether there is enough evidence to charge someone.

**How grand jury proceedings work:**

| Aspect | Details |
|---|---|
| **Composition** | 12–23 citizens (in RP, often 6–12 for practicality) |
| **Secrecy** | Proceedings are sealed — no public access, no defense attorney present |
| **Burden of proof** | Probable cause — much lower than "beyond a reasonable doubt" |
| **Who presents** | Only the prosecution presents evidence and calls witnesses |
| **Outcome** | True bill (indictment) or no bill (charges not supported) |
| **Subpoena power** | Grand jury can compel testimony and document production |

> **AUSA:** "Members of the grand jury, the evidence will show that Officer Daniel Reeves of the LSPD accepted bribes on no fewer than four occasions from a known organized crime figure in exchange for tipping off the organization about upcoming raids. We will present bank records, surveillance photographs, and testimony from a cooperating witness inside the organization."

**Grand jury RP creates tension because:**
- The target does not know they are being investigated until indictment
- Witnesses who testify may be in danger
- The secrecy requirement means no one can discuss what happens inside the grand jury room
- An indictment from a grand jury carries enormous weight — it means the state is coming for you

## RICO Cases

RICO (Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations Act) is the DOJ's weapon against organized crime. A RICO case does not just target a single crime — it targets the entire organization.

**What RICO requires:**
- An **enterprise** — a group of people operating together (gang, mafia, corrupt government ring)
- A **pattern of racketeering activity** — at least two related predicate crimes within a defined period
- A connection between the enterprise and the racketeering activity

**Predicate crimes (examples):**

| Crime Category | Specific Offenses |
|---|---|
| Violence | Murder, kidnapping, assault, arson |
| Financial | Bribery, extortion, money laundering, fraud, embezzlement |
| Drug-related | Drug trafficking, manufacturing, distribution |
| Other | Witness tampering, obstruction of justice, gambling operations |

**Why RICO is devastating:**
- Everyone in the organization can be charged for the crimes of any member
- The leader who never pulled a trigger can be convicted of murder
- Asset forfeiture allows the DOJ to seize everything the organization owns
- Sentences are dramatically enhanced — decades in prison

> **AG:** "Ladies and gentlemen, this is not a case about one crime. This is a case about an organization — the Vargas Cartel — that has operated in San Andreas for over a year, committing murder, trafficking narcotics, laundering money, and corrupting public officials. RICO allows us to hold every member of that organization accountable — from the street-level dealers to the man at the top. Today, we begin dismantling it."

**RICO RP is endgame content.** It requires weeks or months of investigation, cooperation between multiple agencies, and a trial that involves dozens of pieces of evidence and multiple defendants. It is the most complex legal RP possible — and the most rewarding.

## Witness Protection

When witnesses against powerful organizations cooperate with the DOJ, they become targets. Witness protection keeps them alive long enough to testify.

**Witness protection elements:**

| Element | What It Involves |
|---|---|
| Relocation | Moving the witness to a new location — different neighborhood, different city |
| New identity | Providing documents under a new name (if server mechanics allow) |
| Security detail | DOJ investigators or marshals assigned to protect the witness |
| Communication restrictions | Limiting who the witness can contact to prevent leaks |
| Court preparation | Preparing the witness for testimony while keeping them safe |

> **DOJ Investigator:** `/me meets the witness at the safe house in Paleto Bay, checks the windows, and locks the door.`
> "You don't leave this house without me. You don't call anyone — no friends, no family, no one from the old neighborhood. When you testify next week, we'll transport you under escort. After the trial, we'll set you up somewhere new. But right now, your only job is to stay alive."

**Witness protection creates RP for:**
- The witness — living in hiding, fear, isolation, moral conflict about cooperating
- The DOJ — managing security, preventing leaks, preparing testimony
- The criminal organization — trying to find and silence the witness
- Law enforcement — providing security, investigating threats

## Death Penalty Cases

Capital cases are the most serious proceedings in the legal system. The DOJ must decide whether to seek the death penalty — a decision with massive RP implications.

**When the death penalty may apply:**
- First-degree murder with special circumstances (multiple victims, murder of a law enforcement officer, murder during commission of another felony)
- Terrorism
- Serial killings

**The decision process:**

| Step | Who Decides |
|---|---|
| Initial charge | AUSA recommends, AG reviews |
| Death penalty review | AG convenes a committee to evaluate whether to seek death |
| Factors considered | Severity of crime, defendant's history, victim impact, likelihood of conviction, public interest |
| Final decision | AG makes the call |

> **AG:** "I've reviewed the committee's recommendation on the Martinez case. Five counts of first-degree murder, committed during an armed robbery, with evidence of premeditation. The committee is split. But after considering the severity of the crimes and the impact on the victims' families, I am authorizing the death penalty to be sought in this case."

**OOC note:** Death penalty cases should be handled with extreme care. They involve permadeath implications for the defendant's character and should only proceed with full OOC consent and server administration coordination.

## Policy Directives

The AG can issue policy directives that affect how law is enforced across the state.

**Examples of AG directives:**

| Directive | Effect |
|---|---|
| Mandatory minimum sentencing guidelines | Standardize sentences for specific crimes |
| Prosecution priorities | Direct AUSAs to prioritize certain case types (e.g., gun crimes, drug trafficking) |
| Use-of-force guidelines | Establish standards for acceptable law enforcement conduct |
| Civil asset forfeiture policy | Set rules for when and how assets can be seized |
| Interdepartmental cooperation protocols | Define how agencies share information and coordinate operations |
| Whistleblower protections | Establish protections for government employees who report misconduct |

> **AG Memo to All DOJ Staff:**
> "Effective immediately, all plea negotiations involving charges of corruption against public officials must be approved by my office before any offer is extended. These cases represent a breach of public trust and will be treated with the maximum severity the law allows. No exceptions."

## Common DOJ RP Scenarios

**The dirty cop:**
> A detective in the LSPD has been leaking information to a drug organization. The DOJ receives a tip from an anonymous source. You open an investigation — but the detective has allies in the department, and anyone you interview might warn him. How do you build a case without tipping off the target?

**The political scandal:**
> The mayor's campaign accepted illegal donations from a construction company that subsequently won a no-bid city contract. The AG must decide whether to investigate — knowing that the mayor appointed them to the position. Independence versus political survival.

**The reluctant witness:**
> A gang member is willing to testify against the shot-caller in a RICO case, but only if their family is protected. The family lives in Davis, deep in the gang's territory. The DOJ has to relocate the entire family before the witness will cooperate. One leak and everyone is dead.

**The jurisdictional fight:**
> The LSPD arrests a suspect in a case the DOJ has been building for months. The detective wants to prosecute locally. The AUSA wants to take the case federal. The suspect's defense attorney plays them against each other. Who blinks first?

## Getting Started in DOJ RP

**Entry points:**
- **AUSA (Assistant US Attorney)** — The most common starting position. Handle cases, build your reputation, earn trust from the AG.
- **DOJ Investigator** — The field arm of the DOJ. Investigate, interview, surveil, and build cases for the attorneys to prosecute.
- **Legal intern / Clerk** — Work under an AUSA or the AG, learn the system, handle paperwork, and observe proceedings.
- **Transfer from local prosecution** — A city prosecutor who catches the attention of the DOJ and gets recruited to the federal level.

The DOJ is not a starting role. It requires understanding of the legal system, courtroom experience, and the patience to build cases over weeks rather than days. Earn your way in.

---

## Final Thoughts

The Department of Justice is the final line of defense against the abuse of power. When police officers go corrupt, the DOJ investigates. When politicians take bribes, the DOJ prosecutes. When criminal organizations grow too powerful for local law enforcement, the DOJ brings RICO. When witnesses are in danger, the DOJ protects them.

It is slow, methodical, and often invisible work. You will spend more time reading documents and interviewing witnesses than you will in a courtroom. But when the indictment drops — when months of investigation culminate in handcuffs on a powerful person who thought they were untouchable — there is nothing in RP that matches that moment.

Build the case. Follow the evidence. Let justice speak.
