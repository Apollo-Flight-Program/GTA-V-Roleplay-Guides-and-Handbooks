# Starting a Business — Entrepreneurship Roleplay

Every empire in Los Santos started with someone deciding they were done working for other people. Starting a business in GTA V RP is one of the most rewarding long-term commitments you can make — it gives your character purpose, generates organic interactions with dozens of other players, and anchors you in the server's economy. This guide covers everything from your first business idea to managing a thriving operation.

---

## Why Business RP Matters

A business creates a world that moves without you having to chase action. Customers come to you. Employees depend on you. Competitors watch you. Government officials inspect you. Criminals case you. A single business owner touches every faction on the server simply by opening their doors each day.

Business RP also creates stakes. Your character has something to lose — their livelihood, their reputation, their investment. That vulnerability makes every interaction more meaningful than it would be for a character with nothing on the line.

---

## Choosing Your Business Type

Pick something your character would actually pursue. Their background, skills, and personality should drive this decision.

| Business Type | Startup Cost | Difficulty | Social Interaction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Food truck | $30,000 – $75,000 | Low | High — mobile, meets everyone |
| Barbershop / salon | $50,000 – $150,000 | Low | High — regular clientele |
| Mechanic shop | $100,000 – $300,000 | Medium | High — everyone drives |
| Restaurant / bar | $150,000 – $500,000 | Medium–High | Very High — social hub |
| Car dealership | $300,000 – $1,000,000 | High | Medium — big-ticket clients |
| Nightclub | $500,000 – $2,000,000 | High | Very High — event-driven |
| Import/export | $200,000 – $800,000 | High | Low–Medium — B2B focus |
| Law firm | $100,000 – $300,000 | Medium | Medium — case-based |
| Real estate agency | $75,000 – $200,000 | Medium | High — always networking |

Your character's backstory needs to justify the choice. A former line cook opening a restaurant makes sense. A character with no financial background suddenly running an investment firm does not — unless you roleplay the learning curve.

## Business Registration

Starting legally means going through the city's bureaucratic process. This is roleplay, not a chore — lean into it.

**Step 1 — Draft a business plan**
Write a basic plan outlining what your business does, where it will operate, and how it will make money. Some servers require this for approval. Even if yours does not, having a plan shows you have thought it through.

**Step 2 — Register with the city**
Visit City Hall in Pillbox Hill or contact the Department of Commerce. File your business name, type, ownership, and address. Pay the registration fee. A government employee processes your paperwork — interact with them, answer their questions, and be patient with the process.

**Step 3 — Obtain required permits**
Depending on your business type, you may need:
- General business license
- Health and safety permit (food service)
- Liquor license (bars, clubs, restaurants)
- Firearms dealer license
- Building occupancy permit
- Signage permit

**Step 4 — Secure a location**
Buy or lease a commercial property. Work with a realtor or contact the property owner directly. Negotiate the terms. Factor in foot traffic, parking, visibility, and proximity to your target customers.

**Step 5 — Open for business**
Set a date, tell people, and open your doors. The first day is always chaotic — embrace it.

| Vespucci Beach / Del Perro | High | Medium–High | Tourists, casual crowds, beach culture |
| Burton / Rockford Hills | Medium | High | Wealthy clientele, upscale tastes |
| La Mesa / El Burro Heights | Medium | Low | Working class, budget-conscious |

| Strawberry / Davis | Medium | Low | Diverse community, high energy |

A high-end cocktail bar in Sandy Shores will struggle. A bait shop in Rockford Hills makes no sense. Let the location match the business and the business match the location.

## Hiring Employees

You cannot run everything alone — and you should not want to. Employees create RP for multiple people and make your business feel alive.

### The Hiring Process

1. Post a job listing — social media, word of mouth, flyers in-game
2. Accept applications — ask for name, experience, availability
3. Conduct interviews — meet candidates in person, ask about their character's background
4. Trial shift — let them work one session before committing
5. Onboard — explain expectations, pay, schedule, and shop rules

### Managing Staff

**Pay structure options:**

| Model | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Hourly wage | Fixed amount per hour worked | Entry-level, shift work |
| Salary | Set amount per week/pay period | Managers, full-time staff |
| Commission | Percentage of sales they generate | Sales roles, dealerships |
| Tips + base | Low base wage supplemented by tips | Restaurants, bars, service |
| Profit sharing | Percentage of business profits | Senior staff, partners |

**Common staff issues and how to handle them:**
- Employee shows up late repeatedly — verbal warning, then written, then termination
- Two employees have a personal conflict — mediate privately, keep it professional
- Someone is stealing from the register — investigate quietly, confront with evidence
- A great employee wants a raise — negotiate, consider what losing them would cost
- Seasonal slowdown — cut hours fairly or find creative ways to stay busy

## Managing Finances

Money is the heartbeat of your business. Track it or bleed out.

**Income tracking:**
- Log every transaction, even small ones
- Separate business funds from personal funds
- Set aside money for taxes, maintenance, and emergencies
- Review income weekly to spot trends

**Expense categories:**

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| Rent / mortgage | Monthly property payment |
| Payroll | Employee wages and benefits |
| Inventory / supplies | Product stock, raw materials, consumables |
| Utilities | Power, water, phone, internet |
| Maintenance | Repairs, cleaning, equipment upkeep |
| Marketing | Flyers, ads, events, sponsorships |
| Taxes | Business tax, sales tax, payroll tax |
| Insurance | Property, liability, workers' comp |
| Miscellaneous | Unexpected costs, legal fees, fines |

**Pricing your products or services:**
Charge enough to cover costs and turn a profit, but not so much that customers go elsewhere. Research what competitors charge. Offer value — better service, better quality, better experience — to justify higher prices if needed.

## Marketing In-Character

Advertising in RP is not about menus and UI — it is about word of mouth, presence, and reputation.

**Effective IC marketing:**
- Host a grand opening event with food, music, and giveaways
- Offer first-time customer discounts
- Build relationships at community events — hand out business cards
- Partner with complementary businesses (a mechanic and a tow company, a bar and a DJ)
- Use in-game social media or phone apps to post updates
- Sponsor local events — car meets, charity drives, community gatherings
- Create loyalty programs — "Buy 5, get the 6th free"

**Word of mouth is king.** One customer who has an excellent experience tells three friends. One customer who has a terrible experience tells everyone. Your reputation is your most valuable marketing asset.

## Tax and Government Compliance

Taxes are not exciting, but they create RP with government characters and add realism to your operation.

**What to expect:**
- Business income tax — a percentage of profits paid quarterly or monthly
- Sales tax — collected from customers and remitted to the city
- Payroll tax — paid on employee wages
- Property tax — if you own the building

**Government inspections:**
- Health inspectors visit food businesses to check cleanliness, food storage, and preparation standards
- Fire marshals check occupancy limits, exit access, and fire suppression systems
- Building inspectors verify structural safety and code compliance
- Tax auditors review your books for accuracy

Failing an inspection creates RP. You get cited, fined, or shut down temporarily. Fix the violations and schedule a re-inspection. Fight the fine in court if you believe it is unjust. These bureaucratic conflicts add texture to your business story.

## Building a Brand

A brand is what people say about you when you are not around.

**Brand elements:**
- **Name** — memorable, fits the vibe, easy to say
- **Reputation** — are you the best, the cheapest, the friendliest, the shadiest?
- **Consistency** — deliver the same quality every time
- **Visual identity** — if your server supports custom signage or uniforms, use them
- **Story** — why does this business exist? What is the owner's vision?

A taco truck called "Tio Benny's" run by a character who learned to cook from his grandfather in Mexico has a brand built into its story. A generic "Los Santos Food" has nothing. The name, the backstory, and the way you treat customers all build brand identity.

## Customer Relations

Customers are not transactions. They are characters with their own stories, and how you interact with them defines your business.

**Handling complaints:**
> Customer: "This food was cold when it got to my table. I've been waiting thirty minutes."
> Owner: "I apologize for the wait. Let me get you a fresh plate right away, on the house. We got slammed tonight, but that's no excuse."

Do not get defensive. Acknowledge the issue, fix it, and move on. A complaint handled well earns more loyalty than a perfect experience.

**Building regulars:**
- Remember their name and their usual order
- Ask about their day, their family, their work
- Give them small perks — a free drink, priority seating, a heads-up about upcoming events
- Make them feel like they belong to your business, not just that they buy from it

---

## Dealing with Competition

Competition is healthy and creates RP for everyone.

**Ways to compete:**
- Differentiate your offering — specialize where they generalize
- Outwork them — be open more hours, be more consistent, be more present
- Collaborate — partner on events, refer customers you cannot serve, build the whole industry up
- Undercut on price — risky, but effective short-term
- Innovate — introduce new products, services, or experiences they do not offer

**What not to do:**
- Do not trash-talk competitors to customers (bad look, unprofessional)
- Do not sabotage their business (unless that is your character arc — and be prepared for consequences)
- Do not copy them exactly — find your own lane

## Supply Chains

Where do your products come from? Roleplay the sourcing.

- A restaurant buys produce from a farmer in Grapeseed and meat from a supplier at the docks
- A car dealership purchases inventory from wholesalers, auctions, or trade-ins
- A clothing store orders stock from offshore manufacturers and waits for shipments
- A bar sources liquor from a licensed distributor and craft beer from a local brewer

Supply chain issues create natural RP problems. Your supplier is late. The shipment is damaged. The price went up. You need to find a new vendor. Each of these is a scene waiting to happen.

## Common Mistakes

**Opening too big.** Start small. A food truck before a restaurant. A pop-up shop before a storefront. Build your customer base and your skills before scaling up.

**No character motivation.** "I want to make money" is not a story. Why does your character want this specific business? What drives them? What are they afraid of losing?

**Ignoring employees.** Your staff is not background decoration. Give them tasks, autonomy, and story involvement. A business where only the owner does anything interesting is a one-person show, not an enterprise.

**Inconsistency.** The business that is open three hours one week and not at all the next dies fast. Set a schedule and stick to it. Players will learn when you are open and plan their RP around it.

**Refusing to fail.** Businesses struggle. They have slow days, bad reviews, financial problems, and staffing crises. Embrace these setbacks — they are the most interesting part of business RP.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The grand opening:**
> You have spent weeks preparing. The signage is up, the staff is trained, and you have been telling everyone in the city about tonight. The first customer walks through the door. Your bartender drops a glass. The cook burns the first order. It is chaos — and it is perfect. By the end of the night, you are exhausted, your register is full, and your business is officially real.

**The tax audit:**
> A government auditor shows up unannounced. They want to see your books. Your records are a mess — some transactions were cash, some were never logged. The auditor gives you two weeks to get it together or face fines. Now you need an accountant.

**The hostile takeover:**
> A wealthy character offers to buy your business. The offer is generous but the intention is to shut you down — you are competition for their own venture. Do you sell? Do you fight? Do you negotiate a deal that keeps you involved?

**The loyal employee:**
> Your best employee has been with you since day one. They ask for a raise you cannot afford. Do you find the money, offer them a partnership stake, or risk losing them to the competition?

---

## Final Thoughts

A business is not a mechanic for making money. It is a stage. Every customer who walks through the door is a scene partner. Every employee is a supporting character. Every slow Tuesday and every packed Friday night is part of the story.

Build something your character cares about. Then let the city test whether they can keep it.
