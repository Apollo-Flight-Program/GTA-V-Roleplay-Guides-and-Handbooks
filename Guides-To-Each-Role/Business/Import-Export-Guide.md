# Import/Export Guide — Trade and Logistics Roleplay

The Port of Los Santos never sleeps. Cargo ships dock at Terminal 4, containers get loaded onto flatbeds, and goods flow into the city — some documented, some not. Import/export RP puts your character at the center of the supply chain, where global commerce meets local business and the line between legal trade and smuggling gets blurred by opportunity. This guide covers how to build and run a trade operation from the ground up.

---

## Why Import/Export RP Matters

Most businesses in the city depend on goods that come from somewhere else. Restaurants need imported ingredients. Dealerships need vehicles shipped in from manufacturers. Clothing stores need product from overseas suppliers. Your character is the one who makes those deliveries happen — or who moves things the city was never supposed to see.

Import/export RP creates connections across the entire server. You interact with business owners who need product, government officials who regulate trade, dock workers who move freight, truckers who haul cargo, and — depending on your operation — criminals who want to use your shipping lanes for their own purposes. No other business role touches as many factions with as little visibility.

## Types of Operations

Your operation's identity depends on what you move and how you move it.

| Operation Type | Goods | Legality | Profit Margin | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Licensed importer | Consumer goods, food, electronics, auto parts | Legal | Low–Medium | Low |
| Vehicle importer | Cars, motorcycles, boats | Legal | Medium–High | Low–Medium |
| Specialty importer | Luxury items, art, antiques, rare goods | Legal | High | Medium |
| Export broker | Local products shipped to outside contacts | Legal | Medium | Low |
| Gray market trader | Unmarked goods, knockoffs, no-questions-asked cargo | Semi-legal | Medium–High | Medium–High |
| Smuggler | Drugs, weapons, stolen goods, contraband | Illegal | Very High | Very High |

Most operators start legal and face the temptation of the gray market as profits plateau. That moral slide is one of the best character arcs in business RP.

## Setting Up Your Operation

### Legal Requirements

To operate a legitimate import/export business in Los Santos:

1. **Business registration** — register with the Department of Commerce at City Hall
2. **Trade license** — separate from a general business license, specifically for import/export activity
3. **Customs broker certification** — required to clear goods through port customs
4. **Warehouse permit** — if storing goods in a commercial warehouse
5. **Tax registration** — import duties and sales tax collection
6. **Insurance** — cargo insurance for goods in transit, liability insurance for operations

Each permit requires interaction with a government player or NPC — filing paperwork, paying fees, answering questions about your operation. Bureaucracy is slow and frustrating. Roleplay it.

### Securing a Warehouse

Your warehouse is your base of operations.

| Location | Advantages | Disadvantages |
|---|---|---|
| Elysian Island / Terminal | Close to the port, fast turnaround | Industrial, limited security, expensive |
| La Mesa | Central, affordable, good road access | Higher crime area, potential theft |
| El Burro Heights | Near the docks, moderate rent | Rough neighborhood, limited parking |
| Cypress Flats | Industrial zone, large available spaces | Isolated, minimal foot traffic |
| Paleto Bay | Cheap, remote, low oversight | Far from the port, longer transport times |
| Route 68 / Grand Senora | Affordable, open space, discreet | Very remote, suspicious if high traffic |

**Warehouse setup:**
- Loading dock for truck access
- Shelved or organized storage areas for inventory
- Office space for paperwork and meetings
- Security — cameras, locks, alarm system (or guard staff)
- Climate control for perishable or sensitive goods

## Customs Procedures

Every legal import passes through customs. This is where your paperwork meets reality.

### The Import Process

**Step 1 — Place the order**
Contact your overseas supplier. Agree on the goods, quantity, price, and shipping timeline. Confirm the shipping method — container ship, air freight, or overland.

**Step 2 — Documentation**
Prepare the following paperwork:
- Bill of lading — official record of the shipment
- Commercial invoice — itemized list of goods and their declared value
- Packing list — detailed breakdown of contents by container or crate
- Import license — proof of your authorization to import these goods
- Certificate of origin — where the goods were manufactured

**Step 3 — Arrival at port**
The shipment arrives at the Port of Los Santos (Elysian Island or Terminal). You or your customs broker goes to the port authority office to present documentation.

**Step 4 — Customs inspection**
A customs officer reviews your paperwork and may inspect the cargo physically. They check for:
- Accuracy of declared goods vs. actual contents
- Prohibited or restricted items
- Correct valuation (to prevent duty evasion)
- Proper labeling and safety compliance

**Step 5 — Duty payment**
Pay the assessed import duties and taxes. Rates vary by goods category:

| Goods Category | Duty Rate |
|---|---|
| Consumer electronics | 5–10% |
| Vehicle parts | 8–15% |
| Food and beverages | 10–20% |
| Luxury goods (art, jewelry, designer items) | 15–25% |
| Industrial equipment | 3–8% |
| Textiles and clothing | 10–18% |

**Step 6 — Release and transport**
Once cleared, arrange transport from the port to your warehouse. Hire a trucking company, use your own vehicles, or work with dock workers to load and deliver.

### Example Customs Interaction

> Customs Officer: "Morning. Bill of lading for container LSPT-44219?"
> Importer: `/me hands over the folder with the bill of lading, commercial invoice, and packing list.` "Here you go. Twenty pallets of auto parts from overseas — brake pads, rotors, and suspension components. All itemized on page three."
> Customs Officer: `/me flips through the documents, cross-referencing the packing list with the bill of lading.` "Declared value of sixty-two thousand?"
> Importer: "That's correct. All invoices from the manufacturer are attached."
> Customs Officer: "Alright. I'm going to need to inspect four pallets at random. Pull up to Bay 7 and we'll have a look."

## Shipping Logistics

Moving goods is the core of your daily operation.

### Transport Methods

| Method | Speed | Cost | Capacity | Best For |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Container ship | Slow (days–weeks) | Low | Very High | Bulk goods, vehicles, non-urgent cargo |
| Air freight | Fast (hours–1 day) | High | Low | Perishable, urgent, high-value small items |
| Overland trucking | Medium | Medium | Medium | Local distribution, warehouse-to-client |
| Rail | Medium | Low–Medium | High | Heavy goods, long-distance inland |
| Personal delivery | Fast | Varies | Very Low | Sensitive items, VIP clients, discreet jobs |

### Fleet Management

If you operate your own delivery vehicles:

| Vehicle | Use Case | Capacity |
|---|---|---|
| MTL Pounder | Large commercial deliveries | 8–10 pallets |
| Vapid Speedo Custom | Medium deliveries, city routes | 4–6 pallets |
| MTL Flatbed | Oversized cargo, vehicle transport | 1–2 vehicles or heavy equipment |
| Vapid Bobcat XL | Small deliveries, rural routes | 2–3 pallets |
| Dinghy / Tug | Port-side marine transport | Varies |
| Cargobob (if available) | Emergency or inaccessible deliveries | 1 vehicle or heavy cargo |

Maintain your fleet. A broken-down delivery truck on the highway delays the entire supply chain and creates an RP scene — calling a tow truck, contacting the client about the delay, rescheduling delivery.

## Trade Partnerships

Your business depends on relationships — suppliers, clients, and intermediaries.

### Building Supplier Relationships

- Start with one reliable supplier and expand gradually
- Negotiate better rates as your volume increases
- Pay on time, every time — late payments destroy trust
- Visit suppliers when possible (if your server supports travel RP or phone/email RP)
- Diversify — never depend on a single source for critical goods

### Client Management

| Client Type | Needs | Relationship Approach |
|---|---|---|
| Restaurant owner | Regular food and beverage shipments | Reliable schedule, consistent quality |
| Car dealership | Vehicle and parts imports | Large orders, competitive pricing |
| Retail shop | Consumer goods, clothing, electronics | Variety, trend awareness, fast turnaround |
| Construction company | Building materials, equipment | Bulk volume, heavy logistics |
| Private collector | Art, antiques, rare items | Discretion, white-glove handling |
| Criminal organization | Contraband, weapons, substances | Trust, secrecy, deniability |

Each client relationship generates recurring RP. Monthly orders, supply issues, pricing renegotiation, new product requests — these are not one-time transactions but ongoing partnerships.

## The Gray Market and Smuggling

This is where the money multiplies — and so does the danger.

### Why Go Gray

Legal importing has thin margins. After duties, taxes, transport, and overhead, a $60,000 shipment might net you $8,000. A single container of undeclared goods can net ten times that. The temptation is constant, and many legitimate operators eventually face the choice.

### How Smuggling Works in RP

**Common smuggling methods:**
- Hidden compartments in legitimate cargo containers
- Falsified documentation — declaring high-value goods as low-value to reduce duties
- Off-schedule port access — moving cargo outside normal operating hours
- Beach landings — using boats to bypass the port entirely (Chumash, Paleto Cove, Elysian coast)
- Overland routes — moving goods through Blaine County to avoid port customs altogether
- Corrupted officials — paying a customs officer to look the other way

**Risks:**
- Customs inspection reveals hidden goods — arrest, seizure, charges
- A partner talks to the police — your operation is compromised
- Rival smugglers intercept your shipment — loss of goods and potential violence
- Government audit of your financials reveals unexplained income
- Undercover law enforcement posing as a buyer or supplier

### The Moral Slide

The best import/export RP follows the arc of a legitimate business owner who gradually crosses lines:

1. Start fully legal — pay every duty, file every form
2. A client asks you to "not declare" one small item — you do it as a favor
3. A supplier offers you a side deal on unmarked goods at half the cost
4. You make more on one gray shipment than a month of legal work
5. Now you are maintaining two sets of books and checking over your shoulder

This gradual corruption creates deeply compelling character development.

## Government Inspections

Government characters will interact with your operation regularly.

### Types of Inspections

| Inspector | What They Check | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| Customs officer | Cargo contents vs. documentation | Every import |
| Tax auditor | Financial records, declared income, duty payments | Quarterly or random |
| Health inspector | Food imports — storage temperature, expiration, contamination | Per shipment (food) |
| Fire marshal | Warehouse safety — exits, fire suppression, storage compliance | Annual or after incidents |
| Law enforcement | Suspicious activity, anonymous tips, warrant-based searches | As needed |

### Surviving an Audit

**If your books are clean:**
- Cooperate fully — open records, answer questions, provide documentation
- Be professional and polite — auditors remember who made their job difficult
- Follow up afterward to address any minor findings

**If your books are not clean:**
- You have a problem. A good accountant (another player) can help you restructure records before the audit
- Stall for time if possible — request a rescheduled date, claim your accountant is unavailable
- Do not lie to an auditor's face — if caught, the penalties escalate dramatically
- Consider coming clean and negotiating a reduced penalty — or prepare for the legal fallout

## Profit and Loss

Understand your margins or drown in expenses.

**Revenue calculation for a typical legal shipment:**

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| Goods purchased (wholesale) | $60,000 |
| Shipping and transport | $8,000 |
| Import duties (12%) | $7,200 |
| Warehouse costs (monthly share) | $2,000 |
| Insurance | $1,500 |
| **Total cost** | **$78,700** |
| Sale price to clients | $92,000 |
| **Net profit** | **$13,300** |
| **Margin** | **~14%** |

Compare this to a smuggled shipment where duties and documentation costs are eliminated — the margin jumps to 30–40%. That gap is the temptation.

**Managing cash flow:**
- Invoice clients promptly and follow up on late payments
- Maintain a cash reserve for unexpected costs (damaged cargo, delayed shipments, fines)
- Reinvest profits into scaling — bigger warehouse, more vehicles, additional shipping lanes
- Keep personal and business finances strictly separate

## Port Operations

The Port of Los Santos is your workplace. Know it intimately.

**Key port areas:**
- **Terminal 4 (Elysian Island)** — main commercial shipping terminal, container yards
- **Customs office** — where paperwork is filed and inspections are scheduled
- **Loading docks** — where containers are loaded onto trucks for transport
- **Dry dock** — ship maintenance and repair area
- **Port authority office** — administrative center for permits and scheduling
- **Warehouse row** — rental storage facilities near the terminal

**Port etiquette:**
- Arrive on time for scheduled pickups — dock slots are limited
- Have your paperwork ready before you get to the customs window
- Do not loiter in restricted areas without authorization
- Tip the dock workers (RP gesture) — they will prioritize your loads
- Respect the port security protocols — they take it seriously

## International Contacts

Your suppliers and trade partners exist beyond Los Santos. Roleplay these relationships even if the other party is not a player.

**Maintaining overseas contacts:**
- Regular phone calls or encrypted messages to check on shipments
- Negotiating new deals when market prices shift
- Handling disputes when shipments arrive damaged or short
- Building loyalty — send holiday gifts, honor exclusive agreements, pay premium prices for premium reliability
- Expanding your network by asking existing contacts for introductions

**Example phone RP:**
> `/me dials the international number and leans back in the office chair, waiting for the connection.`
> "Marco? It's Alejandro from Los Santos. Listen, that last shipment of textiles — we're twelve bolts short and three rolls had water damage. I need a credit or a replacement shipment by Friday... No, I understand the port in Vice City was backed up. But my client is breathing down my neck. What can you do for me?"

These one-sided phone conversations, overhead by employees or visitors, add texture and believability to your operation.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The delayed shipment:**
> Your biggest client — a restaurant chain — is expecting 40 cases of imported olive oil. The ship is stuck outside the port due to a dock workers' strike. Your client calls every four hours asking for an update. You scramble to find an alternative source, negotiate with a competitor who has stock, and deliver a partial order at cost just to keep the relationship alive.

**The customs flag:**
> A random inspection catches something you did not expect — a supplier packed undeclared items into your container without telling you. Customs holds the entire shipment. You face questions you do not have answers to. Are you an innocent victim of a shady supplier, or did you know? How you handle the next 24 hours determines whether you walk away clean or face charges.

**The smuggling offer:**
> A well-dressed stranger visits your warehouse and makes a proposal: move one unmarked crate per month through your legitimate shipping channel. No questions. $15,000 per crate. You do not know what is inside. You do not want to know. But $15,000 a month would solve a lot of your financial problems.

**The rival trader:**
> Another import/export business undercuts your prices by 20%. They are either eating losses to steal your clients or they are cutting corners somewhere — skipping duties, using cheaper transport, or dealing in counterfeit goods. Do you compete on price, report them to customs, or find out what they are really up to?

**The warehouse break-in:**
> You arrive Monday morning to find your warehouse lock cut and three pallets missing. $30,000 in electronics, gone. Insurance covers some of it, but the deductible hurts. Was it random theft or targeted? Who knew what was in that warehouse? The investigation — whether you involve police or handle it yourself — becomes a storyline.

---

## Final Thoughts

Import/export is the invisible engine of Los Santos commerce. Every product in every store, every part in every mechanic's shop, every bottle behind every bar — it all came from somewhere. Your character is the one who made it arrive.

The work is unglamorous most days — paperwork, phone calls, logistics, and waiting at the port. But the connections you build, the money you move, and the choices you face when the legal path stops being profitable enough — that is where the story lives.

Move the cargo. Manage the risk. Keep the city supplied.
