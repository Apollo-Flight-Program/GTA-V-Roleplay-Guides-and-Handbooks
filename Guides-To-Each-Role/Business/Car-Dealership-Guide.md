# Car Dealership Guide — Auto Sales Roleplay

Cars are currency, identity, and obsession in Los Santos. A car dealership sits at the intersection of money and desire — every character who walks onto your lot wants something, and it is your job to figure out what that is and sell it to them. This guide covers everything from stocking your inventory to closing a six-figure deal on a Pegassi Zentorno.

---

## Why Car Dealership RP Matters

A dealership is one of the highest-interaction business roles in GTA V RP. Every character needs transportation. Some need a budget sedan to get to work. Others want a status symbol that announces them three blocks before they arrive. The car they choose tells you who they are — or who they want to be.

Dealership RP combines sales performance, financial negotiation, customer profiling, and inventory management into a role that is part business owner, part psychologist, and part showman.

---

## Types of Dealerships

Not every lot sells the same kind of car. Your specialization defines your clientele.

| Dealership Type | Inventory Focus | Price Range | Target Customer |
|---|---|---|---|
| Economy / used lot | Reliable daily drivers, older models | $5,000 – $50,000 | New arrivals, budget buyers, first cars |
| Mid-range | Popular sedans, SUVs, trucks | $30,000 – $150,000 | Working professionals, families |
| Luxury | High-end sports cars, premium sedans | $100,000 – $1,000,000+ | Wealthy characters, collectors |
| Trucks and off-road | Pickups, SUVs, utility vehicles | $25,000 – $200,000 | Workers, ranchers, off-road enthusiasts |
| Motorcycle dealer | Sport bikes, cruisers, choppers | $10,000 – $150,000 | Riders, clubs, commuters |
| Exotic / specialty | Rare imports, supercars, classics | $500,000 – $5,000,000+ | Ultra-wealthy, collectors, show-offs |

A used car lot in La Mesa serves a completely different story than a luxury showroom in Rockford Hills. Pick your lane and own it.

### Location

| Location | Best For | Vibe |
|---|---|---|

| Route 68 / Sandy Shores | Off-road, bikes, used | Desert grit, no-frills |
| Del Perro / Vespucci | Mid-range, convertibles | Lifestyle-oriented, flashy |

### Lot Layout

Organize your inventory for maximum visual impact:
- **Front row** — your best-looking or most affordable vehicles to draw people in
- **Featured vehicle** — one standout car on a raised platform or center display
- **Test drive staging area** — a designated spot where customers pick up keys
- **Office / showroom** — where negotiations and paperwork happen
- **Service bay** — for pre-delivery inspections and detailing

## Inventory Management

Your lot is only as good as what is on it.

### Sourcing Vehicles

| Source | How It Works | RP Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Wholesale auctions | Buy in bulk at reduced prices | Bidding wars, auction RP, supply gambles |
| Trade-ins | Customers trade their old vehicle as part of a deal | Appraising trade value, negotiation |
| Private purchases | Buy directly from individual sellers | Haggling, inspections, spotting hidden damage |
| Manufacturer orders | Order specific models to fill gaps in inventory | Wait times, factory customization, anticipation |
| Repo and impound | Purchase repossessed or unclaimed vehicles | Discounted stock, unknown vehicle history |
| Imports | Source vehicles from contacts outside the city | Shipping delays, customs paperwork, exotic finds |

### Inventory Tracking

Maintain a log of every vehicle on your lot:

| Vehicle | Make/Model | Year | Mileage | Condition | Purchase Price | List Price |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Unit 001 | Karin Sultan RS | 2019 | 32,000 | Good | $18,000 | $28,500 |
| Unit 002 | Vapid Dominator GTX | 2021 | 8,500 | Excellent | $35,000 | $52,000 |
| Unit 003 | Benefactor Schafter V12 | 2022 | 3,200 | Like New | $85,000 | $125,000 |
| Unit 004 | Bravado Gauntlet Classic | 1970 | 98,000 | Fair — needs work | $12,000 | $22,000 |
| Unit 005 | Pegassi Tempesta | 2023 | 1,100 | Excellent | $280,000 | $385,000 |

Know your margin on every vehicle. A car you bought for $18,000 and list at $28,500 gives you $10,500 to work with in negotiation. Never sell below cost unless you are desperate to move inventory.

## The Sales Process

Selling a car is a performance. Every step matters.

### Step 1 — The Greeting

First impressions set the tone. Read the customer before you approach.

> Salesperson: "Afternoon! Welcome to Pinnacle Motors. I'm Danny. Are you browsing today, or do you have something specific in mind?"

Do not pounce. Let them look. Some customers want to be guided; others want space. Learn the difference.

### Step 2 — Needs Assessment

Ask questions to understand what they actually need:
- "What will you primarily use the vehicle for? Commuting? Work? Fun?"
- "Do you have a budget range in mind?"
- "Are you looking for something specific — make, model, color?"
- "Will anyone else be driving this vehicle?"
- "Is this replacing a current vehicle?"

The answers tell you which three cars on your lot to show them — not which fifteen.

### Step 3 — The Presentation

Walk the customer to the vehicle. Present it like you are proud of it.

> `/me opens the driver's door and gestures for the customer to have a seat.`
> "This is the 2021 Vapid Dominator GTX. Four hundred and fifty horsepower, rear-wheel drive, sport-tuned suspension. Comes with the premium audio package and a clean service history. Only 8,500 miles."

Highlight features that match their stated needs. If they said they commute, talk about fuel efficiency and comfort. If they want performance, talk about horsepower and handling.

### Step 4 — The Test Drive

Never skip the test drive — it is where emotional attachment forms.

**Test drive protocol:**
1. Copy the customer's driver's license for your records
2. Walk around the vehicle and note its condition before departure
3. Ride along in the passenger seat (or follow in another vehicle)
4. Suggest a route — highway for speed, city streets for handling, hills for power
5. Let them drive. Do not talk the whole time. Let them feel the car.
6. Return to the lot and ask what they thought

> Customer: "That thing pulls hard. I love the way it sounds when you get on it."
> Salesperson: "She's a special one. That exhaust note is factory — no aftermarket needed. So, what do you think? Can you see yourself driving this every day?"

### Step 5 — Negotiation

This is where the deal lives or dies.

**Negotiation tactics:**
- **Anchor high** — start with the list price and justify it with vehicle condition and market comparison
- **Concede slowly** — drop price in small increments, never large chunks
- **Bundle value** — instead of dropping price, add value (free first service, floor mats, window tint)
- **Use silence** — after making an offer, stop talking. Let the customer respond.
- **Create urgency** — "I've had two other people look at this one this week"

**Example negotiation:**
> Customer: "Fifty-two grand is steep. I was thinking more like forty."
> Salesperson: "I understand. Let me see what I can do. The lowest I can go on this one is forty-eight — that's already below what I'd normally sell a GTX with this mileage for. I can throw in the first oil change and a full detail."
> Customer: "Forty-five, and you've got a deal."
> Salesperson: "Split the difference — forty-six-five, I include the detail and a set of all-weather floor mats. That's my bottom line."
> Customer: "Done."

### Step 6 — Closing and Paperwork

Once the price is agreed:
1. Draw up the bill of sale — vehicle details, agreed price, buyer and seller information
2. Process payment — cash, bank transfer, or financing arrangement
3. Transfer the title and registration
4. Hand over the keys with ceremony — this is their moment
5. Congratulate them and walk them to their new car

> `/me places the keys on the desk and slides them across to the customer with a smile.`
> "Congratulations. She's all yours. Drive safe — and if you need anything, you've got my number."

## Financing Options

Not every customer pays cash. Offering financing expands your customer base.

| Financing Type | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| In-house financing | Dealership acts as the lender, customer pays monthly | Customers with no bank access |
| Bank loan referral | Customer secures a loan through a city bank | Established characters with credit |
| Lease agreement | Customer pays to use the vehicle but does not own it | Characters who want short-term luxury |
| Layaway / deposit | Customer pays a deposit and makes payments before taking delivery | Budget buyers saving up |
| Trade-in + cash | Customer trades old vehicle and pays the difference | Most common transaction type |

Financing creates ongoing RP — monthly payments, missed payments, repossessions, and refinancing conversations.

## Trade-Ins

Trade-ins are negotiation within a negotiation.

**Appraising a trade-in:**
1. Walk around the vehicle and inspect the exterior — dents, scratches, rust, paint condition
2. Check the interior — seats, dashboard, odor, wear and tear
3. Start the engine and listen for issues
4. Check the mileage and service history
5. Research the market value for that make, model, and condition
6. Offer a fair trade-in value — usually 60–75% of retail market

**The delicate conversation:**
> Customer: "My Karin Futo should be worth at least fifteen grand."
> Salesperson: `/me flips through the inspection notes.` "I appreciate the car — it's a fun little ride. But with 87,000 miles, the worn clutch, and the bumper damage, I can offer eight thousand as trade value toward your new purchase. That's fair market for the condition."

Never insult their car. Acknowledge what it is and explain what affects the value.

## Vehicle Inspections and Prep

Every vehicle leaving your lot should be inspected and presentable.

**Pre-delivery checklist:**

| Item | Check |
|---|---|
| Fluid levels | Oil, coolant, brake fluid, transmission, washer fluid |
| Tires | Tread depth, pressure, condition |
| Brakes | Pad thickness, rotor condition |
| Lights | Headlights, taillights, signals, interior |
| Battery | Charge level, terminal condition |
| Exterior | Wash, wax, touch up any chips |
| Interior | Vacuum, wipe down, air freshener |
| Documentation | Title, registration, service history in glove box |

A car that arrives clean, inspected, and documented tells the customer you care about what you sell. A dirty car with mysterious stains says the opposite.

## Customer Profiles

Learn to read who is standing on your lot.

| Profile | What They Want | How to Sell |
|---|---|---|
| The first-time buyer | Affordable, reliable, simple | Reassure them, explain everything, no pressure |
| The enthusiast | Performance, specs, heritage | Talk technical, let them geek out, test drive aggressively |
| The status buyer | Luxury, prestige, appearance | Sell the lifestyle, name-drop the brand, VIP treatment |
| The practical buyer | Value, fuel economy, durability | Focus on total cost of ownership, reliability data |
| The parent | Safety, space, family features | Highlight rear seat space, cargo, safety systems |
| The criminal | Fast, discreet, untraceable | Read between the lines, ask fewer questions, cash only |
| The impulse buyer | Whatever catches their eye | Strike while the iron is hot, minimize friction |

Adapt your approach to each customer. The pitch that works on an enthusiast will bore a practical buyer. The soft sell that works on a first-timer will frustrate an impulse buyer.

## Dealer Events

Events drive traffic and build your reputation.

**Event ideas:**
- **Grand opening** — free food, raffles, and steep first-day discounts
- **Car show weekend** — invite owners to display their vehicles on your lot
- **Midnight madness sale** — after-hours event with special pricing
- **New model reveal** — unveil a high-profile vehicle with ceremony and spectacle
- **Customer appreciation day** — free car washes, food, and discounts for past buyers
- **Charity auction** — donate a percentage of a vehicle sale to an IC cause

Events create concentrated RP moments and attract players who might not otherwise visit your dealership.

## Sales Targets and Competition

A dealership without goals stagnates. Set targets and track performance.

**Individual sales targets:**
- Units sold per week
- Total revenue generated
- Customer satisfaction (repeat customers, referrals)
- Average deal margin (difference between cost and sale price)

**Competing with other dealers:**
- Differentiate on service, not just price — anyone can lower a number
- Build relationships that competitors cannot replicate
- Monitor what they stock and fill gaps they leave open
- Never bad-mouth the competition to a customer — it makes you look desperate

If a competing dealership opens across the street, treat it as a storyline, not a threat. Rivalry is excellent RP.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The dream car:**
> A character has been saving for six months. They walk onto your lot, eyes locked on the Pegassi Tempesta in the front row. They cannot quite afford the sticker price. You negotiate, offer financing, and find a way to make it work. When they drive off the lot, they are grinning. You just made someone's day — and a $40,000 profit.

**The lemon:**
> A customer returns three days after buying a Vapid Stanier, furious. The transmission is slipping, the AC does not work, and there is a mysterious rattle in the dashboard. Did you know about these problems when you sold it? Whether you did or not, you have a reputation decision to make. Fix it for free or lose the customer — and their friends — forever.

**The suspicious buyer:**
> A character in dark clothing walks onto the lot at 10 PM, pays cash for a blacked-out Bravado Buffalo with no questions asked, and does not want the title in their name. Something is off. Do you take the money, or do you ask questions you might not want the answers to?

**The bidding war:**
> Two customers want the same car. You have one unit. Do you sell to the first person who can pay, or do you play them against each other to drive the price up? There is more money in the bidding war — but more risk to your reputation.

---

## Final Thoughts

A car dealership is a stage for negotiation, aspiration, and human connection. Every customer who steps onto your lot brings a story — a reason they need a new car, a budget they are stretching, a dream they are chasing. Your job is not just to sell them a vehicle. It is to be part of that story.

Shine the cars, sharpen the pitch, and open the lot. The customers are already on their way.
