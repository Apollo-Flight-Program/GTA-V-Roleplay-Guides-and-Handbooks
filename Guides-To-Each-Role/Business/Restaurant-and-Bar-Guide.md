# Restaurant and Bar Guide — Food Service Roleplay

The restaurant or bar is the living room of Los Santos roleplay. It is where first dates happen, deals get brokered, friendships form, and fights break out. Running a food service business puts your character at the center of the city's social life — feeding people, serving drinks, and managing the controlled chaos that comes with it. This guide covers everything from building a menu to handling a brawl at last call.

---

## Why Food Service RP Matters

Everyone eats. Everyone drinks. A restaurant or bar is one of the few businesses that naturally attracts every kind of character — cops on lunch break, criminals celebrating a score, couples on a date, lonely drifters at the end of the bar. Your establishment becomes neutral ground where storylines intersect organically.

Food service RP is also performance RP. A bartender shaking a cocktail, a chef plating a dish, a waiter charming a difficult table — every shift is a series of small scenes that build into something larger.

## Choosing Your Concept

Your concept defines everything — the menu, the vibe, the location, and the clientele.

| Concept | Location Fit | Clientele | Vibe |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fine dining | Rockford Hills, Burton | Wealthy, professionals | Formal, quiet, exclusive |
| Casual diner | Mirror Park, Strawberry | Everyone | Relaxed, family-friendly |
| Taco truck / food stand | Vespucci Beach, city streets | Walk-up traffic, workers | Quick, cheap, authentic |
| Sports bar | Del Perro, Pillbox Hill | Groups, regulars, sports fans | Loud, social, casual |
| Cocktail lounge | West Vinewood, Downtown | Professionals, nightlife crowd | Sleek, moody, upscale |
| Dive bar | La Mesa, El Burro Heights | Locals, blue-collar workers | Rough, cheap, character-rich |
| Beachside bar | Vespucci, Chumash | Tourists, beach crowd | Open-air, relaxed, tropical |
| Biker bar | Sandy Shores, Route 68 | Bikers, desert locals | Gritty, loud, no-nonsense |

Pick a concept your character would actually create. A former line cook from Vice City opening a Cuban sandwich shop has depth. A character with no food background suddenly running a Michelin-style kitchen needs justification.

## Menu Creation

Your menu is your identity on paper. Keep it focused — a small menu done well beats a massive menu done poorly.

### Building a Food Menu

**Guidelines:**
- 6–12 items maximum for a restaurant, 4–8 for a food truck
- Include at least one option for dietary restrictions (vegetarian, allergy-friendly)
- Price items consistently with your concept — a dive bar does not charge $40 for a burger
- Name dishes creatively to fit your brand

**Sample diner menu:**

| Item | Description | Price |
|---|---|---|
| Vinewood Burger | Half-pound beef patty, cheddar, lettuce, tomato, house sauce | $14 |
| Mirror Park Club | Turkey, bacon, avocado, sourdough toast | $12 |
| Blaine County Breakfast | Three eggs, bacon, hash browns, toast | $10 |
| Vespucci Fish Tacos | Grilled mahi, slaw, chipotle crema, corn tortillas | $13 |
| Paleto Bay Chowder | New England style, fresh clams, crusty bread | $9 |
| Side of fries | Classic cut, seasoned | $5 |
| House salad | Mixed greens, cherry tomatoes, ranch or vinaigrette | $7 |

## Staffing Your Operation

A restaurant needs a team. Each role creates unique RP opportunities.

### Key Positions

| Role | Responsibilities | RP Focus |
|---|---|---|
| Head Chef / Cook | Prepare food, manage kitchen, design specials | Kitchen creativity, pressure, pride |
| Sous Chef | Assist head chef, run kitchen when chef is absent | Learning, ambition, teamwork |
| Bartender | Mix drinks, serve bar customers, manage bar area | Conversation, multitasking, personality |
| Waiter / Waitress | Take orders, serve tables, handle customer needs | Social skills, patience, hustle |
| Host / Hostess | Greet guests, manage seating, handle reservations | First impression, organization |
| Busser | Clear tables, restock, support waitstaff | Entry-level, hardworking, observant |
| Bouncer / Security | Manage entry, handle disturbances, check IDs | Intimidation, judgment, protection |
| Manager | Oversee operations, handle staff, manage finances | Leadership, problem-solving, diplomacy |

### Staff Training

Train your team on the basics before they take a shift:
- How to greet customers and present the menu
- How to use `/me` commands for food prep and service
- What to do when a customer is unhappy
- How to handle a customer who has had too much to drink
- Emergency procedures — fights, robberies, medical emergencies

### Example Service Flow

**Waiter serving a table:**
> `/me approaches the table with two menus, placing one in front of each guest.`
> "Good evening, welcome to The Rusty Anchor. My name's Maria. Can I start you off with something to drink?"
> *Takes drink order*
> `/me returns with two glasses, setting the beer in front of the gentleman and the wine in front of his date.`
> "Are you ready to order, or do you need a few more minutes?"

The detail in the service creates immersion. Do not just say "here's your food" — describe placing the plate, refilling the water, checking on the table.

## Customer Service Roleplay

Service is the core of food RP. How you treat people defines your establishment.

**The perfect experience:**
- Greet within 30 seconds of seating
- Take drink orders promptly
- Deliver food with a description of the dish
- Check back once after the food arrives
- Offer dessert or another round before clearing
- Present the check without rushing

**Handling complaints:**

> Customer: "Excuse me, I ordered my steak medium-rare and this is well done."
> Waiter: "I'm so sorry about that. Let me take that back to the kitchen and get you a new one. Can I bring you another drink while you wait? On the house."

Never argue with a customer in front of other diners. If someone is being unreasonable, get the manager. The goal is to solve the problem, not win the argument.

## Handling Drunk and Disorderly Patrons

Alcohol creates situations. Expect them and prepare for them.

### Recognizing the Signs

Roleplay the progression of intoxication:
- Slightly tipsy — louder, more talkative, friendlier
- Drunk — slurred speech, unsteady, poor decisions
- Heavily intoxicated — belligerent, unable to stand, needs to be cut off

### Cutting Someone Off

This is a scene, not a confrontation:

> Bartender: `/me sets a glass of water on the bar in front of the customer.`
> "Hey Tommy, I think you've had enough for tonight, brother. Let me call you a cab. Water's on me."

If they refuse, be firm but calm. If they become aggressive, signal security.

### Escalation Protocol

1. **Verbal warning** — politely ask them to calm down or leave
2. **Manager involvement** — the manager steps in with authority
3. **Bouncer involvement** — security physically escorts them out
4. **Police call** — if they refuse to leave or become violent, call LSPD

Document incidents. If a patron is repeatedly a problem, ban them from the establishment.

## Liquor Licensing

Serving alcohol requires a license — and keeping that license requires following the rules.

**Obtaining a liquor license:**
1. Apply at City Hall or the Department of Commerce
2. Pay the licensing fee ($5,000 – $20,000 depending on the server)
3. Pass a premises inspection — proper bar setup, storage, fire exits
4. Receive approval from a government official
5. Display the license visibly on-premises

**Conditions of the license:**
- No serving minors
- No serving visibly intoxicated patrons (your liability if they cause harm)
- Comply with posted operating hours
- Allow government inspections without obstruction
- Renew the license before it expires

**Losing your license:**
A liquor license can be suspended or revoked for:
- Repeated violations during inspections
- Serving alcohol to minors
- Drug activity on the premises
- Excessive violence at the establishment
- Failure to pay renewal fees or taxes

Losing your liquor license is devastating — a bar without alcohol is just a room with uncomfortable stools.

## Health Code and Inspections

Health inspectors create some of the best bureaucratic RP in the game.

**What inspectors look for:**

| Area | What They Check | Common Violations |
|---|---|---|
| Kitchen cleanliness | Surfaces, floors, equipment | Grease buildup, dirty prep stations |
| Food storage | Temperature, labeling, organization | Expired items, improper refrigeration |
| Employee hygiene | Handwashing, gloves, hair nets | No hand-washing station, bare hands on food |
| Pest control | Evidence of rodents, insects | Droppings, open food containers |
| Waste management | Trash disposal, dumpster area | Overflowing bins, improper waste separation |
| Documentation | Permits, licenses, staff certifications | Expired health permit, unlicensed staff |

**Inspection grading:**

| Grade | Meaning | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| A | Excellent — minor or no issues | Continue operating |
| B | Acceptable — some issues noted | Fix within 30 days, re-inspection |
| C | Poor — significant violations | Fix immediately, possible temporary closure |
| F | Critical failure | Shut down until violations are corrected |

Roleplay the inspection thoroughly. Walk the inspector through your kitchen. Let them find problems — or scramble to hide them. The tension of an unannounced inspection is excellent RP.

## Live Entertainment

Music, comedy, and performances bring people to your venue.

**Options for entertainment:**
- Live DJ — plays music, takes requests, controls the vibe
- Live band — acoustic sets, rock shows, jazz nights
- Open mic / karaoke — customers perform, crowd reacts
- Comedy night — stand-up sets, audience interaction
- Trivia night — teams compete, prizes awarded

**Managing entertainment:**
- Book performers in advance and agree on payment
- Set up a performance area — stage, speakers, lighting if available
- Promote the event through IC channels
- Manage sound levels — neighbors and noise complaints are real
- Pay performers at the end of the night and thank them publicly

Entertainment turns a quiet Tuesday into a packed house. Use it strategically.

## VIP Service

Some customers deserve — or demand — special treatment.

**VIP offerings:**
- Reserved seating in a private area
- Dedicated server assigned to their table
- Exclusive menu items or off-menu specials
- Complimentary appetizers or desserts
- Priority entry with no wait
- Bottle service for high-spending groups

**Who gets VIP treatment:**
- High-profile characters (politicians, business moguls, celebrities)
- Regular big spenders
- Characters hosting private events or business meetings
- Anyone willing to pay a premium for the experience

VIP service is not about being unfair — it is about creating a tiered experience that rewards loyalty and spending.

## Closing Time Procedures

The end of the night has its own rhythm.

**Last call routine:**
1. Announce last call 30 minutes before closing — "Last call for drinks, everyone!"
2. Stop accepting new customers
3. Serve final round orders
4. Dim the lights or change the music to signal the evening is ending
5. Begin clearing and cleaning tables
6. Politely ask lingering guests to finish up
7. Security sweeps the venue
8. Lock the doors

**After closing:**
- Cash out the register and count the night's earnings
- Tip out staff — bartenders, waiters, bussers, security
- Clean the kitchen, bar, and dining area
- Restock for tomorrow
- Debrief with staff — what went well, what did not

The post-closing debrief is underrated RP. Staff conversations at the end of a long night, counting money, talking about problem customers — these are the moments that build team dynamics.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The first date:**
> A nervous guy brings his date to your restaurant. He fumbles the wine order, cannot pronounce the menu items, and spills water on the tablecloth. Your waiter rescues the situation with charm and patience, recommending the perfect dish and keeping the mood light. The date goes well. The guy tips 40%.

**The food critic:**
> Someone claiming to be a food critic from the Weazel News food section sits at the bar and orders three dishes. Your chef pours everything into those plates. The review drops two days later — three stars. Not bad, but your chef takes it personally.

**The robbery:**
> Two masked individuals burst in during the dinner rush. They demand the register. Your bartender stays calm. The customers freeze. Someone under a table dials 911. This is not a cooking scene anymore — it is a crime scene. How your staff reacts defines the aftermath.

**The health inspection failure:**
> The inspector finds expired produce in the walk-in, grease buildup behind the fryer, and no hand-washing station near the prep area. You get a C rating and 48 hours to fix it or shut down. The scramble to bring the kitchen up to code involves every employee, a deep clean, and a supply run to replace everything that expired.

---

## Final Thoughts

A restaurant or bar is the heartbeat of civilian RP. It is where characters go when they want to feel normal, celebrate, mourn, plan, confess, and connect. The food is the excuse — the people are the point.

Keep your kitchen clean, your staff trained, your drinks cold, and your doors open. The stories will walk in on their own.
