# Heist Planning Guide — Robbery and Heist Roleplay

The heist is the crown jewel of criminal RP. Nothing generates more adrenaline, coordination, and storytelling potential than a well-planned robbery executed under pressure. From the first meeting where someone lays out the plan on a crumpled napkin to the getaway through the storm drains of Los Santos, heist RP demands preparation, teamwork, and the willingness to accept that everything can go sideways in a heartbeat.

This guide covers every type of robbery in GTA V RP — from convenience stores to the Pacific Standard vault — along with crew assembly, planning phases, hostage management, police negotiation, and what happens when the plan falls apart.

---

## The Heist Hierarchy — Robbery Types

Not all robberies are created equal. Each requires different levels of preparation, crew size, and risk.

| Target | Crew Size | Difficulty | Estimated Take | Police Response |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Convenience store / 24/7 | 1–2 | Low | $500 – $2,000 | 2–4 officers |
| Liquor store | 1–2 | Low | $800 – $2,500 | 2–4 officers |
| Gas station | 1–2 | Low | $500 – $1,500 | 2–4 officers |
| Jewelry store (Vangelico) | 3–4 | Medium | $15,000 – $50,000 | 6–10 officers, possible helicopter |
| Fleeca Bank | 3–4 | Medium | $30,000 – $75,000 | 8–12 officers, negotiator |
| Paleto Bay Bank | 4–5 | High | $75,000 – $150,000 | Full department, SWAT possible |
| Pacific Standard Bank | 5–6 | Very High | $200,000 – $500,000 | Full response, SWAT, helicopter, roadblocks |
| Casino / high-value target | 4–6 | Extreme | $300,000+ | Everything the city can throw at you |

Start small. Rob a convenience store before you try a bank. Rob a Fleeca before you touch the Pacific Standard. Earn your reputation and learn from mistakes that cost you hundreds instead of hundreds of thousands.

## Crew Assembly

A heist is only as strong as its weakest member. Choose your crew carefully.

### Core Roles

| Role | Responsibilities | Key Skills |
|---|---|---|
| Mastermind / Planner | Designs the plan, coordinates the crew, makes decisions during the heist | Strategic thinking, leadership, calm under pressure |
| Driver | Handles the getaway vehicle, knows every route and backup route | Driving skill, knowledge of Los Santos roads, vehicle prep |
| Hacker / Tech | Cracks safes, disables alarms, handles electronic barriers | Technical RP knowledge, patience, focus |
| Muscle / Enforcer | Controls the room, manages hostages, covers entry and exit points | Intimidation, weapons handling, situational awareness |
| Lookout / Scout | Monitors police response, communicates movement, watches entrances | Radio discipline, attention to detail, composure |
| Negotiator | Talks to police during standoffs, buys time, manages demands | Communication skills, ability to think on feet |

Not every heist needs all six roles. A convenience store robbery might be one person with a gun. A Pacific Standard job needs everyone working in sync.

### Choosing Your Crew

**What to look for:**
- Reliability — they show up, they follow the plan, they do not panic
- Discretion — they do not brag about the job before or after
- Complementary skills — a crew of five gunmen with no driver is a crew that dies in the parking lot
- Chemistry — you need to trust these people with your freedom and your life

**What to avoid:**
- Players who improvise during execution without communication
- People who cannot handle getting caught (OOC frustration ruins the scene)
- Crew members who have snitched before (IC consideration)
- Anyone who makes the heist about themselves instead of the team

## Planning Phases

A heist is won or lost in the planning stage.

### Phase 1 — Target Selection

Choose your target based on crew capability, risk tolerance, and potential reward.

Visit the target beforehand. Walk in as a customer. Buy something. Look around.

**What to observe:**
- Number of employees and their positions
- Camera locations and blind spots
- Number and location of exits
- Safe or register location
- Average customer count at different times of day
- Nearby police stations and average response time
- Surrounding roads and alley access

> `/me walks into the Fleeca on Great Ocean Highway, casually looks around while pretending to fill out a deposit slip, noting the camera positions and the single door behind the counter.`

### Phase 2 — Route Planning

The getaway is the most critical part. Plan your primary route and at least two backups.

**Primary route considerations:**
- Distance from target to safe location
- Number of turns and intersections (fewer is better)
- Highway access vs. back roads
- Police station locations along the route
- Traffic patterns at the planned time

**Backup route triggers:**
- Police roadblock on primary route
- Vehicle disabled during escape
- Crew separated and need alternate meeting point
- Helicopter pursuit requiring covered routes (tunnels, bridges, dense urban areas)

**Getaway vehicle preparation:**
- Use a fast, reliable vehicle — stolen and with plates swapped
- Stage the vehicle nearby but not directly outside the target
- Consider a vehicle swap — drive one car from the scene, switch to a clean car at a predetermined location
- Have a backup vehicle if possible

### Phase 3 — The Briefing

Hold a meeting with the full crew. Go over everything.

**Briefing checklist:**
- Target overview — layout, staff, security
- Role assignments — who does what, in what order
- Timeline — when you go, how long each phase should take
- Communication — radio channels, code words, hand signals
- Contingencies — what to do if police arrive early, if someone is injured, if the safe will not open
- Exit strategy — primary and backup getaway routes
- Split plan — how the money is divided and when

> "Alright, here's how this goes. Vinny and Marcus go through the front door at exactly two-fifteen. Vinny handles the tellers, Marcus controls the floor. Dee stays outside as lookout — you see a cruiser, you say 'delivery' on the radio. I'm in the car on Vespucci, engine running. We are in and out in four minutes. Five minutes and we are pushing our luck. Six minutes and we are gambling with our lives."

### Phase 4 — Dry Run (Optional but Smart)

Drive the getaway route. Walk past the target. Time the drive. Identify problems before they happen in the middle of a felony.

## Executing the Robbery

### Convenience Store / Small Target

**Step-by-step:**
1. Enter with your face covered (mask, bandana, helmet)
2. Approach the counter with weapon drawn
3. Demand the money — be clear and direct
4. Control any customers — tell them to get on the ground
5. Take the cash — do not linger
6. Exit and drive the planned route

> `/me pulls the bandana up over his nose, pushes through the door, and levels the pistol at the clerk behind the counter.`
> "Empty the register. Everything in a bag. Do not reach for anything under that counter."

**Common mistakes:**
- Spending too long inside — you have sixty seconds before police start arriving
- Forgetting about cameras — wear a mask, gloves, and different clothes than your usual outfit
- Using your own car — plates get recorded
- Going back for more — you got the money, leave

### Bank Robbery (Fleeca, Paleto, Pacific Standard)

**Phase 1 — Entry and Control:**
- Enter together, weapons visible
- Announce the robbery loudly and clearly
- One person controls the lobby (customers, employees)
- One person handles the tellers / vault access
- Lookout stays outside or at the entrance

**Phase 2 — The Vault:**
- Access the vault (hacking, thermite, lockpicking — depends on server mechanics)
- Grab the money while the timer runs
- Stay calm — rushing causes mistakes

**Phase 3 — Hostage Management:**
- Take at least one hostage before police arrive (bank employees or customers)
- Hostages are your leverage — treat them as a bargaining chip, not a target
- Keep hostages visible to police through windows or cameras — proof of life
- Release hostages strategically during negotiations to earn goodwill or buy time

**Phase 4 — Negotiation (if surrounded):**
- Designate one person as negotiator
- Make demands — a helicopter, a car, safe passage, reduced charges
- Offer hostages in exchange for demands
- Buy time while other crew members prepare the exit
- Never give away your numbers, weapons, or plan details

> "Officer, we've got six people in here with us. Nobody's been hurt and nobody needs to get hurt. We want a helicopter on the roof in twenty minutes. For every five minutes you make us wait, someone walks out of here with a limp."

**Phase 5 — The Getaway:**
- Exit fast once you have the money
- Designated driver has the vehicle running at the planned pickup point
- Follow the primary route unless it is compromised
- Execute vehicle swaps as planned
- Split up only if the plan calls for it — disorganized splitting gets everyone caught individually

## Hostage Management

Hostages are your most valuable tool during a robbery. Handle them poorly and you lose everything.

**Rules of hostage RP:**

| Do | Do Not |
|---|---|
| Talk to your hostages — keep them calm | Ignore them — they are not props |
| Use them as negotiating chips with police | Hurt them without RP justification — escalates police response |
| Release them as concessions during negotiation | Kill them for no reason — most servers prohibit this |
| Give them /me interactions and dialogue | Force them to do unreasonable things (OOC consent matters) |

**Hostage dialogue:**
> `/me crouches next to the woman on the floor and lowers his voice.`
> "Hey. Look at me. You're gonna be fine. We don't want to hurt anyone. Just stay calm, keep your head down, and you'll walk out of here in twenty minutes."

The best hostage RP happens when the hostage plays along and creates their own story — fear, bravery, tears, defiance. Give them space to be a character, not just a bargaining chip.

## When Everything Goes Wrong

The best heist RP often comes from failure.

**Common failures and how to handle them:**
- **The hack fails** — improvise. Try again or leave with what you have
- **Police arrive early** — your lookout missed something. Adapt. Find a new exit or hunker down and negotiate
- **Crew member gets shot** — do you leave them or carry them? This decision defines your crew
- **The getaway vehicle is blocked** — find another car, go on foot, or fight through
- **You get caught** — play the arrest scene. The interrogation, the booking, the phone call to your lawyer. This is RP.

> `/me stumbles out the back door and sees the alley filled with police cars. He stops, drops his weapon, and slowly puts his hands up.`
> "I... I give up. I give up."

Getting caught after a dramatic heist is not a loss — it is the climax of the story.

## Splitting the Proceeds

Money creates conflict. Handle the split before the job, not after.

**Common split models:**

| Model | How It Works | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Equal split | Everyone gets the same cut | Small crews, equal contribution |
| Role-based | Planner and high-risk roles get more | Complex heists with specialized roles |
| Boss takes first | Leader takes 40–50%, rest split the remainder | Gang or organization-backed jobs |
| Expenses first | Costs (vehicles, weapons, equipment) deducted before splitting | Professional crews |

**Discuss the split during planning.** If someone feels shorted after the job, it breeds resentment, distrust, and potential betrayal. Be transparent.

> "Sixty grand total. Twenty for the gear and the cars. That leaves forty. Ten each for the four of us. Anyone got a problem with that, say it now."

## Post-Heist Protocol

The job is not done when you have the money. The aftermath matters.

**Immediate steps:**
1. Ditch the getaway vehicle — burn it, dump it in the ocean, leave it in a remote area
2. Change clothes — burn or dispose of what you wore
3. Dispose of weapons used during the robbery
4. Split up and lay low — do not celebrate publicly
5. Stash the money in a safe location — do not deposit large amounts immediately

**Long-term:**
- Do not spend flashy money right away — a sudden lifestyle change attracts attention
- Launder the cash through a business or other method
- Never talk about the job to anyone outside the crew
- Watch for police investigation — they will be looking at surveillance, witnesses, and forensics
- Be prepared for the heat to last days or weeks

## OOC Considerations

Heist RP affects many players. Respect the process.

- **Coordinate with server mechanics.** Know what your server allows — some require admin approval for major robberies, minimum police counts, or specific cooldowns between heists.
- **Do not rush heists.** Give police time to respond and set up. A heist with no police response is just theft with extra steps.
- **Give hostages RP.** They did not log on to sit on the floor in silence for thirty minutes. Include them in the scene.
- **Accept failure gracefully.** If you get caught, play the consequences. Do not disconnect, rage quit, or OOC complain.
- **Communicate OOC if needed.** If something goes wrong mechanically or someone is uncomfortable, pause and address it.

---

## Final Thoughts

The perfect heist is not the one where you get away clean — it is the one that tells the best story. The planning sessions in a dark warehouse. The tension as the timer ticks down. The look on your driver's face when police sirens fill the rearview mirror. The impossible choice between saving a crew member and saving the money.

Plan the job. Execute the plan. Accept the outcome. Tell the story.
