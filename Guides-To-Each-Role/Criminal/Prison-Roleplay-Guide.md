# Prison Roleplay Guide — Jail and Incarceration RP

Prison is not the end of the story — it is a setting change. When the cell door closes, a new world opens up with its own hierarchy, politics, economy, and survival rules. Some of the most intense, character-defining RP happens behind bars. The kingpin who ran Los Santos is now just another inmate negotiating for an extra tray at lunch. The street kid who got caught on a petty charge discovers who they really are when the noise of the yard replaces the noise of the city.

This guide covers everything about prison and jail RP — from intake to release, covering hierarchy, yard politics, gangs, escape planning, parole hearings, and the unwritten rules that keep the population alive.

---

## Jail vs. Prison — Understanding the Difference

In RP, these terms are often used interchangeably, but there is a difference.

| Aspect | Jail (Bolingbroke Short-Stay) | Prison (Bolingbroke Penitentiary) |
|---|---|---|
| Who goes there | Awaiting trial, short sentences, recent arrests | Convicted on serious charges, long sentences |
| Duration | Minutes to hours (server time) | Hours to days (server time), sometimes longer |
| Population | Rotating — people come and go quickly | Stable — same faces, established dynamics |
| Hierarchy | Loose — not enough time for structure | Rigid — clear power structures emerge |
| RP depth | Quick scenes, booking, holding cell interaction | Deep — alliances, politics, survival arcs |

Most servers use Bolingbroke Penitentiary as the primary incarceration location, whether for short holds or extended sentences.

## Intake Procedures

The first minutes inside set the tone for your entire incarceration arc.

**Step-by-step intake RP:**
1. **Transport** — Ride in the back of a police cruiser or prison bus. This is a scene — your character is processing what just happened.
2. **Arrival** — Pulled out of the vehicle at the facility gate. First look at the walls, the wire, the guards.
3. **Processing** — Fingerprints, mugshot, strip search, medical screening. Personal belongings confiscated.
4. **Uniform** — Given prison jumpsuit and basic toiletries. Your identity is replaced with a number.
5. **Cell assignment** — Escorted to your cell. Meet your cellmate if you have one.
6. **First yard call** — The first time you step into general population. Everyone is watching the new face.

> `/me stands in line wearing the orange jumpsuit, holding a rolled mattress and a small bag of toiletries. He stares at the concrete floor and tries not to make eye contact with anyone.`

**The first hours matter.** How you carry yourself during intake determines how other inmates treat you. Walk in scared and you become a target. Walk in aggressive and you attract the wrong attention. Walk in calm and aware, and people take note.

## Prison Hierarchy

Every prison has a pecking order. Learn it fast or learn it the hard way.

| Position | Description | How They Got There |
|---|---|---|
| Shot-caller | Controls their racial or gang faction within the prison | Reputation, violence, or organizational rank from the outside |
| Enforcer | Carries out the shot-caller's orders, handles discipline | Physical capability and loyalty |
| Hustler | Runs the prison economy — commissary, contraband, services | Resourcefulness and connections |
| Connected inmate | Has outside contacts, lawyer access, or corrupt guard relationships | Money, organization ties, or charisma |
| Regular inmate | General population, no special status | Just doing their time |
| Fish (new inmate) | Recently arrived, unproven, vulnerable | Just got here — has to earn any status |
| Outcast | Someone who broke prison code — snitch, certain offense types | Violated the unwritten rules |

**Moving up the hierarchy:**
- Prove you are reliable — follow through on every promise
- Show you can handle yourself — do not start fights, but finish them
- Bring value — access to commissary, outside connections, legal knowledge, muscle
- Align with the right people — your associations define your safety

## Yard Politics

The yard is the social center of the prison. Everything happens there — deals, disputes, alliances, and violence.

**The unwritten yard rules:**
- Stay with your group — racial, gang, or crew-based. Mixing casually can be dangerous.
- Do not take someone else's spot — benches, workout equipment, and corners of the yard belong to specific groups.
- Settle disputes quickly and quietly — lingering beef attracts guard attention and lockdowns.
- Respect the shot-callers — even if you outrank someone on the outside, the inside has its own rules.
- Exercise time is sacred — do not interrupt someone's workout.
- Do not stare — eye contact is a challenge unless you intend it to be.

**Yard activities:**
- Working out — pushups, pull-ups, weightlifting. Physical fitness is both activity and status symbol.
- Card games — poker, spades, dominos. Stakes range from commissary items to favors.
- Walking the track — conversations happen during walks. Private enough to talk, public enough to not be suspicious.
- Basketball — competitive, social, and a way to earn respect or settle disputes.
- Sitting on the bleachers — watching, talking, plotting.

> `/me drops to the ground near the weight bench and starts his set of pushups, counting under his breath. He nods to the man working out next to him — a brief acknowledgment, nothing more.`

## The Commissary System

Commissary is the prison economy. Items purchased through commissary become currency.

**What commissary provides:**

| Category | Items | Currency Value |
|---|---|---|
| Food | Ramen noodles, chips, candy bars, coffee | Ramen = base currency unit |
| Hygiene | Soap, shampoo, toothpaste, deodorant | Low — necessity items |
| Writing supplies | Paper, pens, envelopes, stamps | Medium — communication is valuable |
| Entertainment | Playing cards, books, magazines | Medium — boredom is an enemy |
| Clothing extras | Extra socks, underwear, t-shirts | Low–Medium |

**The commissary economy:**
- Ramen noodles are often the base currency (one ramen = one unit)
- Services are paid in commissary — a haircut costs three ramens, a tattoo costs ten
- Debts in commissary are serious — failure to pay creates conflict
- Contraband is priced in commissary equivalents but at massive markup
- Some inmates have money on their books from outside — they become commissary rich and gain influence

> "I'll get that done for you, but it's gonna cost you a book of stamps and four soups. Pay up front."

## Visitation Roleplay

Visits from the outside world are emotionally charged scenes.

**Types of visits:**
- **Family visits** — Spouse, children, parents, siblings. Emotional, grounding, sometimes devastating.
- **Lawyer visits** — Case updates, appeal discussions, legal strategy. Private and serious.
- **Gang / organization contact** — Messages passed, orders received, status updates. Risky if guards are watching.
- **Romantic visits** — Partners who have been waiting. Tension between connection and the reality of separation.

**Visitation RP examples:**

> `/me sits at the visitation table and watches his daughter walk through the door. He forces a smile even though his chest feels like it is caving in.`
> "Hey, baby girl. Look at you — you got so big. Come here."

> `/me leans forward and speaks quietly to the man across the table, his mouth barely moving.`
> "Tell Dre to move the stash from Chamberlain to the backup spot. The cops hit the house on Carson last week. And make sure my mother gets her money this Friday. Same amount."

**Guard interaction during visits:**
Guards monitor visits. Passing contraband, whispering too much, or physical contact beyond what is allowed draws attention. The tension between wanting to communicate freely and being watched creates natural RP.

## Prison Gangs

Prison gangs operate differently from street gangs. Inside, racial and ethnic groupings often override outside affiliations.

**Common prison gang dynamics:**
- Groups form along racial, ethnic, or geographic lines
- Each group has a shot-caller who speaks for them
- Shot-callers negotiate with each other to maintain order
- Violence between groups can trigger lockdowns that affect everyone
- New inmates are expected to check in with their group's representative

**The check-in:**
> When a new inmate arrives, they are expected to approach their group's shot-caller and identify themselves — who they are, what they are in for, and their affiliations on the outside.

> `/me approaches the group at the far table in the yard and waits to be acknowledged.`
> "Name's Marcus. From Davis. Got eighteen months for robbery. I'm checking in."

The shot-caller decides if the new person is welcome, if they need to prove something, or if there is a problem with their record. Certain offenses (particularly crimes against children or sexual offenses — often referenced as "chomos" in prison RP) result in immediate rejection and targeting by the population.

## Planning Escapes

Escape RP is rare, dramatic, and should only happen with significant buildup and server coordination.

**Escape planning process:**
1. **Observation** — Study guard rotations, facility layout, blind spots, and weak points over weeks
2. **Resources** — Acquire tools through contraband channels — a sharpened spoon, wire cutters smuggled in, a guard uniform
3. **Allies** — Recruit trusted inmates. An escape alone is nearly impossible. Each person has a role.
4. **Timing** — Choose the moment carefully — shift change, a distraction event, a power outage
5. **Outside help** — Someone on the outside with a vehicle, clothes, and a safe house ready
6. **Execution** — Go. No hesitation. Everything moves fast once it starts.

**Escape methods:**

| Method | Difficulty | RP Value |
|---|---|---|
| Guard uniform disguise | Medium | High — social engineering and tension |
| Tunnel | Very High | Extreme — weeks of RP building to it |
| Visitation room exploit | Medium | High — involves outside help |
| Riot distraction | High | Very High — massive multi-player event |
| Corrupt guard assistance | Medium | High — bribery, blackmail, or leverage |
| Transport escape (during transfer) | High | High — vehicle chase potential |

> `/me lifts the loose floor tile in his cell and pulls out the sharpened piece of metal he has been hiding for three weeks. He wraps it in cloth and tucks it into his waistband.`
> "Tonight. During the shift change. We have four minutes."

**OOC requirements:** Escape RP should be coordinated with server administration. Most servers require approval for prison breaks. Plan the RP over multiple sessions and involve the DOC (Department of Corrections) players in the story — they deserve the RP too.

## Parole Hearings

Parole is the light at the end of the tunnel. The hearing is a roleplay scene that can define your character's next chapter.

**Preparing for parole:**
- Demonstrate good behavior during incarceration
- Participate in rehabilitation programs (if available)
- Prepare a statement about what you have learned and how you have changed
- Have a plan for release — housing, employment, support system
- Secure a lawyer or advocate to speak on your behalf

**The hearing:**

> Board member: "Mr. Cole, you were sentenced to twenty-four months for armed robbery. You have served eighteen. During your time here, you have had one infraction — a fight in the yard three months ago. Tell us why we should grant your parole."

> `/me sits up straight and folds his hands on the table.`
> "Ma'am, I am not going to sit here and tell you I am a different person. I made choices that put me here. Bad choices. But I have spent the last year and a half thinking about what I want the rest of my life to look like. My mother needs me home. I have a job lined up at a mechanic shop in La Mesa. I am not asking for a free pass — I am asking for a chance to do better."

**Parole can be denied.** If your behavior inside has been poor, if the board does not believe you, or if the nature of your crime is severe — they say no. That denial is RP. The anger, the despair, the decision about what to do next.

## Rehabilitation Roleplay

Not every inmate leaves the same way they came in. Rehabilitation is a valid and rich RP arc.

**Rehabilitation activities:**
- Education programs — GED classes, reading groups, skill workshops
- Counseling — One-on-one sessions with a prison counselor or chaplain
- Work programs — Kitchen duty, laundry, maintenance, library
- Mentorship — Older inmates guiding younger ones away from their mistakes
- Religious practice — Chapel services, prayer groups, spiritual guidance

> `/me sits in the plastic chair in the classroom and opens the GED workbook to the math section. He stares at the fractions and exhales slowly. It has been fifteen years since he was in a classroom.`

Rehabilitation does not mean your character becomes a saint. It means they are trying. The struggle between old habits and new intentions is the story.

## Guard Interactions

Guards are not NPCs. They are players with their own characters and stories.

**Types of guard interactions:**
- **The professional** — Treats inmates fairly, follows the rules, does not take sides. Respect them.
- **The hard-ass** — Strict, aggressive, looking for reasons to write you up. Navigate carefully.
- **The corrupt guard** — Can be bribed, blackmailed, or turned. Extremely valuable but dangerous.
- **The sympathetic guard** — Sees inmates as people. Will bend rules slightly for someone they respect. Rare.

> `/me nods to the guard passing his cell.`
> "Officer. Any word on my commissary order? I put it in three days ago."

Building a relationship with a guard — positive or adversarial — creates ongoing RP. The guard who looks the other way when you pass a note. The guard who always searches your cell after count. The guard who tells you about a problem at home because you are the only person who listens.

## Solitary Confinement

The hole. Isolation. The worst punishment the facility can impose short of extending your sentence.

**Why inmates go to solitary:**
- Fighting in general population
- Possessing contraband
- Threatening or assaulting staff
- Refusing orders during a lockdown
- Gang activity (administrative segregation)
- Protective custody (for the inmate's own safety)

**Solitary RP:**
- Your character is alone in a small cell for hours or days (server time compressed)
- No yard, no commissary, no visitors, minimal human contact
- RP the mental effects — isolation, paranoia, talking to yourself, counting the bricks
- When released back to general population, your character may be different — harder, quieter, more volatile

> `/me sits on the concrete slab that passes for a bed and stares at the wall. No window. No sound except the hum of the ventilation. He starts counting to a thousand. Again.`

## Violence Policy and Conflict

Prison is violent. But server rules still apply.

**The shanking:**
Improvised weapons are a reality of prison RP. A sharpened toothbrush, a razor blade melted into a comb, a sock filled with batteries. Violence in prison is sudden, targeted, and purposeful.

**OOC rules for prison violence:**
- Always coordinate violent scenes with the other player OOC
- Agree on the outcome or use server mechanics (dice rolls, health systems)
- Do not kill another player's character without explicit consent (permanent death rules apply)
- Play the medical consequences — stab wounds require EMS, recovery, and potential transfer
- Guards will respond — be prepared for the aftermath (solitary, added charges, investigation)

> `/me walks up beside the man at the phone bank, stands shoulder to shoulder, and speaks without turning his head.`
> "You've got a debt. You were supposed to pay it last week. This is the last time someone talks to you about it."

The threat itself is RP. The violence does not always have to happen — sometimes the implication is enough.

## Making Alliances

Survival in prison depends on who you know.

**Types of alliances:**
- **Racial / ethnic group** — The default grouping. Provides safety and structure.
- **Gang affiliation** — Outside gang connections carry into the prison. Your gang has your back.
- **Mutual interest** — Two people with complementary skills or resources — one has commissary money, the other has muscle.
- **Cellmate bond** — Sharing a cell creates a unique relationship. You trust this person or you do not sleep.
- **Debtor relationship** — Someone owes you, or you owe them. Obligation creates connection.

> `/me sits on the bunk and looks at the man on the lower bed.`
> "Look, we don't have to be friends. But we share this box. I don't touch your stuff, you don't touch mine. We watch each other's backs in the yard. That's the deal. Cool?"

## Release and Re-Entry

Getting out is a moment your character has been waiting for — and it is harder than they expected.

**The release process:**
1. Sentence completed or parole granted
2. Processing — return of personal belongings, paperwork
3. Walk out the gate — the moment itself is a scene
4. First steps of freedom — who picks you up? Where do you go?
5. Re-entry — the world moved on without you. Friends changed. Territory shifted. Nothing is the same.

> `/me steps through the gate and squints in the sunlight. The clothes they gave him back do not fit right anymore. He stands on the sidewalk and looks both ways, like he forgot which direction the city is.`

**Post-release RP challenges:**
- Finding housing when you have a record
- Getting a job when no one will hire you
- Reconnecting with people who moved on
- Staying away from the life that put you inside
- Parole obligations — check-ins, drug tests, curfews
- The pull of old habits when legitimate options feel impossible

---

## Final Thoughts

Prison RP strips your character down to who they really are. No guns, no cars, no money, no territory. Just a person in a box, trying to survive until the door opens again. The alliances you form, the enemies you make, the decisions you face when there is nowhere to run — these define your character more than any heist or shootout ever could.

Do your time. Tell your story. Come out different — or come out the same and face what that means.
