# Organized Crime Guide — Mafia, Cartel, and Syndicate Roleplay

Organized crime is the pinnacle of criminal RP. It is not street-level chaos — it is calculated, patient, and layered. The mafia don who owns half the restaurants in Little Seoul. The cartel boss who controls every gram of cocaine entering Los Santos through the docks. The syndicate leader who has a city councilman on speed dial and a judge who owes them a favor. These are characters who operate behind curtains, move through legitimate society, and only resort to violence when every other tool has failed.

This guide covers the structure, strategy, and storytelling of organized crime RP — from building a family to running an empire, managing internal politics, and surviving the inevitable investigation that comes when you reach the top.

---

## What Separates Organized Crime from Gang RP

Gangs operate on the street. Organized crime operates on every level.

| Aspect | Gang RP | Organized Crime RP |
|---|---|---|
| Territory | Street corners, neighborhoods | Entire industries, supply chains, political spheres |
| Income | Drug sales, robbery, extortion | Diversified — drugs, gambling, real estate, legitimate business, corruption |
| Violence | Frequent, visible | Rare, targeted, surgical |
| Structure | Informal hierarchy | Rigid, formal hierarchy with clear rules |
| Public image | Feared on the street | Respected in public, feared behind closed doors |
| Law enforcement | Beat cops, gang unit | FBI-equivalent, organized crime task force |
| Timeline | Months | Years — these stories unfold slowly |

Organized crime is a long game. If you want fast action, rob a store. If you want to build something that shapes the entire server, build an organization.

## Organizational Structure

Every organized crime group needs a clear chain of command. Ambiguity creates chaos — and in this world, chaos gets people killed.

### Traditional Mafia Structure

| Rank | Title | Role |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Don / Boss | Ultimate authority. Makes all final decisions. Insulated from direct criminal activity. |
| 2 | Underboss | Second in command. Manages day-to-day operations. Acts as boss when the Don is unavailable. |
| 3 | Consigliere | Advisor. Not in the chain of command but counsels the Don on strategy, conflicts, and decisions. |
| 4 | Caporegime (Captain) | Runs a crew of soldiers. Manages specific operations or territories. Reports to the Underboss. |
| 5 | Soldier (Made Man) | Full member of the family. Carries out operations, earns money, follows orders. |
| 6 | Associate | Works with the family but is not a formal member. Earns through the organization but has no protection or vote. |

### Cartel Structure

| Rank | Title | Role |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Jefe / Patrón | Head of the cartel. Controls the entire operation. |
| 2 | Segundo | Right hand. Manages logistics, supply, and distribution networks. |
| 3 | Teniente (Lieutenant) | Oversees a region or specific operation. Commands a team. |
| 4 | Sicario | Enforcer. Handles violence, intimidation, and eliminations. |
| 5 | Halcón (Hawk) | Lookout and intelligence. Watches for police, rivals, and threats. |
| 6 | Operador | Worker. Cooks product, runs deliveries, handles labor. |

### Syndicate / Modern Organization

| Rank | Title | Role |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Director / Chairman | Leads the organization. Often appears as a legitimate businessman. |
| 2 | Vice Director | Manages operations. Primary liaison between leadership and crews. |
| 3 | Division Head | Runs a specific branch — drugs, weapons, financial crimes, real estate. |
| 4 | Operative | Experienced member who handles assignments across divisions. |
| 5 | Recruit | New member under evaluation. Limited access to information and operations. |

Choose the structure that fits your group's cultural identity and RP style. You can modify these templates — what matters is that everyone knows their place and respects the chain.

## Legitimate Business Fronts

The heart of organized crime is the appearance of legitimacy. Your public face must be clean.

**Ideal front businesses:**

| Business Type | Why It Works | Location Ideas |
|---|---|---|
| Restaurant / Italian bistro | Cash-heavy, social hub, easy to inflate revenue | Little Seoul, Vespucci, Pillbox Hill |
| Import/export company | Justifies large transactions, warehouse access, shipping containers | Elysian Island, LSIA area, docks |
| Car dealership | High-value inventory, cash transactions, vehicle access | Burton, Rockford Hills, Pillbox Hill |
| Construction company | Justifies property access, heavy equipment, large payroll | Citywide — wherever there is development |
| Nightclub / bar | Cash-intensive, social center, entertainment cover | Del Perro, Vinewood, Downtown |
| Waste management | Access to disposal methods, citywide routes, government contracts | La Mesa, El Burro Heights |
| Real estate firm | Money laundering through property, legal ownership cover | Rockford Hills, Downtown |
| Laundromat / car wash | Classic cash business, low scrutiny, easy to inflate numbers | Strawberry, Davis, Rancho |

**Running the front:**
- The business must actually function. Serve food. Sell cars. Wash clothes. A front that never has customers is immediately suspicious.
- Keep clean books that an auditor could review. The dirty money gets mixed in carefully.
- Employ legitimate workers who have no idea what happens in the back office.
- The front is also a meeting place, a safe space for the organization, and a symbol of its power.

> `/me sits at the corner booth of the restaurant, a glass of wine on the table and a ledger open beside it. To anyone watching, he is just the owner reviewing the week's numbers.`

## Protection Rackets

Protection is the oldest organized crime revenue stream. You offer "insurance" — and the insured pays because the alternative is worse.

**How to run a racket:**
1. Identify businesses in your area of influence
2. Approach the owner personally — be polite, professional, and clear
3. Offer protection from "the dangers of running a business in this part of town"
4. Set a reasonable price — high enough to be profitable, low enough that they pay without resistance
5. Collect regularly — same person, same time, every week
6. Actually provide the service — if someone causes problems for your client, handle it

**The conversation:**
> "Mr. Chen, I appreciate you taking the time. I know running a business down here is not easy. People break windows, steal inventory, cause problems for your customers. My associates and I can make sure that does not happen. Five hundred a week. You will never have a problem again."

**The unspoken threat:**
The beauty of a well-run racket is that you never explicitly threaten anyone. The implication does the work. If they stop paying, problems start happening — broken windows, missing deliveries, customers scared away. You never say you did it. You just offer to solve it again.

## Political Corruption

True power does not come from the barrel of a gun — it comes from the signature on a permit.

**How organized crime corrupts officials:**
- **Financial support** — Fund a politician's campaign. They owe you when they win.
- **Blackmail** — Collect information about officials' indiscretions. Use it when needed.
- **Bribery** — Direct payments for specific actions — zoning approvals, police reassignments, charges dropped.
- **Favors** — Do something for them that cannot be done through legal channels. Now they are in your pocket.
- **Gradual compromise** — Start with a small request. Then a bigger one. Then an even bigger one. By the time they realize how deep they are, they cannot get out.

**Corrupted official value:**

| Official Type | What They Provide |
|---|---|
| City Council member | Zoning changes, business permits, blocking investigations |
| Judge | Favorable rulings, reduced sentences, dismissed cases |
| Police captain | Warning of raids, reassigning investigators, losing evidence |
| District attorney | Dropping charges, plea deals, selective prosecution |
| Building inspector | Overlooking code violations, approving unsafe properties |
| Port authority | Access to shipping containers, customs avoidance |

> "Your Honor, my client has been a pillar of this community for fifteen years. He owns three businesses that employ forty people. These charges are based on the testimony of a known criminal who has every reason to lie. I respectfully request a dismissal."

## The Code of Silence — Omertà

Omertà is the foundation of organized crime. It is absolute, non-negotiable silence.

**What omertà means:**
- Never speak to law enforcement about the organization. Ever.
- Never acknowledge the existence of the organization to outsiders.
- Never discuss internal business in public or over the phone.
- Accept punishment rather than betray the family.
- Solve all problems internally — courts and police are for civilians.

**Breaking omertà:**
A member who breaks the code faces the harshest punishment the organization can deliver. This is not a guideline — it is the rule that holds everything together. Without it, the organization does not exist.

> `/me places a hand on the young man's shoulder and speaks quietly.`
> "Listen to me carefully. When they bring you in — and they will — you say nothing. Not a word. Not a name. Not a place. Nothing. You take whatever they give you. And when you get out, we will take care of your family. But if you talk? We cannot protect you from what happens next."

## Territory Disputes Between Organizations

When two organizations claim the same territory or resource, the conflict is different from gang warfare. It is chess, not checkers.

**Stages of organizational conflict:**

| Stage | Actions | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| Positioning | Expanding into contested areas, testing boundaries | Cautious, calculating |
| Negotiation | Sit-downs between leadership, proposed divisions | Diplomatic, formal |
| Economic warfare | Undercutting prices, poaching clients, disrupting supply chains | Aggressive but non-violent |
| Intimidation | Targeted messages — not attacks, but reminders of capability | Threatening but controlled |
| Proxy conflict | Using third parties (gangs, independent criminals) to harass the rival | Deniable, strategic |
| Direct conflict | Targeted violence against key figures, not foot soldiers | Surgical, devastating |
| Resolution | New agreement, absorption, or destruction of one side | Final — rarely revisited |

**The sit-down:**
> Two families agree to meet at a neutral location — a restaurant neither controls, a warehouse on neutral ground. Leaders bring one advisor each. Weapons are checked at the door. A mediator — someone respected by both sides — facilitates the discussion. The tone is calm, respectful, and laced with tension.

> "We have no interest in what happens south of the canal. That has always been yours. But the port is shared water, and your people have been taxing our shipments. That stops. Or we find a way to make it stop."

## Hierarchy and Succession

Power transitions are the most dangerous moments in any organization.

**How succession works:**
- **Clear succession** — The Don names an underboss who takes over when the time comes. Smoothest transition.
- **Contested succession** — Multiple capos believe they should lead. Internal politicking, alliances, and potentially violence.
- **Power vacuum** — Leadership is suddenly removed (arrested, killed, fled) with no clear successor. Factions form, the organization fractures.
- **Hostile takeover** — An ambitious underling seizes control through force, blackmail, or betrayal.

Each succession scenario creates incredible RP. A smooth transition means the organization remains strong but internal tensions simmer. A contested succession means weeks of political maneuvering, secret meetings, and possibly bloodshed.

## International Connections

The best organizations have reach beyond Los Santos.

**How to RP international connections:**
- Reference off-screen suppliers, partners, or parent organizations in other cities or countries
- Receive phone calls or messages from contacts abroad that drive storylines
- Handle shipments arriving from "overseas" at the docks or airport
- Use international connections as leverage — "My associates in Bogotá are watching this situation closely"
- Create tension when international partners make demands the local organization struggles to meet

You do not need to physically go to another country. The threat, the influence, and the obligations of international connections create pressure from a distance.

## Handling Betrayal

Betrayal is inevitable. How the organization handles it defines its character.

**Types of betrayal:**
- **Informing to police** — The most severe. The member broke omertà.
- **Skimming money** — Stealing from the family's earnings.
- **Unauthorized operations** — Running side businesses without approval.
- **Conspiring with rivals** — Communicating or working with enemy organizations.
- **Attempted coup** — Trying to take power without legitimacy.

**The response process:**
1. Investigate quietly — gather evidence before acting
2. Bring the evidence to leadership
3. Give the accused a chance to explain (or do not, depending on the severity)
4. Leadership decides the punishment — demotion, exile, violence, or worse
5. Execute the decision cleanly and send a message to the rest of the organization

> `/me sets a photograph on the table in front of Sal and slides it forward with one finger.`
> "That is you. Last Thursday. At the coffee shop on Prosperity Street. With Detective Morrison. You want to tell me what you two were talking about?"

## Common RP Scenarios

**The initiation dinner:**
> The family gathers at the restaurant after hours. Candles on every table. Wine poured. A new associate is brought before the Don. He recites the oath. His finger is pricked. A prayer is spoken in Italian. He is now a made man — a member of the family until death. Everyone raises a glass.

**The federal investigation:**
> An FBI task force has been building a case for months. Wiretaps. Surveillance photos. A cooperating witness. The Don's consigliere discovers the investigation through a corrupt contact and now the family must decide — dismantle operations, eliminate the witness, or prepare for war with the federal government.

**The succession crisis:**
> The Don is arrested on a RICO charge. Three capos each believe they should lead. One has seniority. One has the most soldiers. One has the most money. The underboss tries to hold things together while alliances form and break in the shadows. The family has never been more vulnerable — and their enemies know it.

---

## Final Thoughts

Organized crime RP is not for the impatient. It requires subtlety, long-term planning, and the ability to play a character whose greatest weapon is a handshake and a smile. The violence is real but rare. The money is enormous but always at risk. The power is absolute but fragile.

Build your empire. Protect your family. Trust no one completely. And remember — the higher you climb, the farther you fall.
