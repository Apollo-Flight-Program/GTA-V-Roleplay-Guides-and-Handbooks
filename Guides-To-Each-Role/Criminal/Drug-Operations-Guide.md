# Drug Operations Guide — Manufacturing and Distribution RP

The drug trade is the economic heartbeat of criminal roleplay. It touches every level of the underworld — from the chemist cooking product in a trailer outside Sandy Shores to the kingpin who never touches the merchandise but controls the entire supply chain through six layers of separation. A well-played drug operation creates RP for dozens of players: manufacturers, distributors, dealers, customers, rival suppliers, corrupt cops, and the task force trying to bring it all down.

This guide covers the full lifecycle of drug RP — growing, cooking, distributing, selling, laundering the money, and surviving the consequences.

---

## The Drug Ecosystem

Understanding where you fit in the chain determines your RP experience.

| Role | Position in Chain | Risk Level | Income Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Grower / Cook | Production | High — manufacturing charges are severe | Medium — sells wholesale |
| Supplier / Wholesaler | Bulk distribution | High — large quantities, long sentences | High — buys low, sells in bulk |
| Lieutenant / Distributor | Regional control | Medium–High — manages dealers and territory | High — takes a cut from everything below |
| Street Dealer | Retail sales | Medium — most frequent police contact | Low–Medium — small transactions add up |
| Runner / Mule | Transportation | Medium — carries product between points | Low — paid per trip |
| Lookout | Security | Low — watches for police, does not handle product | Very Low — small flat fee |
| Money Launderer | Financial | Low–Medium — rarely handles drugs directly | Medium — percentage-based fee |

Each role creates completely different RP experiences. A street dealer interacts with dozens of customers daily. A cook might see no one for days while producing a batch. A supplier manages relationships and logistics. Find the role that matches your character.

## Growing and Cooking Operations

Production is where the supply chain begins. Without product, nothing moves.

### Marijuana Growing

Growing weed requires a location, equipment, and patience.

**Location options:**
- **Indoor grow house** — Rented property in a quiet neighborhood. Blacked-out windows, ventilation running constantly, high power consumption that might attract attention.
- **Outdoor farm** — Hidden in the hills above Vinewood, in the forests near Paleto Bay, or on a secluded property in Grapeseed. Lower overhead but harder to protect and dependent on weather.
- **Warehouse grow** — Large-scale operation in a warehouse in El Burro Heights or La Mesa. High output but requires significant investment and security.

**The growing process (RP it step by step):**
1. Acquire seeds or clones from a supplier
2. Set up lighting, irrigation, and ventilation
3. Plant and monitor growth over time (RP the daily checks)
4. Watch for problems — pests, mold, power outages, equipment failure
5. Harvest when ready — trim, dry, and cure
6. Package for distribution

> `/me checks the pH levels on the nutrient reservoir and adjusts the mix, making a note on the clipboard hanging from the wall.`

### Methamphetamine Cooking

Meth production is dangerous, volatile, and attracts intense police attention.

**Location options:**
- Trailers in Sandy Shores or Stab City — remote, disposable, classic
- Rented houses in quiet residential areas — risky but less expected
- Industrial locations in La Mesa or El Burro Heights — blends with legitimate operations
- Mobile RV labs — can relocate when heat builds up

**The cooking process:**
1. Source precursor chemicals — pseudoephedrine, methylamine, solvents
2. Set up a lab with proper equipment — glassware, heating elements, ventilation
3. Follow the cook process — measure, mix, heat, monitor
4. Handle waste carefully — dumped chemicals leave evidence and attract attention
5. Test the product for purity
6. Package and distribute

> `/me adjusts the temperature on the hotplate, watching the liquid carefully as it begins to change color. The ventilation fan hums overhead.`
> "Keep that door closed. If anyone smells this from the road, we're done."

**OOC note:** You do not need to know real chemistry. Use general terminology and focus on the roleplay — the tension, the risk, the partnership between characters. Never research or share actual drug manufacturing processes.

## The Supply Chain

Moving product from production to the street requires planning, trust, and logistics.

**The chain:**

```
Producer → Supplier → Distributor → Street Dealer → Customer
```

Each hand the product passes through adds a markup. Each transfer point is a risk — robbery, police interception, or betrayal.

**Supply chain management:**
- **Never let one person know the entire chain.** If the dealer gets arrested, they should not be able to give up the producer.
- **Use intermediaries.** The supplier never meets the street dealer. A runner handles the handoff.
- **Vary your routes and times.** Predictability gets you caught.
- **Use burner phones.** Dispose of them regularly. Communicate in code.
- **Stash houses** — Never keep large quantities at your home or primary location. Use rented apartments, storage units, or trusted associates' locations.

## Dealer Hierarchy

A well-organized drug operation has clear structure.

**Typical structure:**

| Position | Responsibilities | Typical Cut |
|---|---|---|
| Boss / Kingpin | Sets prices, secures supply, makes strategic decisions | 30–50% of profits |
| Lieutenant | Manages a team of dealers in a territory | 15–25% of territory revenue |
| Senior Dealer | Handles high-volume or high-value clients | Buys at wholesale, keeps markup |
| Street Dealer | Works corners and serves walk-up customers | Buys at dealer price, keeps small markup |
| Runner | Delivers product between stash houses and dealers | Flat fee per run ($200–$500) |

**Pricing structure (server economy dependent):**

| Product | Wholesale Cost (per unit) | Street Price (per unit) | Markup |
|---|---|---|---|
| Marijuana (bag) | $50 – $80 | $100 – $150 | 50–100% |
| Cocaine (gram) | $100 – $150 | $200 – $350 | 100–130% |
| Meth (gram) | $80 – $120 | $150 – $300 | 80–150% |
| Pills (per pill) | $10 – $20 | $30 – $60 | 100–200% |
| Heroin (bag) | $75 – $125 | $150 – $275 | 80–120% |

These numbers should match your server's economy. The point is not the exact dollar amount — it is that every hand in the chain takes a cut, and that creates tension.

## Cutting Product

Cutting drugs stretches supply but risks reputation and safety.

**The dilemma:**
- Cut too much and the product is weak — customers go elsewhere
- Cut too little and you leave money on the table
- Cut with the wrong substances and someone overdoses — creating a massive police response and moral consequences

> "This batch is stepping on my reputation, man. Three people told me the stuff we moved last week was garbage. If we keep cutting it this heavy, we're gonna lose the east side to whoever's selling clean."

**Quality control RP:**
- Test batches before distribution
- Get customer feedback through your dealers
- Address quality complaints immediately — your reputation is your brand
- If a bad batch causes overdoses, prepare for police escalation and community backlash

## Stash Houses

Never keep product where you sleep.

**Stash house principles:**
- Use a location not connected to any known gang member or associate
- Rotate locations periodically — a stash house discovered is a stash house burned
- Minimal traffic — only trusted runners access the location
- Basic security — cameras, reinforced doors, a lookout
- Plausible deniability — the location should look unremarkable from outside

**Common stash house locations:**
- Rented apartments in Mirror Park or Vinewood Hills
- Storage units in La Mesa or El Burro Heights
- Back rooms of legitimate businesses (the back of a barbershop, a restaurant storage room)
- Abandoned properties in Chamberlain Hills or Rancho
- Rural properties in Grapeseed or along Route 68

## Money Laundering

Dirty money needs to become clean money. Large unexplained cash raises questions.

**Laundering methods:**

| Method | How It Works | Risk Level |
|---|---|---|
| Cash-intensive business | Run a car wash, laundromat, or food truck. Mix drug money into daily receipts. | Low — hard to detect if done carefully |
| Real estate | Buy property with cash, sell it later through legal channels. | Medium — large purchases attract attention |
| Smurfing | Break large amounts into small deposits across multiple accounts. | Medium — banks flag unusual patterns |
| Shell companies | Create a fake business that invoices for services never rendered. | Medium–High — audits can expose it |
| Crypto / in-game equivalent | Convert cash to untraceable digital currency. | Varies — depends on server mechanics |
| Gambling | Buy chips with dirty money, cash out clean money after light play. | Medium — casinos watch for this |

> "We're running forty grand a week through the car wash. On paper, that little spot on Innocence Blvd washes three hundred cars a day. Nobody's counting."

## Dealing with Law Enforcement

The police are always watching. How you handle them determines how long your operation survives.

**Counter-surveillance:**
- Know your neighborhood. Spot undercover vehicles — unmarked Crown Vics, surveillance vans, unfamiliar cars parked for hours.
- Use lookouts with radios or phones on every corner you operate.
- Change your patterns — do not deal at the same time and place every day.
- Never discuss operations on the phone. Meet in person for important conversations.
- Have a clean car, clean clothes, and no product on your person when traveling.

**When you get caught:**
1. Say nothing. Ask for a lawyer. Repeat.
2. Do not consent to searches.
3. Do not react to what police claim to know — they may be bluffing.
4. Contact your organization's lawyer immediately.
5. Accept the RP. Getting caught is part of the story.

> `/me keeps his hands on the steering wheel and stares straight ahead as the officer approaches.`
> "Evening, officer. What seems to be the problem?"

## Informants

The most dangerous threat to any drug operation is someone on the inside talking to police.

**Signs of an informant:**
- A member who was arrested and released unusually quickly
- Someone who asks too many questions about the supply chain
- A person who always seems to avoid getting caught while others do not
- New associates who appeared right after a bust

**Handling suspected informants:**
- Feed them false information and see if it reaches the police
- Limit their access to operations without tipping them off
- Investigate quietly — do not accuse without evidence
- If confirmed, the organization's leadership decides the response (which should follow server rules about conflict RP)

**The moral complexity:**
Some informants are coerced. They got caught and the detective gave them a choice — cooperate or do ten years. Playing an informant is some of the most intense RP available. The constant fear of being discovered, the guilt of betraying people who trust you, and the pressure from law enforcement create incredible story opportunities.

## Rival Suppliers

Competition in the drug trade is violent, strategic, and constant.

**Competitive strategies:**
- Undercut their prices to steal customers
- Offer better product quality
- Apply pressure — intimidation, robbery, attacking their dealers
- Turn their people — offer better pay or protection
- Cut off their supply — find their source and disrupt it
- Report them to the police while keeping your own operation hidden

**When rivals move in on your territory:**
1. Assess the threat — how strong are they, who is backing them
2. Attempt diplomacy first — divide territory, share routes, find compromise
3. If diplomacy fails, escalate strategically — hit their money first, not their people
4. Full conflict is the last resort — war attracts police attention that hurts everyone

## Common RP Scenarios

**The first sale:**
> Your hands are shaking. You have a bag in your pocket and a stranger just asked if you are "holding." You look around. No cops. No one watching. You make the exchange in the alley behind the 24/7 in Strawberry. It takes ten seconds. You walk away with cash in your hand and adrenaline in your chest. You are a drug dealer now.

**The bad batch:**
> Three overdoses in one night, all from the same batch. The police are swarming Davis. Your phone is blowing up — dealers are scared, customers are angry, and your lieutenant is demanding answers. Was the product contaminated? Was it cut wrong? Or did your supplier send you something they knew was dangerous?

**The undercover buyer:**
> A new customer has been buying regularly for two weeks. Good money, no problems. Then you notice a wire under their shirt during a handoff. Or maybe you do not notice — and the next time you meet them, ten officers appear from nowhere.

---

## Final Thoughts

Drug RP is not about glamorizing substance abuse — it is about telling stories of ambition, desperation, loyalty, and consequence. The best drug RP involves characters who are in too deep, organizations that are one bust away from collapse, and the constant tension between profit and survival.

Cook the product. Move the weight. Count the money. Watch your back.
