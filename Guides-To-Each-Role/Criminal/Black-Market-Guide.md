# Black Market Guide — Underground Economy Roleplay

Behind every legitimate economy is a shadow one. The black market is where you buy what you cannot get legally — weapons, documents, information, modified vehicles, and services that no licensed professional would touch. It operates on trust, reputation, and the understanding that everyone involved has something to lose if the operation is exposed. There are no receipts, no refunds, and no calling the police when a deal goes bad.

This guide covers the full spectrum of underground economy RP — from weapons dealing and fencing stolen goods to information brokering, dead drops, and building the trust networks that keep the whole operation alive.

---

## Why Black Market RP Matters

The black market creates connections between criminal factions that would otherwise have no reason to interact. The gang that needs weapons talks to the arms dealer. The arms dealer sources from the import smuggler. The smuggler bribes the port authority contact. Every transaction is a web of relationships, favors, and vulnerabilities.

It also provides:
- **Economic depth** — money and goods flow through channels that mirror real underground economies
- **Cross-faction RP** — dealers serve gangs, independents, and sometimes corrupt officials
- **High-stakes tension** — every transaction carries the risk of robbery, arrest, or betrayal
- **Unique character niches** — arms dealers, fences, forgers, and brokers are distinct roles that no one else fills

## Weapons Dealing

The arms trade is the most dangerous and lucrative corner of the black market.

### Sourcing Weapons

**Where weapons come from:**

| Source | Risk Level | Reliability | Typical Inventory |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stolen from police / military | Very High | Low — sporadic | Service pistols, shotguns, rifles |
| Imported through the docks | High | Medium — depends on contacts | Varied — handguns to automatic weapons |
| Manufactured locally (ghost guns) | Medium | Medium — slow production | Pistols, basic firearms |
| Stolen from gun stores / civilians | Medium | Low — opportunistic | Whatever was available |
| Other dealers (resale) | Low–Medium | High — established supply chain | Varies by dealer |

> `/me opens the trunk of the car in the parking garage, pulls back a blanket, and reveals four handguns and two shotguns laid out in foam cutouts.`
> "Everything here is clean. Serial numbers filed. Never been used in anything. You want to test fire, I know a spot out past Raton Canyon."

### Pricing Weapons

| Weapon Type | Black Market Price Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Pistol (basic) | $2,000 – $5,000 | Common, easy to source |
| Heavy pistol / revolver | $4,000 – $8,000 | More stopping power, less common |
| Shotgun (pump) | $5,000 – $10,000 | Effective, intimidating, bulky |
| SMG / machine pistol | $8,000 – $15,000 | Compact automatic, high demand |
| Assault rifle | $15,000 – $30,000 | Serious hardware, attracts serious buyers |
| Sniper rifle | $20,000 – $40,000 | Specialized, rare, specific clientele |
| Body armor | $3,000 – $8,000 | Consumable, always in demand |
| Ammunition (bulk) | $500 – $2,000 per box | Consistent revenue stream |

Prices vary by server economy. The key is that black market weapons cost significantly more than legal alternatives — that markup is the price of anonymity and untraceable hardware.

### Rules of Arms Dealing

- **Know your buyer.** Selling to someone who gets caught with your weapon creates a trail back to you.
- **Never sell from your home.** Use neutral locations — parking garages, remote roads, industrial lots.
- **Limit your inventory exposure.** Do not carry everything at once. Use stash locations.
- **Change your meeting spots.** Never use the same location twice in a row.
- **Have security.** A partner watching from a distance in case the deal goes bad.

## Fencing Stolen Goods

A fence buys stolen property and resells it through legitimate or semi-legitimate channels.

### What Fences Buy

| Item Category | Buy Price (% of value) | Resale Method |
|---|---|---|
| Electronics (phones, laptops) | 15–30% of retail | Resold through pawn shops or online |
| Jewelry | 20–40% of value | Melted down or resold to collectors |
| Vehicles (whole) | 20–35% of value | VIN swap and resale, or parted out |
| Vehicle parts (chopped) | 30–50% of part value | Sold to mechanics, shops, or direct |
| Designer goods (clothes, bags) | 15–25% of retail | Resold at markets or through connections |
| Firearms (stolen) | 40–60% of street value | Resold on the black market |
| Art / collectibles | 10–30% of appraised value | Private collectors, underground auctions |

**Running a fencing operation:**
1. Establish a reputation among thieves — they need to know you buy
2. Set fair prices — too low and thieves go elsewhere; too high and you lose profit margin
3. Move product quickly — holding stolen goods increases risk
4. Have a disposal method — a pawn shop front, a storage unit, connections to out-of-city buyers
5. Inspect everything — stolen goods with tracking devices, GPS, or identifiable markings are liabilities

> `/me examines the watch under the desk lamp, turning it over to check for engravings.`
> "Rolex Submariner. Real. But there's an engraving on the back — 'To Michael, Love Sarah.' I can move it, but I've gotta send it out of state. I'll give you eight hundred."

## Counterfeit Documents

Fake IDs, forged permits, cloned licenses, and falsified records. Document forgery is a specialist trade that serves criminals, undocumented characters, and anyone who needs to be someone else.

### Document Types and Pricing

| Document | Purpose | Price Range |
|---|---|---|
| Fake driver's license | Basic identification, age verification | $500 – $1,500 |
| Forged vehicle registration | Clean a stolen car on paper | $1,000 – $3,000 |
| Cloned license plates | Match a stolen car to a clean VIN | $500 – $1,500 |
| Fake passport | Flee the city, create a new identity | $3,000 – $10,000 |
| Forged business license | Operate an unlicensed business | $1,500 – $5,000 |
| Counterfeit money | Direct financial fraud | Varies — typically sold at 20–30% of face value |
| Falsified medical records | Fake prescriptions, insurance fraud | $500 – $2,000 |
| Forged gun permits | Carry illegally obtained firearms "legally" | $2,000 – $5,000 |

> `/me slides a manila envelope across the table.`
> "California driver's license, clean VIN registration for a 2019 Benefactor Schafter, and matching plates. The name is David Torres. Memorize it. If someone runs this, it will come back clean for at least thirty days before anyone notices the duplicate."

**The forger character:**
A document forger is a specialist. They need equipment (a printer, laminator, blank card stock, software), knowledge (what real documents look like down to the holograms and micro-printing), and discretion. They rarely leave their workshop and they never meet clients at their production location.

## Illegal Vehicle Modifications

Some modifications cannot be done at a legitimate shop — because they are designed for crime.

### Modification Types

| Modification | Purpose | Price Range |
|---|---|---|
| VIN swap / re-stamping | Change vehicle identity to clean a stolen car | $2,000 – $5,000 |
| Police scanner installation | Monitor law enforcement radio traffic | $1,000 – $3,000 |
| Hidden compartments (traps) | Conceal weapons, drugs, or cash in the vehicle | $2,000 – $8,000 |
| Ramming reinforcement | Strengthen front/rear bumpers for pursuit situations | $3,000 – $7,000 |
| Plate flipper | Electronically swap between two sets of plates | $3,000 – $6,000 |
| Engine swap (performance) | Upgrade engine beyond what legal shops will install | $5,000 – $15,000 |
| Kill switch | Hidden engine cutoff only the driver knows about | $500 – $1,500 |

> `/me pops the trunk and lifts the false floor panel, revealing a compartment large enough to hold several kilos of product or a pair of rifles.`
> "Hydraulic latch. You press the brake three times and hold the trunk release for two seconds. Otherwise, it's just a normal trunk floor. No drug dog is finding this."

These modifications require a mechanic who is willing to do the work — and willing to keep quiet about it. That mechanic charges a premium for their silence.

## Underground Fight Clubs

Organized fighting in basements, warehouses, and parking garages. No rules. No referees. Cash on the barrel.

**Setting up a fight club:**
1. Find a location — warehouses in El Burro Heights, basements in La Mesa, parking structures after hours
2. Recruit fighters — street toughs, desperate characters, thrill seekers
3. Set rules (or lack thereof) — bare knuckle, weapons allowed, first blood, knockout only
4. Handle betting — take a percentage of every bet as the house cut (10–20%)
5. Charge admission — entry fee for spectators
6. Security — someone watches the door and someone watches for police

**Fight club economics:**

| Revenue Source | Typical Take |
|---|---|
| Fighter entry fee | $500 – $2,000 per fighter |
| Spectator admission | $100 – $500 per head |
| House cut on bets | 10–20% of total wagered |
| Fighter purse (winner) | 60–70% of combined entry fees |
| Fighter purse (loser) | 0–10% — losing does not pay |

> `/me stands in the center of the crowd and raises both hands.`
> "Alright, listen up! Tonight we got two fighters. In this corner — Mikey Two-Knuckles from Strawberry. In the other — Big Ray from El Burro. Bare knuckle. No weapons. Fight ends when someone can't stand up. Place your bets now."

**OOC considerations:** Fight RP should be coordinated between participants. Use `/me` and `/roll` or server fight mechanics. Both fighters should agree on the outcome beforehand or use a fair system. Do not powergame — take hits, show damage, and make it a scene.

## Gambling Dens

Illegal gambling operations — poker rooms, dice games, sports betting, and numbers running.

**Setting up an underground casino:**
- Find a discreet location with a back entrance — apartment basement, back room of a business, warehouse office
- Provide tables, chairs, cards, and chips (or cash games)
- Hire a dealer if running table games
- Have security at the door — invitation only
- The house takes a rake on every pot (5–10%)

**Games commonly run:**

| Game | House Edge | Player Appeal | RP Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| Texas Hold'em poker | Rake-based | High — skill and luck combined | Excellent — tension, bluffing, character dynamics |
| Blackjack | Built into rules | Medium — fast, easy to learn | Good — dealer interaction |
| Craps / dice | Built into rules | High — group energy, excitement | Great — crowd scenes |
| Sports betting | Juice / vig | High — everyone has opinions | Good — requires bookmaker character |
| Numbers / lottery | House sets odds | Low–Medium — daily routine | Moderate — community interaction |

> `/me deals the river card face-up on the table and leans back, watching the two remaining players.`
> "Ace of spades. Pot's sitting at twelve grand. What's it gonna be, gentlemen?"

## Information Brokering

Information is the most valuable commodity on the black market. It cannot be stolen back, it cannot be traced, and it can destroy or save lives.

**What information brokers sell:**

| Information Type | Value | Typical Buyer |
|---|---|---|
| Police patrol routes and schedules | $1,000 – $5,000 | Criminals planning operations |
| Upcoming raid locations | $5,000 – $20,000 | Drug operations, stash house owners |
| Personal information on a target | $500 – $3,000 | Anyone with a grudge or a plan |
| Business financial records | $2,000 – $10,000 | Competitors, extortionists |
| Witness identities in criminal cases | $5,000 – $25,000 | Defendants facing serious charges |
| Corrupt official identities | $3,000 – $15,000 | Criminals seeking leverage or protection |
| Rival gang operations and plans | $2,000 – $10,000 | Opposing gangs or organizations |

**How information is gathered:**
- Paid informants in various factions — a bartender who overhears conversations, a clerk at the courthouse
- Hacking (RP) — accessing digital records, phone data, security cameras
- Surveillance — physical observation of targets over time
- Social engineering — befriending people to extract information naturally

> `/me slides a phone across the bar table with a single photo on the screen — a police detective entering a motel with someone who is not his wife.`
> "Detective Harmon. Vice division. He has been looking into your operation for three weeks. Maybe he stops looking. Maybe this photo finds its way to his wife. Or his captain. That's up to you. The information is five grand."

## Dead Drops and Coded Communications

When face-to-face meetings are too risky, the black market uses indirect methods.

### Dead Drops

A dead drop is a predetermined location where one party leaves goods or information for another to retrieve — without ever meeting.

**How to set up a dead drop:**
1. Choose a location both parties can access without suspicion — a park bench, a trash can, behind a dumpster, inside a locker
2. Agree on a signal that the drop has been made — a chalk mark on a wall, a specific text message, a missed call
3. The first party places the item and signals
4. The second party retrieves the item within the agreed window
5. Neither party is present at the same time

**Good dead drop locations in Los Santos:**
- Trash cans in Legion Square
- Behind the dumpster at the 24/7 on Route 68
- Lockers at Vespucci Beach
- Under a specific bench in Mirror Park
- Inside the mailbox at a predetermined house in Paleto Bay

### Coded Communications

Never discuss business in plain language over the phone or radio.

**Common code systems:**

| Real Meaning | Code Example |
|---|---|
| Drugs | "Groceries," "t-shirts," "packages" |
| Weapons | "Tools," "hardware," "party supplies" |
| Money | "Books," "tickets," "lunch" |
| Police | "Delivery drivers," "weather," "blue" |
| A specific location | Use numbered street references or nicknames |
| Meet in person | "Let's grab coffee" |
| Abort / danger | "My grandmother is sick" |

> Phone call: "Hey, I got those t-shirts you wanted. Four mediums and two larges. You want me to drop them at the usual spot, or you want to pick them up at the coffee shop on third?"

Translation: "I have six bags of product — four regular and two large. Dead drop or in-person meeting?"

## Trust Networks

The black market runs on trust. Without it, nothing moves.

**Building trust:**
- Start with small transactions — prove reliability before moving to high-value deals
- Be consistent — deliver what you promise, when you promise it
- Protect your clients — never reveal buyer or seller identities
- Handle disputes fairly — if product is bad, make it right
- Develop a reputation — word of mouth is your advertising

**Vouching:**
New contacts enter the network through vouching. An existing trusted member introduces someone they personally trust. If the new person betrays the network, the person who vouched for them shares responsibility.

> "This is Ray. I've known him for three years. He's solid. He needs plates for a car and a clean registration. Can you help him out?"

The fence helps Ray because they trust the person who brought him. If Ray turns out to be an informant, both Ray and his voucher have a problem.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The bad deal:**
> You show up to buy a pistol. The seller shows you the weapon, you hand over the cash, and the gun turns out to be broken — firing pin removed. You have been scammed. Do you eat the loss? Do you track the seller down? Do you put the word out that they cannot be trusted? Your response defines your reputation.

**The police sting:**
> A new buyer has been purchasing small amounts for weeks, building trust. Then they want a large order — ten pistols and body armor. The deal is set. You arrive and the buyer is already there, smiling. Then you notice the van parked at the far end of the lot. The buyer reaches for their ear. Sting operation.

**The desperate forger:**
> A woman comes to you needing documents — a new identity, a clean passport, everything. She is running from someone. She has enough money for half of what she needs. Do you help her for free? Do you take what she has and give her less? Do you turn her away? Or do you help her and she now owes you a favor — the most valuable currency there is?

---

## Final Thoughts

The black market is not a place — it is a network of relationships built on mutual need and fragile trust. Every deal is a negotiation. Every transaction carries risk. Every contact is simultaneously an asset and a liability. The best black market operators are not the ones who move the most product — they are the ones who build the deepest trust while revealing the least about themselves.

Deal in shadows. Trade in trust. Keep your secrets. Keep your friends closer and your leverage closest.
