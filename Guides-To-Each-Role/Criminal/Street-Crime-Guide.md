# Street Crime Guide — Petty and Street-Level Criminal Roleplay

Not every criminal starts with a master plan. Most start with empty pockets, bad options, and a moment of opportunity. Street crime is where criminal careers begin — the muggings, car thefts, shoplifts, and acts of desperation that put your character on the wrong side of the law for the first time. It is raw, personal, and close to the ground. There are no boardrooms or offshore accounts here — just adrenaline, risk, and the constant question of whether the next score is worth the consequences.

This guide covers every aspect of street-level criminal RP — from petty theft to building a reputation, escalating your career, and understanding the consequences that follow.

---

## Why Street Crime RP Matters

Street crime is the foundation of the criminal ecosystem. Without it, there are no future kingpins, no gang leaders, no criminal masterminds. Everyone starts somewhere, and that somewhere is usually a stolen wallet or a boosted car.

Street crime also provides:
- **Organic police interaction** — patrol officers need calls to respond to
- **Victim RP** — civilians who get robbed, carjacked, or threatened get a scene to play
- **Escalation opportunities** — a petty thief today can be a heist planner in three months
- **Low-barrier entry** — new players can start criminal RP without an organization or connections
- **World building** — a city where crime happens at the street level feels alive and real

## Types of Street Crime

### Mugging

The classic. Approach a target, threaten them, take their valuables.

**How to mug properly:**
1. Choose your target — someone alone, in a quiet area, unlikely to resist
2. Approach from a position of advantage — behind them, in an alley, at a dead end
3. Announce your intentions — weapon drawn or implied
4. Make demands — wallet, phone, jewelry, keys
5. Take what you want and leave quickly
6. Do not linger. Do not monologue. Do not escalate unnecessarily.

> `/me steps out from behind the dumpster and blocks the alley exit, pulling a knife from his waistband.`
> "Don't scream. Don't run. Give me your wallet and your phone. Do it fast and you walk away."

**Choosing your location:**

| Location | Risk Level | Foot Traffic | Police Proximity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Back alleys in Strawberry | Low | Low | Medium |
| Parking garages (Pillbox, Rockford) | Low–Medium | Low at night | Varies |
| Under Del Perro Pier | Low | Low at night | Low |
| Vinewood back streets | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| Mirror Park side streets | Low | Low | Low |
| La Mesa industrial area | Low | Low | Low |
| Davis side streets | Medium | Medium | Medium–High |

**OOC etiquette:**
- Give the victim time to respond and roleplay the scene
- Do not rob someone who just got robbed by someone else
- Take valuables proportional to the scene — do not strip them of everything they own
- If the victim complies, do not hurt them
- The scene should take a few minutes, not thirty seconds — make it RP, not a transaction

### Car Theft (Boosting)

Stealing cars is a staple of street crime. Whether you are taking a Vapid Dominator for a joyride or stealing a Pegassi Zentorno for a buyer, car theft creates immediate action.

**Methods of stealing vehicles:**

| Method | Time Required | Noise Level | Skill Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slim jim / lockout tool | 30–60 seconds | Silent | Low |
| Smash and grab (break window) | 10 seconds | Loud — attracts attention | None |
| Hotwiring | 45–90 seconds | Quiet | Medium |
| Key theft (steal keys from owner) | Varies | Silent | Medium — requires social engineering |
| Carjacking (force the driver out) | Immediate | Loud — direct confrontation | Low — but high risk |

> `/me crouches beside the driver's door of the parked Schyster Fusilade, slides a slim jim between the window and the door frame, and works the lock mechanism.`

**What to do with the stolen car:**
- **Joyride** — Drive it around, enjoy it, ditch it. Low reward, low consequence.
- **Chop shop** — Deliver it to a buyer who strips it for parts. Moderate reward.
- **VIN swap / resell** — Change the vehicle identification and sell it. High reward, high complexity.
- **Use for a crime** — Steal a car to use in a robbery, then abandon it. Disposable transportation.
- **Personal use** — Swap plates and keep it. Risky — the car is on a stolen list.

### Petty Theft and Shoplifting

Small-scale theft from businesses. Low reward, but also low risk when done right.

**How to shoplift in RP:**
1. Enter a store as a normal customer
2. Browse naturally — do not rush
3. Use `/me` to conceal items — jacket pockets, bag, waistband
4. Move toward the exit casually
5. Leave without paying

> `/me pretends to look at magazines near the register, then slides two energy drinks into his jacket pocket while the clerk is looking at the security monitor.`

**If you get caught by the clerk:**
This is where RP happens. The clerk confronts you. Do you run? Do you talk your way out? Do you threaten them? Do you put the items back and apologize? Each choice takes the scene in a different direction.

### Vandalism and Property Damage

Tagging, smashing windows, slashing tires, keying cars. Vandalism is not about profit — it is about expression, territory, or spite.

**Types of vandalism RP:**
- **Graffiti / tagging** — Mark territory, leave messages, disrespect rivals
- **Window smashing** — Target businesses or vehicles for intimidation or access
- **Arson** — Setting fire to property. Major escalation. Serious consequences.
- **Keying vehicles** — Personal grudge, petty revenge
- **Tire slashing** — Immobilize a target, send a message

> `/me pulls out a can of spray paint and starts tagging the wall behind the convenience store — the crew's initials in green and black, bold and sloppy.`

### Purse Snatching and Pickpocketing

Quick, low-violence crimes that rely on speed and opportunity.

**Purse snatching:**
- Target someone carrying a visible bag or purse
- Approach from behind or the side at a jog
- Grab the bag and run
- Have a route planned — alleys, corners, a waiting car

> `/me jogs up behind the woman on the sidewalk, grabs the strap of her purse with one hand, and yanks it off her shoulder as he breaks into a sprint toward the alley.`

**Pickpocketing:**
- Get close to the target in a crowd or while they are distracted
- Use `/me` to lift their wallet, phone, or watch
- Walk away calmly — running draws attention

> `/me bumps into the man's shoulder as they pass on the sidewalk, using the contact to slip his wallet out of his back pocket.`
> "Sorry, man. My bad." `/me keeps walking without looking back.`

**OOC consent:** Pickpocketing in particular should be handled with care. Some servers require the victim to have a reasonable chance to notice (a dice roll, a reaction window). Know your server's rules.

## Creating Tension Without Major Violence

Street crime does not have to end in gunfire. Some of the best scenes come from tension alone.

**Techniques for building tension:**
- Stand in someone's path and stare them down without saying anything
- Follow someone for a block before they notice
- Make a vague threat without specifying what you will do
- Show a weapon but do not use it — the implication is the threat
- Invade personal space slowly

> `/me steps into the man's path on the sidewalk and stops. He does not say anything — just stares, jaw set, hands at his sides, blocking the way.`

This approach creates fear, discomfort, and uncertainty without triggering a shootout. The victim has to decide — confront, flee, comply, or call for help. That decision is the RP.

## Escalation of a Criminal Career

Street crime is where you start, not where you stay. Build your way up.

| Phase | Activities | How You Advance |
|---|---|---|
| Desperate newcomer | Shoplifting, mugging, panhandling | Prove you are willing to take risks |
| Petty criminal | Car theft, burglary, small-time dealing | Get noticed by bigger players |
| Reliable earner | Running errands for gangs, regular boosting, fencing goods | Build connections and a reputation |
| Crew member | Joining a team for robberies, handling territory assignments | Demonstrate loyalty and competence |
| Rising criminal | Planning small heists, managing a corner, recruiting others | Show leadership and strategic thinking |
| Established figure | Running operations, mentoring new criminals, earning respect | Your reputation precedes you |

**The progression should be organic.** Do not announce that you are ready for the next level — show it through your actions. The shot-caller who watches a street kid handle a dangerous situation calmly and decides to bring them into the crew — that is the kind of natural advancement that makes criminal RP meaningful.

## Consequences of Getting Caught

Getting caught is not a failure — it is the next scene. How you handle consequences defines your character.

**What happens when you get arrested for street crime:**

| Charge | Typical Consequence | RP Opportunity |
|---|---|---|
| Petty theft / shoplifting | Fine, short hold, release | Learning experience, first brush with law |
| Grand theft auto | Moderate fine, jail time (30–90 min server time) | Booking scene, first real criminal record |
| Mugging / armed robbery | Significant fine, extended jail | Interrogation, lawyer RP, courtroom potential |
| Assault with a weapon | Heavy fine, long jail sentence | Serious legal defense, character defining |
| Vandalism / arson | Fine, community service, or jail | Government interaction, probation RP |
| Evading arrest | Added charges, increased sentence | Chase scene, standoff, foot pursuit |

**Building a criminal record:**
Your record follows you. Getting arrested twice for the same thing means the judge gives you more time. A character with three felonies faces different treatment than a first-time offender. Let your record shape your story.

> "Your Honor, my client is a first-time offender. He made a mistake. He took a candy bar from a convenience store because he hadn't eaten in two days. We ask for leniency."

That sentence only works once. The fifth time your character is in court, the judge remembers your name. That is consequence. That is roleplay.

## Building a Criminal Reputation

Reputation is currency on the street. It opens doors, keeps threats away, and determines who trusts you.

**How reputation is built:**
- **Consistency** — Always follow through on what you say you will do
- **Success** — Pull off jobs cleanly and split fairly
- **Loyalty** — Never snitch, never abandon your crew
- **Toughness** — Handle confrontation without flinching
- **Intelligence** — Be smart about your crimes. Plan ahead. Do not get caught for stupid reasons.

**How reputation is destroyed:**
- Snitching — instant death of credibility
- Burning a partner — stealing from or betraying someone who trusted you
- Getting caught constantly — seen as sloppy and a liability
- Running from every fight — seen as a coward
- All talk, no action — promising big things and delivering nothing

> "You know Marcus from Davis? Yeah, I know him. He's solid. Did a year in Bolingbroke and didn't say a word. If he says he's good for it, he's good for it."

That reputation was built over months of consistent play. There are no shortcuts.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The desperate first crime:**
> Your character has not eaten in two days. They have no money, no job prospects, and the rent is due tomorrow. They stand outside the 24/7 on Strawberry Avenue and watch people walk in and out. Finally, they pull their hoodie up, walk inside, grab food off the shelf, and head for the door. The clerk yells. What do you do?

**The wrong target:**
> You mug someone in an alley near Forum Drive. You take their wallet and phone. An hour later, three guys in green roll up on you. Turns out the person you robbed is the sister of a gang lieutenant. They want the stuff back — and they want something extra for the disrespect.

**The car that won't start:**
> You smash the window of a Benefactor Schafter, hop in, and try to hotwire it. It takes longer than you expected. A couple walks out of the restaurant across the street and stares at you. Then one of them pulls out their phone. You are thirty seconds from a 911 call. The wires spark but the engine does not turn over.

**The partnership:**
> You have been boosting cars alone for two weeks. Another solo thief approaches you — they have a buyer who wants specific models but they need a second person to run lookout. You team up. For the first time, you are not alone. But can you trust them?

## OOC Best Practices for Street Crime

- **Always provide RP to your victims.** A mugging should be a scene, not a five-second interaction.
- **Do not target AFK players.** If someone is clearly not at their keyboard, leave them alone.
- **Vary your targets.** Robbing the same person or the same store repeatedly is not RP — it is harassment.
- **Accept losses.** If a victim fights back and you lose, play the consequences.
- **Respect cooldown rules.** Many servers have cooldown timers between robberies. Follow them.
- **Scale your violence.** A shoplifting does not require a gun. A mugging does not require a murder. Match the force to the crime.

---

## Final Thoughts

Street crime is the proving ground. It is where your character discovers what they are willing to do, what they are capable of, and how far they are willing to fall. Every kingpin started by stealing something small. Every gang leader was once a kid on a corner with empty pockets and a decision to make.

Start small. Learn the streets. Build your name. And decide how far down this road your character is willing to go.
