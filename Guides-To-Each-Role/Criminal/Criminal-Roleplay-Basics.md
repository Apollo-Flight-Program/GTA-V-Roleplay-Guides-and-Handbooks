# Criminal Roleplay Basics — The Foundation of Crime RP

Every server needs criminals. Without crime, there are no police chases, no courtroom dramas, no tense hostage negotiations, and no reason for half the city to exist. Criminal roleplay is not about being a nuisance or racking up a body count — it is about creating conflict, tension, and stakes that make the entire server more alive. A well-played criminal character generates more quality RP in a single evening than most characters produce in a week.

This guide covers the fundamentals of criminal roleplay — how to build a criminal character, how to play the role without breaking server rules, and how to create stories that other players actually want to be part of.

---

## Why Criminal RP Matters for the Ecosystem

Crime is the engine that drives interaction between factions. Without it, police have nothing to do. Lawyers have no clients. Hospitals see fewer patients. News reporters have nothing to cover. The underground economy dries up. Legitimate businesses lose their edge because there is no risk.

A good criminal does not just create problems — they create opportunities for everyone around them.

| Faction | What Criminal RP Creates for Them |
|---|---|
| Police / Sheriff | Investigations, pursuits, arrests, undercover operations |
| EMS / Doctors | Gunshot wounds, stabbings, overdoses, trauma RP |
| Lawyers / Judges | Defense cases, plea deals, trials, appeals |
| Civilians | Witnesses, victims, bystanders pulled into the story |
| Business Owners | Robbery targets, protection racket interactions, shady deals |
| Government | Policy debates, corruption arcs, law reform pressure |
| Other Criminals | Rivalry, alliances, territory disputes, power struggles |

The best criminal characters understand that they exist within a larger story. Getting arrested is not losing — it is the next chapter.

## Types of Criminal Characters

Not every criminal is the same. Choose a type that fits the story you want to tell.

**The Petty Criminal:**
Shoplifts, steals cars, commits small fraud. Lives on the edge of poverty and desperation. Their crimes are about survival, not ambition.

**The Street Hustler:**
Deals drugs on corners, runs small scams, knows everyone in the neighborhood. Streetwise, resourceful, and always looking for the next opportunity.

**The Career Criminal:**
Robs banks, plans heists, operates at a high level. Crime is their profession — they take it seriously, plan meticulously, and treat every job like a business operation.

**The Gang Member:**
Part of a larger organization. Their identity is tied to the group — colors, territory, loyalty. They operate within a hierarchy and answer to leadership.

**The Organized Crime Figure:**
Mafia, cartel, syndicate. They run operations behind legitimate business fronts. They wear suits, speak softly, and control entire supply chains. Violence is a last resort — leverage is preferred.

**The White-Collar Criminal:**
Embezzlement, fraud, money laundering, identity theft. They never get their hands dirty but cause more financial damage than any armed robber ever could.

**The Opportunist:**
Not a full-time criminal, but they will cross the line when the opportunity presents itself. A mechanic who strips stolen cars. A business owner who launders money. A civilian who witnesses a crime and decides to profit from it instead of reporting it.

## Building a Criminal Backstory

Your character needs a reason to be a criminal. "I like crime" is not a backstory.

**Backstory questions to answer:**
- What was their childhood like? Poverty? Abusive household? Privilege they threw away?
- When did they first break the law? What drove them to it?
- Do they see themselves as a criminal, or do they justify their actions?
- What line will they not cross? Everyone has one.
- Who do they care about? Family, friends, a partner?
- What is their long-term goal? Get rich? Get out? Get revenge? Survive?

**Example backstory:**
> Marcus Cole grew up in Davis. His mother worked two jobs and still could not keep the lights on. He started running packages for older guys in the neighborhood at fourteen — did not even know what was in them at first. By eighteen he was dealing. He does not think of himself as a bad person. He is doing what the city forced him to do. He sends money home to his mother every week. His goal is to save enough to get her out of Davis and into a real house. He tells himself he will stop when that happens. He will not.

The best criminal characters have depth. They have people they love, dreams they chase, and contradictions that make them human.

## The Criminal Code — Unwritten Rules of the Life

Every criminal ecosystem has unwritten rules. Break them and you do not just face punishment — you lose respect, allies, and eventually your life.

**The core principles:**

| Rule | What It Means |
|---|---|
| No snitching | Never cooperate with law enforcement. Never name names. Take your charges. |
| Loyalty first | Stand by your crew, your gang, your family. Betrayal is the worst offense. |
| Respect territory | Do not operate in another crew's area without permission. |
| Handle it internally | Disputes within the organization stay within the organization. |
| Earn your place | Reputation is built through action, not talk. Prove yourself before expecting respect. |
| Never involve innocents | Civilians, children, and people outside the life are off-limits (OOC rule on most servers). |
| Pay your debts | If you owe someone money, product, or a favor — pay it. Debts create enemies. |

These rules create natural conflict. What happens when loyalty and self-preservation collide? When snitching could save your life but destroy your reputation? When a friend breaks the code and you have to decide what to do about it?

## Relationship to Law Enforcement RP

Criminal RP and police RP are two sides of the same coin. Neither works without the other.

**How to interact with police well:**
- Give them something to work with. Leave clues. Make mistakes. Be human.
- Getting arrested is RP, not punishment. Play the scene — the interrogation, the booking, the phone call to your lawyer.
- Do not run every single time. Sometimes getting caught creates better stories than getting away.
- Respect the officer's RP the way you want them to respect yours.
- Do not argue mechanics OOC during a scene. If you disagree with something, handle it after.

**The interrogation room:**
This is where some of the best RP happens. The detective lays out what they know. You decide what to admit, what to deny, and what to say nothing about.

> `/me sits back in the chair and folds his arms, glancing at the camera in the corner before looking back at the detective.`
> "I don't know what you're talking about. I was at my cousin's place all night. You can ask her."

Play the cat-and-mouse game. Make the detective work for it. Give them just enough to keep the scene interesting without rolling over.

## Consequences and Jail RP

Getting caught is not the end — it is a pivot point in your story.

**What happens after arrest:**
1. Booking — fingerprints, mugshot, charges read
2. Holding cell — waiting for processing, interacting with other inmates
3. Arraignment — appear before a judge, enter a plea
4. Bail or remand — pay bail and continue your story, or sit in jail and play that arc
5. Trial (if applicable) — full courtroom RP with lawyers, witnesses, and a verdict
6. Sentencing — fines, jail time, probation, community service

**Jail is not AFK time.** If your character goes to jail, play it. Talk to other inmates. Start conflicts. Make alliances. Plan your next move. Jail RP is its own ecosystem with its own hierarchy, politics, and stories.

## Starting Small vs. Going Big

Every criminal empire started with a stolen candy bar. Do not show up on day one planning a Pacific Standard heist.

**The criminal career progression:**

| Phase | Activities | Reputation |
|---|---|---|
| Street level | Petty theft, car boosting, small-time dealing | Unknown — just another face |
| Hustler | Regular dealing, fencing stolen goods, running errands for bigger players | Known in the neighborhood |
| Mid-level | Armed robbery, crew operations, territory control | Respected or feared locally |
| High-level | Bank heists, major drug operations, organizational leadership | City-wide reputation |
| Kingpin | Running an empire, political connections, untouchable (until you are not) | Legendary — everyone knows your name |

Earn your way up. The progression is the story. Skipping steps robs you of the most interesting RP and annoys other players who built their reputation over weeks or months.

## IC vs. OOC Responsibilities

Criminal RP requires more OOC awareness than most roles because of its potential to negatively impact other players.

**OOC rules to always follow:**
- Always get OOC consent before permanent actions (killing a character, destroying a business permanently)
- Do not target the same player repeatedly unless the story justifies it and they are okay with it
- Robberies should create RP, not just take someone's money and leave
- Give your victims a scene — talk to them, threaten them, make them feel part of the story
- Do not use criminal RP as an excuse for toxicity — there is a line between IC intimidation and OOC harassment
- If someone is clearly not enjoying the interaction, adjust your approach

**Your character is not you.** Your criminal does terrible things. You, the player, should be considerate, communicative, and respectful. The best criminal RPers are often the nicest people OOC.

## Common Mistakes in Criminal RP

**The murder hobo:** Kills everyone, has no story, just wants chaos. This is not roleplay — it is griefing with extra steps.

**The invincible gangster:** Never gets caught, never gets hurt, never loses a fight. This is powergaming. Your character needs to lose sometimes.

**The lone wolf who runs everything:** No one operates a criminal empire alone. Build relationships. Trust people. Get betrayed. Trust again.

**No consequences:** If your character commits murder on Monday and is casually buying coffee on Tuesday with no heat, you are not playing the consequences of your actions.

**Speed-running crime:** You just created your character and you are already robbing the Pacific Standard Bank. Slow down. Build your network, your skills, and your reputation first.

**OOC salt after getting caught:** Your character got arrested. You, the player, should be excited about the next chapter of the story, not angry that you lost your gun.

## Common RP Scenarios

**The first job:**
> Your connect hands you a small bag and an address. Deliver this, bring back the money, do not look inside. Simple. You get in the car and start driving. Halfway there, you see a police cruiser behind you. Your hands tighten on the wheel. They are probably not following you. Probably. Do you stay cool or panic?

**The moral dilemma:**
> Your crew is planning to rob a convenience store. You pull up and see that the clerk is an elderly man — reminds you of your grandfather. Your crew is already putting on masks. Do you go through with it? Do you try to call it off? Do you do the job but make sure the old man is not hurt?

**The snitch decision:**
> You are looking at five years. The detective says they will drop it to probation if you give them a name. One name. Someone in your crew. Someone who trusts you. The detective slides a notepad across the table and clicks a pen. What do you do?

---

## Final Thoughts

Criminal roleplay is a responsibility. You are creating conflict that affects real players investing real time in their characters and stories. Do it with intention, creativity, and respect. Build a character with depth, play the consequences of your actions, and remember that the best crime stories are not about getting away with it — they are about what happens when you do not.

The city needs criminals. Be a good one.
