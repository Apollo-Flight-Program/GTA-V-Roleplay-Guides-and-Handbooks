# IC vs OOC — Understanding the Divide

One of the most fundamental concepts in roleplay is the separation between **IC** (In-Character) and **OOC** (Out-of-Character). Understanding this divide is the difference between good roleplay and breaking the experience for everyone around you.

---

## What is IC (In-Character)?

IC refers to everything that exists **inside the story world**. Your character's thoughts, words, actions, and knowledge are all IC.

When you are IC:
- You speak as your character, not as yourself
- You only know what your character would realistically know
- You react to events as your character would emotionally and logically
- Your character's name, job, relationships, and history are all IC facts

---

## What is OOC (Out-of-Character)?

OOC refers to everything that exists **outside the story** — the real world, the players, the game mechanics, technical issues.

When you are OOC:
- You speak as yourself, the player
- You can acknowledge game bugs, crashes, or technical problems
- You can discuss rules, clarify misunderstandings, or call a time-out
- You are no longer bound by your character's perspective

---

## How to Signal IC vs OOC

Most servers use specific formatting to distinguish between IC and OOC communication:

| Type | Common Format | Example |
|---|---|---|
| IC speech | Just speak normally in voice or /say | *"Step out of the vehicle, sir."* |
| IC text/phone | /me, /do, /text commands | */me reaches for his radio* |
| OOC (local) | Double brackets or /ooc | *(( hey my game crashed, give me 2 mins ))* |
| OOC (global) | Server OOC chat | Depends on server |

> Always check your specific server's rules for how they handle IC/OOC communication.

---

## The Golden Rule

**What happens IC stays IC. What happens OOC stays OOC.**

This means:
- If your character gets arrested, your character is upset — not you the player
- If a player makes a mistake OOC, do not punish their character IC for it
- If two characters are enemies, the players behind them can still be friends OOC
- If you learn something OOC (from a stream, Discord, friend), your character does not know it IC

---

## Common IC/OOC Violations

**Using OOC information IC:**
> You watched a stream and saw where someone hid their drugs. Your character then goes directly to that location. This is a violation — your character had no way of knowing that information.

**Reacting OOC to IC events:**
> Your character gets robbed. You get angry as a player and start arguing OOC with the other player about how it was unfair. This breaks immersion and is poor conduct.

**Breaking character unnecessarily:**
> In the middle of a serious scene you type "(( lol that was funny ))" in local chat. This destroys the moment for everyone involved.

---

## When is it Okay to Break Character?

Breaking character is acceptable — sometimes necessary — in these situations:

- A genuine technical issue (crash, bug, disconnect)
- A real-life emergency
- To clarify a serious misunderstanding about consent or server rules
- When a scene involves sensitive content and a player needs to check in

When you break character, make it clear with double brackets `(( ))` or your server's OOC signal, keep it brief, and return to the scene as quickly as possible.

---

## Summary

| IC | OOC |
|---|---|
| Inside the story | Outside the story |
| Your character's world | The real world / game mechanics |
| Character knowledge only | Player knowledge |
| Stay in character | Used sparingly for real issues |

Mastering the IC/OOC divide is what separates a good roleplayer from a great one. Protect the story — for yourself and for everyone around you.
