# The Golden Rules of Roleplay

Let's be honest, probably nobody has the time to read the whole repository about roleplaying. That's gonna be boring. But if there is any, props to you.

But if you are trying to grasp everything at once before playing, maybe don't. Just get in there, meet some people, and have fun.

This guide gives you the golden rules of roleplay.

Every server has its own rulebook. Every community has its own culture. Serious, semi-serious, casual, text-based — they all have different standards, different expectations, different ways of doing things.

But underneath all of it, every form of roleplay that has ever existed — from tabletop dungeons to GTA V server rooms — shares the same foundation. These are not rules invented by any one server. They are the principles that every experienced roleplayer, across every platform and every culture, has independently arrived at.

You can ignore every other document in this repository and still be a good roleplayer if you understand and live by these.

---

## Rule 1 — Your fun is not more important than someone else's story

This is the foundation everything else is built on.

***In every character you meet in the game, there is someone behind a screen that tries to have fun. Same like you. Can we respect that?***

Roleplay is not a solo game. Every character you interact with is being played by a real person who has invested time, creativity, and emotional energy into their story. When you act without regard for that — when you shoot someone for no reason, when you ruin a scene for a laugh, when you disconnect the moment things stop going your way — you are not just breaking a rule. You are wasting a real person's time and disrespecting their work.

The moment you enter a roleplay server, you become a co-author of a shared story. Your freedom ends where someone else's story begins.

**In practice:** Before doing anything significant, ask yourself — *"Does this serve the story, or just myself?"*

You wouldn't believe how many servers or communities are ruined because the majority ignored this rule. And this is one of the most common phenomena in gaming & roleplay communities. Read how [ignoring this rule keeps breaking everything](Articles/The-One-Rule-That-Keeps-Breaking-Everything.md)

---

## Rule 2 — Play your character, not yourself

You are not your character. Your character is not you.

Your character can be afraid when you are not. Your character can lose when you do not want to. Your character can be wrong, be hurt, be arrested, be outsmarted — and none of that reflects on you as a person.

The best roleplayers in the world are the ones who let their characters fail. Failure creates drama. Drama creates story. Story is the entire point.

If you are playing to protect your own ego — to never lose, never be embarrassed, never face consequences — you are not roleplaying. You are using a roleplay server as a stage for wish fulfillment, and everyone around you can tell.

**In practice:** When your character is in a situation you personally dislike, ask — *"What would my character realistically do here?"* Then do that, even if it costs you something.

---

## Rule 3 — Your character only knows what they have lived

Your character exists inside the story. They cannot know things that happened outside it.

They do not know what you watched on a stream. They do not know what your friend told you on Discord. They do not know the name floating above someone's head. They do not remember dying.

This rule — separating what the player knows from what the character knows — is the single most broken rule in all of GTA V roleplay. It is broken more often by accident than by intention. And it destroys immersion every single time.

A world only feels real when information matters. When everyone already knows everything, there is no mystery, no investigation, no discovery. There is only chaos pretending to be a story.

**In practice:** When your character is about to act on information, pause and ask — *"How does my character know this?"* If the honest answer involves anything outside the story, stop.

---

## Rule 4 — Give before you take

Roleplay is an exchange. You get out what you put in — but only if you put in first.

Before you rob someone, give them a scene worth being robbed in. Before you arrest someone, give them a reason to respect the encounter. Before you kill someone, make sure there is a story that justifies it.

The most respected players on any server are not the ones who win the most fights or pull off the biggest heists. They are the ones who make every interaction interesting — who give the other person something to work with, something to react to, something to remember.

Taking without giving — robbing with no setup, killing with no story, arresting with no engagement — is the roleplay equivalent of skipping to the ending of a book and calling it reading.

**In practice:** Before any significant action against another player, ask — *"Have I given them enough to make this scene worth their time?"*

---

## Rule 5 — Lose gracefully

Losing is not failure. Losing is content.

The most cinematic moments in roleplay history — the ones people talk about for months, the ones that get clipped and shared — are almost always moments where someone lost. A character gets arrested and delivers a quiet, devastating monologue. A gang leader gets betrayed by someone they trusted. An officer makes a split-second decision and lives with the consequences.

None of those moments happen if everyone refuses to lose.

When you resist every arrest, escape every situation, survive every injury, and win every confrontation — you are not protecting your character. You are impoverishing the story. You are making the world smaller and less interesting for everyone inside it.

Losing gracefully is one of the rarest and most valuable skills in roleplay. It is also what separates the players people want to share scenes with from the ones they avoid.

**In practice:** When things go against you, lean into it. The question is not *"How do I get out of this?"* It is *"What does my character do when they cannot get out of this?"*

---

## Rule 6 — The scene is not over when it stops being fun for you

Roleplay has slow moments. Uncomfortable moments. Moments that do not go your way. Moments that feel unfair.

Leaving — disconnecting, going AFK, suddenly having somewhere else to be — the moment a scene stops being entertaining is one of the most corrosive behaviors in any roleplay community. It tells every person in that scene that their time meant nothing to you.

Staying is not just a rule. It is respect. It is what allows trust to build between players — the knowledge that when things get hard, people do not disappear.

There are legitimate reasons to leave a scene — a real emergency, a genuine technical problem, content that crosses a personal line that you have communicated. But discomfort is not an emergency. Losing is not a technical problem. Boredom is not a personal line.

**In practice:** When you want to leave a scene because it is going badly for you, stay for ten more minutes. What happens in those ten minutes is often better than anything that came before.

---

## Rule 7 — Treat the person behind the character with respect

The character might be your enemy. The player is a person.

You can hate a character and respect the player who plays them. You can be at war in-character and be friendly out-of-character. You can arrest someone's character, destroy someone's business, betray someone's trust — all within the story — and never once be unkind to the human being on the other side of the screen.

When in-character conflict bleeds into out-of-character hostility, the story stops being fiction and starts being a genuinely harmful experience. That is the line. Stay on the right side of it.

**In practice:** If you find yourself genuinely angry at a player — not their character, but them — step away from the scene. Come back when the emotion has passed. The story will be there.

---

## The One Sentence Version

If you can only remember one thing from this entire document, remember this:

> **Roleplay as if the person on the other side of every scene deserves a good story — because they do.**

Everything else follows from that.

---

## A Final Note on Rules

Rules exist because principles are hard to enforce. When a community cannot trust its members to act with good judgment and genuine respect for others, it writes rules to compensate.

The servers with the shortest rulebooks are almost always the ones with the strongest cultures — because their players do not need to be told not to ruin someone else's experience. They already understand why they should not.

Read the rulebook of whatever server you join. Follow it. But do not mistake rule-following for good roleplay. You can follow every written rule on a server and still be the kind of player that makes the world worse.

The golden rules are not a checklist. They are a way of showing up.

Show up that way.
