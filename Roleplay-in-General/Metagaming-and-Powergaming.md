# Metagaming & Powergaming — What to Avoid

Two of the most common ways players ruin roleplay — for themselves and for others — are **metagaming** and **powergaming**. Understanding what these are, why they happen, and how to avoid them is essential for any serious roleplayer.

---

## Metagaming

### What is it?

Metagaming is using **out-of-character (OOC) knowledge** to influence your character's **in-character (IC) actions**.

Your character only knows what they have experienced within the story. The moment you use information your character could not realistically have, you are metagaming.

### Common Examples

**Stream sniping:**
> You watch a streamer's live feed and use their real-time location to find and ambush them in-game. Your character had no way of knowing where they were.

**Using player names above heads:**
> You see a nametag floating above a character and address them by name. Your character has never met them — they would not know their name.

**Discord/phone calls during RP:**
> Your friend is in a police chase and calls you on Discord. You drive to help them. Your character received no IC communication about this.

**Using death knowledge:**
> Your character dies. When you respawn, you use everything you knew before death to track down the people who killed you. Your character has no memory of their death.

**Reading the map:**
> You use the minimap or GPS blips to track other players in ways your character realistically could not.

### The Test

Ask yourself: **"How does my character know this?"**

If the honest answer involves anything outside the story — a stream, a Discord message, a nametag, a previous life — it is metagaming.

---

## Powergaming

### What is it?

Powergaming is forcing outcomes in roleplay that give your character an **unrealistic or unfair advantage**, often by ignoring the agency of other players or the physical limits of your character.

It is essentially playing as if your character is invincible, all-knowing, or impossible to stop.

### Common Examples

**Ignoring injuries:**
> You get shot three times and keep running at full speed. Your character would realistically be incapacitated.

**Performing impossible actions:**
> */me disarms the officer, takes his weapon, handcuffs him, and escapes before anyone can react*
> One action forced onto another player with no chance to respond.

**Refusing all consequences:**
> No matter what happens — arrest, injury, ambush — your character always escapes or wins. You never allow the story to go against you.

**Invincible character syndrome:**
> Your character is an ex-Special Forces Navy SEAL sniper who is also a doctor, lawyer, and billionaire. No realistic weaknesses, no vulnerabilities.

**God-modding in /me actions:**
> Using emotes to force actions on other players without giving them a chance to respond or resist.

### The Test

Ask yourself: **"Am I giving the other player a fair chance to respond?"** and **"Would this realistically be possible?"**

If your character is never wrong, never loses, never gets hurt, and always wins — you are powergaming.

---

## Why People Do It (And Why It Still Matters)

Most metagaming and powergaming is not malicious. Players do it because:
- They are new and do not know the rules yet
- They are emotionally invested and do not want to lose
- It feels natural to use all available information
- They want to protect their character at all costs

But the impact on other players is the same regardless of intent. It breaks immersion, invalidates other people's stories, and makes the world feel unfair and unenjoyable.

**Good roleplay requires letting go of the need to win.**

## Quick Reference

| Term | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Metagaming | Using OOC info IC | Watching a stream to find someone's location |
| Powergaming | Forcing unfair outcomes | Writing an emote that gives others no chance to respond |
| Stream sniping | Specific metagaming via live streams | Watching someone's Twitch to ambush them |
| God-modding | Specific powergaming via invincibility | Character never gets hurt, always wins |
| NLR (New Life Rule) | Death wipes character memory of that life | After dying, you cannot remember how or by whom |

---

## The Bottom Line

Roleplay is a collaborative story. The goal is not to win — it is to create something worth experiencing. Every time you metagame or powergame, you are prioritizing your own outcome over the shared story.

The best roleplayers are the ones who lose gracefully, react authentically, and make every scene better for everyone in it.
