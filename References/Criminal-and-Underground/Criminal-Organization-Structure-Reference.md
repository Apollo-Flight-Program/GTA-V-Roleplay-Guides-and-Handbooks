# Criminal Organization Structure Reference

A reference for how criminal organizations are structured in roleplay — from street gangs to organized crime syndicates. Understanding hierarchy, roles, and operations enables more grounded and realistic criminal RP.

---

## Why Structure Matters

A criminal organization without structure is a group of players who happen to commit crimes together. A criminal organization **with** structure has internal politics, chain of command, loyalty dynamics, succession crises, and the kind of tension that generates compelling RP.

Structure does not have to be rigid. Even a loosely organized street crew has informal hierarchy. The question is not whether structure exists — it is whether the people involved understand and respect it.

---

## Street Gang Structure

Street gangs are typically neighborhood-based and less formally organized than criminal enterprises. Hierarchy is informal but real.

```
OG (Original Gangster) — Senior member(s). Founders or long-serving veterans. Respect is social, not contractual.
  └── Shot Caller — The person actively running operations. May or may not be the OG.
        ├── Enforcer — Handles violence, protection, territory disputes.
        ├── Trap Boss — Oversees drug sales operation for a specific corner or area.
        │     └── Trapper / Runner — Street-level dealer, runner of product.
        └── Lookout — Watches for police; lowest-risk but critical position.
```

### Typical Gang Progression
1. **Hang-around / Associate** — Known to the group, not yet a member. Doing small tasks to prove themselves.
2. **Prospect** — Undergoing evaluation. Active participant, some trust extended.
3. **Member / Rider** — Full member. Recognized. Has taken the gang's oath or passed initiation.
4. **Veteran** — Long-serving member with earned respect.
5. **OG** — Elder status. Usually not in active operations but commands deference.

## Organized Crime Structure (Mafia / Cartel Model)

Organized crime operates more like a corporation — with formal ranks, defined roles, and business-like operations.

### Traditional Crime Family Structure

```
Boss / Don — Absolute authority over the family.
  └── Underboss — Second in command. Manages operations on behalf of the boss.
        ├── Consigliere — Advisor. No direct authority but significant influence.
        └── Capo / Captain — Leads a crew of soldiers. Reports directly to the underboss.
              └── Soldier / Made Man — Full member. Carries out operations.
                    └── Associate — Not a formal member. Works for the family but has no rank.
```

### How Decisions Flow
- **Boss** sets direction and makes major decisions
- **Underboss** implements those decisions and manages daily operations
- **Capos** are middle management — they translate orders into actions
- **Soldiers** execute
- **Associates** are contractors — used and trusted selectively

### The Commission Model
Large organizations may have multiple families or factions operating under a **commission** — a governing council of bosses who resolve inter-family disputes and set territory boundaries. Highly advanced RP; requires sustained commitment from multiple faction leaders.

## Cartel Structure

Cartels are more hierarchical and geographically distributed than traditional crime families.

```
Cartel Leader (El Jefe / El Patrón)
  ├── Plaza Boss — Controls operations in a specific city or territory.
  │     ├── Operational Commander — Runs day-to-day criminal activity in the plaza.
  │     │     ├── Sicario (Enforcer/Hitman) — Military enforcement arm.
  │     │     ├── Drug Trafficker — Manages supply and distribution.
  │     │     └── Money Manager — Oversees financial operations, laundering.
  │     └── Intelligence Cell — Monitors rivals, law enforcement, and internal threats.
  └── Supplier Network — Sources for product, weapons, and materials.
```

## Roles Reference

### Universal Roles

| Role | Function |
|---|---|
| **Leader / Boss** | Ultimate authority. Makes final decisions. Sets strategy. Rarely in the field. |
| **Second in Command** | Day-to-day operations manager. Leads in the boss's absence. |
| **Enforcer** | Physical muscle. Handles violence, threats, collections, protection. |
| **Fixer** | Solves problems — bribes officials, handles cleanup, makes evidence disappear. |
| **Money Manager / Accountant** | Manages criminal finances — laundering, distribution, investment. |
| **Intelligence / Spotter** | Gathers information — surveillance, informants, monitoring rivals and police. |
| **Supplier** | Sources product, weapons, and materials for the organization. |
| **Street Boss** | Middle management — runs a specific territory or operation set. |
| **Runner** | Transports money or goods. Low trust, high risk. Entry-level role. |
| **Associate** | Loose affiliation. Not a full member but does work for the organization. |

## Territory and Operations

### Territory Rules (IC Logic)
- Territory is claimed by consistent presence and enforcement, not just declaration
- Entry into another gang's territory by a rival without permission is a provocation
- Neutral zones exist — areas no gang controls, often used for meetings
- Territory can be lost through combat, attrition, or negotiated treaty

### Operations Types
| Operation | Description |
|---|---|
| **Drug operation** | Growing/cooking, transporting, selling product across a distribution network |
| **Protection racket** | Demanding regular payments from businesses in exchange for not harming them |
| **Weapons trafficking** | Acquiring and distributing illegal firearms |
| **Smuggling** | Moving contraband (any type) across controlled checkpoints |
| **Money laundering** | Converting criminal cash into clean money through fronts |
| **Contract work** | Paid violence or theft on behalf of another party — mercenary operations |
| **Intimidation** | Using the threat of violence to compel behavior |
