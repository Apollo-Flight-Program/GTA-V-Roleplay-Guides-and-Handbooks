# Triage and Treatment Reference

A quick reference for medical triage procedures and common treatment protocols used in EMS roleplay. This covers how to assess multiple patients in a mass casualty situation, how to prioritize care, and what treatments are appropriate for common injuries.

This reference is for **in-character use** by EMS and medical characters. It is written in the language of working medical professionals.

---

## Triage Overview

Triage is the process of rapidly sorting patients by the severity of their injuries to determine the order in which they receive care. The goal is to do the greatest good for the greatest number of people.

**When to triage:** Any time there are more patients than can be treated simultaneously. A single critical patient does not require triage — direct treatment begins immediately. Two or more patients with a limited number of responders require triage.

---

## START Triage System

**START = Simple Triage and Rapid Treatment**

The primary triage system used in mass casualty incidents. Assess each patient in under 30 seconds. Assign a tag color based on assessment.

### Step 1 — Walking Wounded
Announce loudly: *"If you can walk, move away from the scene and stand over there."*
All patients who comply are tagged **Green (Minor)**. Move to the remaining patients.

### Step 2 — Assess Breathing

| Finding | Action |
|---|---|
| Not breathing — reposition airway | Does not breathe after repositioning: **Black (Expectant/Deceased)** |
| Not breathing — starts breathing after repositioning | **Red (Immediate)** |
| Breathing, rate > 30 per minute | **Red (Immediate)** |
| Breathing, rate ≤ 30 per minute | Continue to Step 3 |

### Step 3 — Assess Perfusion (Circulation)

| Finding | Tag |
|---|---|
| Radial pulse absent | **Red (Immediate)** |
| Capillary refill > 2 seconds | **Red (Immediate)** |
| Capillary refill ≤ 2 seconds | Continue to Step 4 |

### Step 4 — Assess Mental Status

| Finding | Tag |
|---|---|
| Cannot follow simple commands | **Red (Immediate)** |
| Can follow simple commands | **Yellow (Delayed)** |

## Triage Tag Categories

### 🔴 Red — Immediate
**Treat first.** Life-threatening injuries that are survivable with immediate intervention.

Examples:
- Airway obstruction
- Severe uncontrolled bleeding
- Penetrating chest trauma with respiratory distress
- Hemodynamic shock
- Altered consciousness with treatable cause

Action: Begin treatment immediately. Assign dedicated personnel.

---

### 🟡 Yellow — Delayed
**Treat second.** Serious injuries that are stable for a short period. Can wait minutes to hours without immediate loss of life.

Examples:
- Fractures without vascular compromise
- Burns less than 40% BSA without airway involvement
- Eye injuries
- Moderate blood loss — controlled

Action: Stabilize and monitor. Reassess regularly — Yellow can become Red.

---

### 🟢 Green — Minor (Walking Wounded)
**Treat third.** Minor injuries. Can self-care or wait for basic treatment.

Examples:
- Minor lacerations
- Sprains, minor fractures of extremities
- Mild burns
- Psychological distress without injury

Action: Direct to a treatment area. Can assist with other lightly-injured patients. Reassess periodically.

---

### ⬛ Black — Expectant / Deceased
**Treat last or not at all.** Injuries incompatible with survival given available resources, or confirmed deceased.

**Deceased:** No pulse, no breathing after airway repositioned. Do not continue assessment.

**Expectant:** Injuries so severe that survival is unlikely even with the best care. Resources are directed to salvageable patients. *This is the hardest triage decision.*

Examples of Expectant injuries in a resource-limited MCI:
- Massive burns covering majority of body with inhalation injury
- Unsurvivable penetrating head injury with fixed pupils
- Blunt trauma with absent vital signs for extended period

## Common Injury Treatments

### Severe Bleeding (Hemorrhage Control)
1. **Direct pressure** — firm, continuous pressure with gauze over the wound
2. **Wound packing** — for deep wounds, pack gauze tightly into the cavity while maintaining pressure
3. **Tourniquet** — for extremity bleeds that cannot be controlled otherwise. Apply 2–3 inches above the wound. Document application time.
4. **Hemostatic dressing** — gauze treated with clotting agents for high-priority bleeds

### Airway Management
1. **Head-tilt chin-lift** — basic airway opening maneuver (no suspected cervical injury)
2. **Jaw thrust** — if cervical spine injury is suspected
3. **Oropharyngeal airway (OPA)** — keeps airway open in unconscious patient
4. **Nasopharyngeal airway (NPA)** — gentler alternative; tolerated in semi-conscious patients
5. **BVM ventilation** — manually breathing for the patient
6. **Intubation** — advanced airway; requires training and appropriate medications

### Chest Injuries
- **Open chest wound (sucking chest wound):** Apply a vented chest seal over the wound to allow air out but not in. Do not seal airtight.
- **Tension pneumothorax:** A life-threatening buildup of air in the chest cavity. Signs: absent breath sounds on one side, tracheal deviation, extreme respiratory distress. Treatment: needle decompression.
- **Rib fractures:** Analgesia, careful monitoring for respiratory compromise. Flail chest (multiple broken ribs creating an unstable segment) requires positive pressure ventilation.

### Shock
Shock = inadequate tissue perfusion. Treat the cause.

- **Hemorrhagic shock:** Control bleeding, establish IV access, administer IV fluids cautiously
- **Obstructive shock (tension pneumo, tamponade):** Decompress or drain the obstruction
- Signs of shock: pale diaphoretic skin, weak rapid pulse, altered mental status, low blood pressure

### Burns
- **Stop the burning process** — remove clothing, irrigate chemical burns with water
- **Cool the burn** — cool water for up to 20 minutes. Do not use ice.
- **Cover** — sterile dry dressing for small burns; burn sheet for large areas
- **Do not:** Pop blisters, apply butter, cream, or ice
- **Airway priority:** Facial burns or singed nasal hair indicate inhalation injury — intubate early before swelling closes the airway

### Head Injuries / Suspected Spinal
- Full spinal precautions: cervical collar, backboard
- Do not remove a helmet from a motorcycle rider unless airway access is required
- Elevated head of stretcher 30 degrees if no spinal injury and suspected increased intracranial pressure
- Frequent neuro reassessment: GCS, pupils, motor response
