# Emergency Medical Services — In-Character Glossary

Language used by **EMS and medical characters** in the world of Los Santos. These are the words, phrases, and abbreviations that paramedics, EMTs, and hospital staff use in daily work — over radio, at the scene, and in the hospital.

---

## Dispatch and Response Language

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **The Bus** | An ambulance. "Get the bus rolling to the scene." |
| **The Barn** | The EMS station or base. "Heading back to the barn to restock." |
| **Tone** | The alert that notifies EMS of a new call. "We just got a tone — structure fire on Elgin." |
| **Working a call** | Actively responding to or on scene of an emergency. |
| **Clear / Available** | Ready to take a new call. "Unit 3 is clear, ready for the next call." |
| **Lights and Sirens / L&S** | Responding with emergency equipment active — Code 3 response. |
| **Rolling Code 3** | Driving to a scene with full lights and siren. |
| **Staged** | Waiting at a safe distance from a scene, not yet committed, waiting for LEO clearance. |
| **On scene / Scene arrival** | Just arrived at the location of the emergency. |
| **Patient contact** | Making first physical contact with a patient. The clock starts here for documentation. |

## Patient Assessment Language

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **ABC** | Airway, Breathing, Circulation — the first three things assessed on any patient. |
| **LOC (Level of Consciousness)** | How alert and aware a patient is. Ranges from fully conscious to completely unresponsive. |
| **GCS (Glasgow Coma Scale)** | A numerical scale (3–15) rating consciousness based on eye opening, verbal response, and motor response. |
| **Unresponsive** | Patient is unconscious and not reacting to stimulation. |
| **Responsive** | Patient is conscious. Subdivided: alert, verbal (only responds to voice), pain (only responds to pain stimulus). |
| **Presenting** | How the patient appears when EMS arrives. "Patient is presenting with altered LOC and pale skin." |
| **Chief complaint** | The primary problem the patient reports. |
| **MOI (Mechanism of Injury)** | How the injury happened — fall, GSW, MVC, etc. |
| **GSW** | Gunshot wound. |
| **MVC** | Motor vehicle collision. |
| **Penetrating trauma** | A wound where something entered the body — stab, gunshot. |
| **Blunt trauma** | Injury from impact without penetration — hit by a vehicle, struck by a blunt object. |
| **Vitals** | The basic physiological measurements: blood pressure, pulse rate, respiratory rate, SpO2 (oxygen saturation), temperature. |
| **Pupils** | Checked for size, equality, and reaction to light. "Pupils are PERRL" (equal and reactive to light) is normal. |
| **Diaphoretic** | Sweating profusely — a sign of shock or severe stress. |
| **Pallor** | Unusual paleness of the skin — associated with blood loss, shock, or poor circulation. |
| **Cyanosis** | Blue or gray discoloration of the skin, especially around the lips — indicates poor oxygenation. |

## Interventions and Treatment

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **C-spine** | Cervical spine precautions — immobilizing the neck due to potential spinal injury. |
| **O2 / Oxygen** | Supplemental oxygen delivered by mask or nasal cannula. |
| **IV / IV access** | Intravenous line — a needle placed in a vein to administer fluids or medications. |
| **NS / Normal Saline** | Standard IV fluid used to replace volume in bleeding or shock patients. |
| **Tourniquet** | A band applied tightly above a limb wound to stop severe bleeding. "Applied a TQ to the left arm." |
| **Packing the wound** | Filling a deep wound with gauze to control bleeding from inside. |
| **Sealing the chest** | Applying an occlusive dressing to a chest wound to prevent air from entering the chest cavity. |
| **CPR** | Cardiopulmonary resuscitation — chest compressions and rescue breathing for cardiac arrest. |
| **Defib / AED** | Defibrillator — delivers an electrical shock to restart a heart in certain rhythms. |
| **Intubation** | Inserting a breathing tube through the mouth into the trachea. |
| **BVM** | Bag-valve mask — a device used to manually breathe for a non-breathing patient. |
| **Cric** | Cricothyrotomy — an emergency airway procedure when intubation is impossible. |
| **Load and go** | Rapidly packaging a critical patient and transporting immediately, performing treatments en route. |
| **Stay and play** | Spending more time on scene treating a patient before transport — appropriate for more stable patients. |
| **Packaging** | Preparing a patient for transport — stabilized, secured on stretcher, ready to move. |

## Transport and Hospital

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Transport** | Moving the patient from the scene to a medical facility. |
| **LSMC** | Los Santos Medical Center. The primary hospital destination. |
| **Receiving** | The hospital accepting the incoming patient. "Receiving is on standby for our arrival." |
| **Handoff / Report** | Briefing hospital staff on the patient's condition and treatment upon arrival. |
| **Trauma bay** | The emergency room area set up to receive critical trauma patients immediately. |
| **Stable** | Patient's condition is not immediately life-threatening and is not worsening. |
| **Critical** | Patient's condition is life-threatening and unstable. |
| **DOA** | Dead on arrival. Patient was already deceased when EMS made contact. |
| **Working arrest** | Patient in full cardiac arrest — CPR and resuscitation actively in progress. |
| **ROSC** | Return of Spontaneous Circulation — the patient's heart has restarted. |

## Triage Terms

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **MCI (Mass Casualty Incident)** | An event with more patients than available resources can handle simultaneously. |
| **Triage** | The process of sorting patients by injury severity to determine treatment priority. |
| **Red tag (Immediate)** | Critical patient — life-threatening injuries, needs immediate treatment to survive. |
| **Yellow tag (Delayed)** | Serious injuries, stable enough to wait briefly for treatment. |
| **Green tag (Minor / Walking wounded)** | Minor injuries. Can wait and may be able to self-care. "Walking wounded." |
| **Black tag (Expectant / Deceased)** | Either already deceased or injuries so severe that survival is unlikely even with treatment. In MCI, resources go to salvageable patients first. |
