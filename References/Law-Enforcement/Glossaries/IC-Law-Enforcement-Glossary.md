# Law Enforcement — In-Character Glossary

This glossary covers language that **law enforcement characters** use in-character — the spoken vocabulary of policing as it exists inside the world of Los Santos. These are terms officers, detectives, and dispatchers use in daily speech, over radio, and in written reports.

This is not a list of 10-codes or dispatch codes (see separate reference sheets). This is the **informal and formal language of the job** — the way officers actually talk to each other.

---

## Everyday Officer Language

### On the Job

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **The Job** | The police department as an institution. "I've been on the job twelve years." Also: law enforcement work in general. |
| **The Department** | The officer's specific agency — LSPD, BCSO, SASP, etc. |
| **The Bureau** | An investigative division, particularly detectives. "She moved to the bureau last month." |
| **The Brass** | Senior officers and command staff — captains, chiefs, commissioners. "The brass wants this wrapped up." |
| **The Watch** | A shift or duty period. "Who else is on watch tonight?" |
| **The Beat** | An officer's assigned patrol area. "Back on my beat after the call." |
| **The Desk** | Administrative work or a desk assignment — usually considered less desirable than street patrol. "She caught a desk assignment for two weeks." |
| **The Street** | Active patrol duty, as opposed to administrative or detective work. "I'd rather be on the street than pushing paper." |
| **Roll Call** | The briefing at the start of a shift where assignments, BOLs, and updates are distributed. |
| **EOT / End of Tour** | End of shift. "EOT in twenty — finishing up paperwork." |
| **BOT / Beginning of Tour** | Start of shift. |
| **Swing** | Afternoon shift. "I'm on swing this week." |
| **Graveyard / Midnights** | The overnight shift. |

### Suspects and Subjects

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Perp** | Perpetrator — a person who committed a crime. |
| **Suspect / Susps** | A person believed to have committed or be involved in a crime. |
| **Collar** | An arrest. "That's a good collar." To "make a collar" is to arrest someone. |
| **Vic** | Victim. |
| **Wit** | Witness. |
| **CI** | Confidential Informant. A person who provides information to police in exchange for consideration. |
| **Plain clothes** | An officer not in uniform. |
| **Undercover / UC** | An officer operating covertly, often embedded in criminal organizations. |
| **John/Jane Doe** | An unidentified person, alive or deceased. |
| **Unknown / UNK** | Information that is not yet determined. "Suspect is a white male, UNK age." |

### Patrol and Response

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **On a call / Running a call** | Actively responding to or handling a dispatched incident. |
| **Clear** | Available and finished with a call. "I'm clear, what do you have?" |
| **Wrap** | To finish a scene or call. "I'll wrap this and head over." |
| **Hot stop** | A high-risk traffic stop where the occupants are suspected of being armed or dangerous. |
| **Felony stop** | A vehicle stop conducted with maximum caution — officers drawn, commands from distance. |
| **Box it in** | To position police vehicles to surround and block a subject's vehicle. |
| **Set a perimeter** | Establish a boundary around a scene to prevent suspects from escaping or civilians from entering. |
| **Contain** | Keep a suspect or situation from spreading or escalating. |
| **Foot pursuit / On foot** | Chasing a suspect who is running. |
| **Lose visual** | Lose sight of a fleeing suspect or vehicle. |

### Reports and Documentation

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Case number** | The official identifying number assigned to an investigation. |
| **Face sheet** | The cover page of a report summarizing the basic facts of an incident. |
| **Narrative** | The written description of events in a police report, in the officer's own words. |
| **Supplement** | An additional report added to an existing case — used when new information becomes available. |
| **Arrest package** | The collection of documents — report, booking form, evidence list — submitted when arresting someone. |
| **Probable cause / PC** | Legal justification to make an arrest, conduct a search, or detain someone. |
| **Articulate** | To clearly explain the legal basis for a stop, search, or arrest. "Can you articulate that stop?" |
| **Exigent circumstances** | An emergency situation that justifies action without a warrant. |

### Detectives and Investigation

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Case** | An active investigation assigned to a detective. |
| **Lead** | A promising piece of information that may advance an investigation. "We've got a new lead." |
| **Dead end** | An investigative path that produced no useful results. |
| **Cold case** | An investigation that has stalled or been inactive for an extended period. |
| **Connect the dots** | Find the logical links between separate pieces of evidence. |
| **Build a case** | Accumulate enough evidence to legally and successfully prosecute a suspect. |
| **Work a source** | Develop information from a CI or informant over time. |
| **Canvass** | Interview people in an area near a crime scene to gather witness information. |
| **Scene** | An active crime location. "The detective is on scene." |
| **The lab** | The crime scene unit or forensics division. |
| **Sheet** | A person's criminal history. "Pull his sheet." |

## Slang and Informal Language

| Term | IC Meaning |
|---|---|
| **On the job** | Currently a police officer — active duty. |
| **Dinosaur** | A veteran officer, often resistant to change. |
| **Rookie / Boot** | A new officer, especially one still in field training. |
| **Heat** | Pressure — either from command, the community, or media attention. "The brass is putting heat on this case." |
| **Paperwork** | Administrative tasks. Often used as light punishment: "More paperwork for me." |
| **Rip** | To be disciplined or receive a formal reprimand. "He got ripped for that." |
| **The bag** | Accepting bribes or acting corruptly. "He's in the bag." |
| **Ghost** | To go unresponsive or avoid contact. "He ghosted the department after the incident." |
