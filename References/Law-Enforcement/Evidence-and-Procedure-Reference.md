# Evidence and Procedure Reference

A guide to how evidence is identified, collected, handled, and presented in law enforcement and legal roleplay. Covers the chain of custody, evidence categories, and report-writing expectations.

---

## Types of Evidence

### Physical Evidence
Tangible objects that can be collected and examined.

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Weapons** | Firearms, knives, blunt instruments |
| **Contraband** | Narcotics, illegal weapons, stolen goods |
| **Documents** | Written notes, contracts, ledgers, photos |
| **Vehicle evidence** | Matching tire tracks, blood, fibers, forced entry marks |
| **Trace evidence** | Hair, fibers, glass, paint transfer |

### Documentary Evidence
Records used to establish facts.
- Financial records showing payment or transfers
- Surveillance footage from a location
- Phone records (call logs, text records)
- GPS data from a vehicle

### Testimonial Evidence
Statements from people with knowledge.
- Eyewitness accounts
- Victim statements
- Expert witness opinions

### Digital Evidence
- Cell phone data
- Computer files
- Messages and emails
- Server logs

## Chain of Custody

The **chain of custody** is the documented record of who collected evidence, who handled it, and how it was stored — from the moment of collection to courtroom presentation.

### Why It Matters
If chain of custody is broken — evidence was mishandled, unaccounted for, or improperly stored — it may be challenged or suppressed in court.

### Chain of Custody Steps

1. **Collection** — Officer identifies and collects evidence at the scene
2. **Documentation** — Note the time, location, condition, and who collected the item
3. **Packaging** — Placed in labeled, sealed evidence bags
4. **Transport** — Transferred directly to evidence storage; no unauthorized handling
5. **Logging** — Entered into the evidence management system with a case number
6. **Storage** — Secured in a locked evidence room with restricted access
7. **Transfer** — Any movement of evidence (to lab, to court) is logged
8. **Presentation** — In court, officer testifies to the chain and identifies the item

## Evidence Collection at a Crime Scene

### Scene Security First
Before any evidence collection:
- Establish a perimeter
- Remove unauthorized personnel
- Document the scene in its original state (notes, photos)

### Documentation Before Collection
1. **Photograph** — The item in its found position, wide shot and close-up
2. **Note** — Exact location, orientation, and condition
3. **Sketch** — Optional; a diagram of the scene with evidence positions marked

### Collection
- Use gloves at all times
- Use appropriate containers (paper bags for biological, plastic for inorganic)
- Label immediately after packaging: case number, date, location, officer initials
- Do not mix evidence from different sources in the same container

## Evidence Categories (Investigative Priority)

| Category | Priority | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Weapons** | Highest | Firearm recovery, fingerprints, serial numbers |
| **Biological** | High | Blood, DNA — time-sensitive; degrades quickly |
| **Contraband** | High | Weighed, photographed, lab-tested for identity |
| **Digital devices** | High | Do not power off (may encrypt); photograph screen state |
| **Documents** | Medium | Originals preserved; copies made for working use |
| **Trace** | Medium | Hair, fiber — requires lab analysis |
| **Vehicle** | Varies | VIN check, interior search, exterior examination |

## Investigation Report Standards

A thorough investigation report includes:

### Required Sections

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Header** | Case number, reporting officer, date/time, incident type |
| **Initial call** | How officers were dispatched, what information they received |
| **Scene observation** | What officers observed upon arrival, exact descriptions |
| **Persons involved** | Victim, suspect, witness names, descriptions, statements summary |
| **Evidence collected** | Itemized list with collection details |
| **Actions taken** | What officers did — detentions, arrests, canvasses |
| **Follow-up required** | What investigation is still needed |

### Writing Standards
- Use precise, objective language — describe what was observed, not interpreted
- Avoid phrases like "the suspect obviously..." — say "subject stated..." or "the evidence indicated..."
- Use exact times (1423 hrs, not "around 2:30")
- Quote witness statements accurately

## Suppression of Evidence

Evidence may be suppressed (excluded from court) if:

| Grounds | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **Illegal search** | Evidence found without a warrant, consent, or valid exception |
| **Broken chain of custody** | Evidence mishandled, unaccounted for, or improperly stored |
| **Coerced statement** | Confession obtained through threats, promises, or force |
| **Miranda violation** | Statements made during custodial interrogation without Miranda warning |
| **Fruit of the poisonous tree** | Evidence derived from an initial illegal action |

## Digital Evidence Specifics

Digital evidence requires special handling:

1. **Do not power off the device** — May trigger encryption
2. **Photograph the screen** — Note what is open/visible
3. **Use a Faraday bag if possible** — Blocks remote wiping signals
4. **Request forensic analysis** — Digital forensics team extracts data properly
5. **Document the device** — Make, model, serial number, IMEI if phone
