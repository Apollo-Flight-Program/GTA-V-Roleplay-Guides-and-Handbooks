# Radio Etiquette Guide

Radio communication is one of the most visible indicators of roleplay quality. A player who uses their radio correctly — clear, concise, professional — immediately elevates every scene they are part of. A player who treats radio like a casual chat channel breaks immersion for everyone listening.

This guide covers the principles, structure, and language of realistic radio communication for GTA V roleplay.

---

## Why Radio Etiquette Matters

In real law enforcement, military, and emergency services, radio discipline is a matter of life and death. Unclear transmissions cause delayed responses. Talking over someone can mean a critical message is missed. In roleplay, the same logic applies to immersion — every radio interaction is a performance that either builds or breaks the world.

Good radio etiquette signals to other players that you take the RP seriously. It is one of the easiest and highest-impact habits to develop.

---

## The Basic Structure of a Transmission

Every radio transmission should follow this structure:

```
[Who you are calling] + [Who you are] + [Your message] + [Over / Out]
```

**Example:**
> *"Dispatch, Unit 14 — I have a 10-50 at the intersection of Alta and Innocence. Requesting 10-52. Over."*

Breaking it down:
- **"Dispatch"** — who you are calling
- **"Unit 14"** — who you are
- **"10-50 at Alta and Innocence"** — the message
- **"Requesting 10-52"** — the request
- **"Over"** — signals you are done transmitting and awaiting a response

## Essential Radio Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Over** | I am done transmitting, awaiting your response |
| **Out** | Transmission is complete, no response needed |
| **Copy / Roger** | I understood your message |
| **Say again** | Please repeat your last transmission |
| **Stand by** | Wait — I will respond shortly |
| **Negative** | No |
| **Affirmative** | Yes |
| **Break** | Interrupting to send an urgent message |
| **Go ahead** | I am ready to receive your message |
| **Come in** | Requesting a response from a specific unit |
| **Radio check** | Testing — can you hear me? |
| **Loud and clear** | Response to radio check — good signal |
| **Mayday** | Emergency distress call — life-threatening situation |
| **All units** | Transmission addressed to everyone on the channel |

## Rules of Good Radio Discipline

### 1. Think before you transmit
Plan what you are going to say before you key the radio. Silence while you think mid-transmission sounds unprofessional and wastes channel time.

### 2. Keep it short
Say what needs to be said. Nothing more. Radio is not a conversation channel — it is a coordination tool.

**Bad:**
> *"Hey dispatch, um, so I am over here near the, uh, hospital area and I think I saw a suspicious vehicle, it was like a black car or maybe dark blue, going kind of fast..."*

**Good:**
> *"Dispatch, Unit 9 — suspicious vehicle, dark-colored sedan, heading northbound on innocence. Requesting a 10-28. Over."*

### 3. Never talk over another transmission
If someone is already transmitting, wait until they finish before keying your radio. Interrupting a transmission is poor discipline and can cause critical information to be lost.

The only exception is a genuine emergency — use "Break, break, break" to signal an urgent interruption.

### 4. Identify yourself every time
Always state your unit number or callsign at the start of every transmission. Dispatch and other units need to know who is speaking immediately.

### 5. Use plain language when codes do not apply
10-codes are for efficiency. If a situation is complex or unusual, plain language is clearer than forcing codes that do not quite fit. Real officers use plain language regularly alongside codes.

### 6. Confirm receipt of important information
When you receive a critical message — a location, a description, an order — read it back to confirm you received it correctly.

> *"Copy, Unit 14 — confirming 10-52 en route to Alta and Innocence. ETA two minutes. Over."*

### 7. Channel discipline
Know which channel is for what. Most servers have separate channels for general patrol, tactical operations, command, and inter-agency communication. Transmitting on the wrong channel is a common beginner mistake.

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correct Approach |
|---|---|
| Saying "over and out" | Use either "over" OR "out" — never both together |
| Using first-person casually ("I'm gonna...") | Use unit designations ("Unit 4 is en route...") |
| Leaving the channel open (hot mic) | Always release your transmit key when done |
| Responding to every transmission unnecessarily | Only respond when a response is needed or requested |
| Using slang or casual language | Keep transmissions professional and clear |

> Note on "over and out": This is a common Hollywood mistake. "Over" means you expect a reply. "Out" means the conversation is finished. Saying both simultaneously is a contradiction.

## Example Transmissions

**Routine patrol check-in:**
> *"Dispatch, Unit 7 — 10-8, beginning patrol in the downtown district. Over."*

**Requesting backup:**
> *"All units, Unit 3 — 10-78 at the Maze Bank Tower parking structure. Suspect is armed. Requesting immediate backup. Over."*

**Pursuit:**
> *"Dispatch, Unit 12 — 10-80 northbound on the freeway. Suspect vehicle is a red coupe, partial plate — Romeo, Sierra, Fower. Speeds exceeding 120. Over."*

**Officer down:**
> *"Break, break — Unit 6, officer down at the Del Perro Pier. Repeat, officer down. Requesting 10-52 and all available units. Over."*

**Traffic stop:**
> *"Dispatch, Unit 9 — 10-38 on a black SUV at Vespucci Boulevard. Running a 10-27 and 10-28. Stand by. Over."*

**End of shift:**
> *"Dispatch, Unit 14 — 10-42, ending tour of duty. Good night. Out."*

## Inter-Agency Communication

When communicating with units from a different department (police to EMS, military to police), always:

- Identify your agency as well as your unit: *"LSPD Unit 4 to EMS dispatch..."*
- Use plain language rather than agency-specific codes the other party may not know
- Keep the transmission even more concise than usual — do not assume the other agency knows your internal protocols

---

## Radio in High-Stress Scenes

Under pressure, radio discipline is hardest to maintain — and most important. When everything is happening at once, slow down your transmission rather than speeding up. A fast, garbled transmission that has to be repeated wastes more time than a slightly slower, clear one.

Breathe. Key. Speak clearly. Release.

That is all radio is, even when the scene around you is chaos.
