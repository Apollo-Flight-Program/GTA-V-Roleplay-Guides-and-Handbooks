# Political Procedures Reference

A reference guide to how political processes work in GTA V roleplay. Covers elections, legislation, public hearings, and the IC conduct of government business — including how the democratic process can be used as a vehicle for meaningful RP.

---

## Elections

### Types of Elections

| Election Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **General Election** | Scheduled election for major offices (Mayor, Governor, City Council) |
| **Special Election** | Unscheduled election to fill a vacancy mid-term |
| **Primary Election** | Intra-party contest to select the party's nominee |
| **Runoff Election** | Second election held when no candidate wins a majority in the first round |
| **Recall Election** | Vote to remove an incumbent official before their term ends |

---

### Election Campaign Process

#### 1. Declaration of Candidacy
A character formally announces they are running. This requires:
- Filing a Declaration of Candidacy with the City Clerk or Secretary of State
- Payment of a filing fee (IC, if the server tracks this)
- Proof of eligibility (citizen, meets age requirements, not disqualified)

#### 2. Campaign Phase
- **Fundraising** — Collecting campaign contributions; large donations from organizations may create conflict of interest storylines
- **Public appearances** — Town halls, press conferences, debates
- **Endorsements** — Other political figures, organizations, or factions voicing support
- **Opposition research** — Digging into a rival's background for damaging information
- **Advertising** — Press releases, social media posts, flyers (IC newspapers, billboards)

#### 3. Debate
A formal, moderated exchange between candidates:
- Each candidate states their position on key issues
- Moderator enforces time limits
- Candidates may challenge each other's statements
- Audience may submit questions

#### 4. Voting
In RP, voting is typically run as:
- A structured community event
- A poll or server vote representing IC electoral participation
- Counted and certified by the City Clerk or election authority

#### 5. Certification and Transition
Winner is certified by the appropriate authority. Transition period may include briefings, staff handover, and formal inauguration or swearing-in ceremony.

## Legislation: How a Bill Becomes Law

### City Ordinance Process

| Step | Description |
|---|---|
| **Introduction** | Council member introduces the ordinance (proposed law) |
| **First Reading** | Title read aloud; referred to committee for review |
| **Committee Review** | Committee studies the proposal, holds hearings, may amend |
| **Public Comment Period** | Citizens may submit written or oral comments |
| **Second Reading** | Full council considers the ordinance with committee recommendations |
| **Vote** | Council votes; simple majority (or supermajority for certain types) required |
| **Mayoral Action** | Mayor signs (enacts), vetoes, or allows to pass without signature |
| **Veto Override** | Council may override a veto with a supermajority |

### State Legislation Process
Identical in principle, with:
- Two chambers (Senate and Assembly) both must pass identical versions
- Governor signs or vetoes
- Legislature may override a gubernatorial veto

## City Council Meetings

### Meeting Types

| Type | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Regular Meeting** | Scheduled business session; routine agenda |
| **Special Meeting** | Called for a specific urgent matter |
| **Emergency Meeting** | Convened for an immediate crisis |
| **Work Session** | Informal discussion without formal votes; used for deliberation |
| **Closed Session (Executive Session)** | Private meeting for personnel matters, litigation, or security issues |

### Meeting Procedure (Robert's Rules of Order)

| Step | Description |
|---|---|
| **Call to order** | Presiding officer declares the meeting open |
| **Roll call** | Confirm quorum (minimum members present to conduct business) |
| **Approval of minutes** | Last meeting's record approved |
| **Public comment** | Citizens may address the council on agenda items |
| **Consent agenda** | Routine items voted on together without discussion |
| **Regular agenda** | Each item discussed individually and voted on |
| **Council announcements** | Non-voting informational items |
| **Adjournment** | Meeting formally closed |

### Common Motions

| Motion | Effect |
|---|---|
| **Main motion** | Introduces a proposal for consideration |
| **Amend** | Modify the wording of a main motion |
| **Substitute** | Replace the main motion with a different one entirely |
| **Refer to committee** | Send the item to a committee for further study |
| **Table / Postpone** | Delay consideration to a later date |
| **Call the question** | End debate and proceed immediately to vote |
| **Point of order** | Challenge that the rules are being violated |

## Political Corruption Storylines

Political RP gains depth through corruption and conflict:

| Type | IC Manifestation |
|---|---|
| **Bribery** | Developer pays a council member to vote for a rezoning |
| **Kickback** | City contracts go to companies that funnel money back to officials |
| **Patronage** | Political supporters get government jobs regardless of qualifications |
| **Obstruction** | An official suppresses an investigation or fires a prosecutor |
| **Vote buying** | Paying or coercing individuals to vote a specific way |
| **Conflict of interest** | Official votes on a matter where they have a personal financial stake |

---

## Public Hearings and Community Engagement

| Event Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Town Hall** | Open meeting where officials take questions and comments from the public |
| **Public Hearing** | Formal proceeding to receive testimony on a specific proposal |
| **Community Forum** | Less formal; facilitated discussion of community issues |
| **Petition** | Citizens gather signatures requesting government action |
| **Protest / Demonstration** | Public expression of political opinion; may generate RP tension |
| **Press Conference** | Official statement and Q&A with media representatives |
