# Journalism Standards Reference

A guide to how journalism and media RP is conducted. Covers the principles of professional journalism, how to write a story, ethical standards, source protection, and how media characters interact with law enforcement, government, and criminals.

---

## Core Journalism Principles

A media character worth roleplaying should operate by recognizable professional standards. These create the friction and depth that makes journalism RP interesting.

| Principle | Description |
|---|---|
| **Accuracy** | Report only what can be verified. One source is not enough. |
| **Fairness** | Give all parties an opportunity to respond before publishing. |
| **Independence** | The newsroom is not a PR arm for the government, the police, or the mob. |
| **Accountability** | Journalists are answerable for their errors; corrections should be published. |
| **Minimize harm** | Consider the impact of publishing sensitive information, especially on victims. |
| **Seek truth** | The job is to inform the public, even when powerful people prefer silence. |

## Story Development Process

### Step 1: Tip or Assignment
- **Tip** — Information from a source (anonymous or named) suggesting a story exists
- **Assignment** — Editor directs coverage of a known event (court hearing, press conference, incident)

### Step 2: Verification
Before writing, a journalist verifies:
- **Two independent sources** for any major factual claim
- Documents, records, or physical evidence where possible
- The other side's response

### Step 3: Reporting
- Conduct interviews (note or record — inform subject they're being recorded if required)
- File public records requests for government documents
- Observe events directly
- Research background

### Step 4: Writing the Story

#### Inverted Pyramid Structure
The most important information goes first:

```
WHO / WHAT / WHEN / WHERE / WHY / HOW
         (most important)
              |
        Supporting detail
              |
     Background / Context
              |
        Least important
```

- **Lede (Lead)** — First sentence; answers the key questions immediately
- **Nut graf** — A sentence or two telling the reader why the story matters
- **Body** — Supporting evidence, quotes, detail
- **Tail** — Background, context, less critical information

### Step 5: Editor Review
Stories are reviewed by an editor for:
- Accuracy of facts
- Legal exposure (defamation risk)
- Fairness to all parties
- Clarity and structure

### Step 6: Publication
Story is published — in a city newspaper, broadcast over TV/radio, or posted online IC.

## Source Types and Confidentiality

| Source Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **On the record** | Source is named; everything they say may be quoted and attributed. |
| **On background** | Information may be used but the source is not named. *"According to a senior official..."* |
| **Deep background** | Information may guide reporting but cannot be quoted or referenced at all. |
| **Off the record** | Cannot be used in any form. May only be used to seek confirmation from other sources. |
| **Anonymous source** | Source is known to the journalist but not identified in the story. Editor should know who they are. |
| **Confidential source** | Reporter may protect the identity even from editors in sensitive cases. |

**Shield protection:** Journalists are generally protected from being compelled to reveal their sources by shield laws. In RP, this creates conflict when law enforcement or courts demand source disclosure.

## Press Credentials and Access

| Credential | What It Grants |
|---|---|
| **Press pass** | Identifies a journalist as media; may grant access to press pools and briefings |
| **Press pool access** | One journalist attends and shares information with others when space is limited |
| **Media briefing** | Scheduled press briefing from a government office, police spokesperson, or organization |
| **Embedded** | Journalist travels with a unit (law enforcement, military) with agreed coverage limitations |
| **Press conference** | Open event where an official makes a statement and takes questions |

## Working the Beat

### Law Enforcement Beat
- Build relationships with PIO (Public Information Officer)
- Attend daily or weekly press briefings
- FOIA/records requests for incident reports, crime statistics
- Observe court proceedings
- Cultivate sources within the department (carefully — never compromise an investigation)

### Political Beat
- Attend all public government meetings; document
- Develop sources in political offices
- Request government documents, contracts, budgets
- Follow the money — public financial disclosures are fair game
- Maintain relationships across party lines

### Criminal / Investigative Beat
- Cultivate sources in the community, not just official channels
- Approach with care — criminal sources are often unreliable or self-interested
- Document what you're told; triangulate with official records
- Never pay for information or offer protection for news value
- Understand the risk of being used as a conduit for misinformation

## Ethical Scenarios in RP

### Exclusive Tip from a Criminal
A criminal offers a reporter an exclusive story in exchange for favorable coverage or burying a different story. The ethical response: report the tip; don't make deals.

### Government Asks You to Suppress a Story
A government official claims publishing will endanger ongoing operations. The ethical response: independently assess the risk; don't automatically comply with censorship requests, but consider the genuine harm.

### Source Asks to Remain Anonymous
Standard in journalism. Protect the source. Do not reveal their identity even under pressure, unless the source lied to you.

### Publishing vs. Protecting a Victim
A crime victim shares their story but later asks you not to publish. The ethical response: consider the public interest; give significant weight to victim safety and consent.

## Story Types

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Breaking news** | First report of an event as it unfolds |
| **Follow-up** | Update on a previously reported story |
| **Investigative piece** | Deep, documented reporting on systemic wrongdoing |
| **Feature story** | In-depth profile or narrative exploration of a topic |
| **Editorial / Opinion** | The writer's own argued perspective; clearly labeled as opinion |
| **Op-ed** | Opinion piece by someone other than staff |
| **Profile** | In-depth character study of a person |
| **Column** | Regular opinion feature by a named writer |
