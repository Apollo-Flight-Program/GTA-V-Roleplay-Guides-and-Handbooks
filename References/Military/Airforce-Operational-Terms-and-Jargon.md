# Airforce Operational Terms, Military Jargon, and Callouts

A comprehensive reference of terminology used in military aviation roleplay. This covers standard callouts used on radio, aviation-specific jargon, tactical terms, and in-character language used by SAMAF and military aviation personnel.

Use this as a study reference before flying and as a quick lookup during training. Knowing the language is as important as knowing the aircraft.

---

## SECTION 1: RADIO CALLOUTS AND TRANSMISSION TERMS

These are the spoken words and phrases used over aviation radio. They have precise, standardized meanings. Using them correctly signals professionalism. Using them incorrectly creates confusion that can cost lives.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Affirm / Affirmative** | Yes — confirmed. (Never "yeah," "yep," or "yes.") |
| **Negative** | No. Hard, clear, no ambiguity. |
| **Copy** | I have received and understood your transmission. |
| **Roger** | I have received your last transmission. (Does not confirm compliance — just receipt.) |
| **Wilco** | Will comply. Confirms you received the order AND will execute it. Combines "Roger" and "I will do it." |
| **Over** | My transmission is complete. I expect a response from you. |
| **Out** | Transmission complete. This conversation is finished. No response expected. |
| **Say Again** | Repeat your last transmission. ("Say again" — not "repeat," which in military contexts means requesting artillery fire on the same target.) |
| **Stand By** | Wait — do not respond yet. I am occupied or gathering information. |
| **Go Ahead** | I am ready to receive your transmission. Proceed. |
| **Break** | Interrupting a transmission to pass priority information. Used sparingly. |
| **Break Break Break** | Emergency interruption. Urgent traffic follows immediately. Clear the frequency. |
| **Come In** | Requesting a response from a specific station. |
| **Radio Check** | "Can you hear me? How is my signal?" |
| **Loud and Clear / Lima Charlie** | Your signal is good — I can hear you clearly. |
| **Weak and Unreadable** | Poor signal — I can barely hear you. Reposition or relay. |
| **Read Back** | Repeat what I just told you back to me so I can confirm you received it correctly. |
| **All Stations** | Transmission addressed to every unit on this frequency. |
| **How Copy** | How well did you receive that transmission? / Do you understand? |
| **Relay** | Transmit this message to another station on my behalf. |
| **Wait Out** | Stand by — I will respond shortly but not immediately. |
| **Acknowledge** | Confirm to me that you received this transmission. |
| **Unable** | I cannot comply with your request or instruction. |
| **Standby for traffic** | I have information to pass — be ready to copy. |
| **Correction** | I made an error in my last transmission — what follows is the correct information. |
| **Nothing Follows** | There is no additional information after this transmission. |
| **Figures** | Spoken before numbers to clarify that what follows is a numerical value. Example: "Distance is figures five kilometers." |

## SECTION 2: FLIGHT STATUS AND POSITION CALLS

These terms describe the aircraft's current status and what it is doing. Used on Air Ops and with command.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Airborne** | The aircraft has lifted off and is now in flight. |
| **On Deck** | On the ground. Aircraft has landed or has not yet departed. |
| **Climbing** | Aircraft is ascending. |
| **Descending** | Aircraft is losing altitude. |
| **Level** | Aircraft is flying at a constant altitude. |
| **Inbound** | Heading toward a destination — base, target, LZ. |
| **Outbound** | Heading away from base or a specific point. |
| **Established** | Aircraft has settled into a pattern, orbit, or heading. "Established on final." |
| **On Final** | Flying the final approach leg toward the runway. Landing is imminent. |
| **Go Around** | Aborting the landing approach — climbing back up to attempt the approach again. |
| **Bingo Fuel** | Fuel has reached the minimum level required to return to base with a safe reserve. Return immediately. |
| **Winchester** | All weapons have been expended. The aircraft has no more ordnance. |
| **Joker Fuel** | Fuel level at which you begin planning your return — above bingo, still time to act but it's time to think about it. (Less formal than bingo, used as a personal awareness call.) |
| **RTB** | Return to Base. Coming home. |
| **Feet Dry** | The aircraft has crossed from over water to over land. |
| **Feet Wet** | The aircraft has crossed from over land to over water. |
| **Pushing** | Departing the current position or area toward the target or next waypoint. "Pushing to the objective." |
| **Holding** | Circling or loitering in a defined area, waiting for instructions. |
| **Egress** | Departing the target area after a strike or mission action. |
| **On Station** | Arrived at the assigned operating area and ready to perform the mission. |
| **Tally** | I have visual contact with the specified aircraft or target. "Tally on the bandit." |
| **No Joy** | I do not have visual contact with the specified target. "No joy on the target." |
| **Blind** | I have lost sight of the friendly aircraft I was tracking. |
| **Visual** | I have the friendly aircraft in sight. (Used for friendly contacts — "Tally" is used for enemy/target.) |
| **Padlocked** | Eyes are fixed on a specific target — I am not looking at anything else right now. |
| **Heads Up** | Pay attention — something of interest is coming. |
| **Check Six** | Look behind you. Six o'clock is directly behind the aircraft. Typically a warning. |
| **Bogey** | Unidentified aircraft — not yet confirmed hostile or friendly. |
| **Bandit** | Confirmed hostile aircraft. |
| **Friendly** | Confirmed friendly aircraft. |
| **Spike** | Aircraft has been locked by an enemy radar or missile system — threat warning. |
| **Fox One / Two / Three** | Weapon type declarations when firing air-to-air missiles. (Fox One: semi-active radar. Fox Two: heat-seeking/infrared. Fox Three: active radar.) |
| **Guns Guns Guns** | Declaring cannon employment — pilot is firing the gun. |
| **Rifle** | Air-to-ground missile fired. "Rifle" announces the release. |
| **Pickle** | Weapons release — the bomb or missile has been dropped or fired. |
| **Lasing** | Designating a target with a laser for a laser-guided weapon. |

## SECTION 3: FORMATION AND FLIGHT DISCIPLINE TERMS

These terms govern how aircraft fly together. Formation discipline prevents collisions, maintains tactical integrity, and keeps the flight coordinated.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Lead / Flight Lead** | The primary aircraft in a formation. All decisions flow from lead. |
| **Wingman** | The aircraft flying in support of and beside the lead. |
| **Flight of Two / Three / Four** | Indicates how many aircraft are in the formation. "Flight of two" = two aircraft. |
| **Element** | A sub-unit within a larger formation. A flight of four typically breaks into two elements of two. |
| **Dash Two / Dash Three** | Positional designation for aircraft in a formation. Lead is Dash One, second is Dash Two, etc. Often shortened — "Two, crossing right" means the second aircraft is crossing to the right. |
| **Spread** | The formation is widening — aircraft are increasing lateral separation. |
| **Contract** | The formation is tightening — aircraft are reducing lateral separation. |
| **Rejoin** | Return to close formation after being separated. |
| **Echelon** | A diagonal line formation — all aircraft staggered off one wingtip of lead. |
| **Trail** | Aircraft follow in a column one behind the other. Used for canyon transits, LZ approaches. |
| **Vic / Victor** | V-shaped formation — lead forward, two wingmen behind to each side. |
| **Line Abreast** | Aircraft fly side by side in a horizontal line. Used for sweep searches and attack approaches. |
| **Combat Spread** | Wide line abreast with large separation (500m+). Used for mutual support in threat environments. |
| **Crossing** | Calling your movement when crossing behind or in front of another aircraft. "Two, crossing right." |
| **Extending** | Increasing separation from the lead or the threat — pulling away. |
| **Breaking** | Executing a hard turn — typically a defensive or tactical maneuver. "Breaking left." |
| **Pitching Out** | Departing the formation to establish separation for individual landing approaches. |
| **Deconflict** | Ensuring aircraft are not going to occupy the same airspace at the same time. Altitudes and routes must be deconflicted before multi-ship missions. |
| **Stack** | Aircraft separated by altitude above and below. Used for holding and approach spacing. |
| **Two has lead** | The second aircraft in the flight is assuming the lead position — typically when the original lead is incapacitated, fuel-critical, or mission-complete. |

## SECTION 4: CLOSE AIR SUPPORT (CAS) TERMS

These are the specific terms used during CAS coordination between ground forces and aviation assets. Ground commanders and pilots both need to know these.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **9-Line** | The nine-element format used to pass a Close Air Support request from ground to air. Standardized worldwide. |
| **IP (Initial Point)** | A known landmark used as the starting reference point for the attack run. |
| **Offset** | The attack is not coming from directly above the target but from an angle — specified as left or right of the attack heading. |
| **Run-In Heading** | The direction the aircraft flies on the final attack approach, measured in degrees magnetic. |
| **Cleared Hot** | The ground commander authorizes the aircraft to fire on this pass. Required for every weapons pass. |
| **Abort** | Cancel the attack run immediately. Do not fire. Pilot exits the target area and confirms the abort. |
| **In** | The aircraft is beginning its attack run. "Viper One, IN from the north." |
| **Off** | The aircraft has completed the pass and is exiting the target area. "Viper One, OFF north." |
| **Splash** | Ordnance has impacted. Used to tell the ground commander to observe for effects. |
| **BDA (Battle Damage Assessment)** | The pilot's report of what was observed after the strike — what was destroyed, damaged, or missed. |
| **Mark** | The ground element is marking the target with smoke, laser, strobe, or IR beacon. "I am marking with green smoke." |
| **Identify the Mark** | The pilot calls what they see before confirming it is the correct target. "I see green smoke, confirm." |
| **Friendlies** | The ground commander's declaration of where their own forces are relative to the target. This is the most critical element of the brief. |
| **Talk On** | The pilot does not have the target in sight and needs the ground controller to guide them verbally. "I am talk on, south of the ridge." |
| **JTAC (Joint Terminal Attack Controller)** | The ground-based specialist who coordinates air support. JTAC has authority to clear pilots hot. |
| **Terminal Control** | The JTAC or forward observer is now directly guiding the aircraft to the target. |
| **Push** | Directing the pilot to move their aiming point or approach in a specific direction. "Push south." |
| **Call Complete** | The ground element confirms they do not need further air support at this time. |
| **Holding East / South / etc.** | Where the aircraft is orbiting while waiting for the CAS brief to be complete or for clearance. |
| **Time on Target (TOT)** | The specified time at which the attack must occur. Coordinated precision timing with ground maneuvers. |
| **Playtime** | How much time the aircraft has remaining on station before fuel forces it to leave. "My playtime is twenty minutes." |

## SECTION 5: TRANSPORT AND LANDING ZONE TERMS

Used during tactical transport operations, troop insertions, and landing zone coordination.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **LZ (Landing Zone)** | A designated area where a helicopter will land to insert or extract personnel. |
| **DZ (Drop Zone)** | An area designated for an airdrop — parachute insertion or cargo drop. |
| **PZ (Pickup Zone)** | An area designated for picking up troops or cargo. |
| **HLZ (Helicopter Landing Zone)** | A more specific term for a rotary-wing landing zone, distinct from fixed-wing LZs. |
| **LZ Hot** | The landing zone is under fire or threatened by hostile activity. Approach with caution or abort. |
| **LZ Cold** | The landing zone is clear of immediate threats. Safe to land. |
| **LZ Green** | The landing zone is secure, marked, and ready to receive the aircraft. |
| **LZ Secure** | Friendly forces have cleared the landing zone of threats and are in position to protect it. |
| **Clear to Land / Cleared In** | The ground element has confirmed the LZ is safe and the aircraft may land. |
| **Abort LZ** | Do not land — the LZ is compromised, hot, or unsuitable. |
| **Divert** | Go to an alternate LZ or destination. "Divert to LZ Charlie." |
| **Pop Smoke** | Instruction for the ground element to deploy a smoke grenade to mark their position. |
| **Identify Smoke** | The pilot has seen smoke and is calling its color to confirm it is the right marking. Ground confirms. |
| **Touchdown** | The aircraft has landed. |
| **Short Final** | The aircraft is on the last portion of the approach and will land very shortly. |
| **Hover Taxi** | The helicopter is moving low and slow while still airborne — transitioning between positions on the LZ. |
| **Troops In** | Personnel are boarding the aircraft. |
| **Troops Out** | Personnel are exiting the aircraft. Do not move the aircraft until troops are clear. |
| **Clear of the Blade** | A ground safety call — personnel have moved far enough from the helicopter that rotor downwash and the rotor arc are no longer a hazard. |
| **Sling Load** | External cargo carried beneath a helicopter by a cable or hook. |
| **Approach From the South / North / etc.** | Ground element directing the aircraft's approach direction — typically to avoid terrain or threats. |
| **Offset Approach** | The aircraft approaches the LZ from an angle to reduce exposure to any known threat. |
| **Lost Comms with Ground** | The pilot has lost radio contact with the receiving ground element. Standard action: abort and hold until contact is reestablished. |

## SECTION 6: SEARCH AND RESCUE / CSAR TERMS

Used during Combat Search and Rescue (CSAR) and general search operations.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **CSAR (Combat Search and Rescue)** | The recovery of downed aircrew or isolated personnel from a contested or hostile environment. |
| **SAR (Search and Rescue)** | General search and rescue — can be non-combat. |
| **Dustoff** | A medical evacuation helicopter mission. Also used as a callsign for medevac helicopters. Historically refers to the helicopter lifting dust on landing — now standard medevac slang. |
| **MEDEVAC** | Medical Evacuation — transporting wounded personnel to a medical facility. |
| **CASEVAC (Casualty Evacuation)** | Non-medical evacuation of casualties — moving wounded without a dedicated medical crew. Distinct from MEDEVAC. |
| **Survivor** | A downed aircrew member or isolated personnel awaiting rescue. |
| **Authentication** | A pre-briefed code or challenge and response used to confirm the survivor is genuine and not a trap or a coerced transmission. |
| **SERE Code** | Survival, Evasion, Resistance, and Escape authentication code. Briefed before every mission. Survivor uses it to confirm identity. |
| **Evading** | The survivor is moving to avoid enemy contact while awaiting rescue. |
| **Compromised** | The survivor's location is known to the enemy. The situation is now time-critical. |
| **Hold for Authentication** | The rescue aircraft has heard the survivor but will not proceed until authentication is confirmed — prevents flying into a trap. |
| **Feet Dry / Feet Wet** | Whether the survivor is over land or over water. Changes the rescue approach entirely. |
| **Pop Smoke / Flash Strobe / Mirror** | Survivor marking procedures to help rescuers identify their location. |
| **Extract** | Recovering the survivor — either by landing, hoist, or fast rope extraction. |
| **Hot Extract** | Extracting under fire — the helicopter may not land, crew descends on ropes or the survivor must move to the aircraft. |
| **Package** | The survivor and rescue team together — also used to describe everything the rescue operation consists of. |

## SECTION 7: THREAT AND TACTICAL AWARENESS TERMS

Used to describe threats, tactical states, and enemy activity.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **THREAT** | A general call that a hazard has been identified — typically follows with the type and direction. "Threat, ground fire, left two o'clock." |
| **Hostile** | Confirmed enemy — authorized to engage under applicable ROE. |
| **Neutral** | Not identified as friend or foe — do not engage. |
| **Squawking** | Broadcasting a transponder code — used to identify aircraft to radar. |
| **Squawking Emergency** | Aircraft is broadcasting the emergency transponder code (7700) — distress. |
| **Squawking Hijack** | Aircraft is broadcasting the hijack code (7500) — aircraft under unlawful interference. |
| **Squawking Lost Comms** | Aircraft is broadcasting the lost communications code (7600). |
| **BRAA** | Bearing, Range, Altitude, Aspect. A standard format for passing target information air-to-air. "Bandit, BRAA, 270 / 15 miles / 2,000 feet / hot." |
| **Bogey Dope** | Requesting updated information on the location of an unidentified contact. "Give me bogey dope." |
| **Picture** | A summary of the tactical air situation — what contacts are out there and where. "Give me a picture." |
| **Clean** | No radar or visual contacts in the area. The sky is clear. |
| **Mud** | Surface-to-air radar contact detected. Ground-based threat is scanning or tracking. |
| **Singer** | A surface-to-air missile system has locked onto the aircraft. |
| **Magnum** | HARM (anti-radiation missile) fired at a radar emitter. (Contextual — used when suppressing air defenses.) |
| **Defending** | The aircraft is taking evasive action against an air-to-air or air-to-surface threat. |
| **Drag** | Flying away from a threat to create separation — typically drawing an enemy away from a protected area or buying time. |
| **Crank** | Turning the aircraft to maintain radar lock while increasing separation — defensive geometry. |
| **Notch** | Flying perpendicular to a threat to defeat radar tracking geometry. |
| **Flares / Chaff** | Defensive countermeasures. Flares defeat heat-seeking missiles. Chaff defeats radar-guided missiles. |
| **Spiked** | A missile or radar has acquired the aircraft. Immediate defensive action required. |
| **Safe** | The threat has disengaged or been defeated. Aircraft is no longer being targeted. |
| **Splash (Air-to-Air)** | Enemy aircraft has been shot down. "Splash one bandit." |
| **Knocked it Off** | Call to stop an engagement or training maneuver. Everyone returns to a neutral state. Used in both training and live operations to de-escalate. |
| **Abort** | Stop the current action immediately. In CAS, abort is absolute. In air combat, it ends an engagement. |

## SECTION 8: AIRFIELD AND GROUND OPERATIONS TERMS

Terms used at the airfield, on the flight line, and during ground operations.

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Ramp** | The paved area of an airfield used for aircraft parking, loading, and servicing. Also called the apron. |
| **Flight Line** | The area of the airfield where aircraft are parked and maintained. A restricted area. |
| **Hangar** | A covered structure for housing and maintaining aircraft. |
| **Hardstand** | A designated aircraft parking area on the ramp. Each aircraft has an assigned hardstand. |
| **Arming Area** | The location where weapons are loaded and unloaded on aircraft. Separate from the main hangar for safety. |
| **Hot Pad** | An area near the runway where alert aircraft are parked, armed, and ready for immediate launch. |
| **Crew Chief** | The enlisted specialist responsible for a specific aircraft's maintenance and airworthiness. Knows the aircraft better than anyone. |
| **Walkaround / Pre-Flight** | The physical inspection of an aircraft conducted before every flight. |
| **Squawk / Discrepancy** | A maintenance issue noted by the pilot or crew chief. Logged and must be addressed before the aircraft flies. |
| **Red X** | A maintenance notation that grounds an aircraft — it is not airworthy and cannot fly. A red X is never overruled by a pilot. |
| **Cleared to Taxi** | Tower has authorized the aircraft to move on the ground toward the runway. |
| **Holding Point** | The designated stop position before entering the runway. Aircraft hold here until cleared for takeoff. |
| **Runway Incursion** | An unauthorized entry onto an active runway. One of the most dangerous events at an airfield. |
| **Cleared for Takeoff** | Tower has authorized the aircraft to begin its takeoff roll on the active runway. |
| **Cleared to Land** | Tower has authorized the aircraft to land on the specified runway. |
| **Go Around** | Tower instructs the aircraft to abort the landing approach and go around again. |
| **Line Up and Wait** | The aircraft is cleared to position on the runway but not yet cleared to take off. |
| **Expedite** | Move faster than normal — Tower needs you to clear the runway or taxiway promptly. |
| **Traffic in Sight** | The pilot has visually identified the other aircraft that Tower mentioned as a traffic advisory. |
| **Wilco Altimeter** | Acknowledgment when Tower passes the altimeter setting — pilot will set it. |
| **Hot Refueling** | Refueling an aircraft with the engine still running — used for quick turnarounds. Hazardous — only qualified crew. |
| **Shutdown** | Engines off. Flight is complete. |
| **Chocked** | Wheel chocks placed under the aircraft — it cannot roll. Aircraft is secure. |
| **Marshaller** | A ground crew member who directs aircraft with hand signals during ground movement. |

## SECTION 9: COMMUNICATION AND FREQUENCY TERMS

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Guard** | The emergency frequency — monitored by all aircraft at all times. Transmit on Guard only for emergencies or interceptions. |
| **Air Ops** | The internal military aviation frequency for air-to-air and air-to-command coordination. |
| **Tower** | The air traffic control frequency for the airfield — arrivals, departures, ground movement. |
| **Ground Tactical** | The frequency used to coordinate with ground forces during operations. |
| **Command Net** | The command-level radio net — used for reporting to and receiving orders from the Air Wing Commander. |
| **Primary / Secondary** | Most aircraft carry two radios. Primary is the frequency you are actively using. Secondary is a backup or supplemental channel. |
| **Frequency Change** | Call when switching between frequencies. "Viper One, switching to Air Ops. Out." |
| **Monitor** | Listening on a frequency without actively transmitting. "Monitor Guard at all times." |
| **Net** | A radio network — a group of stations using the same frequency for a coordinated purpose. |
| **Crypto** | Encrypted communications. "Going crypto" means switching to encrypted channel. |
| **Clear Text** | Non-encrypted transmission. If sensitive information must be passed on clear text, use brevity codes to reduce the risk. |
| **Brevity Code** | Standardized short words and phrases (like those in this document) that pass complex information efficiently and reduce radio time. |
| **Zulu (Z) Time** | Coordinated Universal Time — the standard time reference for all military operations. All mission timings are in Zulu. |
| **Local (L) Time** | Local time at the operating location. Distinguish clearly in all communications whether a time is Zulu or Local. |

## SECTION 10: NAVIGATION AND POSITION TERMS

| Term / Callout | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Grid** | A coordinate reference for a specific ground location — passed as a series of numbers. |
| **Waypoint** | A defined point in space (by GPS, grid, or landmark) used for navigation. |
| **Checkpoint** | A visual or geographic reference point used to confirm the aircraft's position during a flight. |
| **Bearing** | A direction measured in degrees from north, used to describe the direction to or from a target or position. |
| **Range** | The distance to a target or position, in kilometers or nautical miles. |
| **Angels** | Altitude in thousands of feet. "Angels five" = 5,000 feet. |
| **Cherubs** | Altitude in hundreds of feet, used for low altitude operations. "Cherubs five" = 500 feet. |
| **AGL (Above Ground Level)** | Altitude measured from the ground directly below. Most relevant to low-level flight, approaches, and CAS. |
| **MSL (Mean Sea Level)** | Altitude measured from sea level. What an altimeter normally shows. |
| **Altitude Block** | An assigned band of altitudes in which an aircraft must remain. "Your block is angels three to angels five." |
| **Orbit** | Flying a circular or oval pattern around a fixed point. Used for surveillance, holding, and CAS waiting. |
| **Offset Orbit** | Orbiting at a distance from the actual target or point of interest — used to avoid revealing what you are watching. |
| **Fence** | The boundary of a hostile or restricted area. "Crossing the fence" means entering the threat environment. |
| **Egress Route** | The planned route out of the target area after completing the mission. Briefed beforehand. |
| **Transit** | Flying from one place to another — the travel portion of a mission. |
| **Ingress** | Flying into the target area on the planned approach. |
| **Wheel** | A circular holding pattern at a defined point and altitude. |
| **Push Point** | The location from which the aircraft departs toward the target at a specific time. |
| **Contact Point** | A location where two elements will meet — either aircraft to aircraft or air to ground. |

## SECTION 11: PERSONNEL AND UNIT TERMS

Military aviation has its own language for people, units, and roles.

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Aviator** | A qualified military pilot. Earned title — not given to students. |
| **IP (Instructor Pilot)** | A qualified pilot certified to train others. Signs off on qualifications. |
| **Check Pilot** | A pilot authorized to conduct formal flight evaluations. |
| **SIC (Second in Command)** | Co-pilot — the second pilot in a multi-crew aircraft. |
| **PIC (Pilot in Command)** | The pilot who has ultimate authority over the aircraft. In multi-crew, typically the senior or mission-assigned pilot. |
| **FNG** | "Friendly New Guy" — a new pilot who has just arrived and has not yet proven themselves. Not derogatory, just a label of inexperience. |
| **High Time** | A pilot with significant flight hours — experienced. "He's high time on the Lazer." |
| **Currency** | Maintaining required minimum flight hours in an aircraft type to stay qualified to fly it. Pilots who do not fly regularly become non-current and must requalify. |
| **RTU (Replacement Training Unit)** | The training unit where new pilots go to learn a specific aircraft type before joining an operational squadron. |
| **Stick and Rudder** | Basic flying skills — the fundamental physical act of controlling the aircraft. "Good stick and rudder" means the pilot is physically competent. |
| **NATOPS (or local equivalent)** | Naval Air Training and Operating Procedures — or whichever standardized procedures document governs the aircraft. The bible for how the aircraft is operated. |
| **Sortie** | One flight by one aircraft from takeoff to landing. A single operational or training flight. |
| **Hop** | Informal term for a flight or sortie. |
| **Mission** | A defined operational task assigned to an aircraft or formation. Multiple sorties can support one mission. |
| **Air Mission Commander (AMC)** | The officer responsible for coordinating all air assets supporting a given operation. |
| **Package** | The collection of all aircraft assigned to a coordinated strike or operation. The "strike package." |
| **SEAD (Suppression of Enemy Air Defenses)** | Missions designed to destroy or suppress enemy radar, missiles, and anti-aircraft systems so other aircraft can operate safely. |
| **CAP (Combat Air Patrol)** | Fighter aircraft orbiting in a defined area, ready to intercept and engage hostile aircraft. "Flying CAP" means maintaining this defensive posture. |
| **Alert** | Pilots on duty and ready to launch on short notice. "On alert" means you are in the ready room, in flight gear, waiting for the scramble call. |
| **Scramble** | An immediate launch order for alert aircraft. The mission is already in progress and you are needed now. |
| **Stand Down** | An order to cease operations. Aircraft do not fly. Personnel are released from operational readiness. |
| **Debrief** | The post-mission review where every action taken during the flight is examined — what went right, what went wrong, what to improve. |

## SECTION 12: COMMON BREVITY PHRASES IN CONTEXT

These are examples of how the terms above are used in actual transmissions. Reading these helps internalize the rhythm of aviation communications.

**Formation check-in:**
> *"Viper flight, check in."*
> *"Two." "Three." "Four."*
> *(Each aircraft calls its position number to confirm they are on frequency and ready.)

**Target contact:**
> *"Viper One, tally on the target — single truck, stationary, east side of the compound, no joy on the second vehicle."*
> *(I can see the first vehicle but not the second.)

**Weapons call:**
> *"Viper One, IN from the north."*
> *"Viper One, cleared hot."*
> *"Rifle. Off north. Splash."*
> *(Attack run: entering, cleared by ground, missile fired, exiting, ordnance impacted.)

**Bingo fuel:**
> *"Air Ops, Viper One, bingo fuel. Fifteen minutes of playtime remaining. Requesting relief or RTB authorization."*
> *(I am low on fuel — what do you want me to do?)

**LZ coordination:**
> *"Dustoff One, LZ is green. Pop smoke is purple. Friendlies are all north of the smoke. Cleared in."*
> *"I see purple. Inbound LZ, two minutes."*
> *(Ground marks the LZ, pilot confirms the mark, cleared to land.)

**MAYDAY:**
> *"Mayday, mayday, mayday. Viper Two, bird strike, engine failure, one mile east of Zancudo, six hundred feet, one soul, attempting forced landing."*

**Lost wingman:**
> *"Air Ops, Viper One. Lost sight of Viper Two, last known three miles south of the ridge at angels two. Initiating search."*

**Interception:**
> *"Unidentified aircraft on Guard — San Andreas Military. You are in restricted airspace. Turn heading 090 immediately and follow the intercepting aircraft to Sandy Shores. This is your final warning."*

## SECTION 13: DO NOT SAY LIST

These are common mistakes in aviation radio communications. Using them marks you as unprofessional and creates confusion.

| Wrong | Correct | Why |
|---|---|---|
| "Over and out" | Either "Over" OR "Out" | Over = awaiting reply. Out = finished. They are mutually exclusive. |
| "Repeat" (when asking for a retransmission) | "Say again" | In military, "Repeat" means fire the same artillery mission again. Say "Say again" for retransmissions. |
| "Yeah," "Yep," "No problem" | "Affirm," "Negative," "Wilco" | Casual language on radio sounds unprofessional and can be misheard. |
| "I'm going to..." | "[Callsign] is proceeding to..." | Use your callsign and third-person reference. "I" is never used on military radio. |
| "Uh," "Um," pauses mid-transmission | Think before you key | Dead air on the radio while you think wastes frequency time and sounds unprofessional. |
| "10-4" | "Copy" or "Roger" | 10-codes are for police radio. Military aviation uses standard ICAO brevity. |
| Skipping your callsign | Always open with your callsign | If no one knows who is talking, the transmission is useless. |
